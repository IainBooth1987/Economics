Street Light
Economics
Your street is dark. Someone needs to fund a light — but once it's on, everyone benefits whether they paid or not. Can rational self-interest ever produce collective good? Play to find out.

Economics concepts illustrated by this experiment
Public good
The street light is non-excludable (you cannot stop any neighbour benefiting once it's on) and non-rivalrous (one person's use doesn't reduce availability to others). These two features make voluntary private provision unreliable — why pay when you'll benefit either way?
Free rider problem
The dominant strategy is to contribute £0. Each player reasons: "If others fund the light, I benefit for free. If nobody does, at least I kept my money." This is the Nash equilibrium — individually rational, collectively catastrophic. The street stays dark.
Market failure
Free markets under-provide public goods. Full cooperation earns each player £16/round with a lit street. The Nash equilibrium earns £10 with darkness. The £6/player/round difference is deadweight welfare loss — value that could exist but doesn't.
Government solution
Taxation solves the free rider problem by making contribution compulsory — the real-world equivalent of a council tax levy for street lighting. No opt-out means the light is funded regardless of individual incentives. This is why councils, not private markets, provide street lighting.
Tragedy of the commons
Related but distinct: the tragedy involves over-use of a shared resource (e.g. overfishing). The public goods problem involves under-provision. Same root cause — individual incentives misaligned with collective welfare — but different directions of failure.
