🎣 Tragedy of the Commons: A Strategic Economics Game

How to Play

You are the owner of a fishing company competing with 5 other firms for a shared ocean fishery. Each round, you must decide how aggressively to fish.

Your Goal: Maximize your profit over 10 rounds while managing the shared resource sustainably.

Game Mechanics

Phase 1 - Choose Your Catch: Drag the slider to select exactly how many fish to catch — from 0 (sustainable) to 120 (maximum exploitation)

Phase 2 - Deploy Nets: Your fishing fleet automatically catches the targeted amount based on your strategy

Phase 3 - Market Results: See your profit, competitor actions, and ecosystem impact plus strategic analysis

Fish Population: Regenerates between rounds, but overfishing can lead to collapse!

Strategic Considerations

• More aggressive fishing = higher short-term profits but depletes the shared resource

• If population drops too low, the fishery collapses and the game ends

• Competitors react to market conditions and your reputation

• Random events (weather, disease) add uncertainty and risk

• Prices fluctuate based on total market supply

🎯 Tragedy of the Commons

Coined by Garrett Hardin (1968), this concept explains how individuals acting independently and rationally according to their self-interest behave contrary to the group's long-term best interests by depleting a shared resource.

🔄 Market Failure & Externalities

Negative Externality: When your fishing imposes costs on others (resource depletion) that aren't reflected in your private decision-making. This is a classic market failure where the free market produces an inefficient outcome.

Private Cost vs Social Cost: Your private cost is just your fishing expenses, but the social cost includes the ecosystem damage affecting everyone.

🎲 Game Theory: The Prisoner's Dilemma

Nash Equilibrium: Each firm's dominant strategy is to overfish (since others will do the same), leading to collective disaster even though mutual cooperation would benefit everyone.

Cooperative Outcome: If all firms fish conservatively, the ecosystem thrives and provides sustainable profits indefinitely.

The Dilemma: Individual rationality (maximizing personal catch) conflicts with collective rationality (sustainable fishing).

🏛️ Common Pool Resources

Fish stocks are a common pool resource - they are rivalrous (one person's catch reduces availability for others) but non-excludable (hard to prevent access without regulation).

Unlike pure public goods, common pool resources are vulnerable to overuse and depletion, requiring governance mechanisms.

💡 Solutions to Tragedy of the Commons

1. Government Regulation: Quotas, licenses, protected areas (command-and-control)

2. Property Rights: Assign individual transferable quotas (ITQs) - market-based solution

3. Community Management: Local agreements and social pressure (Elinor Ostrom's work)

4. Technology: Monitoring, enforcement, and information systems

Real-World Fishery Collapses

🇨🇦 Grand Banks Cod Fishery (1992)

Location: Newfoundland, Canada

History: For 500 years, the Grand Banks supported one of the world's richest fishing grounds. European and Canadian fleets caught millions of tonnes of Atlantic cod annually.

The Collapse: In 1992, the Canadian government imposed a complete moratorium after cod populations fell to 1% of historical levels. 40,000 people lost their jobs.

Cause: Technological improvements (larger boats, better nets, sonar) allowed massive overfishing. Each nation and company had incentives to maximize catch before others did.

Legacy: 30+ years later, the cod population has still not recovered despite the complete fishing ban. The ecosystem permanently changed.

🇵🇪 Peruvian Anchoveta Fishery (1972)

Location: Pacific Ocean off Peru

History: Peru developed the world's largest fishery in the 1960s, catching 12 million tonnes of anchoveta annually for fishmeal production.

The Collapse: In 1972, catches dropped by 75% due to overfishing combined with El Niño warming. The industry collapsed.

Cause: Open access and competitive fishing led to overcapacity. When El Niño reduced fish populations naturally, the weakened stocks couldn't recover.

Recovery: Peru later implemented strict quotas and Individual Vessel Quotas (IVQs), allowing partial recovery. Now managed more sustainably.

🇰🇪 Lake Victoria Nile Perch (1990s-2000s)

Location: Lake Victoria, East Africa (Kenya, Uganda, Tanzania)

History: Nile perch was introduced in the 1950s and became a valuable export commodity worth $300 million annually.

The Crisis: Overfishing decimated perch populations and destroyed native species. Local communities lost food security as fish became too expensive.

Cause: Lack of coordinated management among three countries. International demand drove intensive fishing. Small-scale fishers were forced to use illegal gear as large fish disappeared.

Economic Impact: Processing factories closed, thousands lost livelihoods, and local protein sources disappeared while exports continued.

📊 Common Patterns

• Open Access: No effective property rights or access controls

• Technological Improvement: Better fishing technology increased catch capacity beyond sustainable levels

• Short-term Incentives: Economic pressure to maximize current catch rather than preserve future stocks

• Delayed Recognition: By the time collapse was evident, it was too late to prevent

• Slow Recovery: Ecosystem changes and lost breeding populations make recovery extremely difficult

• Socioeconomic Impact: Communities dependent on fishing suffered severe poverty and displacement
