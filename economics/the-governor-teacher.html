What started as £4.6bn of panicking savers ended as £895bn of central bank money. Notice the order: a small bank run forced a single bank rescue, which forced a national rescue package, which forced an entirely new monetary tool — and that tool then got used again and again for over a decade. Crises escalate faster than policy can plan for, and tools introduced as "temporary emergency measures" tend to become permanent — that's the through-line of today's game. (Teacher — every figure is real and published: ① Northern Rock £4.6bn: BoE/Treasury estimates of deposit withdrawals within days of 14 Sept 2007, the UK's first bank run in over 150 years. ② RBS £45.8bn: total UK government investment in RBS across the October 2008 recapitalisation and the further November 2008 injection, peak 84% public ownership (Wikipedia/NAO/OBR figures). ③ UK rescue package £500bn: announced 8 Oct 2008 by PM Gordon Brown and Chancellor Alistair Darling — £50bn recapitalisation fund, £200bn Special Liquidity Scheme, £250bn Credit Guarantee Scheme. ④ First QE tranche £75bn: Bank of England MPC announcement, March 2009. ⑤ Total QE stock £895bn: Bank of England, peak holding by November 2020/end 2021, comprising £875bn gilts and £20bn corporate bonds, equivalent to over 40% of annual UK GDP per the Bank's own Independent Evaluation Office report.)

What Happens Next — the Exit Problem

★ Bonus: max 20 pts

The emergency is over. The Governor's hardest question comes last: every emergency tool has to be unwound eventually — so how do you get out without causing a new crisis? Unwind too fast and you risk strangling a fragile recovery; unwind too slow and you risk the inflation and asset bubbles the emergency money created.

THE BANK'S ANNUAL REPORT · THE UNWIND

The cure starts to look like the disease

The emergency tools worked — the recession that could have happened, didn't. But three years on, inflation is running hot and the same balance sheet that stabilised the crisis is now being unwound. The Bank starts reducing its bond holdings (quantitative tightening) while raising rates at the same time — tightening from two directions at once.

Teams: calculate the size of the U-turn — the percentage-point distance from the emergency low to the peak, and what that means for a mortgage holder's monthly payment on a £150,000 loan. Then answer: was printing the money worth the inflation that followed — and could it have been avoided?

The answers

5.15 percentage points of tightening (0.1% → 5.25%) → roughly +£640/month on a £150,000 repayment mortgage
Was it worth it?
  1. The counterfactual case: without the emergency cuts and QE, the 2008 recession (and the 2020 lockdown shock) would very plausibly have been deeper and longer — most independent assessments credit QE with helping prevent a repeat of the 1930s Great Depression.
  2. The cost that followed: ultra-loose policy for over a decade, plus huge fiscal spending and energy-price shocks, contributed to the inflation surge of 2021–23 — though economists still argue over how much of that was monetary policy versus supply shocks (energy, war, post-pandemic demand) that no interest rate could have prevented.
  3. Distributional cost: QE inflates the value of assets like shares and property. Research shows older, wealthier households — who own more assets — gained disproportionately, even though the policy was designed to help the whole economy.
  4. The lesson for next time: the tools that save you from one crisis can create the conditions for the next one. A central bank's job doesn't end when the recession does — it ends when the unwind is finished too.
