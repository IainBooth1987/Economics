Sequencing Guide

Running the Game in Order

Read this before the lesson
Before you start
  • Hand out student workbooks. Ask teams to read the Charter carefully. Do not say it contains clues — that is the trap.
  • Run the Starter first — unscored warm-up, 2 mins + reveal.
  • Work through Rounds 1–3 in sequence. Each round's diagnosis informs the next.
  • Round 4 is two-stage: (1) teams commit before the trial; (2) run the Trial tab on the console; (3) students calculate rates from raw counts; (4) reveal rates; (5) award points by trial result.
  • Run Round 5 last — the sludge trap only works if students have not been warned. Any team that re-read the Charter carefully will catch C and D.
  • The Bonus works in the same lesson or as homework.
The through-line

Charter → diagnosis (R1) → positioning (R2) → pricing the problem (R3) → design and test (R4 + Trial) → ethics and value (R5) → limits of nudging (Bonus). The deepest lesson: the most "effective" option (sludge in R5) is not the right one — and that is simultaneously an economics lesson and an ethics lesson.

Starter — Predict the Human

Correct Order and Effects

Unscored warm-up  ·  2 minutes + reveal
Correct ranking — biggest effect first
  1. Make it the default (auto-enrolment) — +43 percentage points. Private-sector pension participation 32% → 75% (ONS, 2012–2021). Over 10 million workers enrolled without being persuaded.
  2. One sentence in a tax letter — +5 points. Social norm letter accelerated over £9m in 23 days (Hallsworth et al., 2017, Journal of Public Economics).
  3. Redesign the fine notice — +3.1 points. On-time payment from under 15% to nearly 18% (NSW Behavioural Insights Unit).
  4. A text stating the cost — +2.7 points. NHS missed appointments 11.1% → 8.4%, replicated (Hallsworth et al., 2015, PLoS ONE).
  5. One question on a webpage — approx. +1 point. Organ donor sign-ups increased; rolled out nationally → 529,000 extra registrations by Oct 2017 (Sallis et al., 2018, Trials).
Teaching points after the reveal
  • The default beat everything by miles. Nobody persuaded anyone — the architecture did the work.
  • Don't dismiss "+1": on 17m road-tax renewals a year, one question produced over half a million organ donor registrations. Effect sizes look small; scaled populations make them large.
  • Ask: "Why did the default work so much better than a letter?" → connects to inertia and present bias — the diagnosis for Round 1.

Round 1 — Diagnose the Human

The Minister's Verdict

Max 10 pts·4 minutes
Correct ranking
  1. C — Present bias. "Most late payers eventually pay in full — they are not refusing, they are postponing." The deadline is distant; the hassle is now. Classic procrastination economics.
  2. E — Inertia & friction. "The payment form runs to fourteen pages." Every extra page is a reason to do it tomorrow. People don't fight friction — they obey it.
  3. B — Social norms. "In focus groups, late payers guessed 'half the country' pays late. The real figure is one in ten." A false belief that lateness is normal makes lateness feel safe.

Why the decoys don't fit:

  • A (loss aversion) is a lever a letter might pull, but nothing in the Charter says fear of loss is causing the lateness. People aren't weighing losses — they're procrastinating.
  • D (anchoring) and F (overconfidence) have no Charter evidence. A strong answer uses evidence in both directions: what fits and what doesn't.
Mark scheme
MarksCriterion
2 pts eachCorrect bias (C, E, or B) anywhere in top 3 — max 6 pts
+1 pt eachBias in exactly the right position (1st=C, 2nd=E, 3rd=B) — max 3 pts
+1 ptJustification quotes Charter evidence AND explains why a rejected bias (e.g. loss aversion) does not fit — evidence used both ways

Maximum 10
Common misconceptions
  • Loss aversion first: students have confused the cause of late payment with an intervention that might fix it. Ask: "What does the Charter actually say is causing the lateness?"
  • Overconfidence in top 3: "they think they'll remember" is plausible but has no Charter evidence. Strong answers only use evidence from the briefing.
  • Correct order with no quotations scores 6, not 10. Push for Charter evidence.
Debrief hook

"Which of your three diagnosed biases connects most directly to a letter intervention?" → Present bias and social norms both point toward the trial letters in Round 4. This is the lesson: diagnosis before design.

Round 2 — The Intervention Ladder

The Minister's Position

Max 10 pts·4 minutes
Correct answer: 4 or 5 — Nudge

Evidence pulling DOWN the ladder:

  • "I will not ban my way to a healthier Britain."
  • "No one should wake up to find choices made for them."

Evidence pulling UP from zero:

  • "Doing nothing is a choice with a body count."

The synthesis: "Where a gentle hand works, we will not raise a fist." Redesign the choice, keep every option open: 4–5 on the ladder.

This is libertarian paternalism (Thaler & Sunstein): steer toward better choices while leaving every option open. Teams at 8+ heard "government action" and stopped reading; teams at 1–2 heard "freedom" and did the same.

Common misconceptions
  • Defaulting to extremes: going to 8–10 ("he wants to fix the problem") or 0–1 ("he believes in freedom") without reading the Charter both ways. The question is about the Minister's position.
  • Students sometimes choose a rung while describing a different one. Clarify: the task is placing the Minister's philosophy, not designing an intervention.
A-Level concept: libertarian paternalism

After the reveal, name it explicitly: libertarian paternalism. It's paternalist in intent; libertarian in method. Ask: "Is that actually freedom, or just steering with extra steps?" — this is a live debate worth seeding for the essay.

Round 4 — Design the Letter

Trial Results and Marking

Max 10 pts·Commit → trial → reveal

Sequencing reminder: collect team commitments before running the trial. Award base points by trial result only after rates are revealed.

Common misconceptions
  • Choosing D on "more investment = commitment": students haven't separated attention from behavioural mechanism. A beautiful leaflet that doesn't change how someone thinks about their choice is expensive wallpaper.
  • Choosing C students reasoned well — don't penalise harshly. C is the second-strongest and its logic is well-grounded in the diagnosis.
  • Statistical vs practical significance: D's +0.7 is probably a real effect on 100,000 people — but at 8× the cost, it is economically trivial. Worth discussing this explicitly.
Why the social norm won

Nobody could have known B would beat C without testing — being confidently wrong is the default human condition, and the RCT is the cure. The norm corrected the false belief seeded in the Charter: late payers thought "half the country" pays late; hearing they're in a small minority was powerfully uncomfortable. And the deeper A-Level point: real HMRC trials found no evidence that loss-framed messages beat gain-framed ones — the threat's instinctive appeal is exactly the kind of intuition trials exist to check.

Common misconceptions
  • Choosing C: "auto-enrolment is standard." True — but legitimate defaults have an obvious opt-out. "Opt-out in clause 14" is not obvious. Charter line: "we never hide the exit."
  • Choosing D: "a deadline is just urgency framing." No — a fake deadline is a lie. Ask: "Would you defend this on the front page?"
  • Choosing F: ethically clean, mathematically disastrous. £1.5m for 30,000 people = £50 each vs £4.67 for A+B+E. Students who choose F often haven't calculated the cost per outcome.
The hardest question in the game

Ask teams who chose sludge: "Did the Charter tell you this would happen?" Yes — the line "we never hide the exit" was on page 1, before Round 1. The trap was always readable.

For clean-portfolio teams: "ABE reached 300,000 — exactly what the sludge promised. Did you lose anything by passing the publicity test?" In this case, no. But make them argue it.

When a nudge is NOT enough — strong answer points
  • When the harm is large and urgent: nobody nudges drink-driving. The bigger the externality, the stronger the case for a higher ladder rung.
  • When effects fade and the problem doesn't: the +12 → +6 fade proves nudges habituate; taxes and mandates keep working without anyone reading a letter.
  • When the behaviour is profitable to resist: firms re-engineer around nudges. A sugar levy changed recipes across an entire industry because it changed the price.
  • Counter-case (strong evaluation): nudging is nearly free, preserves choice, and politically survives where bans die — the smart unit treats the ladder as a toolkit, not a contest.
Essay connection

Every piece of evidence from the game — effect sizes, cost per outcome (£4.67), £108-per-£1 return, EAST framework, the fade, sludge — is KAA for the AQA 25-marker: "Evaluate the view that governments should rely on nudges rather than taxes and regulation." Strongest answers use the ladder as a framework: nudges are not competitors to taxes and bans, they are complements — deploy the gentlest effective tool and climb only when necessary.

Key Terms — Model Definitions

Glossary Answers

AQA-appropriate language. Students should have filled these in during the game.

Behavioural economics

The study of how psychological, cognitive and social factors affect economic decisions, producing predictable departures from the rational actor model.

Bounded rationality

The idea that individuals make decisions with limited information, time and cognitive capacity — satisficing rather than optimising.

Nudge

Any aspect of choice architecture that alters behaviour in a predictable way without forbidding options or significantly changing economic incentives. Every exit must remain open.

Choice architecture

The design of the environment in which choices are made. How options are presented influences which options are chosen, independently of their content.

Default / opt-out

The option that applies automatically unless someone actively chooses otherwise. Legitimate defaults keep the exit obvious; sludge hides it.

E.g. auto-enrolment in workplace pensions (legitimate); opt-out in clause 14 (sludge).

Loss aversion

The tendency for losses to feel approximately twice as painful as equivalent gains feel pleasurable (Kahneman & Tversky). Drives avoidance behaviour and regret.

Present bias

The tendency to over-weight immediate costs and benefits relative to future ones, leading to procrastination even when people intend to act differently.

E.g. "I'll pay the tax bill next week" — every week.

Social norms

Unwritten rules about what most people do or what is considered acceptable. Descriptive norms describe actual behaviour; injunctive norms describe what people should do.

Inertia / friction

The tendency to remain in the current state due to the effort required to change. Complexity and extra steps reduce take-up of even highly beneficial actions.

E.g. a 14-page tax form causes procrastination.

Framing effect

The same information presented differently produces different choices. A loss frame and a norm frame convey identical obligations but prompt different responses.

Anchoring

Judgements are influenced disproportionately by the first number seen, even when irrelevant.

E.g. a high suggested donation increases average donations.

EAST framework

BIT's practical design guide. Easy — reduce friction. Attractive — make it salient. Social — use norms. Timely — act at the right moment in the decision journey.

Sludge

Choice architecture designed to make it harder to exercise choices or opt out. It exploits biases rather than helping people overcome them. The opposite of a nudge.

Libertarian paternalism

The view that government may legitimately steer people towards better choices while preserving freedom of choice (Thaler & Sunstein, Nudge, 2008). Paternalist in intent; libertarian in method.

Randomised Controlled Trial (RCT)

An experiment in which participants are randomly assigned to treatment or control groups. Random assignment ensures groups are alike in every other way, so differences are caused by the treatment alone. The gold standard of causal evidence.

Status quo bias

A preference for the current state of affairs — any change is perceived as a loss relative to the default. Closely related to loss aversion and inertia.

Teacher Notes

Debrief, Extension and Exam Links

The Charter clue trail — what students were meant to find
  • Round 1: "they are not refusing, they are postponing" (present bias); "fourteen pages" (friction); "half the country / one in ten" (social norm misperception).
  • Round 2: "I will not ban my way…" (down) vs "doing nothing is a choice with a body count" (up) + "where a gentle hand works, we will not raise a fist" (synthesis).
  • Round 3: 1,000,000 / 60% / £900 / £50 — all in paragraph 3 of the Charter.
  • Round 5 disqualifier: "we never hide the exit" — one sentence that detonates on options C and D. This was on page 1 before the game started.
Extension tasks for high-attaining students
  • Calculate cost per outcome for each individual Round 5 option: A=£4.17 · B=£5 · C=£0.67 (sludge) · D=£2.67 (sludge) · E=£5 · F=£50.
  • Why does the social norm letter's effect fade in year two? What does this tell us about the durability of nudges vs taxes?
  • Redesign option C to make it pass the publicity test. What changes, and what is the cost to its effectiveness?
  • Read: Hallsworth, List, Metcalfe & Vlaev (2017), Journal of Public Economics — the real HMRC trial.
AQA 7136 exam links
  • 4.1.2 Behavioural economics: irrational behaviour, nudges, choice architecture, defaults, social norms — all directly assessed.
  • Market failure: information failures (the norm misperception in R1) are a form of market failure nudges can address.
  • Government intervention: the ladder maps directly to the spectrum of intervention the specification covers.
  • Evaluation skills: the fade, backfire risk, sludge distinction, and cost-effectiveness comparison are all genuine evaluative arguments for 25-mark essays.

Target essay: "Evaluate the view that governments should rely on nudges rather than taxes and regulation when seeking to change consumers' behaviour." (25 marks)
