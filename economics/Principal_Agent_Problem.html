📚 The Principal–Agent Problem as Information Failure

A principal–agent problemPrincipal–Agent ProblemArises when one party (principal) delegates a task to another (agent) whose actions cannot be perfectly observed. A form of market failure caused by information asymmetry. occurs whenever one person delegates decisions to another, but cannot perfectly observe what that person does. It is a direct consequence of asymmetric informationAsymmetric InformationA situation where one party to a transaction has more or better information than the other. Can cause markets to fail or produce inefficient outcomes. — one party knows more than the other.

Two problems arise from this information gap:

  • Adverse selectionAdverse SelectionPre-contractual information failure. The uninformed party (principal) cannot distinguish high-quality from low-quality agents before hiring. Bad types are disproportionately attracted. Linked to Akerlof's "Market for Lemons". — before hiring: you cannot verify a candidate's true ability
  • Moral hazardMoral HazardPost-contractual information failure. Once hired, the agent may reduce effort or take risks because the principal cannot fully observe their actions. The agent bears less than the full cost of their behaviour. — after hiring: you cannot perfectly observe whether they work hard

The result is a form of market failureMarket FailureWhen free markets allocate resources inefficiently. Information failure is one of four main causes (alongside externalities, public goods, and monopoly power). — resources are not allocated efficiently because contracts are incompleteIncomplete ContractA contract that cannot specify every possible action the agent might take or every contingency that might arise. The principal must use incentive mechanisms instead of direct instruction..

This simulation makes several simplifying assumptions to isolate key principles:

  • Manager quality varies (50–100 scale) but is unobservable — you can only infer it from signals sent by the contract
  • Effort is unobservable — you see outcomes but not inputs, the essence of the moral hazard problem
  • Contract design signals expectations — salary, autonomy, and monitoring all convey information to applicants (signalling theory)
  • Monitoring costs are real and non-linear — they scale exponentially with intensity
  • Single-period model — real relationships involve reputation effects over time, not modelled here
  • Risk premium not explicitly priced — in full models, risk-averse agents demand extra pay for variable income

Step 2: The Information Failure in Action

A critical decision reveals why asymmetric information matters

⚠️ Why This Creates a Problem

As the principal you must make a decision without knowing the agent's true type. The agent has private informationPrivate InformationInformation known to one party but not the other. When agents have private information about their ability or intentions, they may exploit the principal's ignorance. about their own risk appetite, effort, or intentions. This creates an opportunity for opportunistic behaviour — what economists call agency costsAgency CostsThe costs borne by the principal as a result of the agent acting in their own interest rather than the principal's. Includes monitoring costs, bonding costs, and residual loss from misaligned decisions..

🎯 Incentive Mechanisms

Because contracts are incompleteIncomplete ContractCannot specify every contingency. Requires incentive mechanisms to substitute for direct instruction. Source of ongoing agency problems., principals must use indirect tools. Your four levers reflect key economic mechanisms:

  • Base salary — reduces adverse selectionAdverse SelectionHigher wages attract higher-ability workers. Efficiency wage theory argues paying above market rate screens for quality and reduces turnover. by attracting better applicants
  • Performance bonus — tackles moral hazardMoral HazardLinking pay to outcomes aligns agent interests with principal's. But high bonuses may induce excessive risk-taking (as in the 2008 financial crisis). by aligning incentives with outcomes
  • Autonomy — signals trust and attracts confident, capable agents (signallingSignallingSpence (1973): contracts signal what type of agent the principal wants, affecting who applies. High autonomy + low monitoring signals trust; the opposite signals distrust.)
  • Monitoring — directly reduces shirking but has real costs and reduces intrinsic motivationIntrinsic MotivationMotivation from within — interest, pride, professional identity. Crowded out by excessive monitoring (crowding-out effect). Over-monitoring can reduce total effort.

✅ Core Economic Theory Covered

  • Adverse selection occurs before the contract — your design signals what type of agent you want and affects who applies
  • Moral hazard occurs after the contract — because effort is unobservable, agents may shirk or pursue misaligned goals
  • Incomplete contracts force principals to use indirect incentive mechanisms (bonuses, monitoring) rather than direct instruction
  • Signalling theory (Spence): contract terms send signals that sort applicant types — the basis of separating vs pooling equilibria
  • Monitoring costs are real agency costs — resources spent on oversight cannot be used productively elsewhere
  • Market failure arises because private markets cannot resolve information asymmetries without government intervention or clever mechanism design
