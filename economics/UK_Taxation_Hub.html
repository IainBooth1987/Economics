Direct Taxation in the UK

Direct taxes are levied directly on individuals and organisations based on income, wealth, or capital. They form the backbone of the UK's progressive tax system, accounting for approximately 55% of total tax revenue, and embody the ability-to-pay principle.

Theoretical Foundations

Ability to Pay: Direct taxes are based on the taxpayer's capacity to contribute, measured by income or wealth. This underpins the progressive structure.

Horizontal Equity: Taxpayers in similar circumstances should pay similar amounts — achieved through standardised rates and allowances.

Vertical Equity: Those with greater ability to pay should contribute proportionally more.

Benefit Principle: Some elements, such as NIC, link contributions to future benefits, creating a quasi-insurance system.

Income Tax

20% Basic | 40% Higher | 45% Additional

Base: Wages, salaries, dividends, rental income, interest, pensions

Personal Allowance (2024/25): £12,570 (frozen until 2028)

Bands (2024/25):

  • Basic Rate: £12,571–£50,270 (20%)
  • Higher Rate: £50,271–£125,140 (40%)
  • Additional Rate: Over £125,140 (45%)
  • Note: Personal allowance tapered away above £100,000, creating an effective 60% band

Revenue (2023/24): ~£240bn (33% of total tax revenue)

National Insurance Contributions

8% Employee | 13.8% Employer

Base: Earnings from employment and self-employment

Primary Threshold: £12,570

Employee Rate (2024/25): 8% (cut from 10% in Jan 2024, then 12% previously)

Upper Earnings Limit: £50,270 (above which 2% applies — making NIC effectively regressive at higher incomes)

Revenue (2023/24): ~£170bn — links to state pension and benefit entitlements

Corporation Tax

25% Main Rate | 19% Small Profits

Base: Company profits including trading profits, capital gains, investment income

Small Profits Rate: 19% on profits up to £50,000

Marginal Relief: Tapered rate between £50,000–£250,000

Revenue (2023/24): ~£85bn

International Context: OECD global minimum of 15% from 2024 (Pillar Two)

Capital Gains Tax

10%/18%/24%/28% depending on asset & rate

Base: Gains on asset disposals above annual exempt amount

Annual Exempt Amount (2024/25): £3,000 (cut from £12,300 in 2022/23)

Rate Structure:

  • Basic rate taxpayers: 10% (18% residential property)
  • Higher/additional rate: 20% (24% residential property from April 2024)

Business Asset Disposal Relief: 10% on qualifying disposals (lifetime limit £1m)

Inheritance Tax

40% Standard | 36% with charitable relief

Nil-Rate Band: £325,000 (frozen since 2009)

Residence Nil-Rate Band: £175,000 (family home to direct descendants)

Taper Relief: Lifetime gifts become exempt after 7 years

Revenue (2023/24): ~£7.5bn

Coverage: Only ~4% of estates — often called a "voluntary tax" given available reliefs

Council Tax & Property Taxes

Property Band Based (A–H)

Valuation: Based on 1991 property values (England) — widely considered outdated

Revenue: ~£42bn collected by local authorities

Key Critique: Regressive relative to income; outdated valuations create geographic distortions favouring high-value properties relative to their 2024 worth

Reform Pressure: IFS and OBR have repeatedly called for revaluation

✅ Advantages of Direct Taxes

  • Progressive structure reflects ability to pay
  • Act as automatic stabilisers — revenue falls in recessions
  • High and predictable revenue yield from income tax base
  • Transparent — taxpayers clearly aware of their burden
  • PAYE system gives low collection costs

❌ Disadvantages of Direct Taxes

  • High marginal rates may discourage work effort (disincentive effect)
  • Can discourage saving — double taxation of capital income
  • Revenue highly sensitive to economic cycles
  • Complex administration; 12m self-assessment returns annually
  • Mobile tax bases limit policy options — high earner migration risk

Indirect Taxation in the UK

Indirect taxes are levied on goods and services, with the burden potentially shifted from the initial taxpayer to consumers through higher prices. They provide approximately 45% of UK tax revenue and serve dual purposes: revenue generation and behavioural modification through price signals.

Theoretical Framework

Tax Incidence Theory: The burden of indirect taxes depends on price elasticities of supply and demand. Inelastic demand (necessities) results in more of the tax being passed to consumers.

Ramsey Rule: Efficiency requires higher rates on goods with lower demand elasticity to minimise deadweight loss.

Pigouvian Taxes: Taxes on goods with negative externalities (tobacco, alcohol, fuel) can improve social welfare by internalising external costs.

Value Added Tax (VAT)

20% Standard | 5% Reduced | 0% Zero-rated

Revenue (2023/24): ~£163bn (22% of total)

Registration Threshold: £90,000 annual turnover (from April 2024)

  • Standard (20%): Most goods/services, electronics, clothing, hospitality
  • Reduced (5%): Domestic fuel, children's car seats, contraceptives
  • Zero-rated: Food, books, children's clothing, public transport
  • Exempt: Education, health, financial services, insurance

Post-Brexit flexibility: UK can now set its own VAT rates — previously constrained by EU minimums

Fuel Duty

52.95p per litre (plus 20% VAT)

Total tax burden: ~60% of pump price

Revenue: ~£25bn (declining as EV adoption rises)

Policy History: Frozen since 2011 — abandonment of the fuel duty escalator under political pressure

Future Challenge: £25bn+ revenue shortfall predicted as petrol/diesel vehicles phase out by 2035. Road pricing likely replacement.

Regressive effects: Higher burden on rural and lower-income drivers

Alcohol & Tobacco Duties

Pigouvian / Sin Taxes

Alcohol (2023 reform): New strength-based system — lower ABV products taxed proportionally less; draught relief for pub sales

Tobacco: £6.33 + 16.5% of retail price per 20 cigarettes

Combined revenue: ~£21bn

Public health rationale: Internalise external costs of ill health on NHS and society

Distributional issue: Regressive — lower-income groups spend a higher share of income on these goods

Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT)

Progressive rates 0–12%

Revenue: ~£11bn (highly volatile, property cycle dependent)

Standard residential rates (2024/25):

  • 0% up to £250,000
  • 5% on £250,001–£925,000
  • 10% on £925,001–£1.5m
  • 12% above £1.5m

First-time buyers: 0% threshold raised to £425,000

Criticism: Creates "lock-in" — disincentivises moving, reducing labour mobility

✅ Advantages of Indirect Taxes

  • Difficult to evade — VAT collected at point of sale
  • More stable revenue during recessions than income tax
  • Can correct market failure (externalities)
  • Some choice element — can avoid by not consuming taxed goods
  • Lower compliance burden for most individuals

❌ Disadvantages of Indirect Taxes

  • Regressive relative to income — higher % burden on the poor
  • Can be inflationary — push up price level
  • Reduce consumer surplus and create deadweight loss
  • Revenue from sin taxes declines as behaviour changes (a success that erodes its own base)
  • Do not redistribute wealth

Tax Incidence & Burden Shifting

The statutory incidence of a tax (who legally pays it) often differs from the economic incidence (who ultimately bears the cost). Understanding burden shifting is essential for evaluating the true distributional effects of tax policy.

The Core Principle

When a government imposes an indirect tax, firms may pass the cost to consumers through higher prices (forward shifting) or absorb it through lower profits/wages (backward shifting). The actual split depends on the relative elasticities of supply and demand.

Scenario Demand Elasticity Who bears the burden? UK Example
Inelastic demand Low (PED < 1) Mostly consumers Tobacco duty — consumers absorb most of the tax
Elastic demand High (PED > 1) Mostly producers Luxury goods VAT — firms must absorb to maintain sales
Perfectly inelastic PED = 0 Entirely consumers Theoretical — e.g. life-saving medication
Perfectly elastic PED = ∞ Entirely producers Competitive commodity markets
Corporation tax N/A (direct tax) Mixed: shareholders (lower dividends), workers (lower wages), consumers (higher prices) Empirically contested; IFS evidence suggests wages bear ~50%

The UK Direct vs Indirect Mix

Current split: approximately 55% direct / 45% indirect (down from 70/30 in the 1970s).

International comparison: OECD average ~60/40. USA 75/25 (more direct). France 55/45. Denmark 80/20 (highly progressive).

Policy implication: A shift toward indirect taxation tends to be regressive unless accompanied by compensating transfers. The 2011 VAT rise (17.5%→20%) reduced bottom quintile real income by ~3.3% vs ~0.9% for the top quintile.

Criterion Direct Taxes Indirect Taxes Evidence
Equity Progressive — higher earners pay more as % of income Regressive — lower earners pay higher % of income IFS distributional analysis 2023
Efficiency Distorts work/leisure trade-off Distorts consumption choices Optimal tax theory (Mirrlees, 2011)
Revenue Stability Volatile — income tax fell 6% in 2008–09 More stable — VAT fell only 2% in same period OBR Fiscal Sustainability Reports
Avoidance More susceptible to avoidance/evasion Harder to evade when collected at point of sale HMRC Tax Gap 2023: £36bn total gap

UK Tax Policy: Major Case Studies

Two decades of UK tax reform reveal how economic crises, political ideology, and international pressures reshape policy. Click any case study to expand it.

Context: The global financial crisis required unprecedented government intervention to prevent economic collapse. Keynesian demand stimulus was the dominant theoretical framework.

Key measures: VAT temporary cut 17.5%→15% (Dec 2008–Dec 2009); accelerated capital allowances; stamp duty holiday for first-time buyers below £175,000.

Outcome: VAT cut cost £12.4bn. Limited evidence of a significant consumption boost — much was saved rather than spent, questioning effectiveness. The fiscal deficit expanded, requiring subsequent austerity.

Lesson: Indirect tax cuts as demand stimulus have weak transmission when consumer confidence is depressed. Fiscal multiplier was below 1.

Context: Labour introduced a 50% rate on incomes above £150,000 — the highest UK rate since the 1980s — as both a fiscal and fairness measure.

Design detail: Withdrawal of personal allowance above £100,000 created an effective 60% marginal rate band (still exists in 2024/25).

Revenue vs expectation: Projected £2.5bn/year; actual yield ~£1bn due to extensive income-shifting, timing adjustments, and avoidance activity.

HMRC estimated: Revenue-maximising rate ~45–47%, supporting the 2013 reduction to 45%.

Lesson: Behavioural responses at the top of the income distribution are large and fast — providing rare real-world Laffer Curve data.

Strategy: 75% spending cuts, 25% tax increases to reduce deficit from 11% to 5% of GDP.

Key changes: VAT rise 17.5%→20% (Jan 2011, +£13bn/yr); corporation tax cut 28%→21%; personal allowance rises from £6,475 to £10,600; CGT rises (18%→28% for higher rate taxpayers); bank levy introduction.

Distributional outcome: Net shift from direct to indirect taxation was regressive overall. Bottom quintile lost 3.3% of income; top quintile lost 0.9%.

Lesson: Tax mix matters as much as tax level for distributional impact. Revenue-neutral shifts from direct to indirect taxes tend to hit the poorest hardest.

Scale: Over £400bn in support measures — the largest peacetime fiscal expansion in UK history.

Tax system innovations: HMRC's Real Time Information (RTI) database used to verify furlough scheme eligibility; VAT payment deferrals providing £30bn+ cash flow relief; 5% reduced VAT for hospitality; stamp duty holiday stimulating property market.

Gaps revealed: The newly self-employed, company directors, and gig economy workers were largely excluded — highlighting limitations of tax-based targeting mechanisms.

Fiscal legacy: Public debt rose from ~85% to ~100% of GDP, creating structural pressure for subsequent tax increases (NIC hike 2022, freeze of personal allowances until 2028).

Ideology: Pure supply-side economics — large unfunded tax cuts intended to stimulate growth without corresponding spending reductions or a credible fiscal framework.

Proposed measures: Scrap 45p additional rate; cancel corporation tax rise; remove banker bonus cap; cut basic rate; significant stamp duty reductions. Total unfunded cost: ~£45bn.

Market response: Gilt yields soared to 20-year highs; pound fell to near-parity with dollar; mortgage rates spiked; Bank of England intervened in government bond market to prevent pension fund collapse.

Outcome: Full policy reversal within weeks. Both Chancellor and PM resigned within 45 days.

Lesson: Market credibility is a binding constraint on fiscal policy. Unfunded supply-side tax cuts cannot generate sufficient short-run growth to offset revenue losses. International financial markets discipline domestic policy autonomy.

Synthesis: Key Lessons from Reform Experience

Behavioural responses dominate: High earners and multinationals adapt quickly through avoidance, timing, and restructuring — often reducing revenue yield well below static projections.

Credibility is a constraint: As the Truss mini-budget demonstrated, markets now impose rapid discipline on governments that abandon fiscal credibility.

Technology enables innovation: RTI data and digital infrastructure allowed rapid COVID deployment — but also create new challenges for taxing digital and gig economy activity.

Future pressures: EV adoption threatens £25bn fuel duty base; demographic ageing increases demand; digital economy erodes traditional tax bases.

📚 Understanding the Three Systems

Progressive: The effective tax rate rises with income. Higher earners pay a larger share. Promotes vertical equity but may distort work incentives at high marginal rates. Example: UK income tax.

Regressive: The effective tax rate falls with income. Lower earners pay a higher share. Violates ability-to-pay but may be administratively simple. Example: VAT, flat fees, NIC upper earnings limit.

Proportional (Flat): The effective tax rate is constant across all incomes. Horizontal equity is preserved. Does not redistribute. Example: Corporation tax (single rate), some Eastern European income tax systems.

⚖️ The Equity vs Efficiency Trade-off

Tax policy faces a fundamental tension between equity (fairness in distribution) and efficiency (minimising economic distortions). This is Okun's "leaky bucket" problem — redistributing income through taxation loses some value due to behavioural responses and administrative costs.

The Laffer Curve: Tax revenue rises with rates up to a point, then falls as people work less, avoid tax, or emigrate. HMRC evidence from the 50p rate suggests the UK revenue-maximising rate is approximately 45–47% for income tax.

Optimal tax theory (Mirrlees, 2011): Top rates should be high but finite. The very bottom of the income distribution should face low marginal rates to encourage workforce participation. Tax bases with lower elasticity should be taxed more heavily.

The UK Tax System: A Mixed Picture

The UK does not operate a single "type" of tax system. In reality, it uses a combination — progressive direct taxes and regressive indirect taxes — with the overall distribution depending on the balance between them. Understanding this is key to high-level evaluation.

UK Tax Type System Classification Key Evaluation Point
Income Tax Direct 🔺 Progressive Top 1% pay ~28% of income tax revenue. But personal allowance taper creates 60% effective marginal rate band — a hidden inefficiency.
NIC Direct 🔺 Progressive (then regressive) Progressive up to £50,270, then effectively regressive — the 2% rate above the UEL means higher earners pay a lower share. Often called a "second income tax".
Corporation Tax Direct ➡️ Proportional (broadly) Incidence disputed — shareholders, workers, or consumers may ultimately bear the cost. Two-rate structure (19%/25%) adds some progressivity for small firms.
VAT Indirect 🔻 Regressive (by income) Lower-income households spend a larger share of income → pay more VAT as % of income. Zero-ratings mitigate this but do not eliminate it.
Fuel Duty Indirect 🔻 Regressive Rural and lower-income households more car-dependent. Also faces existential fiscal challenge from EV transition — £25bn+ revenue at risk.
Tobacco/Alcohol Duty Indirect 🔻 Regressive (Pigouvian) Lower-income groups spend higher share on tobacco and alcohol. Regressive but justified on externality grounds — a classic equity/efficiency tension.
Council Tax Direct (property) 🔻 Regressive Based on 1991 values — a person in a £500,000 house in 2024 pays relatively less than in 1991. Widely regarded as the most distorted major UK tax.
Inheritance Tax Direct 🔺 Progressive Despite progressive intent, extensive reliefs mean only ~4% of estates pay. Agricultural and business property relief particularly controversial.

For a High-Mark Evaluation Answer

Avoid describing the UK tax system as simply "progressive." The best answers acknowledge that:

  • Direct taxes are progressive; indirect taxes tend to be regressive
  • The overall progressivity depends on the balance between them
  • Behavioural responses (avoidance, income shifting) reduce the effective progressivity of high marginal rates
  • The Truss episode demonstrated that market constraints now limit the feasible range of tax policy
  • The future tax base is under structural pressure: EV transition, digital economy, demographic ageing

Key Conflicts to Explore

  • Equity vs Efficiency: Progressive rates redistribute but may distort work/investment incentives
  • Revenue vs Behaviour: Higher sin taxes reduce revenue as they succeed in changing behaviour
  • Short run vs Long run: Indirect tax cuts may boost demand short-term but create structural deficits long-term (2008 VAT cut lesson)
  • National vs International: Corporation and income tax rates constrained by international competition and OECD minimum tax agreements
  • Present vs Future: Freezing allowances is a stealth tax rise — politically easier but erodes household real income gradually

Suggested Essay Framework: "Is the UK Tax System Fair?"

Yes — argue fairness: Progressive income tax; top 1% pay ~28% of income tax; automatic stabiliser function; zero-rated VAT on essentials; IHT targets inherited wealth.

No — argue unfairness: VAT regressive; council tax based on 1991 values; 60% effective marginal rate band; NICs regressive above UEL; extensive IHT reliefs mean wealthy estates often pay little; wealth taxed far less than income.

Judgement: Fair relative to many countries, but structural distortions (council tax, NIC taper, allowance freeze) undermine the equity objective. True progressivity is less than headline rates suggest once indirect taxes and avoidance are factored in.
