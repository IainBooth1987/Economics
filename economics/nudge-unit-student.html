CHARTER

The Minister's Charter — Read This First

The Minister has published a Charter for his new unit. Every team should read it carefully — it contains the principles you must work within, and it may be relevant later.

M
The Minister for Behavioural Insight
"A million people pay their tax late every year, and it is not because they are villains. Before anyone designs anything, tell me why. Diagnose the patient — the evidence is in your briefing."

On what we know about late payers: most late payers eventually pay in full — they are not refusing, they are postponing. In focus groups, late payers guessed "half the country" pays late. The real figure is one in ten. The payment form runs to fourteen pages. After the standard reminder letter, 60% pay within 30 days.

On the role of government: "Doing nothing is a choice with a body count — but I will not ban my way to a healthier Britain. No one should wake up to find choices made for them. Where a gentle hand works, we will not raise a fist."

On what this unit will and will not do: "We design with evidence, not intuition. Opinions are free and usually wrong. We don't argue about which letter is best — we test. And we are not in the business of tricking people into good behaviour. Every intervention must pass the publicity test: we defend it on the front page, and we never hide the exit."

On the contract: "£2 million. Maximise the good you do per pound. Choose three interventions from the menu. And before you do — I'd re-read my Charter, every line of it. Some of you will skim."

Notes — any phrases that might be useful later?

1 Round 1 — Diagnose the Human & the Ladder

Which Bias Is Driving Late Payment?

Part A of 2  ·  Max 20 pts total for Round 1  ·  8 minutes

M
"A million people pay their tax late every year, and it is not because they are villains. Before anyone designs anything, tell me why. Diagnose the patient — the evidence is in your briefing."
Jargon

Traditional economics assumes people are perfectly rational. Behavioural economics studies how real people actually decide — with limited time, willpower and attention (bounded rationality) — and the predictable biases that result. The first job of any nudge unit is diagnosis: which bias is driving the behaviour?

Your task

From the six biases below, rank the three most responsible for late tax payment (1 = biggest driver). Justify your top pick with evidence from the Charter. All six are real biases — the points are for matching the bias to this specific behaviour.

A Loss aversion

  • Losses hurt about twice as much as equal gains feel good
  • People work harder to avoid losing £50 than to gain £50

B Social norms

  • We copy what we believe most people do
  • Behaviour shifts when we learn "most people" act differently from what we assumed

C Present bias

  • Today's small hassle outweighs next month's big consequence
  • We procrastinate even when we intend to act — the deadline always feels far away

D Anchoring

  • Judgements stick to the first number seen
  • An early number — even irrelevant — shapes all subsequent estimates

E Inertia & friction

  • Effort, complexity and defaults decide more than intentions do
  • Every extra step is a reason to do it tomorrow instead

F Overconfidence

  • We overrate our own judgement and memory
  • "I'll remember to pay" — and then don't

Part B — The Intervention Ladder

Where Does the Minister Stand?

Part B of 2  ·  4 minutes

M
"Half my party wants me to ban things. The other half wants me to do nothing and call it liberty. You've read my Charter — where on the ladder do I actually stand?"
Jargon

Governments have a ladder of tools, gentlest to most forceful. Climbing the ladder buys effectiveness — and costs freedom. The middle rungs — nudge (redesign the choice, all options open), restricted choice, mandated choice — sit between pure information and outright compulsion. This is sometimes called libertarian paternalism: steer people toward better choices while leaving every option open.

Price the Status Quo

Max 15 pts  ·  6 minutes

M
"The Treasury thinks my unit is a luxury. So before we design a single letter, price the problem for me. What does a million people paying late actually cost this country every year?"
Jargon

Before a unit spends a pound, it prices the status quo — what the problem costs if nothing changes. That number is the benchmark every intervention is judged against, and it's how a £2m contract gets justified to the Treasury.

3 Round 3 — Design the Letter

Commit to One Variant — the Live Trial Decides

Max 10 pts  ·  5 minutes to commit, then the trial runs live — no separate tab, it all happens right here

M
"Four letters are on my desk and every consultancy in this room swears theirs will work. We don't argue in this unit — we test. Commit to one letter. The trial decides who was right."
Jargon

The unit designs with EAST: Easy (cut friction), Attractive, Social, Timely. And note framing: the same information presented differently changes choices. "Penalties will apply" is a loss frame; "9 out of 10 pay on time" presents the same duty as a descriptive social norm. The best letter pulls the lever that matches your Round 1 diagnosis.

Your task

Choose one letter variant. Your written answer must: (a) identify the behavioural concept each letter is pulling (one of them is barely pulling anything), (b) justify your choice against your Round 1 diagnosis, and (c) state the best case against your own letter. Then hold your nerve — the trial runs immediately after, in this same round, and decides the points.

  • Cost: standard letter, ~50p each

"Here is the contract: £2 million, and your pick of three interventions from the menu. Maximise the good you do per pound. And before you choose — I'd re-read my Charter, every line of it. Some of you skimmed."
Jargon

Sludge = choice architecture turned against people: hidden opt-outs, fake deadlines, buried small print. It often produces spectacular numbers — which is exactly why serious units ban it. A nudge keeps every exit open and survives daylight; sludge needs the dark. The publicity test: would you be comfortable defending this on the front page of a newspaper?
