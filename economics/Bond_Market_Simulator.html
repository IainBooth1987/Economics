Understanding the Crisis Impact

Before the Crisis: Government bonds trade at stable yields reflecting low risk. Existing bonds have fixed coupon rates based on pre-crisis market conditions.

During the Crisis: Investors panic and demand much higher returns to compensate for increased risk. Existing bond prices crash as yields spike (inverse relationship). When governments need to issue new bonds, they must offer dramatically higher coupon rates to attract any buyers at all.

The Vicious Cycle: Higher borrowing costs strain government finances further, potentially triggering downgrades, more panic, and even higher yields. This is why fiscal credibility matters so much.

Key Terms ▼

Yield
The annual return an investor receives from a bond, expressed as a percentage. When bond prices fall, yields rise — and vice versa.
Coupon Rate
The fixed annual interest payment on a bond, set when the bond is first issued. A bond with a £100 face value and 3% coupon pays £3 per year.
Face Value (Par)
The amount the bondholder receives at maturity — typically £100. Bonds trade above or below par depending on market yields.
Inverse Relationship
Bond prices and yields move in opposite directions. If investors demand higher yields, existing bonds with lower coupons become less attractive, so their price falls.
Maturity
The date when the bond issuer repays the face value. Longer-maturity bonds are more sensitive to yield changes — a small yield rise causes a larger price drop.
Credit Risk / Default Risk
The risk that a government (or issuer) fails to make interest or principal payments. Higher perceived risk means investors demand higher yields as compensation.
Gilt
A UK government bond. The name comes from the original certificates having gilded (gold) edges. Considered one of the safest bond markets globally.
Yield Curve
A graph plotting bond yields against their maturity. Normally slopes upward (longer = higher yield). An inverted curve can signal recession expectations.
Basis Points (bps)
One hundredth of a percentage point. A rise from 3.00% to 3.50% is a 50 basis point increase. Used because small yield changes have large effects on price.
Spread
The difference in yield between two bonds — often between a risky bond and a 'safe' benchmark like US Treasuries or German Bunds. Wider spreads signal greater perceived risk.
