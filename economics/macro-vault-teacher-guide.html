The Macro Vault — Teacher's Idiot's Guide

Everything you need to run this cold, including if you don't teach Economics. Read this once before the lesson — you won't need anything else.

Before the lesson

  1. Print one Student Workbook per team (3–4 students per team) — teams work through everything together, not individually.
  2. Open macro-vault-teacher-slides.html on the projector/board. Password: MacroVault-Jk9!2026
  3. Keep this guide open on your own device — it has every answer and the full vault code.
  4. The slides are deliberately light — instructions and timing only, not the questions themselves (those are all in the workbook). This keeps the board focused on pace and teamwork rather than duplicating text students already have in front of them.

Vault Key 2

Sum of last digits: 0 + 0 + 0 + 5 + 5 + 0 + 0 = 10. Total is two digits, so take the last digit of 10: 0

If a team's method is right but they slip on this final "last digit of the total" step, let them retry rather than giving the digit away. Q6 ties neatly back to Milton Friedman's quote from Round 1 — a 10% rise in the money supply produces 10% inflation when V and Q are held constant, exactly as the quantity theory predicts.

Since students predict before seeing data, expect a wide spread of guesses — Germany and the UK are common (wrong) guesses across most categories, which is exactly the point: solid all-rounders rarely top a single specific index. Somalia recurring as the "lowest" answer across GDP and HDI reflects a genuine and worth-discussing correlation: prolonged conflict has suppressed both income and human development together, unlike Qatar's case below where wealth and wellbeing pull apart. Qatar is a deliberately counter-intuitive answer for "worst HPI": high income and high HDI, but its very high per-capita ecological footprint (driven by energy use and a small, wealthy population) pulls it to the bottom of wellbeing-per-environmental-impact rankings — a good discussion point on the difference between wealth and sustainable wellbeing.

Round 4 answers — Policy Decisions

Warm-up matching key: Expansionary fiscal policy → E (rise in G/cut in T to raise AD) · Contractionary monetary policy → B (raise rates to reduce AD/inflation) · Quantitative easing → A (central bank asset purchases) · Supply-side policy → C (increases productive capacity) · Automatic stabiliser → F (smooths the cycle without new legislation) · Crowding out → D (more borrowing pushes up rates, squeezes private investment).

This warm-up now feeds part of Vault Key 4 below — it's not just vocabulary pre-loading.

Scenario A — correct answer: D (Supply-side reform). This is a supply-side (cost-push) shock, not a demand-side one — pure demand tools (A, B, E) only address one side of stagflation and risk worsening the other. The debt clue rules out C/F (more borrowing or taxation is fiscally/politically ruled out). The OBR clue rules out G (price caps cause shortages). Only D tackles the root cause.

Scenario B — correct answer: A (Sharp interest rate rise). This is demand-pull inflation with an overheating economy at full employment — a classic case for contractionary monetary policy. The "unanchored expectations" clue signals urgency. The tax-before-an-election clue rules out F as politically unavailable, and there's no supply shock present to justify D.

Scenario C — correct answer: C (Borrowing-funded spending increase). This is a demand-deficient recession with a liquidity trap (rates near zero, so B/E have little extra room) and very low inflation (little risk of overheating from stimulus). The fiscal headroom clue confirms government can afford to borrow here — unlike Scenario A, where debt was already the binding constraint. This contrast is deliberate: the same option can be right in one context and wrong in another.

Vault Key 4 (4 characters)

• Matching — "Automatic stabiliser" is matched to option F → F

• Scenario A → D, Scenario B → A, Scenario C → C

Key 4 = FDAC

Running it as a non-specialist / cover teacher

  • You don't need to understand the economics — every answer and its reasoning are above.
  • A "hint" just means reading a team the relevant clue sentence (Round 4) or pointing at the correct row in the data table (Round 3) — log 60 seconds against their time, doesn't need to be precise.
  • Round 4 is the trickiest to mark cold: the "why" column above tells you exactly which clue rules out which wrong answers, so you can defend the mark scheme if a team pushes back.
  • If a team's vault key doesn't match, tell them "not quite — check your order/working again" and move to another team rather than debugging their maths live.
