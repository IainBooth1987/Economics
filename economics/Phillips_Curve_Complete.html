🕰️ The Story Behind Both Curves

The Phillips Curve is one of the most famous — and most contested — relationships in macroeconomics. It began as a simple empirical observation about wages, became a cornerstone of post-war policy, collapsed in the 1970s, and was rebuilt into the model still used by central banks today.

Timeline: The Original Curve (1958–1968)

1958 — Phillips' original study

New Zealand economist A. W. (Bill) Phillips, working at the LSE, plotted UK money wage inflation against unemployment using data from 1861–1957. He found a clear inverse, curved relationship: when unemployment was low, wage growth was high, and vice versa. Crucially, his original paper was about wage inflation, not price inflation.

1960 — Samuelson & Solow popularise it

Paul Samuelson and Robert Solow relabelled the relationship in terms of price inflation rather than wage inflation, and presented it as a "menu" of policy choices: governments could pick a point on the curve, trading off a bit more inflation for less unemployment, or vice versa.

1960s — The "Golden Age" of policy use

Western governments, including the UK, used the curve to actively fine-tune demand management. Increase demand a little → unemployment falls, inflation rises modestly. The trade-off looked stable and exploitable.

Timeline: The Expectations Critique (1967–Present)

1967 — Phelps' contribution

Edmund Phelps developed a microeconomic, "islands" model of search and information in labour markets, showing why unemployment has a natural, equilibrium rate that monetary policy cannot push below for long without accelerating inflation.

1968 — Friedman's AEA Presidential Address

Milton Friedman's address, "The Role of Monetary Policy," argued explicitly against the idea of a stable, exploitable Phillips Curve. He introduced the concept of the Natural Rate of Unemployment — the rate consistent with correctly anticipated inflation — and argued that any attempt to hold unemployment below it would require ever-accelerating inflation.

1972–73 — Adaptive expectations formalised

Economists built models where people form expectations of inflation based on past inflation (adaptive expectations). Each time the government tries to exploit the trade-off, expectations catch up, and the short-run curve shifts outward.

1973–79 — Real-world confirmation: stagflation

The oil shocks delivered exactly what Friedman and Phelps predicted: persistent attempts to maintain demand led to inflation becoming embedded in expectations, while unemployment still rose — proof that the long-run trade-off had disappeared. This broke the original 1958 curve's apparent stability completely.

1976 — Lucas critique reinforces the point

Robert Lucas argued (more generally) that if people form rational expectations, they may anticipate policy changes immediately, meaning even short-run trade-offs could be smaller or disappear faster than under adaptive expectations.

1980s–present — Central bank orthodoxy

The NAIRU and expectations-augmented framework became the standard model behind inflation-targeting central banks (including the Bank of England): monetary policy can manage demand in the short run, but cannot permanently lower unemployment below its natural rate without ever-rising inflation.

2008–2015 — The "missing disinflation" puzzle

After the financial crisis, unemployment rose sharply but inflation fell only modestly — less than the curve predicted. Economists debated whether the curve had become "flatter," reflecting globalisation, weaker union power, and well-anchored inflation expectations.

2021–2023 — Revival in the post-Covid era

Post-pandemic supply shocks and tight labour markets reignited debate. Some economists argued the curve was "back" as inflation surged alongside falling unemployment; others pointed to supply-side shocks (energy, supply chains) as the real driver, not a stable demand-side trade-off.

The Original Research

Phillips (1958)

"The Relation between Unemployment and the Rate of Change of Money Wages," Economica, 25(100), pp. 283–299.

Used nearly a century of UK data (1861–1957) to plot a scatter of unemployment against wage inflation, fitting a smooth curved line through it. He found the relationship was non-linear: at low unemployment, small further falls produced large wage increases.

Samuelson & Solow (1960)

"Analytical Aspects of Anti-Inflation Policy," American Economic Review, 50(2), pp. 177–194.

Translated Phillips' wage-based curve into a price-inflation version for the US economy, and explicitly framed it as a menu of choices available to policymakers.

Friedman (1968)

"The Role of Monetary Policy," American Economic Review, 58(1), pp. 1–17.

Argued monetary policy cannot durably target a real variable like unemployment; introduced the Natural Rate Hypothesis and warned that exploiting the short-run Phillips Curve only works through unanticipated inflation.

Phelps (1967, 1968)

"Phillips Curves, Expectations of Inflation and Optimal Unemployment over Time," Economica, 34(135), pp. 254–281.

Modelled wage-setting and job search under imperfect information, deriving an equilibrium ("natural") unemployment rate independent of the inflation rate in the long run.

"There is, however, this oddity ... that the figures fall into a curved rather than a straight line." — paraphrased from Phillips' description of the non-linear shape he observed.
Friedman's central claim, paraphrased: a government can buy lower unemployment with inflation only by repeatedly surprising people — and surprises cannot be repeated indefinitely.

📘 The Short-Run Phillips Curve

The short-run Phillips Curve (SRPC) shows an inverse relationship between the rate of unemployment and the rate of inflation. The economic logic runs through the labour market:

  1. As unemployment falls, labour becomes scarcer relative to demand for it.
  2. Firms competing for fewer available workers must offer higher wages to attract and retain staff.
  3. Rising wages increase firms' costs, which are passed on as higher prices — i.e. inflation.
  4. Equally, lower unemployment usually reflects higher aggregate demand, which itself pulls prices upward (demand-pull inflation).

This gives policymakers an apparent short-run choice between two "bads": accept more inflation to get lower unemployment, or accept higher unemployment to keep inflation down.

📘 Short Run vs Long Run

The expectations-augmented model says the position on a Phillips Curve depends on the gap between actual and expected inflation:

π = πe − α(U − Un)

where π is actual inflation, πe is expected inflation, U is unemployment, Un is the natural rate of unemployment (NAIRU), and α reflects how responsive wages/prices are to labour market slack.

  • If U is below Un, actual inflation exceeds expected inflation — the economy is "above" its short-run curve.
  • If U equals Un, actual inflation equals expected inflation — no surprise, no temporary stimulus.
  • Each short-run curve (SRPC) is drawn for a given πe. A higher expected inflation shifts the whole curve upward.

The Adjustment Story (Step by Step)

1. Demand stimulus

Government/central bank stimulates demand. Unemployment falls below Un as firms hire more than is sustainable, and inflation rises above what people expected.

2. Workers are fooled (briefly)

Real wages have actually fallen, but in money terms wages look attractive, so workers take jobs — unemployment falls along the SRPC.

3. Expectations catch up

Workers and firms revise πe upward once they see realised inflation. Wage bargaining incorporates the new, higher expected inflation.

4. Back to the natural rate

With expectations now matching reality, the temporary employment gain disappears — unemployment returns to Un, but now at a permanently higher rate of inflation. The SRPC has shifted upward.

The Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC)

Because every SRPC eventually collapses back to Un once expectations adjust, joining up all these long-run resting points gives a vertical line at Un. This is the key policy implication: in the long run, there is no trade-off between inflation and unemployment — monetary/fiscal policy can determine the inflation rate, but not the long-run unemployment rate, which is set by structural/supply-side factors (skills, labour market flexibility, frictional and structural unemployment).

🏗️ Why Supply-Side Policy Is the Real Lever Here

This is the single most important application for A-Level: demand-side policy (fiscal/monetary stimulus) can only move the economy along or between short-run curves — it cannot move the LRPC itself. Only supply-side policy can shift the vertical LRPC, because supply-side policy changes Un — the natural/structural rate of unemployment — directly, rather than working through demand and expectations.

The key distinction

Demand-side policy changes how far above or below Un the economy sits temporarily, by creating inflation surprises. Supply-side policy changes where Un itself sits, by tackling the structural causes of unemployment — so it shifts the whole vertical LRPC left (improving the trade-off permanently) or right (worsening it permanently). Crucially, successful supply-side policy can reduce unemployment without generating any extra inflation, because it isn't relying on surprising anyone.

How Different Supply-Side Policies Shift the LRPC

Each of the policies below targets a specific type of structural, frictional or real-wage unemployment that makes up the "natural rate." Reducing that component shifts Un — and therefore the LRPC — to the left.

1. Education, training and skills policy

Mechanism: tackles structural unemployment caused by a skills mismatch between what employers need and what the unemployed can offer. Better-matched, more productive workers are easier to re-employ and need less time searching, so the natural rate falls.

UK example: apprenticeship reforms and the Apprenticeship Levy, and sector-based work academies that retrain workers from declining industries (e.g. retraining former coal/steel workers for renewable energy or logistics roles) aim to reduce structural unemployment and shift Un leftward.

2. Labour market flexibility / deregulation

Mechanism: reducing the cost and risk to firms of hiring (e.g. lower minimum wages relative to productivity, weaker employment protection, less restrictive trade union legislation) lowers the "natural" unemployment generated by wage rigidity and hiring caution.

UK example: the trade union reforms of the 1980s (reducing union bargaining power and the closed shop) are widely credited by supply-side economists with helping reduce the UK's NAIRU through the 1990s and 2000s by making wages more responsive to labour market conditions.

3. Improving job search and matching (frictional unemployment)

Mechanism: better information and faster matching between vacancies and job-seekers shortens the average time people spend searching, lowering frictional unemployment.

UK example: Jobcentre Plus services, the Universal Jobmatch/Find a Job online portal, and conditionality requirements under Universal Credit (claimant commitments, mandatory work search activity) are designed to speed up matching and reduce frictional unemployment.

4. Benefits and welfare reform

Mechanism: if out-of-work benefits are very generous relative to wages, this can raise the "reservation wage" and lengthen voluntary unemployment. Reforming the taper rate or conditionality can increase the incentive to accept work, lowering Un.

UK example: the introduction of Universal Credit and changes to the benefits taper rate were partly justified on the grounds of strengthening work incentives and reducing the "natural rate" component caused by weak incentives to take low-paid work.

5. Geographic mobility and housing policy

Mechanism: high housing costs or planning restrictions in high-employment regions can trap workers in high-unemployment regions, raising structural unemployment. Policies that improve mobility (transport links, housing supply, regional investment) reduce this mismatch.

UK example: "Levelling Up" investment and transport infrastructure projects (e.g. HS2-linked regeneration, Northern Powerhouse Rail) were partly justified as reducing regional structural unemployment by connecting workers in deprived areas to jobs.

6. Competition policy and enterprise incentives

Mechanism: policies that encourage new firm formation, investment and innovation (lower corporation tax for SMEs, deregulation of start-ups, R&D tax credits) raise the economy's underlying capacity to create jobs, supporting a lower sustainable unemployment rate.

UK example: the Enterprise Investment Scheme and R&D tax credits aim to boost business formation and labour demand at the structural level, rather than through short-term demand stimulus.

Why this matters for evaluation

Supply-side policy is often presented as the "permanent fix" missing from demand management: because it tackles the structural causes of Un directly, it can deliver lower unemployment without fuelling extra inflation — unlike a demand-side attempt to push unemployment below the (unchanged) natural rate, which the expectations-augmented model shows is only temporary and inflationary.

Can Supply-Side Policy Make Things Worse?

Just as policy can shift the LRPC left, poorly designed or absent supply-side policy can shift it right — raising the natural rate. Examples include: very generous, poorly conditional welfare systems that weaken work incentives; restrictive planning laws that prevent labour mobility; skills shortages from underinvestment in technical/vocational education; or excessive employment protection that makes firms reluctant to hire. A-Level evaluation should always consider both directions of supply-side shift, not just improvements.

⚖️ Evaluating the Short-Run Phillips Curve

Strengths

  • Captures a genuine short-run mechanism: tight labour markets do tend to push up wage growth.
  • Still used by central banks (e.g. in New Keynesian Phillips Curve form) to think about the costs of disinflation.
  • Useful as a simple diagrammatic tool for exploring policy trade-offs at A-Level/IGCSE/degree level.

Weaknesses / Criticisms

  • The simple short-run curve broke down during 1970s stagflation (high inflation and high unemployment together).
  • Assumes a stable, exploitable trade-off — Friedman/Phelps showed this only holds temporarily.
  • The position and shape of the curve are very hard to measure precisely in real time.

⚖️ Evaluating the Expectations-Augmented Model

Strengths

  • Successfully explains stagflation, which the original Phillips Curve could not.
  • Provides strong theoretical justification for central bank independence and credible inflation targets (anchoring πe low).
  • The NAIRU concept underpins most modern macroeconomic policy frameworks.
  • Distinguishes clearly between short-run demand-side effects and long-run supply-side outcomes.

Weaknesses / Criticisms

  • The NAIRU itself is unobservable and notoriously difficult to estimate accurately in real time.
  • Assumes expectations adjust in a fairly mechanical (adaptive) way — rational expectations theorists argue adjustment could be much faster.
  • Post-2008 "missing disinflation" and post-Covid dynamics suggest the relationship between expectations, slack and inflation may be more complex or unstable than the model implies.
  • Treats unemployment as purely demand/expectations driven, underweighting structural factors like automation, globalisation or hysteresis effects.

Exam-Style Application

For Economics/Business essays, use the short-run Phillips Curve to discuss policy conflicts between full employment and low/stable inflation objectives. Then evaluate using the long-run vertical curve and NAIRU to show why demand-side stimulus alone cannot permanently reduce unemployment below its natural rate — and conclude by assessing how specific supply-side policies (education and training, labour market flexibility, welfare reform, mobility, enterprise incentives) can shift the long-run curve itself, delivering lower sustainable unemployment without the inflationary cost of demand-side stimulus.
