Income vs Wealth: The Essential Distinction

Although often used loosely in everyday speech, income and wealth are economically distinct concepts, and the distributions of each across UK households look quite different from one another.

Income
A flow of earnings received over a given time period (e.g. per week, per year) — including wages and salaries, self-employment profit, rent, interest, dividends, and state benefits.
Wealth
A stock of assets held at a single point in time, minus any liabilities (debts) — including property, savings, shares, pension pots, and business assets, net of mortgages, loans, and other debts.
Flow vs Stock — Why the Distinction Matters: income is measured per period (like water flowing into a bathtub), while wealth is measured at an instant (like the total water already in the bathtub). A person can have high income but low wealth (a newly-qualified doctor with significant student debt and no savings yet), or low current income but high wealth (a retired homeowner with a fully paid-off house and a large pension pot, but modest ongoing income). Confusing the two is one of the most common errors when discussing inequality.
★ REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE: Two Very Different UK Households
A 28-year-old graduate earning £45,000 a year in a City finance job may have very high income relative to the UK average, yet hold negative or near-zero net wealth once student loan debt and a lack of savings or property are accounted for. A 75-year-old retired homeowner, by contrast, may have a modest state and private pension income of £18,000 a year, yet own outright a London house worth £700,000 — giving them far greater wealth than the young professional, despite lower income. This is precisely why income inequality and wealth inequality must be examined separately (Section 8), and why wealth in the UK is typically far more unequally distributed than income.

Factors Influencing the Distribution of Income

The distribution of income across UK households reflects a wide range of underlying factors, many of which connect directly to the labour market theory covered in the companion BIZ-OMICS Labour Market guide.

FactorExplanation
Wages and the labour marketDifferences in skills, education, occupation, and bargaining power directly determine wage levels (companion Labour Market guide, Sections 2–8), and are the single largest source of income differences for working-age households.
State benefits and transfersMeans-tested and universal benefits (Section 19) raise the income of low-income households, directly narrowing the income distribution at the bottom.
Investment incomeRent, interest, and dividends flow disproportionately to households that already hold significant wealth (Section 3), reinforcing income differences that originate from wealth differences.
Household compositionDual-earner households, and households with fewer dependants relative to earners, tend to have higher income per head than single-earner households or those with many dependent children.
Regional variationAverage incomes vary substantially across UK regions, with London and the South East consistently recording higher average earnings than most other UK regions, reflecting differences in industry mix, productivity, and cost of living.
Age and the life cycleIncome typically follows a predictable life-cycle pattern — low in early working life, rising through peak earning years, then falling in retirement — meaning some income inequality reflects people at different life stages rather than genuinely different long-run living standards.
Why Life-Cycle Effects Complicate Simple Inequality Measures: a snapshot of income inequality at any moment always mixes together genuine, structural inequality between different people, and ordinary variation across an individual's own life cycle — a 25-year-old graduate and a 55-year-old senior manager may have very different current incomes despite eventually earning similar lifetime totals, a distinction worth raising in evaluative answers about the true scale of inequality.

Factors Influencing the Distribution of Wealth

Wealth is influenced by a distinct, and in some respects more entrenched, set of factors compared with income (Section 2) — helping to explain why wealth inequality in the UK is consistently greater than income inequality (Section 8).

FactorExplanation
InheritanceWealth passed down between generations directly transmits existing wealth inequality forward in time, largely independent of the recipient's own income or effort.
HomeownershipHousing is the single largest component of wealth for most UK households; owning property (and benefiting from long-run house price growth) versus renting throughout one's life produces enormous differences in accumulated wealth.
Pension savingsAccess to generous workplace pensions (more common in higher-paid, more secure employment) compounds over a working life into substantial differences in retirement wealth.
Financial assets and business ownershipShares, savings, and business equity are held disproportionately by higher-income and older households, further concentrating wealth.
The compounding effect of existing wealthWealth itself generates further income (interest, dividends, rent, capital gains), which can then be reinvested to generate yet more wealth — a compounding process that tends to widen wealth gaps over time faster than income gaps widen.
Why Wealth Inequality Is Structurally "Stickier" Than Income Inequality: because wealth compounds and can be inherited, existing wealth gaps tend to persist and widen across generations even without any single dramatic event — a household that already owns property and has pension savings continues to accumulate further wealth through compounding, while a household starting with little wealth finds it correspondingly harder to begin accumulating any, even if their income is similar. This structural "stickiness" is a key reason wealth inequality is generally considered more difficult to reduce through policy than income inequality (Section 18).

Equality vs Equity

Discussions of income and wealth distribution frequently confuse two related but distinct concepts: equality and equity.

Equality
A situation in which people are treated identically, or receive identical outcomes — for example, every household receiving exactly the same income regardless of their circumstances.
Equity
A situation judged to be fair or just, which may specifically require unequal treatment to account for differing needs, circumstances, or contributions — for example, a progressive tax system deliberately taxes higher earners at a higher rate, which is not equal treatment, but which many regard as more equitable.
Why This Distinction Is Explicitly Tested: the specification requires you to recognise that equality can be objectively measured (via the Gini coefficient, Section 6) and directly observed, but equity always involves a value judgement about what counts as "fair" — reasonable people can look at the exact same measured level of inequality and reach entirely different conclusions about whether it is equitable, depending on their underlying political and moral views (Section 13).
★ REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE: Progressive Taxation as Inequitable "Equality" vs Equitable "Inequality"
A flat-rate tax system, where every taxpayer pays the identical percentage of their income in tax, is a genuinely equal system in the strict sense — everyone is treated the same. Yet many people consider this less equitable than the UK's actual progressive income tax system, in which higher earners pay a higher marginal rate, precisely because it fails to account for the fact that an additional pound is worth less, in terms of sacrificed living standards, to a high earner than to a low earner. This illustrates precisely how equal treatment and equitable treatment can point in opposite directions.

The Lorenz Curve

The Lorenz curve is the standard graphical tool for representing how equally (or unequally) income or wealth is distributed across a population.

Lorenz Curve
A curve plotting the cumulative percentage of total income (or wealth) on the vertical axis against the cumulative percentage of the population, ranked from poorest to richest, on the horizontal axis.
Line of Perfect Equality
The 45-degree diagonal line representing a hypothetical situation of perfect equality, where the poorest 20% of the population hold exactly 20% of total income, the poorest 40% hold exactly 40%, and so on.
Fig 1: The Lorenz curve. The 45-degree diagonal represents perfect equality. The bowed red curve shows the actual cumulative distribution of income — the further the Lorenz curve bows away from the diagonal, the more unequal the distribution. A Lorenz curve for wealth is typically bowed even further from the diagonal than a Lorenz curve for income (Section 8), reflecting greater wealth inequality.
Reading the Curve: a point on the Lorenz curve at, say, (60, 36) means that the poorest 60% of the population hold only 36% of total income — the gap between this point and the perfect-equality line (where 60% of the population would hold exactly 60% of income) is a direct visual measure of inequality at that point in the distribution.

The Gini Coefficient: Interpretation

The Gini coefficient condenses the entire Lorenz curve into a single summary number between 0 and 1, making it possible to compare inequality between countries or over time at a glance.

Gini Coefficient
A single summary measure of inequality, calculated as the ratio of the area between the line of perfect equality and the Lorenz curve (area A) to the total area beneath the line of perfect equality (area A + area B), giving a value between 0 (perfect equality) and 1 (perfect inequality).
Fig 2: The Gini coefficient defined visually. Gini = Area A ÷ (Area A + Area B). If the Lorenz curve sat exactly on the diagonal (perfect equality), Area A would be zero, giving a Gini of 0. If one person held 100% of all income (perfect inequality), the Lorenz curve would hug the horizontal and right-hand axes, Area B would shrink to zero, and Gini would approach 1.
Gini Coefficient ValueInterpretation
0Perfect equality — every individual or household has an identical income (or wealth).
Approximately 0.25–0.35Relatively low inequality — typical of many Northern European countries.
Approximately 0.35–0.45Moderate inequality — broadly where the UK's income Gini coefficient has sat in recent decades (Section 8).
Above 0.5High inequality — typical of many countries with severe income disparities, and broadly the range of the UK's wealth Gini coefficient (Section 8), which is markedly higher than its income Gini coefficient.
1Perfect inequality — a single individual or household holds 100% of all income (or wealth), with everyone else holding none.
Interpretation Only — Calculation Is Not Required by the Specification: AQA expects you to be able to interpret a given Gini coefficient value and to compare Gini coefficients between countries or across time, but you are not required to calculate a Gini coefficient from raw data in the exam. Section 7 nonetheless walks through the calculation method in full, purely as a deeper, optional enrichment for genuine understanding of where the number comes from.

How to Calculate the Gini Coefficient

Beyond the Specification — Enrichment Only

The specification is explicit that you will not be asked to calculate a Gini coefficient in the exam — only to interpret one (Section 6). The step-by-step method below is included purely to deepen genuine understanding of what the number represents; it is not exam-assessed content.

Step 1: Obtain Cumulative Population and Income Share Data

Data is typically presented by quintile (fifths of the population, ranked poorest to richest) or decile (tenths). Suppose a country's income shares by quintile are as follows:

QuintileQ1 (Poorest)Q2Q3Q4Q5 (Richest)
Share of Total Income7%12%17%23%41%

Step 2: Convert to Cumulative Percentages

0%20%40%60%80%100%
Cumulative Population (X)00.200.400.600.801.00
Cumulative Income (Y)00.070.190.360.591.00

(Cumulative income: 7%; 7+12=19%; 19+17=36%; 36+23=59%; 59+41=100%.)

Step 3: Estimate the Area Under the Lorenz Curve (Trapezoidal Rule)

For each interval between consecutive data points, approximate the area as a trapezoid: width (ΔX = 0.20 throughout, since quintiles are equal-sized) × average height ((Yk−1 + Yk) ÷ 2).

Interval 1 (0→20%): (0 + 0.07) ÷ 2 × 0.20 = 0.0070
Interval 2 (20%→40%): (0.07 + 0.19) ÷ 2 × 0.20 = 0.0260
Interval 3 (40%→60%): (0.19 + 0.36) ÷ 2 × 0.20 = 0.0550
Interval 4 (60%→80%): (0.36 + 0.59) ÷ 2 × 0.20 = 0.0950
Interval 5 (80%→100%): (0.59 + 1.00) ÷ 2 × 0.20 = 0.1590
Total area under Lorenz curve (B) = 0.0070+0.0260+0.0550+0.0950+0.1590 = 0.3420

Step 4: Calculate the Gini Coefficient

The total area under the line of perfect equality (Area A + Area B) is always exactly 0.5 (it is a right-angled triangle with base 1 and height 1). So Area A = 0.5 − Area B, and:

Gini = Area A ÷ (Area A + Area B) = Area A ÷ 0.5 = 2 × Area A = 2 × (0.5 − B)
Gini = 1 − 2B = 1 − (2 × 0.3420) = 1 − 0.684 = 0.316

Key point: a Gini coefficient of approximately 0.32 for this dataset indicates moderate income inequality — broadly consistent with the range many developed economies, including the UK's disposable income Gini coefficient, actually fall into (Section 8).

The General Formula: for any number of equal-sized intervals (quintiles, deciles, etc.), Gini = 1 − Σ(Xk − Xk−1)(Yk + Yk−1), where X and Y are cumulative population and income shares expressed as proportions (0 to 1) rather than percentages. This trapezoidal method is an approximation — it becomes more accurate the more data points (e.g. deciles or percentiles rather than quintiles) are used, since the straight-line segments between points more closely trace the true curve.

The Distribution of Income & Wealth in the UK Today

Applying the concepts from Sections 5–7 to real UK data reveals a clear and consistent pattern: wealth is distributed considerably more unequally than income.

MeasureApproximate UK Picture
Income Gini coefficient (disposable income, after tax and benefits)Broadly in the region of 0.34–0.36 in recent years — placing the UK among the more unequal large Western European economies, though well below the most unequal economies globally.
Wealth Gini coefficientSubstantially higher than the income Gini, typically estimated in the region of 0.6–0.7 — reflecting the compounding and inheritance effects discussed in Section 3.
Income share of the poorest 20% of householdsA relatively small single-digit percentage share of total disposable income.
Income share of the richest 20% of householdsRoughly 35–40% of total disposable income — several times the share held by the poorest 20%.
Wealth share of the wealthiest 10% of householdsEstimated at approximately half of all household wealth in Great Britain, according to the Office for National Statistics' Wealth and Assets Survey.
Figures Are Illustrative — Always Check the Latest Data: the figures above reflect broad, well-established patterns rather than a single precise year's statistics, since official measures are revised and updated regularly. For exam and coursework purposes, always cite the most recent Office for National Statistics (ONS) release — particularly its Wealth and Assets Survey and Household Finances statistics — or the Department for Work and Pensions' Households Below Average Income (HBAI) publication, both of which are updated at least annually.
★ WHY WEALTH INEQUALITY EXCEEDS INCOME INEQUALITY IN THE UK
The gap between the UK's income Gini (roughly 0.35) and wealth Gini (roughly 0.6–0.7) is not a statistical quirk — it follows directly from the factors in Section 3. Housing wealth alone is extremely unevenly distributed between outright homeowners (often older households who bought decades ago at much lower prices relative to income) and those who have never been able to afford to buy, particularly younger households priced out of homeownership in high-cost areas such as London and the South East.

How UK Income Distribution Has Changed Over Time

UK income inequality has not been constant — it has moved through distinct phases over the post-war period, closely tied to the economic policy history examined fully in Section 21.

PeriodGeneral Pattern
1950s–1970sIncome inequality was relatively low and broadly stable by historical standards, supported by strong trade union bargaining power (companion Labour Market guide, Section 12), a comprehensive welfare state (Section 21), and progressive taxation.
1980sUK income inequality rose sharply and rapidly — commonly the single largest sustained rise in modern UK economic history — coinciding with reduced trade union power, financial deregulation, rising unemployment in traditional industries, and reductions in the top rate of income tax (Section 21).
1990s–2000sInequality broadly stabilised at the higher level reached in the 1980s, with some modest reduction during the late 1990s and 2000s associated with in-work tax credits and the introduction of the National Minimum Wage (companion Labour Market guide, Section 15).
2010sIncome inequality remained broadly stable to modestly falling on some measures, even as the effects of post-2008 austerity (Section 21) reduced incomes at the bottom of the distribution in absolute terms, partly offset by rising employment rates.
2020s (COVID-19 and its aftermath)The furlough scheme and a temporary Universal Credit uplift (Section 21) cushioned incomes at the bottom during the pandemic; the subsequent cost-of-living crisis, driven substantially by energy and food price inflation, placed particular pressure on lower-income households, who spend a larger share of their budget on these necessities (companion Price Determination guide, Section 6).
Why the 1980s Remain the Key Reference Point: the scale and speed of the rise in UK income inequality during the 1980s makes it the single most frequently cited episode in UK inequality history — any answer discussing long-run UK inequality trends should reference this period explicitly, and Section 21 examines the specific policy changes behind it in detail.

The Case for a More Equal Distribution

A more equal distribution of income and wealth carries genuine likely benefits, but is not without offsetting costs — both sides need to be weighed explicitly in any evaluative answer.

Likely Benefits of Greater Equality

  • Higher aggregate demand: lower-income households tend to have a higher marginal propensity to consume, so redistributing income towards them can raise total consumer spending in the economy.
  • Improved social cohesion: research consistently associates lower inequality with lower crime rates, better measured social trust, and improved public health outcomes.
  • Greater equality of opportunity: reduced income gaps can narrow disparities in access to education, healthcare, and housing across generations, supporting long-run social mobility.
  • Reduced market failure: as Section 12 examines, excessive inequality is itself a source of market failure — reducing it can improve overall resource allocation.

Likely Costs of Greater Equality

  • Reduced incentives: the higher taxation typically required to fund redistribution (Section 18) can reduce the incentive to work extra hours, take on additional responsibility, start a business, or invest, if a large share of additional income or profit is taxed away.
  • Risk of capital flight: very high taxes on income or wealth may encourage high earners or wealth-holders to relocate to lower-tax jurisdictions, reducing the domestic tax base.
  • Potential dampening of entrepreneurship: the prospect of large financial rewards is a significant incentive for risk-taking and innovation (companion Market Structures guide, Section 24); heavily reducing that reward could reduce dynamic efficiency.
  • Administrative and compliance costs: more extensive redistribution requires a larger tax and benefits administration system, itself a real resource cost.
Exam Application: the strongest answers explicitly acknowledge that greater equality is not a free lunch — the size of any efficiency cost (via reduced incentives) needs to be weighed against the equity gain, and this trade-off is precisely why the "optimal" degree of equality is ultimately a value judgement (Section 13), not something economics alone can determine.

The Case for a More Unequal Distribution

Conversely, a degree of inequality is also argued to carry genuine economic benefits, alongside clear and well-documented costs.

Likely Benefits of Greater Inequality

  • Stronger work and effort incentives: the prospect of significantly higher reward for greater skill, effort, or risk-taking can motivate higher productivity and human capital investment (companion Labour Market guide, Section 2).
  • Greater incentive for entrepreneurship and innovation: large potential rewards for successful risk-taking help fund and motivate the creative destruction process (companion Market Structures guide, Section 28) that drives long-run economic growth.
  • Higher savings and investment: wealthier households save a larger proportion of their income, potentially supporting a larger pool of funds available for productive investment.
  • Attracting internationally mobile talent and capital: competitive rewards can help an economy attract and retain highly skilled workers, entrepreneurs, and investment that might otherwise locate elsewhere.

Likely Costs of Greater Inequality

  • Lower aggregate demand: a higher share of income going to those with a lower marginal propensity to consume can reduce total consumer spending relative to a more equal distribution.
  • Higher poverty and its associated costs: greater inequality is closely associated with higher rates of relative poverty (Section 14) and the wide-ranging economic and social costs examined in Section 17.
  • Reduced social mobility: large, persistent gaps in income and wealth (particularly wealth, Section 3) can entrench advantage across generations, undermining equality of opportunity.
  • Political and social instability: excessive inequality is widely linked in economic and political science research to reduced social cohesion and, in extreme cases, political instability.
There Is No Single "Correct" Level of Inequality: comparing Sections 10 and 11 makes clear that both greater equality and greater inequality carry a mix of benefits and costs — economics can describe these trade-offs, and can measure the current level of inequality precisely (Section 6), but cannot on its own determine the "right" amount of inequality a society should accept, since that judgement depends on how heavily a decision-maker weighs efficiency against equity (Section 13).

Inequality as Cause & Consequence of Market Failure

The specification requires a specific, more sophisticated point: excessive inequality is not simply a separate problem sitting alongside market failure — it is both a cause of market failure and a consequence of market failure, a two-way relationship worth setting out explicitly.

Inequality as a Cause of Market Failure
Where a household's income is too low to access a good with positive externalities (such as education or healthcare, companion BIZ-OMICS Market Failure guide), the resulting under-consumption represents a misallocation of resources relative to the social optimum — inequality is directly generating market failure in these related markets.
Inequality as a Consequence of Market Failure
Where a market failure (such as imperfect information or a labour market imperfection, companion Labour Market guide, Sections 9–13) already exists, it can itself worsen the distribution of income — for example, monopsony power (companion Labour Market guide, Section 11) systematically depresses wages for affected workers, widening inequality as a direct consequence of that pre-existing market failure.
1
A market failure (e.g. monopsony power, imperfect information, under-provision of education) depresses income for a group of workers

This widens income inequality — inequality as a consequence of market failure.

2
Lower income for this group reduces their ability to afford goods with positive externalities, such as further education or preventative healthcare

Under-consumption of these goods relative to the socially optimal level is itself a market failure — inequality as a cause of a further market failure.

3
This further market failure (under-investment in education/health) further depresses this group's future income and productivity

Reinforcing and widening the original inequality — completing a self-reinforcing cycle between inequality and market failure.

★ REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE: The Inequality – Education Cycle
Children from low-income UK households are statistically less likely to attend university and more likely to leave education with lower qualifications, partly reflecting reduced access to private tutoring, educational resources, and stable, low-stress home environments conducive to study — a market failure in the under-consumption of a good (education) with significant positive externalities. Lower educational attainment then translates into lower lifetime earnings for these individuals as adults, reinforcing the very income inequality that contributed to the reduced educational investment in the first place.
Exam Application: when asked to explain why inequality matters from an efficiency (not just equity) perspective, this two-way cause-and-consequence relationship with market failure is the strongest available line of argument, since it shows inequality is not merely an equity concern but a genuine source of allocative inefficiency and lost potential output for the whole economy.

Value Judgements & the Politics of Equity

While the degree of inequality can be measured objectively via the Gini coefficient and Lorenz curve (Sections 5–6), whether any given distribution is equitable (Section 4) is fundamentally a matter of value judgement, not a question economics alone can resolve.

PerspectiveView on What Constitutes an Equitable Distribution
Egalitarian perspectivePlaces strong weight on equality of outcome, viewing large income and wealth gaps as inherently unfair, and generally favouring extensive redistribution regardless of the source of the inequality.
Libertarian perspectivePlaces strong weight on property rights and freedom from interference, viewing inequality that arises from voluntary market transactions and legitimately earned reward as inherently fair, and generally opposing extensive redistribution.
Equality-of-opportunity perspectiveDistinguishes between inequality arising from differences in effort or choice (viewed as more acceptable) and inequality arising from unequal starting points, such as family background (viewed as less acceptable), favouring policies that level the starting line rather than guaranteeing equal outcomes.
Utilitarian perspectiveFavours the distribution that maximises total welfare across society, which (given the diminishing marginal utility of income, companion BIZ-OMICS Individual Economic Decision Making guide, Section 3) can imply some redistribution from rich to poor raises total welfare, but stops well short of complete equality once incentive effects (Section 10) are accounted for.
Why This Matters for Policy Prescriptions: because these perspectives rest on different underlying values rather than different facts, two people can agree entirely on the measured Gini coefficient (Section 6) for a country, yet reach completely opposite conclusions about whether government should act to reduce it — this is precisely why redistribution remains one of the most persistently contested areas of economic policy, and why Section 22 explicitly frames its evaluation in terms of competing moral and political perspectives rather than a single "correct" economic answer.

Relative vs Absolute Poverty

Poverty can be defined and measured in two distinct ways, each capturing a different aspect of hardship and each with quite different policy implications.

Absolute Poverty
A household is in absolute poverty if its income falls below a fixed, real-terms threshold representing the minimum needed to afford basic necessities (food, clothing, shelter) — this threshold is typically fixed in a base year and then adjusted only for inflation over time, not for changes in the wider standard of living.
Relative Poverty
A household is in relative poverty if its income falls below a fixed proportion (commonly 60%) of the contemporary median household income — this threshold rises and falls automatically as median income rises and falls across society, so relative poverty tracks a household's position relative to the rest of society rather than an absolute minimum standard.
ScenarioEffect on Absolute PovertyEffect on Relative Poverty
Median income rises rapidly, while the poorest households' incomes rise only slowlyAbsolute poverty may fall, since the poorest households' real income has still risen above the fixed threshold.Relative poverty may rise, since the poorest households have fallen further behind the (now higher) median.
A recession reduces incomes across the whole distribution roughly proportionatelyAbsolute poverty may rise, as real incomes fall below the fixed threshold.Relative poverty may stay broadly unchanged, or even fall, since median income has fallen too, pulling the relative poverty line down with it.
Why the Distinction Is Frequently Misunderstood: a government can genuinely raise the real incomes of the poorest households (reducing absolute poverty) while relative poverty simultaneously rises, if incomes further up the distribution are rising even faster — this is not a contradiction, but a direct consequence of the two measures capturing different things. Exam answers should always specify which measure of poverty is being discussed, since a policy's effect on one can differ sharply from its effect on the other.

Measuring Poverty & How UK Poverty Has Changed

UK poverty is officially tracked primarily through the Department for Work and Pensions' Households Below Average Income (HBAI) statistics, alongside complementary measures of material deprivation and food insecurity.

MeasureWhat It Captures
Relative poverty (before/after housing costs)The proportion of the population living in households below 60% of contemporary median income, calculated both before and after deducting housing costs — the "after housing costs" measure is particularly important in the UK given large regional variation in housing costs.
Material deprivationA direct measure of whether households can afford specific essential items and activities (adequate heating, a warm coat, an annual holiday, etc.), used alongside income-based measures to capture living standards more directly.
Food bank usage (Trussell Trust data)Widely cited as an indicator of acute financial hardship, though it reflects both the underlying level of need and the availability/awareness of food banks in a given area.
PeriodGeneral UK Poverty Trend
Late 1990s–2000sRelative child poverty fell substantially, widely attributed to the introduction of tax credits and above-inflation increases in benefits for families with children (Section 21).
2010s (austerity era)Benefit freezes and welfare reforms are widely associated with rising in-work poverty and a marked increase in food bank usage, even as employment rates rose.
2020 (COVID-19)The temporary Universal Credit uplift and furlough scheme (Section 21) are estimated to have prevented a substantial rise in poverty that would otherwise have resulted from the sharp fall in economic activity.
Post-2021 (cost-of-living crisis)Sharp rises in energy and food prices disproportionately affected lower-income households (who spend a larger share of their budget on these necessities), pushing in-work poverty and food bank usage to historically elevated levels.
Always Cite Current Official Data: as with income and wealth distribution (Section 8), specific UK poverty rates change from year to year and are subject to periodic revision — always reference the latest DWP Households Below Average Income release, or equivalent Joseph Rowntree Foundation and Trussell Trust reports, for precise up-to-date figures rather than relying on a single remembered statistic.

The Causes of Poverty

Poverty arises from a combination of labour market factors, personal circumstances, and structural economic conditions, many of which connect directly to concepts covered in the companion BIZ-OMICS Labour Market guide.

CauseExplanation
UnemploymentLoss of earned income is one of the most direct causes of poverty, particularly where unemployment benefits replace only a modest fraction of previous earnings.
Low payEven in full employment, workers in low-paid occupations (companion Labour Market guide, Section 8) may earn too little to escape relative poverty, particularly in high-cost-of-living areas — a phenomenon commonly termed "in-work poverty."
Ill health and disabilityReduces earning capacity directly, while often simultaneously increasing living costs (e.g. specialist equipment, additional heating needs), a double burden that significantly raises poverty risk.
Family structureSingle-parent households, with only one potential earner and childcare responsibilities that can restrict working hours, face a substantially elevated risk of poverty compared with dual-earner households.
Low educational attainmentDirectly limits access to higher-paid occupations (companion Labour Market guide, Section 4), and interacts with the inequality-market-failure cycle examined in Section 12.
DiscriminationWage discrimination and occupational crowding (companion Labour Market guide, Sections 17–18) can depress the earnings of affected groups below their genuine productivity, directly raising poverty risk for those groups.
Regional and structural economic declineThe decline of traditional heavy industry in parts of the UK has left some regions and towns with persistently higher unemployment and lower average wages than the national picture, concentrating poverty geographically.
High and rising housing costsBecause poverty (after housing costs) is measured net of housing costs (Section 15), areas with very high rents relative to income (particularly London) can push otherwise moderate-income households into poverty once housing costs are deducted.

The Effects of Poverty

Poverty carries significant costs both for the individuals directly affected and for the wider economy, extending well beyond the immediate lack of income.

EffectExplanation
Poorer health outcomesPoverty is strongly associated with worse physical and mental health outcomes and lower life expectancy, partly through poorer diet, housing quality, and access to healthcare, and partly through the direct stress of financial hardship.
Reduced educational attainmentChildren growing up in poverty tend to have lower educational outcomes on average, contributing to the intergenerational transmission of poverty examined in Section 12.
The poverty trapHigh effective marginal withdrawal rates on means-tested benefits (Section 20) can mean that a household gains very little extra net income from working additional hours or accepting a pay rise, weakening the incentive to increase earnings and escape poverty.
Reduced social mobilityPoverty limits access to opportunities (private tutoring, extracurricular activities, stable housing, work experience networks) that support upward mobility, reinforcing outcomes across generations.
Higher crime ratesAreas with high concentrations of poverty tend to experience higher rates of certain types of crime, imposing costs on both victims and the wider criminal justice system.
Lost economic outputAt an economy-wide level, poverty represents underutilised human potential — workers unable to invest in training, or forced into part-time or insecure work by circumstance, represent lost productive capacity relative to what the economy could otherwise achieve.
Higher public spendingPoverty raises demand for means-tested benefits, social services, and healthcare, increasing government spending and, indirectly, the tax burden needed to fund it.
Why Poverty's Effects Reinforce Its Causes: several of these effects — reduced educational attainment, poorer health, the poverty trap — directly feed back into Section 16's causes of poverty (low educational attainment, ill health, weak work incentives), creating a self-reinforcing cycle very similar in structure to the inequality-market-failure cycle described in Section 12. This cyclical, self-reinforcing quality is one of the strongest arguments for early and sustained policy intervention (Section 19) rather than relying on the effects of poverty to resolve themselves.
18

Policies to Redistribute Income & Wealth

Governments have a range of policy tools available to influence the distribution of income and wealth, operating through the tax system, the benefits system, and direct labour market intervention. Every policy in this section works towards the same visual outcome: shifting the Lorenz curve closer to the line of perfect equality.

Fig 3: How redistribution policy shifts the Lorenz curve. The outer red curve shows the distribution of market income — income before any government intervention. Progressive taxation and means-tested benefits (the policies in the table below) pull the curve inward, closer to the line of perfect equality, producing the inner green curve for disposable income (after taxes and transfers). The smaller the gap between the after-tax curve and the diagonal, the lower the resulting Gini coefficient (Section 6) — this is precisely how the ONS separates market income inequality from disposable income inequality when reporting UK statistics, and the gap between the two curves is a direct visual measure of how much redistribution the UK tax and benefits system is currently achieving.
PolicyMechanism
Progressive income taxationCharging a higher marginal tax rate on higher income bands directly redistributes from higher to lower earners once tax revenue funds spending that benefits lower-income households.
Inheritance taxTaxes the transfer of wealth between generations, directly targeting the intergenerational wealth-compounding effect described in Section 3.
Wealth taxes (proposed/limited in the UK)A direct annual tax on the stock of wealth itself (rather than on income or transfers) has been proposed by some UK policymakers as a way to address wealth inequality specifically, though no general UK wealth tax currently exists.
The National Minimum Wage / National Living WageDirectly raises the wage floor for the lowest-paid workers (companion Labour Market guide, Sections 14–16), narrowing wage inequality at the bottom of the distribution.
Progressive public spendingSpending disproportionately benefiting lower-income households (state education, the NHS, social housing) acts as a redistribution "in kind," even without any direct cash transfer.
Trade union support and collective bargaining rightsStrengthening workers' collective bargaining power (companion Labour Market guide, Section 13) can raise wages for lower-paid workers, particularly in monopsony labour markets.
Wealth Redistribution Is Structurally Harder Than Income Redistribution: because wealth is a stock that can be moved, hidden, or held in a wide variety of asset forms (Section 3), taxing wealth directly is administratively more difficult and more prone to avoidance than taxing income, which is why most UK redistribution historically operates primarily through the income tax and benefits system, with wealth taxed more selectively (inheritance tax, capital gains tax, council tax) rather than through a single comprehensive wealth tax.
★ EVALUATIVE CONSEQUENCE: Progressive Taxation and the Laffer Curve
Raising the top marginal income tax rate does not necessarily raise proportionately more revenue, and can in principle raise less, if the rate becomes high enough to significantly discourage effort, encourage tax avoidance, or drive high earners to relocate abroad — the relationship described by the Laffer curve. The UK's own experience with the 50p additional tax rate is a directly relevant test case: introduced in 2010 on incomes above £150,000, HM Revenue & Customs' own subsequent analysis found the rate raised substantially less revenue than a simple static calculation would predict, due to significant behavioural response (income shifting, reduced reported income, and some emigration), and the rate was cut to 45p in 2013. This does not prove progressive taxation is self-defeating in general, but it illustrates that the size of any efficiency cost from higher rates (Section 10) is an empirical question, not something that can simply be assumed away.
★ EVALUATIVE CONSEQUENCE: Inheritance Tax and Avoidance
UK inheritance tax, despite a headline rate of 40% on estates above the tax-free threshold, raises a comparatively modest share of total UK tax revenue, largely because of extensive reliefs and exemptions (for agricultural land, certain business assets, and transfers into trusts) that wealthier estates are disproportionately able to make use of with professional advice. This is a direct, practical illustration of Section 3's point that wealth is "stickier" and harder to tax effectively than income — a tax explicitly designed to address the intergenerational compounding of wealth can be substantially reduced in practice by the same wealth-holders' capacity to plan around it, a recurring theme in evaluating any wealth-focused redistribution policy.

Policies to Alleviate Poverty

Alongside broader redistribution (Section 18), governments deploy policies specifically targeted at reducing poverty, often focused on households at the very bottom of the income distribution.

PolicyMechanism
Means-tested benefits (e.g. Universal Credit)Provides direct income support targeted specifically at low-income households, tapering away as earned income rises.
In-work tax creditsTop up the income of low-paid working households, specifically addressing in-work poverty (Section 16) rather than only unemployment-related poverty.
Universal (non-means-tested) benefitsProvided to all households regardless of income (e.g. certain child benefit elements), avoiding the poverty trap effects of means-testing (Section 17) at the cost of also benefiting higher-income households.
Free school meals and the Sure Start programmeTargeted early-years and in-school support aimed specifically at breaking the intergenerational poverty cycle described in Sections 12 and 17.
Social housing and housing benefitDirectly addresses the housing cost component of poverty (Section 16), particularly important given the "after housing costs" poverty measure.
Universal Basic Income (UBI) — a debated policy optionAn unconditional cash payment to all citizens regardless of means or work status, proposed by some as a simpler alternative to complex means-tested benefits, though not currently implemented as UK-wide policy — several small-scale UK pilot schemes have tested the concept.
Means-Tested vs Universal Benefits — A Recurring Policy Trade-Off: means-tested benefits are more precisely targeted at those in genuine need, making them more cost-effective per pound spent on reducing poverty — but the withdrawal of means-tested support as earnings rise creates the poverty trap effect described in Section 17, and can carry social stigma that reduces take-up among eligible households. Universal benefits avoid these problems but are far more expensive, since a substantial share goes to households who do not strictly need the support. This exact trade-off underpins ongoing UK debate over Universal Credit's design and proposals such as UBI.
★ EVALUATIVE CONSEQUENCE: The Universal Credit Rollout
Universal Credit was designed to simplify the UK benefits system and improve work incentives by replacing six separate means-tested benefits with a single payment on a smoother taper. In practice, its rollout revealed significant unintended consequences: a built-in five-week wait for a first payment pushed some new claimants into debt or reliance on food banks before receiving any support, prompting the introduction of repayable "advance payments." The original 63% taper rate (the rate at which benefit is withdrawn as earnings rise) was itself criticised as too steep, discouraging additional hours of work — it was reduced to 55% in 2021, directly illustrating the poverty trap trade-off from Section 20 in live policy design, and showing that even a policy explicitly built to solve a known problem (the old system's complexity and weak incentives) can introduce new evaluative costs of its own.
★ EVALUATIVE CONSEQUENCE: Universal Basic Income Pilot Evidence
Small-scale UBI pilots, including a 2022 Welsh scheme for young care leavers and Finland's 2017–2018 national trial, have generally found improvements in participants' self-reported wellbeing and mental health, but only modest or statistically inconclusive effects on employment rates — evidence that cuts both ways in the wider UBI debate. Supporters point to the wellbeing gains and the removal of poverty-trap disincentives (Section 20) as justification for wider rollout; critics point to the very high fiscal cost of paying a full UBI to the entire population (not just those in need) and the limited evidence of any large positive employment effect as reasons for caution, illustrating precisely the kind of evidence-based but ultimately values-dependent judgement described in Section 22.

The Economic Consequences of Redistribution

Both redistribution (Section 18) and poverty alleviation (Section 19) policies carry real economic consequences beyond their immediate, intended effect on the income distribution — some positive, some negative.

Potential Positive Consequences

  • Higher aggregate demand, as redistributed income typically flows to households with a higher marginal propensity to consume (Section 10).
  • Improved long-run productivity and human capital, if redistribution funds better health, education, and living conditions for lower-income households (breaking the cycle in Section 12).
  • Reduced costs elsewhere in the economy associated with poverty (crime, ill health, lost output, Section 17), potentially offsetting some of the fiscal cost of the policy itself.
  • Greater social and political stability, supporting a more predictable environment for long-run business investment.

Potential Negative Consequences

  • The poverty trap: high effective marginal withdrawal rates from means-tested benefits (Section 19) can leave some households facing very little net financial reward from working more hours or accepting a pay rise — in extreme cases, an effective marginal tax rate exceeding 90% once benefit withdrawal and income tax are combined.
  • Reduced incentives to work, save, or invest among higher earners facing higher marginal tax rates (Section 10), potentially reducing the tax base being used to fund redistribution in the first place.
  • Significant fiscal cost, requiring either higher taxation elsewhere, higher government borrowing, or reduced spending in other areas.
  • Possible unintended behavioural effects, such as changes to household formation or labour supply decisions in response to how benefits are structured (e.g. around cohabitation or part-time hours thresholds).

Illustrating the Poverty Trap

A worker earns an additional £100 in gross pay. Income tax and National Insurance take £32, and means-tested benefits are withdrawn by a further £55 as earnings rise (a 55% taper rate, similar in scale to Universal Credit's taper).

Net gain from an extra £100 earned = £100 − £32 (tax/NI) − £55 (benefit withdrawal) = £13
Effective marginal "tax" rate = (£32 + £55) ÷ £100 = 87%

Key point: the worker keeps only £13 of every additional £100 earned — a very weak financial incentive to work more hours or seek a pay rise, illustrating precisely why the design of benefit taper rates is such a consequential and contested area of poverty-alleviation policy.

Historic UK Policy: When Inequality Grew or Fell

UK economic policy history provides several clear, well-documented episodes in which specific government choices are widely credited (or blamed) for significant movements in income inequality — directly illustrating Section 9's long-run trend.

Period & GovernmentPolicy ChangesEffect on Inequality
1945–1951 (Attlee, Labour)Creation of the NHS and the modern welfare state, including comprehensive National Insurance; large-scale nationalisation of key industries.Inequality fell, as the newly-created welfare state provided a substantial income and in-kind floor beneath the poorest households.
1979–1990 (Thatcher, Conservative)The top rate of income tax was cut sharply (from 83% to 60% and later to 40%); trade union power was significantly reduced through legislation; financial markets were deregulated (the 1986 "Big Bang"); many state-owned industries were privatised.Inequality rose sharply and rapidly — the single largest sustained rise in UK income inequality of the post-war era (Section 9), widely attributed to this specific combination of tax, labour market, and financial policy changes.
1997–2010 (Blair/Brown, Labour)Introduction of the National Minimum Wage (1999); introduction and expansion of in-work tax credits; substantial real-terms increases in child-related benefits and NHS/education spending.Relative child poverty and pensioner poverty fell substantially; overall income inequality broadly stabilised rather than continuing to rise.
2010–2019 (Coalition/Conservative, austerity era)Significant cuts to working-age benefits, a multi-year freeze on many benefit rates, and the gradual rollout of Universal Credit, alongside continued increases in the National Living Wage.Overall income inequality remained broadly stable to modestly falling on some measures (partly due to strong employment growth and NLW increases), but in-work poverty and food bank usage rose significantly, reflecting real-terms cuts to benefit income.
2020–2021 (COVID-19 pandemic)The furlough (Coronavirus Job Retention Scheme) and a temporary £20-per-week Universal Credit uplift provided substantial, if temporary, income support.These measures are estimated to have significantly limited what would otherwise have been a sharp rise in poverty during the pandemic-driven economic contraction.
★ CASE STUDY: The Thatcher Governments (1979–1990) — A Deeper Look

The Thatcher era is worth examining in more depth than any other period in this table, because it is the single episode where the efficiency case for policy and the equity cost of that same policy are both unusually well documented and directly opposed to one another — making it an ideal case study for the evaluative approach set out in Sections 20 and 22.

The efficiency case made at the time: incoming in 1979 to a top marginal income tax rate of 83% (98% on some investment income), the government argued, drawing on supply-side and Laffer curve reasoning, that such high rates were actively discouraging effort, entrepreneurship, and investment, and driving high earners and capital abroad. Cutting the top rate to 60% in 1979 and to 40% by 1988, alongside reducing trade union power (seen as distorting labour markets and protecting insider wages at the expense of the unemployed) and deregulating financial markets (the 1986 "Big Bang"), was presented as removing distortions that were holding back growth and dynamic efficiency (companion Market Structures guide, Section 30) — consistent with the Section 11 case for greater inequality as a spur to effort and enterprise.

The equity cost that followed: the UK's income Gini coefficient rose from around 0.25 in the late 1970s to around 0.34 by the early 1990s — the fastest sustained rise of the entire post-war period (Section 9). The effects were not evenly spread: deindustrialisation and colliery/steelworks closures concentrated unemployment sharply in the North of England, Scotland, and Wales, entrenching a persistent regional "North-South divide" in both income and wealth (companion Labour Market guide, Section 16, on structural/regional causes of poverty). Reduced trade union bargaining power (companion Labour Market guide, Section 12) is widely credited with weakening wage growth specifically for lower-paid workers, widening the gap between low and high earners even before any tax change is considered. The 1980 Right to Buy policy, which let council tenants purchase their homes at a significant discount, increased homeownership and personal wealth for those able to buy, but is also linked to a long-run reduction in the social housing stock, a factor in the housing-cost pressures on poverty measured "after housing costs" today (Section 15).

Why this case resists a simple verdict: whether the Thatcher governments' record on income distribution should be judged a success or a failure depends heavily on which perspective in Section 22 is applied. A free-market perspective can point to the strong revenue eventually raised from top earners even at lower rates, and to the UK's return to sustained economic growth during the 1980s, as evidence that reducing punitive tax rates was ultimately efficient. An egalitarian or communitarian perspective points to the scale and regional concentration of the resulting inequality, and to the social costs associated with it (Section 10), as evidence that the efficiency gains came at an unacceptable equity price. Both readings are consistent with the same underlying data — exactly the value-judgement dependency Section 13 describes.

Exam Application: when asked for a historic example of government policy affecting inequality, the 1980s remains the strongest, most extensively documented single episode to cite for a rise in inequality, while the 1945–1951 Attlee government and the introduction of the National Minimum Wage in 1999 are the strongest episodes to cite for policy-driven reductions — always link the specific policy change explicitly to the mechanism (tax rates, union power, benefit generosity) that plausibly explains the resulting change in measured inequality.

Evaluating Redistribution: Moral & Political Perspectives

Bringing together the value judgements discussed in Section 13, evaluating any specific redistribution or poverty-alleviation policy ultimately requires recognising that different, entirely legitimate moral and political perspectives can reach different conclusions from the same economic evidence.

PerspectiveLikely View on Redistribution Policy
Egalitarian / social democratic perspectiveGenerally supports extensive redistribution through progressive taxation and generous benefits, viewing the resulting efficiency costs (Section 20) as an acceptable price for greater equity and reduced poverty.
Free-market / libertarian perspectiveGenerally favours minimal redistribution, prioritising incentive effects, property rights, and personal responsibility, and viewing extensive state intervention in the distribution of income as itself a source of inefficiency and reduced individual freedom.
"Third way" / targeted perspectiveFavours redistribution targeted specifically at improving equality of opportunity (education, early years support) rather than equalising outcomes directly, seeking to balance efficiency and equity concerns.
Communitarian perspectiveEmphasises the social and community costs of inequality (Section 10) — crime, reduced trust, social division — as a primary justification for redistribution, distinct from purely individual equity or efficiency arguments.
Why a "Best" Evaluative Answer Presents Multiple Perspectives Rather Than a Single Verdict: AQA explicitly expects students to recognise the moral and political dimension of this topic — a genuinely excellent answer on redistribution policy does not conclude simply that a policy "is good" or "is bad," but explains how the same policy would be judged differently by, for example, an egalitarian and a libertarian, tying each judgement back to the specific values (equality of outcome vs individual freedom, equity vs incentive effects) underlying that perspective.

Exam Technique & Model Chains of Reasoning

The Golden Chain: From Labour Market Imperfection to Entrenched Inequality

A labour market imperfection (e.g. monopsony power or wage discrimination, companion Labour Market guide, Sections 11 and 17) depresses wages for an affected group of workers → this widens income inequality between this group and the rest of the workforce (Section 2) → lower income restricts this group's ability to invest in education and training for their children, a good with positive externalities (Section 12) → reduced educational investment lowers this group's future earning potential, widening inequality further into the next generation → because wealth also compounds and can be inherited (Section 3), this income gap gradually translates into an even larger wealth gap over time, explaining why the UK's wealth Gini coefficient consistently exceeds its income Gini coefficient (Section 8).

The Golden Chain: From Tax Policy to Measured Inequality

A government significantly cuts the top marginal rate of income tax (as in the UK during the 1980s, Section 21) → post-tax income for the highest earners rises by more than for lower earners, directly widening the income distribution → this shifts the Lorenz curve further away from the line of perfect equality (Section 5), raising the measured Gini coefficient (Section 6) → whether this change is judged to be a problem depends entirely on the value judgement applied (Section 13) — a libertarian perspective may view the change as a legitimate return of income to those who earned it, while an egalitarian perspective may view the same change as a worsening of an already inequitable distribution (Section 22).

Key Exam Tips

1

Always distinguish income (a flow) from wealth (a stock) explicitly at the start of any answer touching on both (Section 1) — conflating the two is one of the most common and heavily penalised errors in this topic.

2

Remember you are expected to interpret a Gini coefficient or Lorenz curve, not calculate one from scratch (Section 6) — focus exam preparation on confidently reading and comparing given figures rather than memorising the calculation method in Section 7.

3

Always specify whether you are discussing relative or absolute poverty (Section 14) — a policy can very plausibly move the two measures in opposite directions, and examiners specifically reward this distinction being drawn out.

4

When discussing whether a distribution of income is "fair," explicitly flag that this is a value judgement (Sections 4 and 13) rather than asserting a single correct answer — this signals exactly the kind of evaluative sophistication AQA is looking for in this topic.

5

For questions on redistribution policy, always weigh the equity gain against the specific efficiency cost (incentive effects, the poverty trap, Section 20) rather than treating redistribution as costless — quantifying an illustrative effective marginal tax rate, as in Section 20's worked example, is a strong way to demonstrate this.

6

When asked for a historic UK example of policy affecting inequality, have at least one clear example of inequality rising (the 1980s, Section 21) and one of it falling (the 1945–51 welfare state, or the 1999 National Minimum Wage) ready to deploy, each tied to a specific, named policy mechanism.
