1973 – 1981

The 1970s Oil Crises

Two separate supply shocks triggered by geopolitical events reshaped global energy economics, ended the post-war Keynesian consensus, and gave economists a new vocabulary: stagflation. The decade demonstrated how supply-side disruptions can simultaneously raise prices and depress output — a policy-maker's nightmare.

What Happened

On 17 October 1973, OAPEC (Arab members of OPEC) announced an embargo on oil exports to the US, Netherlands, and other Israeli supporters. OPEC cut total production by 25%, causing prices to rise from $3 to nearly $12 per barrel by March 1974.

Economic Mechanism

  • Supply-side shock → cost-push inflation across all oil-dependent industries
  • Rising energy costs passed on as higher prices throughout supply chain
  • Simultaneously, higher energy costs reduced aggregate supply
  • Result: inflation AND recession simultaneously — "stagflation"
Stagflation invalidated the Philips Curve trade-off assumed by Keynesian policy-makers. Governments could not use expansionary policy to escape recession without worsening inflation.

The Policy Dilemma

  • Keynesian demand management assumed inflation and unemployment move inversely
  • Stagflation meant cutting rates to fight unemployment → worsened inflation
  • Raising rates to tame inflation → deepened recession
  • UK inflation hit 25% in 1975; IMF loan required in 1976

Long-Run Consequences

The crisis discredited Keynesian demand management and empowered monetarist thinking (Milton Friedman). It directly led to the rise of Thatcher (1979) and Reagan (1980) — both committed to controlling inflation as the primary policy goal.

The 1976 IMF loan to the UK required £2.5bn in spending cuts — the first post-war austerity, foreshadowing later debates about fiscal policy credibility.

Monetarist Policy in Action

  • Volcker embraced monetarism: control money supply to control inflation
  • Deliberately accepted deep recession to establish anti-inflationary credibility
  • Mortgage rates above 18% froze the housing market entirely
  • Unemployment peaked at 10.8% — highest since 1930s

The Credibility Principle

Volcker's approach established that central bank credibility — the belief that it will act decisively against inflation — is a key policy asset. UK parallels: Thatcher's parallel commitment to tight monetary policy, accepting the deep 1980–81 recession. Long-run inflation expectations were permanently reset downward.

By 1983, inflation fell below 4%, launching the decade-long Reagan boom. The credibility won by accepting the recession was worth the short-run cost — Volcker's lasting legacy to central banking.

The Turbulent 1990s

The decade opened with Japan's spectacular bubble implosion and closed with Russia's sovereign default nearly triggering a global meltdown. In between: the Mexican Tequila Crisis, the Asian Contagion, and the collapse of Long-Term Capital Management — a decade-long series of emerging market crises that rewrote the rulebook on globalisation, IMF conditionality, and the fragility of fixed exchange rates.

Anatomy of a Bubble

  • Bank of Japan kept interest rates low → cheap credit fuelled speculation

  • Keiretsu system encouraged opaque corporate borrowing

The Balance Sheet Recession

  • Corporations prioritised debt repayment over investment — "zombie firms"
  • Banks refused to write off bad loans — regulatory forbearance perpetuated crisis
  • Deflation raised real value of debts, deepening the trap
  • Fiscal stimulus (zero rates + public works) failed to restart private demand

AQA Exam Relevance

Japan's Lost Decade is the key historical precedent cited in any discussion of: liquidity traps, zero lower bound monetary policy, quantitative easing rationale, fiscal multiplier debates, and secular stagnation. It directly influenced post-2008 policy in the UK and US.

Government debt rose from 65% to 140% of GDP — fiscal stimulus alone cannot overcome a balance sheet recession. A lesson that influenced post-2008 austerity debates.

Common Structural Weaknesses

  • Large current account deficits financed by short-term foreign borrowing
  • Fixed or semi-fixed exchange rate pegs to the US dollar
  • Poor financial regulation — crony capitalism concealed true balance sheets
  • Over-investment in real estate and export sectors built on borrowed growth

The IMF Controversy

IMF conditionality required austerity (spending cuts + high interest rates) in countries already in recession — widely blamed for deepening human suffering. Indonesia's middle class was virtually eliminated overnight. Suharto's 32-year rule collapsed. The IMF's approach was later substantially revised.

The human cost was enormous — Indonesia's poverty rate doubled in one year. South Korean unemployment tripled. The crisis directly triggered political regime changes across the region.

LTCM: When Genius Failed

The Global Financial Crisis

The worst financial crisis since 1929 originated in US subprime mortgage markets and rapidly spread through complex financial instruments to every corner of the global economy. Central banks and governments deployed tools without precedent — quantitative easing, bank nationalisations, and trillion-dollar bailout packages — to prevent a second Great Depression.

How the System Failed

  • Originate-to-distribute model removed incentive for careful lending
  • CDOs (Collateralised Debt Obligations) packaged junk mortgages as AAA-rated assets
  • Rating agencies had conflicts of interest — paid by issuers they rated
  • Leverage of 30:1 or more meant small losses wiped out equity buffers

Northern Rock's Fatal Business Model

  • Funded 75% of mortgages via short-term wholesale markets (industry average: 25%)
  • Offered "Together mortgages" — 125% loan-to-value at 6× income multiples
  • When interbank markets froze in August 2007, funding model collapsed
  • Assets grew from £3bn (1997) to £113bn (2007) — reckless expansion
The bank run demonstrated that deposit insurance without full coverage leaves banks vulnerable to coordination failure — each depositor's rational action to withdraw creates the collective catastrophe they fear.

February 17, 2008
Northern Rock Nationalised
After Virgin Group and Olivant fail to meet Treasury terms, the government nationalises Northern Rock — the first UK bank nationalisation since the 1970s. The state assumes £13.4bn of emergency lending.

Nationalisation vs. Bailout

Unlike US TARP which injected capital into private banks, UK nationalisation transferred ownership to the state. This was politically controversial — "socialism for bankers" — but ultimately allowed more direct control over lending behaviour. Northern Rock was later split into a "good bank" (mortgages) and "bad bank" (toxic assets).

The "British Model"

Gordon Brown's comprehensive recapitalisation approach — equity injection in exchange for government stakes — was copied by the US, Germany and France within days. The alternative (only guaranteeing debts) was seen as less effective. RBS required an 84% government stake; Lloyds 43%.

The comprehensive approach — though hugely expensive — did prevent a complete UK banking system collapse. Lloyds was fully returned to private ownership by 2017; RBS/NatWest stake reduced gradually.

How QE Works

  • BoE creates new money electronically and uses it to buy government bonds (gilts)
  • Drives down gilt yields → reduces long-term borrowing costs throughout economy
  • Pushes investors into riskier assets (portfolio rebalancing effect)
  • Wealth effect: rising asset prices boost confidence and spending
  • Exchange rate channel: sterling weakness supports exports
QE's distributional effects were regressive — it disproportionately benefited asset owners. The BoE's own analysis suggested it widened wealth inequality, even while supporting aggregate output.

Iceland's Unorthodox Recovery

Iceland let banks fail, jailed bankers, imposed capital controls, and devalued sharply. Despite initial pain, Iceland recovered faster than Ireland (which guaranteed all bank debt). The comparison remains a live debate about whether bank creditors should be bailed in or bailed out.

Iceland's recovery became a case study in the alternative to bailouts: letting banks fail, imposing creditor losses (bail-in), and using the exchange rate as an adjustment mechanism.

What Was Announced

Why Markets Panicked

The absence of an OBR forecast removed any independent verification of fiscal sustainability. Gilt markets priced in the risk of unsustainable debt dynamics. The combination of: (1) no credible fiscal anchor, (2) already-high inflation, and (3) the BoE actively raising rates created an impossible policy position. Lenders demanded higher yields to compensate for perceived risk.

30-year gilt yields rose 1.3 percentage points in a single day — one of the largest single-day moves in gilt history. Sterling fell 4.7% against the dollar to near-parity. Markets were effectively calling the policy unsustainable.

The LDI Crisis — A Near Miss

  • Defined benefit pension funds used LDI strategies — leveraged gilt holdings to match liabilities
  • When gilt prices fell sharply, funds faced margin calls requiring immediate cash
  • To raise cash, they had to sell gilts → further gilt price falls → further margin calls (fire sale spiral)
  • BoE Governor Bailey later said funds were "hours from insolvency"
The BoE was simultaneously trying to raise rates to fight inflation (selling gilts under QT) and buying gilts to rescue pension funds — a direct contradiction created by government fiscal policy. The institution's credibility was severely strained.

A "Phase One" deal in 2020 provided only partial relief; the structural tensions over technology, intellectual property, and supply chain decoupling remain unresolved.

March 2018
Steel & Aluminium Tariffs — Opening Shot
Trump announces 25% tariffs on steel and 10% on aluminium under Section 232 (national security). China is among the countries affected, marking the start of escalating trade tensions.

Why Section 232?

Using national security justification under Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act (1962) allowed tariffs without the usual WTO challenge process. The move was controversial — allies including the EU, Canada, and Japan were also initially affected, before receiving exemptions. China was not exempted.

China immediately announced retaliatory tariffs on US agricultural goods — deliberately targeting Republican congressional districts to create domestic political pressure on the Trump administration. Soybean exports to China fell 75%.

The Strategic Logic

  • Semiconductors are foundational to AI, military systems, and advanced manufacturing
  • Taiwan (TSMC) produces over 90% of the world's most advanced chips — a single point of failure
  • US controls key chokepoints: chip design (Nvidia, Qualcomm), EUV lithography (ASML, Dutch)
  • Goal: delay China's AI and military modernisation by 10–20 years

Industrial Policy Revival

The CHIPS Act represents the largest US industrial policy intervention since WWII — a fundamental break from 40 years of free-market consensus. The WTO's "level playing field" rules are being informally abandoned by the world's largest economies in strategically critical sectors.

China responded with export controls on gallium and germanium — materials essential for semiconductors. The "chip war" risks fragmenting global technology supply chains permanently, with significant efficiency costs.

The Green Transition Complication

China dominates global EV and battery production — accounting for over 60% of global EV sales and 70% of battery manufacturing. US tariffs on Chinese EVs protect domestic manufacturers (primarily Tesla and emerging US firms) but risk slowing decarbonisation by keeping EV prices high. This creates a direct tension between industrial policy, trade policy, and climate goals.

The bipartisan consensus in the US on China trade restrictions suggests this is a structural shift, not just a Trump-era policy. Decoupling of the world's two largest economies carries substantial global efficiency costs — IMF estimates 5–7% permanent loss in global output from full decoupling.
