What Is an Exchange Rate & Why Does It Matter?

An exchange rate is the price of one currency expressed in terms of another. If £1 = $1.25, that is the price of a pound in dollar terms — exactly like the price of any other good, except the "good" being priced is money itself. Because almost every cross-border transaction — buying a foreign holiday, importing components, a multinational repatriating profits, a government issuing debt to foreign investors — requires converting one currency into another, the exchange rate touches nearly every part of macroeconomic performance.

Direct Quotation
The price of one unit of foreign currency in terms of domestic currency (e.g. a UK resident sees £0.80 = $1). Used by most countries for their own currency.
Indirect Quotation
The price of one unit of domestic currency in terms of foreign currency (e.g. £1 = $1.25). The convention traditionally used for sterling and a small number of other currencies.

Why the Exchange Rate Matters

1
International competitiveness

The exchange rate determines the foreign-currency price of a country's exports and the domestic-currency price of its imports. A stronger currency makes exports more expensive abroad and imports cheaper at home — directly affecting the trade balance (X − M) and therefore aggregate demand.

2
Inflation

A weaker currency raises the price of imported raw materials, components and finished goods, feeding directly into the Consumer Prices Index — a phenomenon known as imported inflation. A stronger currency has the opposite, disinflationary effect. This is one reason the exchange rate is watched so closely by the MPC when setting the Bank Rate.

3
Investment flows

Investors comparing returns across countries must consider not just the interest rate or profit on offer, but what will happen to the exchange rate over the life of the investment. Expected depreciation can wipe out an otherwise attractive return once converted back into the investor's home currency.

4
Cost of living & living standards

For consumers, the exchange rate determines the price of foreign holidays, imported food and fuel (oil and gas are priced in dollars globally), and imported consumer goods — feeding directly into real disposable income.

5
Government & corporate debt

Where governments or firms have borrowed in a foreign currency (common in many emerging economies), a depreciation of the domestic currency increases the domestic-currency cost of servicing that debt — a major channel through which currency crises become debt crises.

How Exchange Rates Can Differ — Nominal, Real & Effective

Students often assume "the exchange rate" is a single number, but economists distinguish between several measures depending on what question is being asked.

MeasureWhat It ShowsWhy It Matters
Nominal Bilateral Exchange RateThe straightforward market price of one currency against one other currency (e.g. GBP/USD). The number you see quoted on the news.Simple to understand but tells you nothing about relative price levels or overall trade competitiveness.
Real Exchange RateThe nominal rate adjusted for the difference in inflation rates between the two countries. Real rate = Nominal rate × (Domestic price level ÷ Foreign price level).Captures true competitiveness. A country can have a stable nominal rate but still lose competitiveness if its inflation persistently runs above its trading partners' — its real exchange rate appreciates even while the nominal rate is unchanged.
Effective (Trade-Weighted) Exchange RateAn index measuring a currency's value against a basket of other currencies, each weighted by the share of trade conducted with that country. Published by central banks (e.g. the Bank of England's Sterling Effective Exchange Rate Index).A single bilateral rate (say GBP/USD) can mislead if a country trades mainly with the Eurozone. The effective rate gives a much more accurate single-figure summary of what is happening to a currency's overall value and competitiveness.
Spot RateThe exchange rate for immediate (typically two-business-day) delivery of currency.The rate used for most everyday transactions and the rate usually quoted in the media.
Forward RateAn exchange rate agreed today for a currency transaction that will actually take place at a specified date in the future.Allows firms to hedge against exchange rate risk — e.g. an exporter can lock in today's rate for payment due in six months, removing uncertainty over what they will actually receive.
★ REAL WORLD: Why the Real Rate Can Diverge From the Nominal Rate
If UK inflation runs at 4% while US inflation runs at 2%, and the nominal GBP/USD rate stays completely flat, UK goods are becoming progressively more expensive relative to US goods each year. The real exchange rate has appreciated even though the nominal rate has not moved at all — UK exporters are quietly losing competitiveness without any headline currency move to explain it.

The Role of the FOREX Market

The Foreign Exchange (FOREX or FX) market is the global marketplace in which currencies are bought and sold. It is the largest financial market in the world by trading volume, operating 24 hours a day as trading passes between financial centres in Tokyo, London, and New York. Unlike a stock exchange, FOREX has no single physical location or central exchange — it is an over-the-counter (OTC) market, made up of a decentralised global network of banks, brokers, and electronic trading platforms.

Who Participates in the FOREX Market?

Commercial Banks
The core of the market, trading on their own account and on behalf of clients. A small number of major global banks account for the majority of daily FOREX turnover.
Central Banks
May intervene to influence the value of their own currency (buying or selling reserves), manage a fixed or managed exchange rate, or simply hold foreign currency reserves for stability purposes.
Corporations (MNCs)
Multinational firms convert currency to pay suppliers, repatriate profits, and hedge future foreign-currency receipts and payments using forward contracts.
Institutional Investors
Pension funds, hedge funds, and sovereign wealth funds trade currency as part of holding foreign assets (bonds, equities) or as a speculative investment in its own right.
Speculators
Traders who buy and sell currency purely to profit from anticipated movements in the exchange rate, with no underlying trade or investment need. Speculative flows can be a significant driver of short-term volatility — and, as Black Wednesday later in this guide shows, can force a change in government policy.

How the FOREX Market Determines a Floating Exchange Rate

In a floating system, the exchange rate is simply the price at which the quantity of a currency demanded equals the quantity supplied — exactly like the market for any other good. Demand for sterling comes from anyone wanting to buy UK exports, invest in UK assets, or hold sterling for other reasons; supply of sterling comes from UK residents wanting to buy foreign currency to pay for imports or invest abroad. Where the two curves intersect determines the equilibrium exchange rate.

Fig 1: Equilibrium in the market for sterling. The exchange rate (e1) is set purely by the intersection of demand for and supply of the currency, at quantity Q1, with no central bank intervention.

Anything that shifts demand for or supply of a currency will move its floating value: a rise in UK interest rates (attracting hot money and shifting demand for sterling to the right, appreciating the pound), a fall in UK export competitiveness (reducing demand for sterling), a current account deficit (more sterling supplied to buy imports than is demanded to buy exports, depreciating the pound), or a shift in speculative sentiment about future UK growth or inflation.

Calculating With Exchange Rates — Worked Examples

AQA calculation questions on exchange rates typically test currency conversion, cross rates, and percentage appreciation/depreciation. Working through each type carefully avoids common errors.

Example 1 — Simple Conversion

£1 = $1.30 → How many dollars does a UK tourist get for £500?

£500 × 1.30 = $650. To convert the other way — how many pounds does $650 buy — divide by the rate: $650 ÷ 1.30 = £500.

Example 2 — The Effect of a Change in the Exchange Rate on Price

A UK exporter sells a machine for £10,000. At £1 = $1.25, a US buyer pays $12,500. If sterling appreciates to £1 = $1.35, the same £10,000 machine (unchanged in pound terms) now costs the US buyer $13,500 — an 8% price rise from the US buyer's perspective, with no change whatsoever in the UK manufacturer's own price. This is precisely why an appreciation damages export competitiveness even when domestic firms have changed nothing about their pricing.

Example 3 — Percentage Appreciation / Depreciation

% change = [(New rate − Old rate) ÷ Old rate] × 100

If the pound moves from £1 = $1.20 to £1 = $1.32: % change = (1.32 − 1.20) ÷ 1.20 × 100 = +10% appreciation. If it instead fell from £1 = $1.20 to £1 = $1.08: % change = (1.08 − 1.20) ÷ 1.20 × 100 = −10% depreciation.

Example 4 — Cross Rates

Cross rates let you calculate an exchange rate between two currencies where only their rates against a third currency (usually the dollar) are known. If £1 = $1.25 and €1 = $1.10, then:

£1 in € = $1.25 ÷ $1.10 = €1.136

Example 5 — Purchasing Power & the Big Mac Index

The Big Mac Index (The Economist) is a light-hearted but genuinely useful illustration of Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) — the theory that in the long run, exchange rates should adjust so that an identical basket of goods costs the same amount in any country once converted into a common currency. If a Big Mac costs £4.00 in the UK and $5.50 in the US, the implied PPP exchange rate is £1 = $1.375 (5.50 ÷ 4.00). If the actual market exchange rate is £1 = $1.25, sterling is judged to be undervalued against the dollar by this measure, since it buys fewer dollars than PPP would predict.

★ REAL WORLD: Why PPP Rarely Holds Exactly
Actual market exchange rates deviate from PPP for long periods because of transport costs, trade barriers, non-tradable services (rent, labour) built into prices, taxes, and — most importantly — speculative and hot money flows that dominate short-run currency movements far more than trade in goods does.

Floating Exchange Rate Systems

A floating exchange rate is one whose value is determined entirely by the free interaction of demand and supply of the currency in the FOREX market, with no target level set or defended by the central bank or government. The rate is free to rise (appreciate) or fall (depreciate) continuously in response to changing market conditions.

Clean (Free) Float
The central bank never intervenes in the FOREX market — the exchange rate is left entirely to market forces. Genuinely clean floats are rare in practice; most "floating" currencies see occasional, limited intervention.
Dirty (Managed) Float
The exchange rate floats and is primarily market-determined, but the central bank occasionally intervenes — buying or selling its own currency — to smooth excessive volatility or nudge the rate in a preferred direction, without committing to any specific target level.

Under a genuine float, market forces provide an automatic adjustment mechanism for a current account imbalance. If a country runs a persistent current account deficit (importing more than it exports), the excess supply of its currency needed to pay for those imports should, in theory, cause the currency to depreciate. That depreciation makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive, gradually correcting the deficit without any need for government or central bank action.

6

Who Floats in 2026 — and Why Development Matters

Most of the world's major, freely convertible currencies operate under a floating (or managed-floating) regime, including the US dollar, the euro, sterling, the Japanese yen, the Swiss franc, the Australian and Canadian dollars, and the South Korean won. Many large emerging economies have also moved towards greater flexibility over time — for example Mexico, Brazil, and South Africa run largely market-determined floats, while others such as India and Indonesia operate managed floats with periodic central bank intervention.

Why More Developed Economies Tend to Float

This pattern is not a coincidence, and it is best explained using the Impossible Trinity (Trilemma): a country can only choose two of the following three policy goals simultaneously — a fixed exchange rate, free movement of capital, and an independent monetary policy. It cannot have all three at once.

The Impossible Trinity: Fixed Exchange Rate  +  Free Capital Mobility  +  Independent Monetary Policy — pick any two. A country with open capital markets that wants to set its own interest rate to manage its own domestic inflation and growth (rather than importing another country's monetary policy) must, by definition, allow its exchange rate to float.

Developed economies typically have deep, liquid, well-regulated financial markets and open capital accounts — foreign investors can move money in and out freely, which is generally seen as necessary for attracting long-term foreign direct investment and for the efficient allocation of global capital. Given open capital mobility, the trilemma forces a choice between a fixed rate (surrendering independent monetary policy) or a floating rate (retaining it). Advanced economies with strong, independent, credible central banks (like the Bank of England or the US Federal Reserve) generally prize the ability to set interest rates according to their own domestic inflation target and business cycle — particularly given that different economies are rarely at the same point in the cycle at the same time. A floating rate is the price of that independence.

Floating rates also tend to suit economies that are diversified and flexible enough to absorb the volatility that comes with letting the market set the price of the currency continuously — deep hedging (forward and futures) markets exist in developed economies specifically to allow firms to manage this exchange rate risk cheaply, something far less available in developing economies. By contrast, many developing and smaller open economies — particularly those heavily reliant on a narrow range of commodity exports, or those seeking to import monetary credibility from a larger trading partner — have historically found a fixed or managed rate more attractive, even at the cost of monetary independence, because it offers greater certainty for trade and can anchor inflation expectations in an economy without its own history of credible, independent monetary policy.

Floating Rates — Benefits & Drawbacks

✓ Benefits of Floating

Automatic correction of the current account

A depreciation caused by a current account deficit makes exports cheaper and imports dearer, gradually rebalancing trade without any deliberate policy intervention or the need to hold large foreign currency reserves.

Monetary policy independence

The central bank is free to set interest rates purely according to domestic objectives — inflation, growth, and employment — rather than having to set rates purely to defend a target exchange rate. This was central to the Bank of England's ability to respond flexibly to the 2008 crisis and COVID-19.

A shock absorber

If a country suffers an asymmetric shock (e.g. a fall in demand for its main export), a floating currency can depreciate to partly offset the shock, cushioning the effect on output and employment, whereas a country in a fixed system or currency union has no such release valve.

No speculative attack risk on the peg itself

Because there is no target rate to defend, there is nothing for speculators to bet against in the way that a fixed rate can be "attacked" — the currency simply continues to find its own market level.

No need to hold vast reserves

Central banks are not obliged to maintain large stockpiles of foreign currency purely to intervene in the FX market, freeing reserves for other purposes.

✗ Drawbacks of Floating

Volatility & uncertainty

Constantly fluctuating rates make it harder for exporters and importers to plan and price contracts with confidence, and increase the cost of hedging against currency risk — this uncertainty can itself deter international trade and investment.

Unpredictable, uncontrolled inflation pass-through

A sharp, unplanned depreciation (e.g. following a political shock) can suddenly raise import prices and feed straight into inflation, forcing the central bank to react rather than plan.

Speculation and overshooting

Short-term speculative flows can dominate trade-based flows, pushing the exchange rate well beyond the level that underlying economic fundamentals would justify, and creating self-reinforcing bubbles or crashes.

Risk of "currency wars"

If several countries simultaneously try to talk down or manage their currencies lower to gain a competitive advantage, floating systems can enable competitive depreciation cycles that damage global trade stability.

Weaker discipline on domestic policy

Because there is no external exchange-rate target forcing discipline, governments and central banks may be more tempted to run looser fiscal or monetary policy than they otherwise would, since a depreciation is a less immediately visible consequence than defending a peg and running out of reserves.

Fixed Exchange Rate Systems — The Different Types

A fixed exchange rate is one in which the government or central bank sets, announces, and actively maintains the value of its currency at (or very close to) a specific target level against another currency, a basket of currencies, or a commodity — rather than letting the market determine it. Not all fixed systems are equally rigid; they exist on a spectrum from absolutely hard pegs to loosely managed regimes.

TypeHow It WorksFlexibilityExample
Currency Board / Hard PegEvery unit of domestic currency in circulation is backed 100% by foreign currency reserves (usually the anchor currency). The central bank has no discretion to change the rate or expand the money supply independently of reserve inflows.Extremely low — effectively no independent monetary policy at all.Hong Kong dollar pegged to the US dollar; Bulgaria's lev was pegged to the Deutschmark/euro under a currency board from 1997 until euro adoption in 2026.
Conventional (Adjustable) PegThe currency is fixed to a single anchor currency or basket at an announced rate, defended through central bank intervention and interest rate policy, but the rate can in principle be reset (devalued/revalued) by government decision if it becomes unsustainable.Low to moderate — some flexibility exists, but changes are rare, disruptive, and can trigger a loss of credibility.The UK's membership of the Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM), 1990–92; many Gulf states' dollar pegs today (e.g. the Saudi riyal).
Semi-Fixed / Managed (Target Zone or Band)The exchange rate is allowed to float freely within a pre-announced band (e.g. ±2%) around a central rate, with the central bank intervening only if the rate threatens to breach the upper or lower limit.Moderate — combines some market-driven flexibility with an anchor for expectations.The Danish krone's band within ERM II; Singapore's managed trade-weighted band; Argentina's 2025–26 currency band, widened monthly in line with inflation.
Crawling PegThe central rate itself is adjusted by small, pre-announced (or rules-based) amounts at regular intervals — usually to accommodate a persistent inflation differential with the anchor country — rather than being fixed permanently or left fully floating.Moderate — provides a predictable path of gradual depreciation/appreciation rather than a single large, disruptive change.Argentina's crawling peg (2024–25), depreciating the peso by a pre-announced 1–2% per month.

The common thread across all of these is a trade-off between credibility and flexibility. The harder and more rigid the peg (a currency board), the greater the credibility it can offer — because it is costly and difficult to abandon, markets believe it — but the government sacrifices almost all ability to respond to domestic shocks. The softer and more flexible the system (a wide band or crawling peg), the more room there is to adjust to changing conditions, but at the cost of reduced credibility, since markets know the government retains discretion to change the rate.

Diagram: A Fixed Exchange Rate Defended by Intervention

The diagram below shows why a fixed rate cannot simply be "announced" — it must be actively defended whenever it does not match the market-clearing (equilibrium) rate. Here, the government has fixed the rate above the free-market equilibrium (i.e. the currency is pegged at an overvalued level). At that fixed rate, the quantity of currency people want to supply (sell) exceeds the quantity others want to demand (buy) — an excess supply. Left alone, this excess supply would push the rate down towards equilibrium. To prevent that, the central bank must step in and buy up the excess currency itself, using its foreign currency reserves.

Fig 2: At the fixed (overvalued) rate, quantity supplied (Qs) exceeds quantity demanded (Qd) — the green bracket shows this excess supply. The central bank must absorb it by buying its own currency, spending foreign currency reserves in the process.

Diagram: A Managed Float — Trading Within a Band

Under a managed float (target zone), the central bank does not fix a single precise rate at all — it instead allows the currency to trade freely wherever demand and supply take it, but only within a pre-announced band between a ceiling and a floor. The market is left to do the work most of the time; intervention is only needed at the edges.

Fig 3: A managed float / target zone. The rate can move freely with genuine market demand and supply anywhere inside the shaded band; the central bank intervenes only if the rate threatens to break through the ceiling or floor.

How Countries Fix a Rate — Mechanisms & Complexities

Announcing a fixed rate is easy; maintaining it is not. A government or central bank has several tools available, each with real costs and limitations.

1
Direct FOREX intervention

If market forces are pushing the currency below its fixed rate, the central bank buys its own currency using its foreign currency reserves, increasing demand for it and supporting the price. If the currency is under pressure to rise above the fixed rate, the central bank sells its own currency (buying foreign currency), increasing supply and holding the rate down. This is only sustainable for as long as the central bank's reserves last.

2
Interest rate manipulation

Raising domestic interest rates attracts hot money inflows seeking a higher return, increasing demand for the currency and supporting its fixed value — but this may be entirely the wrong interest rate for domestic conditions (e.g. raising rates to defend a peg during a domestic recession, deepening the downturn). This tension is the practical, everyday face of the Impossible Trinity.

3
Capital controls

Restricting the ability of residents and foreigners to freely move money in and out of the country reduces the scale of speculative flows that could otherwise overwhelm a fixed rate, giving the central bank more room to manage the peg — though at the cost of the many benefits associated with free capital movement (foreign investment, financial market depth) and increasing the trilemma's flexibility, since fixing plus capital controls can coexist with more independent monetary policy.

4
Currency board backing

Under the strictest hard-peg systems, the central bank commits to hold foreign reserves equal to 100% (or more) of the domestic monetary base, removing any discretion to expand the money supply beyond what is backed by reserves — making the peg mechanically almost impossible to break, at the cost of monetary policy independence being surrendered entirely.

The Complexities Involved

Defending a fixed exchange rate is expensive and constraining. Holding large foreign currency reserves purely for intervention purposes carries a significant opportunity cost — those funds could otherwise be invested domestically or used productively. Raising interest rates to defend a peg can directly conflict with what domestic conditions (inflation, unemployment, growth) actually require, forcing policymakers into an uncomfortable choice between defending international credibility and supporting the domestic economy. And crucially, a peg is only as credible as the market believes the government is willing and able to defend it: if traders conclude the rate is unsustainable — because reserves are running low, because the fixed rate looks overvalued given the economy's underlying inflation and productivity, or because political will to keep defending it is weakening — the mere expectation of a future devaluation can trigger the speculative attack that makes that devaluation happen, precisely the mechanism seen in Black Wednesday (Section 12).

Fixed Rates — Benefits & Drawbacks

✓ Benefits of Fixing

Certainty for trade and investment

Firms trading internationally know exactly what exchange rate they will receive, removing exchange rate risk from cross-border contracts and reducing the cost of hedging — this can be particularly valuable for small, highly trade-dependent economies.

A discipline / credibility effect

Committing to a fixed rate against a low-inflation anchor currency (e.g. pegging to the euro or dollar) can act as a powerful external discipline on domestic monetary and fiscal policy, since persistently high domestic inflation relative to the anchor country would make the peg unsustainable — this can help a country with a poor inflation track record "import" credibility it could not otherwise establish on its own.

Lower transaction costs

Businesses and consumers avoid the costs of currency conversion and hedging associated with trading in a floating currency, particularly valuable for economies that trade overwhelmingly with one dominant partner.

Reduces (successful) speculation, if the peg is credible

A genuinely credible, well-defended peg removes the incentive for destabilising speculative bets, since traders have no expectation of profiting from a rate change.

✗ Drawbacks of Fixing

Loss of independent monetary policy

Interest rates must be set to defend the exchange rate rather than to manage domestic inflation, growth, or unemployment — a country experiencing a recession cannot simply cut rates if doing so would trigger capital outflows and threaten the peg.

Vulnerability to speculative attack

If markets believe a fixed rate has become misaligned with economic fundamentals, they can sell the currency en masse, forcing the central bank to burn through reserves defending an ultimately unsustainable position — as UK policymakers discovered on Black Wednesday.

The need for large reserves

Sustaining a credible peg requires the central bank to hold substantial foreign currency reserves purely for intervention purposes — a significant opportunity cost, particularly for developing economies.

Importing the "wrong" monetary policy

A country pegged to another economy's currency effectively imports that country's monetary policy, even if the two economies are at very different points in their business cycles — exactly the tension that contributed to the UK's ERM difficulties in the early 1990s.

Painful internal adjustment

Without the exchange rate available to absorb a shock, a country under a fixed system that loses competitiveness must instead achieve the same effect through "internal devaluation" — cutting wages, prices and costs domestically — a slower, more painful, and more politically difficult process than a currency adjusting.

Devaluation & Revaluation

It is essential to use precise terminology here, as AQA examiners specifically credit the correct distinction between market-driven and policy-driven currency changes.

TermSystemCause
DepreciationFloating exchange rateA fall in the value of a currency caused by market forces (demand and supply) — no deliberate government action.
AppreciationFloating exchange rateA rise in the value of a currency caused by market forces.
DevaluationFixed exchange rateA deliberate, announced reduction in the official value of a currency by the government or central bank.
RevaluationFixed exchange rateA deliberate, announced increase in the official value of a currency by the government or central bank.

Governments typically devalue to restore lost export competitiveness or correct a persistent current account deficit that has built up under an overvalued fixed rate — making exports cheaper and imports more expensive at a stroke, rather than waiting for a slow and painful process of internal wage and price deflation.

The Marshall–Lerner Condition

A devaluation (or a market-driven depreciation) does not automatically improve the current account. Whether it does depends entirely on how responsive — how price elastic — demand for exports and imports is. This relationship is formalised as the Marshall–Lerner condition.

PEDx + PEDm > 1

(the sum of the price elasticity of demand for exports and the price elasticity of demand for imports, ignoring negative signs, must exceed 1)

If this condition holds, a devaluation will improve the trade balance in the long run. If combined elasticities are less than 1, a devaluation will actually make the trade balance worse, even though it was intended to help. To understand why, it is essential to separate the price effect from the volume effect — because a devaluation moves both at once, and they pull in opposite directions.

Price EffectVolume EffectNet Effect on Revenue/Spending
Exports
(foreign currency revenue = price × quantity)		Devaluation lowers the foreign-currency price of exports — for any given quantity sold, foreign currency revenue falls.Lower foreign-currency prices should raise the quantity of exports demanded by foreign buyers.If export demand is elastic (PEDx > 1), quantity rises proportionately more than price fell — the volume effect dominates and total foreign currency revenue rises. If demand is inelastic (PEDx < 1), quantity barely responds — the price effect dominates and revenue falls.
Imports
(domestic currency spending = price × quantity)		Devaluation raises the domestic-currency price of imports — for any given quantity bought, domestic spending on imports rises.Higher domestic-currency prices should reduce the quantity of imports demanded by domestic buyers.If import demand is elastic (PEDm > 1), the fall in quantity outweighs the rise in price, and total domestic spending on imports falls (favourable). If demand is inelastic (PEDm < 1), quantity barely falls, so spending on imports rises (unfavourable) despite the higher price supposedly discouraging consumption.

Marshall–Lerner simply combines these two effects: the trade balance improves overall only if the combined responsiveness of export and import demand is strong enough that the favourable volume effects together outweigh the unfavourable price effects. This is precisely why responsiveness (elasticity) is the single most important variable determining whether a devaluation actually works — a government can devalue by any amount it likes, but if buyers on both sides of the transaction are unable or unwilling to change how much they buy, the devaluation will fail to correct the deficit and may even deepen it.

★ Why Elasticity Differs by Time-Scale
Price elasticity of demand for exports and imports is not fixed — it depends heavily on how much time buyers have had to respond. This is precisely the insight behind the J-curve, explained in full below: demand for traded goods tends to be inelastic in the short run and considerably more elastic in the long run, once buyers have had time to genuinely change their behaviour.

The J-Curve — Why Time-Scale Matters

The J-curve describes the characteristic path the trade balance follows after a devaluation or depreciation: it typically worsens before it improves, tracing a shape resembling the letter J when plotted against time. Understanding why this happens — and why it takes the specific length of time it does — requires thinking carefully about the real mechanics of international trade and supply chains, not just abstract elasticities.

Fig 5: The J-curve. The trade balance worsens immediately after devaluation, bottoms out, then crosses back through zero (the point marked) before moving into surplus in the long run, as demand for exports and imports becomes progressively more elastic over time.
1
Immediate effect — the trade balance worsens (demand is inelastic)

The moment a devaluation happens, the volumes of exports and imports are essentially frozen — they cannot respond instantly, because the underlying trade is governed by existing supply agreements. Firms are typically locked into contracts signed months earlier at the old prices and quantities, which cannot be renegotiated overnight. Domestic consumers and firms usually have no readily available domestic substitute for the imports they rely on — switching from an imported component to a domestically-made one is not something that can happen the same week, particularly for specialised inputs. And crucially, businesses lack the information needed to act even if they wanted to: finding a new supplier, verifying their quality and reliability, negotiating a contract, and setting up entirely new logistics and shipping routes all take real time to arrange. With quantities essentially unchanged, the only thing that has moved is price: imports (unchanged in volume) now cost more in domestic currency, while the foreign-currency value of a given volume of exports has fallen. The trade balance therefore worsens immediately, exactly as inelastic short-run demand would predict.

2
Delayed effect — imports start to fall, exports start to rise

As months pass, buyers on both sides begin to genuinely respond. Domestic consumers and firms start shifting purchases away from now-expensive imports towards cheaper domestic alternatives as they become aware of them; foreign buyers, having noticed the country's exports are now cheaper, begin placing new orders. Quantities finally start to adjust because the search and information costs from Phase 1 are being worked through — buyers have had time to identify alternative suppliers and firms have had time to begin adjusting production and marketing to capture the new demand.

3
Long-run effect — the trade balance improves, and may move into surplus

Given enough time, several deeper, structural changes complete, and demand becomes genuinely elastic. Existing supply contracts signed at the old exchange rate expire and are renegotiated at prices and quantities that reflect the new competitive reality. The improved competitiveness of domestic firms attracts new investment in productive capacity — both to expand export industries and to build up domestic substitutes for what used to be imported (import substitution), permanently shifting the pattern of trade. And critically, the information and logistics problem has been solved: firms have identified new trading partners, built the relationships and due-diligence processes needed to trust them, and physically established new supply chains — shipping routes, warehousing, distribution networks — connecting buyers to these new, more competitive suppliers. Once this infrastructure exists, the responsiveness of trade volumes to price is far higher than it ever could be in the short run, allowing the full Marshall–Lerner adjustment to finally play out and the trade balance to improve, potentially moving into surplus.

★ The Complexity of International Supply Chains
Students often underestimate just how much practical, physical infrastructure sits behind a single line in a trade statistic. A UK car manufacturer switching a component supplier after a devaluation doesn't simply "buy British" the next day — it must identify a credible domestic (or newly competitive foreign) supplier capable of the required quality and volume, conduct testing and quality assurance on their output, renegotiate or terminate existing multi-year supply contracts (often with penalty clauses for early exit), arrange entirely new shipping, customs, and warehousing logistics, and in many cases wait for that supplier to expand their own production capacity to meet the new order. Each of these steps takes time, explaining why the elasticities relevant to the Marshall–Lerner condition are so much higher in the long run than the short run — and why the J-curve's dip can last well over a year before the intended benefits of a devaluation actually materialise.

Devaluation carries real risks even where Marshall–Lerner eventually holds. It can be inflationary, since imports immediately become more expensive in domestic currency terms. It risks provoking retaliatory devaluations from trading partners seeking to protect their own competitiveness — a "competitive devaluation" spiral that leaves everyone's relative position unchanged while raising global uncertainty. And a devaluation, particularly an unplanned or crisis-driven one, can severely damage a government's credibility and the market's confidence in its commitment to future exchange rate stability.

12

When Fixed Rates Break — Shocks & Crises

History provides several vivid illustrations of what happens when a fixed exchange rate becomes misaligned with underlying economic fundamentals, and the market loses confidence that it can be defended.

★ REAL WORLD: Black Wednesday — 16 September 1992
The UK joined the European Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM) in October 1990, fixing sterling within a band against the Deutschmark at a rate widely judged to be overvalued. Following German reunification, the Bundesbank kept German interest rates high to control inflation in the newly unified Germany — a policy entirely wrong for the UK's own weak, recessionary economy, but one the UK was forced to follow to defend the peg. As the UK economy weakened and speculators (most famously George Soros) judged sterling could not remain in the ERM at its fixed rate, they sold sterling heavily. On 16 September 1992, the Bank of England raised interest rates from 10% to 15% in a single day and spent billions of pounds of reserves attempting to buy sterling and defend the band — but was ultimately forced to withdraw the pound from the ERM that evening, allowing it to float and depreciate sharply. The episode cost the Treasury an estimated £3.3 billion and became the defining case study of a fixed exchange rate collapsing under speculative pressure once markets judged the peg unsustainable.
★ REAL WORLD: The Asian Financial Crisis — 1997
Thailand, along with several other South-East Asian economies, had pegged its currency (the baht) to the US dollar for years, attracting large inflows of foreign capital. When the US dollar strengthened and Thailand's current account deficit widened, speculators judged the baht was overvalued and attacked the peg. Thailand's central bank exhausted its foreign reserves defending the rate before being forced to float the baht in July 1997, which then collapsed in value. Contagion spread rapidly to Indonesia, South Korea, and Malaysia as investors reassessed similar pegged currencies across the region, triggering a wider regional financial and economic crisis.
★ REAL WORLD: Argentina's Currency Board Collapse — 2001–02
From 1991, Argentina operated an extremely rigid currency board, fixing the peso at an exact 1:1 rate with the US dollar, fully backed by dollar reserves. This initially succeeded in ending hyperinflation, but as the US dollar strengthened through the 1990s, the peso became increasingly overvalued, damaging Argentine export competitiveness and deepening a prolonged recession. Investors lost confidence that the government could maintain the peg alongside a deteriorating fiscal position, triggering capital flight. The government abandoned the currency board in early 2002, and the peso collapsed from parity with the dollar to roughly four pesos per dollar within months, alongside a sovereign debt default — one of the most severe currency and economic crises in modern history.
★ REAL WORLD: The Swiss Franc Shock — January 2015
The Swiss National Bank (SNB) had maintained a floor (a form of managed/semi-fixed rate) preventing the franc from strengthening beyond CHF 1.20 per euro since 2011, buying euros to hold the franc down and protect Swiss export competitiveness during the Eurozone debt crisis. On 15 January 2015, facing the prospect of the European Central Bank launching large-scale quantitative easing (which would weaken the euro further and require ever-larger SNB intervention to hold the floor), the SNB abruptly abandoned the policy without warning. The franc surged nearly 30% against the euro within minutes, causing chaos in currency markets and severe losses for firms and traders caught on the wrong side of the move — a stark illustration that even a well-resourced central bank can conclude that defending a managed rate has simply become too costly to continue.
★ REAL WORLD: Argentina's Crawling Peg and Currency Band — 2024–2026
Facing 211% annual inflation on taking office in December 2023, President Javier Milei's government implemented an immediate 54% devaluation of the peso and introduced a crawling peg, depreciating the currency by a small, pre-announced percentage each month to provide a predictable nominal anchor while inflation was brought under control. This evolved into a wider currency band from April 2025 (with limits adjusted monthly), and from January 2026 the band itself began widening in line with actual inflation data. This gradual, managed approach — a deliberate middle path between a rigid peg and a full float — illustrates how modern policymakers use semi-fixed systems to balance the credibility benefits of a fixed anchor against the flexibility needed to avoid another Argentina-style collapse; by mid-2026, annual inflation had fallen from 211% to roughly 33%, though the government remained cautious about moving to a full free float given Argentina's long history of currency crises.

Currency Union — The Ultimate Fixed Exchange Rate

A currency (monetary) union takes the concept of a fixed exchange rate to its logical extreme: rather than merely pegging separate national currencies to one another, member countries abolish their own currencies entirely and adopt a single shared currency, with monetary policy set by a single supranational central bank for the whole union. There is no exchange rate at all between members — the "exchange rate" is permanently and irrevocably fixed at 1:1, because it is literally the same money.

The Creation of the Eurozone

The legal foundation for the euro was laid by the 1992 Maastricht Treaty, which established a three-stage plan for Economic and Monetary Union (EMU) within the European Union, culminating in a single currency and a single central bank — the European Central Bank (ECB). The project drew on economist Robert Mundell's theory of an Optimal Currency Area (OCA): the idea that a group of regions or countries benefits most from sharing a single currency when they have highly integrated economies, synchronised business cycles, free movement of labour and capital between them, and mechanisms (such as fiscal transfers) to cushion any region hit by an asymmetric shock. Beyond the economic rationale, the euro was also explicitly a political project — a deliberate step towards deeper European integration following decades of currency instability and the collapse of previous fixed-rate arrangements (such as the ERM crisis of 1992 discussed above) among EU members.

The euro was introduced as an electronic accounting currency for financial markets and cross-border payments in 1999, with physical notes and coins entering circulation on 1 January 2002 in the eleven founding member states.

Eurozone Membership in 2026: Following Bulgaria's accession on 1 January 2026, the euro area comprises 21 member states: Austria, Belgium, Bulgaria, Croatia, Cyprus, Estonia, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Ireland, Italy, Latvia, Lithuania, Luxembourg, Malta, the Netherlands, Portugal, Slovakia, Slovenia, and Spain. Several EU members remain outside the eurozone, including Poland, Czechia, Hungary, Romania, and Sweden (which have not yet met the entry criteria or joined the Exchange Rate Mechanism II), alongside Denmark, which negotiated a formal opt-out but keeps the krone tightly pegged to the euro within ERM II in practice.

Benefits & Drawbacks of a Currency Union

✓ Benefits

Elimination of transaction costs

Businesses and consumers trading within the union no longer pay currency conversion fees or bear the administrative cost of dealing in multiple currencies — a saving that compounds across millions of daily transactions.

Total elimination of exchange rate risk within the union

Since there is no exchange rate between members at all, firms trading and investing across the eurozone face zero currency risk on those transactions, encouraging deeper cross-border trade and investment than even the most credible fixed peg could offer, since a peg can always in principle be broken, whereas leaving the euro is far more difficult and costly.

Price transparency and competition

Consumers and firms can directly compare prices for identical goods across member states without needing to perform any currency conversion, intensifying competition and helping to keep prices in check.

Imported monetary credibility

Members with a weaker history of monetary discipline benefit from the ECB's independence and inflation-targeting credibility, exactly as Bulgaria's long-standing currency board relationship with the Deutschmark/euro demonstrated even before formal euro adoption.

Attracting foreign investment

Investors gain access to a large, currency-risk-free single market, making the union collectively more attractive as an investment destination than a collection of smaller economies with volatile individual currencies.

✗ Drawbacks

Total loss of independent monetary policy

Unlike even a currency board (which can, in extremis, be abandoned), leaving a currency union is extraordinarily difficult and costly. Interest rates are set once by the ECB for the entire eurozone — a genuine "one size fits all" policy that can be badly wrong for individual members whose economies are at a different point in the business cycle.

No exchange rate to absorb asymmetric shocks

If one member suffers a shock that does not affect others equally (e.g. a collapse in a specific export industry), it cannot devalue its own currency to restore competitiveness, since there is no separate currency left to devalue. Adjustment must instead come through the much slower and more painful route of falling wages and prices (internal devaluation), or through higher unemployment and emigration.

Requires substitute adjustment mechanisms the eurozone largely lacks

Mundell's Optimal Currency Area theory suggests currency unions work best with high labour mobility between members and a shared fiscal system able to transfer resources to regions in difficulty — much as happens automatically between US states. The eurozone has far less labour mobility (due to language and cultural barriers) and no equivalent fiscal union, leaving it poorly equipped to cushion asymmetric shocks compared to a true optimal currency area.

The Eurozone debt crisis as a case study

Between 2010 and 2012, Greece, Ireland, Portugal, Spain, and Italy faced severe sovereign debt and banking crises. Unable to devalue their way back to competitiveness or have their own central bank act as lender of last resort in their own currency, these countries were forced into severe fiscal austerity and painful internal devaluation to restore competitiveness — a process that would have been far faster, though still costly, with an independent, floating currency available to absorb some of the adjustment.

Loss of seigniorage and national symbolism

Members give up the ability to issue their own currency and the (typically modest) revenue this can generate, alongside the political and symbolic significance of a national currency — a factor that has fed public reluctance in some prospective and current member states, as seen in divided public opinion in Bulgaria ahead of its 2026 accession.

Exam Technique & Model Chains of Reasoning

The Golden Chain: A Floating Depreciation Correcting a Current Account Deficit

Persistent current account deficit → more domestic currency supplied (to buy imports) than demanded (to buy exports) → excess supply of the currency in the FOREX market → currency depreciates → exports become cheaper in foreign currency terms, imports become more expensive in domestic currency terms → assuming Marshall–Lerner holds, export volumes rise and import volumes fall (after a J-curve lag) → net exports (X − M) improve → current account deficit narrows.

The Golden Chain: Defending a Fixed Exchange Rate Under Speculative Pressure

Markets judge the fixed rate to be overvalued/unsustainable → speculators sell the currency heavily → central bank must buy its own currency using FX reserves to support the price → reserves fall rapidly → central bank may also raise interest rates sharply to attract capital inflows and support the currency → if reserves are exhausted or the interest rate rise is judged politically/economically unsustainable, the government is forced to devalue or abandon the peg entirely → currency depreciates sharply once floated.

The Golden Chain: Devaluation Restoring Competitiveness

Government judges the fixed rate has become overvalued, damaging export competitiveness and widening the current account deficit → government announces a devaluation, lowering the official fixed rate → exports become cheaper in foreign currency terms, imports become more expensive in domestic currency terms → in the short run (J-curve), the trade balance may briefly worsen as volumes have not yet adjusted → over 12–18 months, assuming Marshall–Lerner holds, export volumes rise and import volumes fall → net exports improve → aggregate demand and the current account both improve, though import prices rising may add to domestic inflation.

The Golden Chain: Why Currency Union Removes the Exchange Rate as a Shock Absorber

A member economy suffers an asymmetric shock (e.g. collapse in a key export industry) → with a national currency, the exchange rate could depreciate to restore competitiveness and cushion the effect on output and employment → inside a currency union, no national currency or exchange rate exists to adjust → the economy must instead undergo "internal devaluation" — falling wages, prices and costs — to restore competitiveness → this process is typically slower, more painful, and more politically contentious than a currency depreciating, and tends to show up instead in higher unemployment and, absent fiscal transfers or labour mobility, prolonged regional recession.

Key Exam Tips

1

Always state whether you are discussing a floating or fixed system before using terminology — depreciation/appreciation belong to floating systems; devaluation/revaluation belong to fixed systems. Mixing these up costs marks.

2

Use the Impossible Trinity explicitly in 25-mark evaluation answers on exchange rate regime choice — it is the single most powerful organising concept for explaining why countries choose fixed vs floating systems.

3

Always mention the Marshall–Lerner condition and the J-curve when discussing the effect of a depreciation or devaluation on the current account — a strong answer never assumes the effect is immediate or guaranteed.

4

Real-world context earns marks: Black Wednesday (1992), the Asian Financial Crisis (1997), Argentina's currency board collapse (2001–02), the Swiss franc shock (2015), and the Eurozone's 21st member Bulgaria (2026) are all high-value examples to deploy precisely.

5

For currency union questions, explicitly reference Optimal Currency Area theory (labour mobility, fiscal transfers, synchronised business cycles) to explain why the eurozone is an imperfect OCA — this is exactly the kind of theoretical anchoring that separates top-band evaluation from descriptive answers.

6

Evaluate, don't just describe: for any 25-mark question, weigh the benefits of your chosen regime against its costs, and consider under what circumstances a different regime might be more appropriate (e.g. a small, trade-dependent economy vs a large, diversified one).
