The Multiplier & Accelerator Effects

Aggregate Demand · Theory · Calculator Practice · Ripple Simulator

🔍 The Big Picture

The multiplier and accelerator are the two great amplification mechanisms in macroeconomics. They explain why the economy does not simply respond to a change in spending in a neat, proportionate way — instead, small shocks are magnified into large swings in output and investment.

This feedback loop is what drives boom-bust cycles and makes macroeconomic stabilisation genuinely difficult.

The Multiplier Effect

An initial injection of spending into the circular flow generates a greater final increase in national income than the original injection. This is because each round of spending becomes income for someone else.

K = 1 ÷ MPW
where MPW = marginal propensity to withdraw (MPS + MPT + MPM)
K = 1 ÷ (1 − MPC)
in a simple closed economy with no government

Every time income leaks out through saving, tax or imports, it does not pass on to the next round. A high MPW means a small multiplier.

ΔY = K × ΔI
Change in national income = multiplier × initial injection

📊 Worked Example

If the government increases spending by £20bn and the MPC = 0.75 (so MPW = 0.25):

K = 1 ÷ 0.25 = 4

ΔY = 4 × £20bn = £80bn increase in national income

What Affects the Size?

  • 📉
    Openness of the economy
    High import propensity (MPM) = large leakage = smaller multiplier. UK's high import share limits its multiplier.
  • 🏭
    Spare capacity
    If the economy is near full employment, the multiplier works through prices (inflation) not output. Real effect is smaller.
  • 💰
    Marginal propensity to consume
    Higher MPC → less saved each round → more passes on → bigger multiplier. Poorer households typically have higher MPCs.
  • 🏦
    Tax rates
    Higher income tax rates increase MPT and reduce the multiplier. This is partly why fiscal multipliers are debated in high-tax economies.
  • ⏱
    Time lags
    The full multiplier effect takes time — successive spending rounds can stretch over months or even years.
  • 😰
    Consumer & business confidence
    If households are pessimistic, they may save extra income rather than spend it, reducing MPC and cutting the multiplier.

The Accelerator Effect

The accelerator shows that net investment depends on the change in output, not the level of output. When demand is growing, firms must invest to expand capacity. When growth slows — even if the economy is still expanding — net investment can fall.

I = v × ΔY
where v = capital-output ratio (accelerator coefficient)

The Capital-Output Ratio (v)

This measures how much capital (£) is needed to produce £1 of output. If v = 3, then each £1 rise in output requires £3 of new capital investment. Capital-intensive industries (e.g. energy, manufacturing) have high values of v.

🔑 The Key Insight

Even a slowdown in growth (not a recession) causes net investment to fall — because firms no longer need to expand capacity as rapidly. This is what makes the accelerator so powerful in amplifying downturns.

🔄 The Multiplier-Accelerator Interaction

The two effects do not operate in isolation — they form a self-reinforcing feedback loop that drives business cycles. This interaction was formalised by Samuelson (1939) and remains central to understanding macroeconomic volatility.

① TRIGGER

An initial injection occurs — e.g. government increases spending by £10bn on infrastructure.

↓
② MULTIPLIER

That £10bn circulates through the economy. Each round of spending generates income for someone else. With K = 4, national income eventually rises by £40bn.

↓
③ ACCELERATOR

Firms see rising consumer demand and need to expand productive capacity. Net investment rises sharply — amplifying the original injection further. This is a new injection into the circular flow.

↓
④ SECOND MULTIPLIER

The investment spending from step ③ is itself subject to the multiplier — it circulates, generates incomes, and further boosts output.

↓
⑤ BOOM

The combined effect far exceeds the original injection. The cycle can generate an inflationary boom — or, in reverse, a severe recession from a small negative shock.

⚖️ Evaluation

⚠ Limitations of the Multiplier

Spare capacity required — if the economy is near full employment, the multiplier effect on real output is minimal; it just causes inflation.
Crowding out — government borrowing may raise interest rates, reducing private investment and partially offsetting the fiscal stimulus.
Time lags — successive rounds of spending take time; by the time the full multiplier works through, conditions may have changed.
Open economy leakages — the UK's high import propensity means a significant share of each round leaks abroad, substantially reducing the multiplier value.

⚠ Limitations of the Accelerator

Assumes instant adjustment — in practice, firms do not immediately invest when output rises; capacity constraints, planning lags, and credit availability all create delays.
Existing spare capacity — if firms already have under-utilised capital, they may not invest even when output is growing.
Pessimistic expectations — if firms expect growth to be temporary, they may not invest; animal spirits (Keynes) matter as much as the model.
Fixed capital-output ratio — in reality, v varies across industries and over time; the model oversimplifies investment decisions.

✔ When Effects are Strongest

Multiplier strongest when: economy has significant spare capacity; MPC is high; economy is relatively closed; tax rates are low.
Accelerator strongest when: firms are already near full capacity utilisation; credit is cheap and accessible; confidence is high.
Interaction most powerful during: early recovery from recession when capacity is spare and consumer confidence is recovering.

✔ Real-World Context

Post-2008: fiscal multipliers proved relatively small in the UK due to austerity, high import leakages, and the liquidity trap reducing the transmission of spending.
COVID-19 stimulus: large government injections had mixed multiplier effects — high household saving rates initially limited the pass-through of income to spending.
Infrastructure investment: capital projects have higher multipliers in underdeveloped regions where local supply capacity exists — a key policy debate.

📊 Aggregate Demand

Aggregate Demand (AD) is the total spending on goods and services produced in an economy over a given time period. It is made up of four components: consumer spending, investment by firms, government spending, and net exports (exports minus imports).

Understanding AD is the foundation for everything else in this resource — the multiplier and accelerator are the mechanisms that explain why changes in AD have much larger amplified effects on national income and investment than the original change might suggest.

📘 GCSE & A-Level

The Law of Aggregate Demand

The AD curve shows the total spending in an economy at different price levels. The key rule: as the average price level in the economy falls, the total quantity of real output demanded rises. This gives the AD curve its characteristic downward slope from left to right.

Think of it this way: if everything in the economy suddenly got cheaper, people could afford more with the same income — so they'd buy more. Three separate effects explain the mechanics behind this:

① Real Wealth Effect

When prices fall, your savings and financial assets buy more in real terms — you feel wealthier even if the number in your bank account hasn't changed. This encourages households to spend more (C ↑) → AD rises.

② Interest Rate Effect

Lower price levels reduce the amount of money people need to borrow for everyday transactions. This pushes interest rates down, making borrowing cheaper → investment by firms (I ↑) and consumer credit spending both rise → AD rises.

③ International Competitiveness

If UK prices fall relative to other countries, UK goods become cheaper for overseas buyers → exports rise (X ↑). At the same time, UK consumers switch from expensive imports to cheaper domestic goods (M ↓). Net exports rise → AD rises.

⚠ Important distinction

Movement along the AD curve = caused by a change in the price level (e.g. inflation falls → move down along AD).    Shift of the AD curve = caused by a change in one of the components — C, I, G or (X−M) — at every price level.

Movement along AD = change in price level. Shift of AD = change in a non-price determinant.

AD = C + I + G + (X − M)

Aggregate demand has four components. Each one can change independently, shifting the whole AD curve left or right. The table below is your starting point — it explains what each component means and what causes it to change.

C = Consumer spending I = Investment by firms G = Government spending X−M = Exports minus Imports
C

Consumer Expenditure

Household spending on goods and services — typically 60–65% of UK AD. The biggest component by far.

Real disposable income
The income households have left after paying taxes. Higher take-home pay → more spending. If taxes rise, disposable income falls → C falls → AD shifts left.
Consumer confidence
If people feel secure about their jobs and future incomes, they spend more today. If they're worried, they save instead — even if their income hasn't changed.
Interest rates
Lower interest rates make borrowing cheaper (e.g. mortgages, credit cards) and reduce the reward for saving → households spend more. Key lever for the Bank of England.
Wealth effects
If house prices or share values rise, homeowners feel richer and spend more — even without extra income. Particularly powerful in the UK given high rates of homeownership.
Availability of credit
When banks lend freely, consumers can borrow to spend beyond their current income. When credit tightens (as in 2008), spending can fall sharply even if incomes hold steady.
Distribution of income
Poorer households tend to spend a higher proportion of any extra income (high MPC) than wealthier ones. Redistribution can raise total consumer spending even without changing total income. (A-Level: link to MPC)
I

Investment

Spending by firms on capital goods — machinery, buildings, technology. The most volatile component and the key link to the accelerator effect.

Interest rates
Firms often borrow to invest. Higher interest rates raise the cost of borrowing, making fewer investment projects worthwhile → I falls → AD shifts left.
Business confidence & expectations
Firms only invest if they expect future profits to outweigh costs. When confidence is low — e.g. during political uncertainty — investment dries up even if rates are low. Keynes called this "animal spirits".
🔗 Accelerator effect
When consumer demand is rising, firms near full capacity must invest to expand. The bigger the rise in national income (ΔY), the bigger the investment response. This is the accelerator: I = v × ΔY
Technological change
New technologies create investment opportunities — e.g. AI, renewable energy. They can also make existing capital obsolete, forcing firms to invest in replacements.
Corporation tax
Lower tax on profits increases the return on investment projects → I rises. Used as a supply-side policy lever by governments.
Availability of finance
Beyond interest rates, firms need access to bank loans, equity investment, or retained profits. If banks are risk-averse, investment can fall even when rates are low. (A-Level: credit channel)
G

Government Expenditure

Spending by central and local government on public services, infrastructure, welfare etc. A direct injection into the circular flow — the multiplier acts here first.

Fiscal policy decisions
The government can choose to increase or cut spending through the annual Budget. An increase in G directly shifts AD right — this is expansionary fiscal policy.
Automatic stabilisers
In a recession, unemployment benefit payments automatically rise and tax receipts fall — so G rises without any deliberate policy decision. This cushions the fall in AD. In a boom, the reverse happens.
🔗 Multiplier effect
G is the most direct injection into the circular flow. The multiplier then amplifies its impact: ΔY = K × ΔG. A £10bn rise in G becomes a much larger final rise in national income.
Government debt levels
If public debt is already high (as in post-pandemic UK), markets may demand higher interest rates on government borrowing, constraining how much G can rise.
Crowding out
If G is financed by borrowing, it may push up interest rates → private investment (I) falls. The net effect on AD depends on whether the multiplier effect outweighs the crowding-out effect. (A-Level evaluation)
Political cycle
Governments may increase spending ahead of elections. The independent Office for Budget Responsibility (OBR) exists partly to limit this kind of short-term fiscal manipulation.
X−M

Net Exports (X − M)

Exports minus imports. If the UK exports more than it imports, this adds to AD. Imports matter twice here: they reduce AD and they are the main leakage that limits the multiplier.

Exchange rate
If the pound falls in value (depreciates), UK exports become cheaper for foreign buyers (X ↑) and imports become more expensive for UK consumers (M ↓). Net exports rise → AD shifts right.
Overseas income & growth
If the UK's main trading partners (US, EU) are growing fast, demand for UK exports rises. UK AD is therefore directly affected by global economic conditions.
Relative inflation
If UK prices rise faster than in competitor countries, UK goods become less competitive internationally → exports fall, imports rise → AD shifts left.
🔗 Import leakage & the multiplier
Every time income rises in the UK, some of it is spent on imports — this leaks out of the circular flow. A high propensity to import (MPM) directly reduces the size of the multiplier. This is a key reason UK fiscal multipliers are relatively small. (A-Level: MPW = MPS + MPT + MPM)
Trade policy
Tariffs and trade agreements affect the relative price of imports. Post-Brexit non-tariff barriers have likely reduced UK trade volumes with the EU, affecting both X and M.
Non-price competitiveness
Quality, reliability, delivery times, and after-sales service all affect export demand — not just price. Important for UK manufacturing and services exports.

🔗 The Bridge: How AD Shifts Trigger the Multiplier & Accelerator

A shift in AD is not the end of the story — it is the beginning. The multiplier and accelerator determine how large the final macroeconomic impact will be.

📈

1. AD Shifts Right

A determinant changes — e.g. government cuts interest rates, raising C and I. Or government increases G. AD curve shifts right.

✕

2. Multiplier Amplifies

The initial injection circulates through the economy. Each round of spending becomes income — the multiplier (K = 1/MPW) magnifies the original shift.

🚀

3. Accelerator Follows

Rising income increases consumer demand → firms near capacity invest to expand → the accelerator triggers further investment (I = v × ΔY) → another injection.

⚠ Why This Matters for Policy

Fiscal policy effectiveness depends on the size of K. A government spending cut (ΔG negative) reduces AD, but the final fall in Y is K × ΔG — potentially much larger. This is why austerity can be self-defeating: the multiplied income loss reduces tax revenues, partially offsetting the deficit reduction.
Investment volatility is explained by the accelerator. Even a small slowdown in consumer spending growth (a shift in the rate of AD change) can cause a sharp fall in investment — amplifying any initial demand shock and making recessions deeper than the original trigger would suggest.
