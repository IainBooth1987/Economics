Northbridge Mortgages
The Agency Game

A-Level Economics / Business · Principal-agent problems, asymmetric information & moral hazard

Welcome to Northbridge. You run a regional mortgage branch. Across this lesson you'll make four connected decisions — and by the end, the consequences of your earlier choices come back to find you. Keep every page in front of you; you'll need to refer back to your own answers.

Throughout, watch for two key terms: moral hazard (a hidden action problem — behaviour changes because it can't be observed) and adverse selection (a hidden information problem — one side of a deal knows more before it's struck). Mixing these up costs marks at A-Level.

1ROUND 1

The Sales Targets

Max 15 points

Jargon check

A principal-agent problem arises when a principal (Head Office) hires an agent (a branch manager) to act for them, but the agent's incentives don't fully match the principal's. When the principal can't observe the agent's true actions, the agent may take a hidden action — this is moral hazard.

"Group lending is up only 4% and the board wants 20% growth by Christmas. I can't watch every front desk — I only see what you sell, not who you sold it to."
Task

Draw a Borrower Risk Card (Safe / Risky / High-Risk) and keep it secret. Decide: stick to full affordability checks, or relax them to hit target? Then as a class, agree ONE pay structure for every branch, for the rest of the game.

1Branch choice

  • Stick to full affordability checks — slower, safer
  • Relax checks to hit target — faster, riskier, hidden

2Head Office pay structure (class vote)

  • Flat salary
  • Commission per mortgage sold
  • Commission + compulsory risk-check sign-off

2ROUND 2

Package It Up

Max 15 points

Jargon check

Securitisation bundles loans into a tradeable bond sold to investors — moving risk off the bank's books. The investor knows even less than the original branch did: asymmetric information gets worse down a chain. A conflict of interest exists if the credit rating agency is paid by the seller, not the buyer.

"Good news from Round 1 — bundle those mortgages into a bond and sell it to investors. That frees up cash to lend all over again."
Task

Bundle your Round 1 loans into a bond. Choose: pay for an independent rating (small cost, honest label) or use the free in-house rating (no cost, overstated). Then set your selling price.

3ROUND 3

The Safety Net

Max 15 points

Jargon check

"Too big to fail" means a firm so large the government is expected to bail it out. If a bank believes it'll be rescued, it has an incentive to take on more risk — keeping the upside while sharing the downside with taxpayers. This is moral hazard at a systemic scale, happening after the original lending decisions.

"Word's spreading the government won't let major banks collapse — there's an implicit guarantee for firms our size. Do we keep lending aggressively, or pull back now?"
Task

Choose: pull back (de-risk now) or lend aggressively (bet a bailout covers the worst losses). Justify in one sentence using "moral hazard" correctly — and say whether this is the same moral hazard as Round 1 or a different kind.

1Branch choice

  • Pull back — slower growth, protects the branch regardless
  • Lend aggressively — chase growth, bet on the bailout

4ROUND 4

The Board Meeting

Max 20 points

Jargon check

Real reforms after financial crises usually combine an incentive fix (changing what people are paid to do), an information fix (independent screening or disclosure), and regulation (capital rules, affordability checks, ending "too big to fail"). No single tool fixes both hidden action and hidden information.

"We've just lived through shirking, a collapsed market for our own bonds, and a bailout bet that backfired. 90 seconds each — pitch ONE policy that would have prevented YOUR branch's worst round."
Task

Pitch one policy fix, explicitly referencing your own Round 1–3 choices. Use the correct vocabulary: principal-agent, moral hazard, adverse selection, signalling/screening, regulation.

★Policy menu — choose one

  • Profit-share / deferred bonus / clawback — fixes hidden action (Round 1)
  • Independent, investor-paid credit rating — fixes hidden information (Round 2)
  • Capital requirements — limits how much risk a bank can take on
  • End implicit bailout guarantees / ring-fencing — fixes Round 3's incentive to gamble
  • Mandatory affordability checks — fixes hidden information at point of sale
