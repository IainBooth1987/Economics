Key Concept

Trade Creation vs Trade Diversion

These two concepts run through every stage of integration. Trade creation occurs when joining a bloc causes a country to switch from a high-cost domestic producer to a lower-cost member state — increasing economic efficiency. Trade diversion occurs when the common external tariff causes a member to switch from an efficient non-member supplier to a less efficient member — reducing global welfare. The higher a bloc's CET, the greater the risk of trade diversion.

Preferential Trade Area

The lightest form of integration — member countries grant each other reduced (but not zero) tariffs on selected goods, while maintaining their own independent trade policies with the rest of the world.

Real World Examples 🇺🇸 GSP (US Generalised System of Preferences) 🇪🇺 EU–ACP Cotonou Agreement 🌍 GSTP (Global System of Trade Preferences) 🇬🇧 UK DCTS (Developing Countries Trading Scheme)
✦ Benefits
  • Stepping stone to deeper integration: PTAs allow developing nations to begin liberalising trade without full reciprocal obligations. Bangladesh has benefited enormously from the EU's Everything But Arms (EBA) initiative — a form of PTA — which granted duty-free access for garments, helping the sector grow to account for over 80% of export earnings and employ roughly 4 million workers, the majority of whom are women.
  • Preserves policy flexibility: because there is no CET, each member retains full autonomy over its tariff schedule with non-members. This matters for developing economies that may need to protect infant industries — something a customs union would prevent.
  • Lower political cost of negotiation: PTAs are typically sector-specific and easier to conclude than comprehensive FTAs. The UK's DCTS (2023) was implemented unilaterally — no reciprocal negotiation required — meaning it could be introduced without domestic political resistance from affected industries.
  • Trade creation without full structural exposure: even partial tariff reductions shift purchasing toward more efficient member-state producers, raising allocative efficiency, while limiting the speed of adjustment for vulnerable domestic sectors that are not yet competitive.
✦ Drawbacks
  • Partial liberalisation distorts rather than resolves: reduced (not zero) tariffs still create a two-tier trading system. Producers just outside the margin of preference face a competitive disadvantage that reflects political decisions rather than genuine comparative advantage — distorting global resource allocation.
  • Preference erosion over time: as WTO most-favoured-nation (MFN) tariffs fall through multilateral rounds, the margin of preference narrows. Mauritius built export industries around EU sugar preferences that became commercially unviable once MFN rates fell — causing significant structural unemployment in cane farming communities.
  • Excludes the most sensitive sectors: PTAs typically carve out agriculture and textiles — precisely the sectors where developing countries hold comparative advantage. The EU's Cotonou Agreement offered generous industrial goods access while maintaining high agricultural tariffs that blocked African food exports from meaningfully benefiting.
  • Dependency and competitiveness risk: reliance on preferential access can reduce the incentive to build genuine productivity gains. When preferences are eventually withdrawn or eroded, domestic industries can be left structurally uncompetitive — a form of aid dependency dressed up as trade policy.
Examiner Focus

PTA vs FTA — Know the Difference

A common misconception is treating PTAs and FTAs as synonymous. A PTA only reduces tariffs on selected goods — a FTA eliminates them across (most) goods. Both retain independent trade policies with non-members. In exams, if asked about "the least integrated form of trade agreement," the answer is the PTA, not the FTA.

Free Trade Area

Member countries eliminate tariffs and quotas on goods traded between themselves, but each retains its own independent trade policy with non-members. No common external tariff exists.

Real World Examples 🌎 NAFTA / USMCA (US, Canada, Mexico) 🇬🇧 CPTPP (UK & 11 Pacific nations) 🌍 AfCFTA (African Continental FTA) 🇬🇧🇪🇺 UK–EU Trade & Cooperation Agreement 🇦🇺 ASEAN–Australia–NZ FTA
✦ Benefits
  • Trade creation and consumer gains: the removal of tariffs allows firms and consumers to access cheaper imports from member states. Under NAFTA, US consumers benefited from lower-priced Mexican manufactured goods, while Mexican exporters gained access to the world's largest consumer market — bilateral US-Mexico trade grew from roughly $80bn in 1993 to over $600bn by the early 2020s.
  • Retention of trade policy sovereignty: each member sets its own tariffs with non-members — a significant advantage over a customs union. The UK joined CPTPP in 2023 partly because its FTA with the EU (the Trade and Cooperation Agreement) allowed it to negotiate independently with the Indo-Pacific, something EU membership prevented.
  • Economies of scale and productivity: firms gain access to a larger market, enabling longer production runs and lower average costs. For small economies, this is particularly significant — New Zealand's dairy and agricultural sectors benefited from scale gains after the ANZCERTA FTA with Australia deepened from the 1980s onwards.
  • FDI attraction: non-member firms may establish operations inside the bloc to access the tariff-free area. Japanese car manufacturers investing in the UK during the 1980s-90s partly sought to access the EU single market — a similar FDI logic applies to FTAs, though the effect is less powerful without a CET deterring imports.
✦ Drawbacks
  • Rules of origin — administrative burden: without a CET, goods could enter via the lowest-tariff member and be re-exported freely. To prevent this, FTAs require proof that goods genuinely originated in a member state. Under the UK-EU TCA, UK exporters must demonstrate sufficient "rules of origin" compliance — particularly complex for automotive supply chains involving components from multiple countries, adding significant costs for firms like Jaguar Land Rover.
  • Structural unemployment in import-competing sectors: industries that lose tariff protection may shed workers faster than local labour markets can absorb them. In the US Midwest under NAFTA, manufacturing towns experienced significant job losses as production shifted to lower-wage Mexico — a distributional consequence that contributed to the political backlash culminating in Trump's renegotiation into USMCA in 2020.
  • Uneven bargaining power: in FTAs between asymmetric economies, larger partners tend to capture disproportionate gains and impose favourable terms. Mexico and Canada found NAFTA's intellectual property and investor-state dispute provisions heavily shaped by US priorities, limiting their regulatory autonomy in practice.
  • Services often excluded or under-liberalised: most FTAs focus on goods trade. The UK-EU TCA, for instance, provides minimal services liberalisation — a significant problem for the UK given financial services account for around 12% of GDP, and UK-based firms lost their EU "passporting" rights post-Brexit.
Key Concept

Rules of Origin — The FTA Problem

Because members keep different external tariffs, a non-member could import goods into whichever FTA member has the lowest tariff, then re-export freely to other members. To prevent this "tariff-hopping," FTAs require proof of origin — adding administrative costs and bureaucracy at borders. This is why many economists argue a customs union (with a CET) is administratively simpler, even though it sacrifices national trade policy autonomy.

Customs Union

Adds a Common External Tariff (CET) to the free trade area — all members charge the same tariff on imports from non-members. This eliminates rules of origin checks but pools trade policy.

Level 3
Integration
✓ No internal trade barriers
✓ Common External Tariff
✗ Factor & Asset Mobility
✗ Common Currency
✗ Common Economic Policy
Real World Examples 🇪🇺 EU Customs Union 🇹🇷 Turkey–EU Customs Union 🌍 Southern African Customs Union (SACU) 🌎 Mercosur (partial) 🇬🇧 UK was in EU Customs Union pre-2021
✦ Benefits
  • Trade creation and efficiency gains: zero internal tariffs encourage switching from high-cost domestic production to lower-cost member producers. When the UK joined the EEC in 1973, trade with Western Europe expanded rapidly as British consumers accessed cheaper French agricultural produce and German manufactured goods — a shift toward more efficient producers that raised living standards.
  • Eliminates rules of origin — administrative simplicity: because all members share the same CET, there is no incentive to import via the lowest-tariff member. Internal borders require no origin checks, reducing transaction costs significantly. This is why, despite leaving the EU Single Market, the UK's continued absence from the customs union adds an estimated £7bn+ annually in extra trade friction costs for goods exporters.
  • Collective bargaining power: the bloc negotiates as a single entity, giving it far greater leverage with non-members than any individual state could achieve. The EU — the world's largest trading bloc — has used this to secure favourable terms in deals with Japan (EPA, 2019) and Canada (CETA, 2017), obtaining better market access than member states could have achieved alone.
  • Attracts FDI by deterring tariff-hopping: the CET makes it attractive for non-member multinationals to invest inside the bloc rather than import, because a single production base serves the entire tariff-free internal market. This was a significant driver of Japanese automotive FDI into Europe in the 1980s.
✦ Drawbacks
  • Trade diversion — the core welfare risk: the CET may cause member states to switch from a globally efficient non-member supplier to a less efficient member, purely because the tariff makes the non-member artificially expensive. If the EU's CET on a product is 10% and a non-member produces it 8% more cheaply than the best EU producer, consumers still pay more than under free trade — global welfare falls even if members gain.
  • Loss of independent trade policy — but how costly in practice? Members cannot negotiate bilateral deals with non-members independently. However, this argument is frequently overstated: trade negotiations are notoriously slow and uncertain. The UK-India FTA has been in negotiation since 2022 with no resolution; the EU-Mercosur deal took over 20 years to reach provisional agreement. Even concluded deals rarely deliver fully free trade — the UK-Australia FTA contains significant agricultural carve-outs. The collective weight of the customs union may therefore deliver better outcomes than individual negotiations for smaller members.
  • CET level matters enormously: a high CET maximises trade diversion and protects inefficient bloc industries at consumers' expense. The EU's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) tariffs on food imports have historically been high — keeping EU food prices above world prices and benefiting large agri-businesses at the expense of European consumers and developing-country exporters.
  • Rotterdam Effect — distributional distortion: tariff revenue is collected at the port of entry into the customs union, not at the country of final consumption. A disproportionate share of EU imports enter via Rotterdam, meaning the Netherlands collects tariff revenue for goods ultimately consumed in Germany or France — creating an ongoing fiscal redistribution dispute within the bloc.
A-Level Spotlight: Trade Diversion

The Core Customs Union Trade-Off

The welfare effect of a customs union depends on whether trade creation exceeds trade diversion. Trade creation raises welfare (switch from high-cost domestic to low-cost member). Trade diversion reduces welfare (switch from low-cost non-member to higher-cost member, caused by the CET). Net welfare rises only if the volume of trade created is larger than the trade diverted. This is why a high CET is problematic — it maximises trade diversion. Viner (1950) first formalised this analysis.

Evaluation Point

How Significant Is the Loss of Independent Trade Policy?

A common criticism of customs unions is the loss of the ability to negotiate independent trade deals — but this argument should be treated with caution. Trade negotiations are notoriously slow and complex: the UK–Australia FTA took over two years to conclude post-Brexit, and the UK–India deal has been in negotiation since 2022 with no guarantee of resolution. The EU–Mercosur deal took over 20 years to reach provisional agreement. Crucially, even when deals are eventually struck, there is no guarantee they will be free — most FTAs contain significant carve-outs, particularly in agriculture and services, meaning market access remains restricted. This suggests the theoretical freedom to negotiate independently may offer far less practical benefit than assumed, and the customs union's collective bargaining power — negotiating as a single bloc — may actually deliver superior outcomes for smaller member states that would have little individual leverage.

Single Market

Extends integration to allow free movement of goods, services, capital and labour (the "four freedoms"). Removes non-tariff barriers through regulatory harmonisation — the deepest form of market integration short of monetary union.

Level 4
Integration
✓ No internal trade barriers
✓ Common External Tariff
✓ Factor & Asset Mobility (4 freedoms)
✗ Common Currency
✗ Common Economic Policy
Real World Examples 🇪🇺 EU Single Market (EEA) 🇳🇴🇮🇸🇱🇮 Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein (EEA non-EU) 🇨🇭 Switzerland (bilateral agreements, partial)
✦ Benefits
  • Eliminates non-tariff barriers — the bigger prize: most trade costs in modern economies come not from tariffs but from differing regulations, product standards, and licensing requirements. The EU single market's principle of mutual recognition and regulatory harmonisation removes these NTBs. The European Commission estimated the single market added around 2.7% to EU GDP by 2017 — roughly equivalent to the entire economy of the Netherlands — primarily through NTB removal rather than tariff cuts.
  • Free movement of labour — efficiency gains: workers can move to where demand is highest, helping labour markets clear more efficiently across the bloc. Following EU enlargement in 2004, the movement of Polish and other Eastern European workers to the UK filled genuine labour shortages in construction, hospitality, and social care — contributing to UK GDP growth and helping moderate wage inflation in tight sectors.
  • Free movement of capital: investment flows toward its highest return across the bloc, improving productivity. The single market allowed UK-based financial institutions to serve EU clients from London under "passporting" — concentrating financial expertise and generating significant agglomeration benefits that made the City of Europe's dominant financial centre.
  • Services liberalisation — unique depth: unlike basic FTAs or even customs unions, the single market formally liberalises services trade. This is critical for modern economies where services dominate GDP. Professional qualifications are mutually recognised — a German architect can practice in Spain, a British lawyer could operate across the EU — reducing barriers that would otherwise require costly re-certification.
✦ Drawbacks
  • Brain drain — a severe cost for peripheral members: free movement of labour is a two-way mechanism, but the flow is asymmetric. Croatia, which joined the EU in 2013, experienced one of Europe's most acute examples: an estimated 15% of its population emigrated within a decade — disproportionately young, educated workers drawn to higher wages in Germany, Austria, and Ireland. This hollowed out Croatia's healthcare system (losing doctors and nurses), depleted its tax base, and created structural labour shortages in domestic industries, constraining long-run growth precisely when investment was most needed.
  • Loss of regulatory sovereignty: members must implement EU directives even where national preferences or economic conditions differ. Small member states with limited administrative capacity — such as Malta or Luxembourg — face a disproportionate burden keeping pace with the acquis communautaire (the full body of EU law), which runs to over 100,000 pages and covers everything from product labelling to employment law.
  • Free movement — domestic political tensions: rapid labour inflows can put pressure on housing, public services, and wages for lower-skilled native workers. In the UK, net migration from the EU (peaking at around 180,000 per year in the mid-2010s) became a central political grievance, directly fuelling the Leave campaign in the 2016 referendum. Whether the economic costs of reduced migration post-Brexit exceed the political benefits of "control" remains a live debate — but the single market's free movement provision made this trade-off unavoidable.
  • Asymmetric adjustment without exchange rate flexibility: single market members who are not in the Eurozone can still adjust their exchange rate — but those inside face the compounded challenge of neither monetary independence nor fiscal transfer mechanisms to offset asymmetric shocks, amplifying regional divergence within the bloc.
Key Concept

The Four Freedoms & Non-Tariff Barriers

The single market goes beyond zero tariffs — it tackles non-tariff barriers (NTBs) such as different product standards, regulations, and licensing requirements. Through the principle of mutual recognition (if a good meets standards in one member state, it can be sold across all) and harmonisation, the single market removes barriers that a customs union simply cannot address. This is why post-Brexit UK faces far more friction with the EU than Turkey does — Turkey is in the customs union but outside the single market.

Monetary Union

Members adopt a single common currency managed by a shared central bank. Interest rates and monetary policy are set collectively — individual members lose their own monetary policy instruments.

Level 5
Integration
✓ No internal trade barriers
✓ Common External Tariff
✓ Factor & Asset Mobility
✓ Common Currency
✗ Common Fiscal / Economic Policy
Real World Examples 🇪🇺 Eurozone (20 EU member states, €) 🌍 West African CFA Franc Zone (WAEMU) 🌍 Central African CFA Franc Zone (CEMAC) 🏝️ Eastern Caribbean Currency Union (ECCU)
✦ Benefits
  • Elimination of exchange rate risk: firms no longer face currency volatility on intra-bloc transactions, reducing hedging costs and encouraging long-term cross-border investment. German exporters selling to France before the euro faced Deutschmark/franc fluctuations that added uncertainty to pricing and contract values — the single currency removed this entirely, and intra-Eurozone trade as a share of members' GDP rose notably in the euro's early years.
  • Price transparency and competitive discipline: consumers and firms can directly compare prices across member states, intensifying competition. The European Commission estimated the euro increased price convergence across the Eurozone, particularly in traded goods, narrowing price gaps that previously reflected exchange rate opacity as much as genuine cost differences.
  • Reduced transaction costs: eliminating currency conversion costs for intra-bloc trade and tourism — the European Commission estimated this was worth around 0.4% of EU GDP annually — a significant gain given the density of intra-EU trade flows.
  • Monetary credibility for weaker economies: smaller or historically inflation-prone members can import the ECB's credibility. Italy, Spain, and Portugal all saw their borrowing costs converge sharply toward German levels after joining the Eurozone, reducing debt servicing costs and freeing fiscal space for investment.
  • FDI magnetism: a large, currency-stable area reduces the exchange rate risk that deters inward investment. The Eurozone as a whole is more attractive to US or Asian multinationals seeking a European base than any individual member currency area would be.
✦ Drawbacks
  • Loss of monetary policy — a blunt instrument for asymmetric shocks: the ECB sets one interest rate for all 20 Eurozone members, but national economic cycles are rarely synchronised. During the Eurozone crisis (2010–15), Germany was growing strongly and arguably needed tighter monetary policy — yet the ECB held rates low to support struggling peripherals. Simultaneously, Greece, Spain, and Portugal needed aggressive monetary stimulus and currency devaluation but had access to neither. The result was prolonged recession: Greek GDP fell by over 25% between 2008 and 2016, with unemployment peaking at 27% — a depression-level contraction that independent monetary policy could at least partially have cushioned.
  • No exchange rate adjustment — internal devaluation is painful: outside a monetary union, a country in recession can devalue its currency to restore export competitiveness without cutting wages. Inside the Eurozone this is impossible. Instead, competitiveness must be restored through "internal devaluation" — cutting nominal wages and prices — which is politically and socially devastating. Greece's public sector wages were cut by 25-30%, triggering civil unrest, mass emigration of working-age professionals, and a permanent reduction in human capital stock.
  • Borrowing cost convergence — a double-edged sword: while cheaper borrowing benefited weaker economies initially, it also enabled unsustainable debt accumulation. Greece, Ireland, and Spain borrowed heavily at near-German rates in the 2000s, financing property booms and fiscal expansion. When the 2008 financial crisis exposed these imbalances, bond spreads exploded — Greece's 10-year yield hit 35% in 2012 — forcing catastrophic fiscal consolidation precisely when counter-cyclical spending was needed.
  • No fiscal union to offset shocks: the Eurozone has monetary union but not fiscal union — the EU central budget is only ~1% of GNI, far too small to provide meaningful transfers to countries in recession. In the US federal system, automatic fiscal stabilisers (unemployment insurance, federal spending) transfer 30–40 cents per dollar of regional income loss. The Eurozone offers nothing comparable, meaning asymmetric shocks are amplified rather than absorbed.
A-Level Spotlight: OCA Theory

Optimum Currency Area — Mundell's Framework

Robert Mundell (1961) argued that a monetary union is beneficial only if the member states form an Optimum Currency Area (OCA). Key OCA conditions include: symmetric economic shocks (members are affected similarly by global events), high labour mobility (workers move freely to cushion regional unemployment), and fiscal transfers (a central budget can redistribute income to struggling regions). Critics argue the Eurozone failed to meet these criteria — particularly fiscal transfers — which explains why the 2008–12 sovereign debt crisis hit peripheral members (Greece, Ireland, Portugal) so much harder than core ones.

Economic Union

The deepest form of integration — adds harmonised or centralised economic policies (fiscal, structural, social) to monetary union. Members effectively operate as a single economic entity with coordinated macroeconomic governance.

Level 6
Integration
✓ No internal trade barriers
✓ Common External Tariff
✓ Factor & Asset Mobility
✓ Common Currency
✓ Common Economic Policy
Real World Examples 🇺🇸 United States (full economic union) 🇪🇺 EU (approaching — partial fiscal coordination) 🇩🇪 German reunification 1990 (rapid economic union) 🇨🇭 Switzerland (federal internal economic union)
✦ Benefits
  • Macroeconomic coordination eliminates policy contradiction: in a monetary union without fiscal coordination, member states can run fiscal policies that pull in opposite directions — one expanding, another contracting, undermining the whole bloc's stability. A true economic union aligns fiscal and monetary stances: US federal spending automatically channels resources to depressed states through Social Security, Medicare, and unemployment insurance, without individual state governments needing to negotiate or vote on transfers each time.
  • Automatic fiscal stabilisers across borders: a central budget large enough to redistribute income to weaker regions provides a powerful shock absorber. When oil prices collapsed in the 1980s, Texas received large implicit federal transfers through the US system — mitigating the impact on employment and output. The EU's inability to replicate this mechanism was the fundamental structural failure exposed by the Eurozone crisis.
  • Policy credibility for structural reform: supranational governance can impose structural reforms — labour market flexibility, pension sustainability, competition policy — that domestic governments, facing electoral pressures, would otherwise delay. The EU's state aid rules and Competition Policy have prevented member governments from subsidising inefficient national champions, forcing long-run productivity improvements.
  • Historical peace dividend: the deepest form of economic integration creates such profound mutual dependency that military conflict becomes economically irrational. The original rationale for European integration was precisely this — binding post-war France and Germany so tightly economically that war between them became unthinkable. Franco-German trade today exceeds €200bn annually; the political economy of that relationship has fundamentally altered European security.
✦ Drawbacks
  • Maximum sovereignty loss — fiscal policy is the most politically sensitive instrument: budgets are how governments express their values and respond to constituents. Ceding control over taxation, spending levels, and deficit rules to supranational bodies fundamentally alters the relationship between citizens and their governments. The EU's Stability and Growth Pact (3% deficit ceiling, 60% debt-to-GDP ceiling) forced Ireland and Spain into pro-cyclical austerity in 2010–12 when their economies were already contracting — a requirement that deepened recessions and extended unemployment crises rather than correcting them.
  • Democratic deficit — accountability without democracy: centralised economic governance lacks the democratic legitimacy of national institutions. The ECB is entirely unelected; the European Commission is not directly elected; even the European Parliament is elected on turnout that averaged below 50% for most of its history. When the Troika (ECB, IMF, European Commission) imposed austerity conditions on Greece in exchange for bailout loans, Greek voters had elected a government on an explicitly anti-austerity platform — but found that the terms were essentially non-negotiable. This tension between democratic mandate and supranational authority remains deeply unresolved.
  • Moral hazard — the transfer union problem: if member states know that fiscal difficulties will ultimately trigger bail-outs or transfers from wealthier members, the incentive to maintain fiscal discipline weakens. Germany's resistance to Eurobonds and the expansion of the EU budget reflects a genuine concern that permanent transfer mechanisms would allow peripheral governments to run chronically loose fiscal policy at the expense of more disciplined members — a problem that no political solution has yet satisfactorily resolved.
  • Practically unachievable without political union: a true economic union requires a federal political structure — an elected government with a significant central budget, taxing and borrowing powers. The EU has been converging toward economic union for decades but remains far short: its budget of ~1% of GNI compares with US federal spending of ~25% of GDP. The fundamental barrier is political, not economic — member states and their populations remain unwilling to accept the degree of sovereignty transfer that genuine economic union requires.
Examiner Focus

Is the EU an Economic Union?

The EU is often cited as the closest example of an economic union — but it is incomplete. The Eurozone has monetary union, the single market provides factor mobility, and the customs union provides a CET. However, fiscal policy remains largely national — the EU budget is only ~1% of GNI, far smaller than the federal budgets of true economic unions like the USA (~25% of GDP). This fragmentation was exposed during the Eurozone crisis: without fiscal transfers, peripheral countries were forced into severe austerity while the ECB could not target their specific needs. The debate over "fiscal union" vs national sovereignty remains the central unresolved tension in European integration.

Supply & demand with tariff wedge — Customs Union analysis

Before Customs Union

Domestic price = Pw+T (world price + tariff). Domestic supply = Q1, domestic demand = Q2. Imports = Q1→Q2, sourced from the cheapest world supplier.

After Joining Customs Union

CET excludes cheapest non-member. Price falls to Pm (member price, cheaper than Pw+T but above Pw). Domestic supply falls to Q3, demand rises to Q4.

Trade Creation (green triangles)

Q1→Q3: domestic producers previously protected by tariff lose market to efficient member. Q2→Q4: lower price stimulates new consumer demand. Both raise welfare.

Trade Diversion (amber rectangle)

Q3→Q1 and Q2→Q4 imports now sourced from less efficient member rather than cheapest world supplier (excluded by CET). Consumer pays Pm not Pw — welfare loss. Net effect depends on relative size of green vs amber areas.

Diagram 2

The Integration Ladder

Stages of economic integration — cumulative features at each level

Key Principle: Cumulative

Each stage includes all the features of those below it. An Economic Union has all six features simultaneously — it does not replace lower stages but builds upon them.

Critical Threshold: Customs Union

The CET is the most analytically important addition. It enables collective bargaining, eliminates rules of origin, but introduces trade diversion risk and surrenders independent trade policy.

Critical Threshold: Single Market

The four freedoms mark a qualitative shift — from a trading arrangement to genuine factor market integration. NTB removal generates larger welfare gains than tariff removal alone.

Sovereignty Trade-off

The higher the rung, the greater the surrender of national policy autonomy. Economic union requires fiscal sovereignty to be pooled — practically inseparable from political union.

Diagram 3

Optimum Currency Area (OCA) Framework

Conditions for monetary union benefit — and the Eurozone's failure to meet them

Symmetric Shocks — Partial ⚠

Core Eurozone economies (Germany, France) are reasonably synchronised. But peripheral members — Greece, Portugal, Ireland — experienced very different cycles pre-2008, meaning the ECB rate was poorly calibrated for both simultaneously.

Labour Mobility — Limited ✗

Language, cultural, and qualification barriers significantly limit labour mobility across EU states compared to the US. Croatian workers moving to Germany helps Croatia's unemployment — but creates brain drain. True OCA mobility requires frictionless movement.

Fiscal Transfers — Absent ✗

The EU budget is ~1% of GNI — far too small to act as an automatic stabiliser. When Greece went into deep recession, there was no equivalent of US federal unemployment insurance flowing automatically. Absence of fiscal transfers is the Eurozone's greatest structural weakness.

Trade Integration — Strong ✓

Intra-EU trade accounts for roughly 60% of member states' total trade — meaning the single currency genuinely reduces a large volume of exchange rate friction. This is the strongest OCA argument for the Eurozone, and partly explains why membership has not been abandoned despite its structural flaws.
