Floating Exchange Rates

Key Definition

A floating exchange rate system is where the value of a currency is determined by market forces of supply and demand in the foreign exchange (FOREX) market, without direct government or central bank intervention. The exchange rate fluctuates freely based on international trade, capital flows, speculation, and economic conditions.

The FOREX Market

The FOREX market is the global decentralised marketplace where currencies are traded. It operates 24 hours a day across major financial centres (London, New York, Tokyo, Singapore). With over $7 trillion traded daily, it is the world's largest and most liquid financial market. Participants include commercial banks, central banks, investment firms, hedge funds, corporations, and retail traders.

How Appreciation & Depreciation Occur

1

Initial Equilibrium

The exchange rate begins where currency supplied equals currency demanded. E.g. at £1 = $1.30, British importers supplying pounds are balanced by American importers demanding pounds to buy UK goods.

2

A Change in Conditions

Economic factors shift supply or demand. If UK exports become more attractive, American consumers want more British goods, increasing their demand for pounds.

3

Simultaneous Market Movements

When Americans demand more pounds, they must supply more dollars. Two simultaneous effects: demand for £ rises (D curve shifts right) and supply of $ rises. These are mirror images of the same transaction.

4

Appreciation of the Pound

Increased demand for £ causes appreciation. The exchange rate rises from £1 = $1.30 to £1 = $1.40. Each pound now buys more dollars.

5

Depreciation of the Dollar

Simultaneously, increased supply of $ causes the dollar to depreciate. Where $1 = £0.77, it may fall to $1 = £0.71. The dollar buys fewer pounds.

6

New Equilibrium

The FOREX market reaches a new equilibrium at the higher rate. This process is continuous, with exchange rates adjusting freely without government intervention.

Benefits & Drawbacks — Click to Expand

✓

Benefits

The exchange rate acts as an automatic stabiliser. If a country faces recession and imports fall, reduced demand for foreign currency causes domestic currency depreciation. This makes exports cheaper internationally, boosting demand and aiding recovery — without government intervention.

Central banks can set interest rates to meet domestic objectives (controlling inflation, supporting employment) without worrying about maintaining a fixed rate. The Bank of England can lower rates during a recession even if it causes pound depreciation.

Since the central bank doesn't intervene, it doesn't need to hold massive foreign reserves. These can instead be invested in productive assets, reducing the opportunity cost and eliminating the risk of depleting reserves during crises.

Persistent trade deficits lead to depreciation, making imports costlier and exports cheaper, automatically reducing the deficit. This self-correcting mechanism helps maintain external balance without deliberate policy intervention.

Unlike fixed rates, floating rates don't provide speculators with a one-way bet. If speculators sell a currency, it simply depreciates gradually rather than forcing a central bank to defend a fixed peg, reducing the risk of sudden catastrophic collapses.

Exchange rates reflect the collective knowledge of millions of participants, incorporating information about fundamentals, policy changes, and future prospects. This price discovery typically allocates resources more efficiently than government-determined rates.

✗

Drawbacks

Floating rates can fluctuate significantly and unpredictably, creating uncertainty for businesses in international trade. A UK exporter agreeing to sell goods for $100,000 in three months might receive payment worth £71,000 or £77,000 — a large and unknowable variance at the time of contract.

Currency markets can be driven by speculation rather than fundamentals. Traders may sell a currency based on rumours or herd behaviour, causing sharp depreciation that doesn't reflect economic reality. Short-term speculative flows can overwhelm longer-term fundamental forces.

Countries might deliberately weaken their currencies to gain export competitiveness, triggering "currency wars" where multiple nations try to depreciate simultaneously. This can destabilise global trade and provoke international tension and retaliation.

Currency depreciation makes imports more expensive, triggering cost-push inflation. For countries heavily dependent on imported oil, food, or raw materials, depreciation can generate significant inflationary pressure, eroding living standards and forcing contractionary policy responses.

The flexibility of floating rates might allow governments to avoid confronting structural economic problems. Poor fiscal policy or deep imbalances may simply manifest as gradual depreciation rather than forcing immediate, necessary correction.

Exchange rate uncertainty can discourage long-term cross-border investment. A company planning a foreign factory must factor in exchange rate risk over many years, making return calculations difficult and potentially deterring productive FDI — especially into developing economies.

✏️

Exam Tip — Floating Rates

For 25-mark essays, a strong evaluation point is that hot money flows can undermine the stability benefits of floating rates — short-term capital movements driven by interest rate differentials or speculation can cause sharp, destabilising swings that have nothing to do with trade fundamentals. Also note that "pure" floating rates are rare — most countries engage in occasional intervention, blurring the boundary with managed floats, which limits how cleanly the theoretical benefits apply in practice. The UK (post-1992) and the USA are your best floating rate case studies.

Fixed Exchange Rates

Key Definition

A fixed exchange rate (also called a pegged exchange rate) is where a country's government or central bank maintains the value of its currency at a predetermined rate against another currency or basket. The central bank actively intervenes in the FOREX market to keep the rate at or very close to the target, regardless of market pressures.

How Central Banks Maintain a Fixed Rate

1

Direct FOREX Intervention

If the currency faces depreciation pressure, the central bank buys domestic currency using foreign reserves, increasing demand. To prevent appreciation, it sells domestic currency, increasing supply. This is the most direct and immediate tool.

2

Interest Rate Policy

Higher domestic interest rates attract foreign capital inflows (hot money), increasing demand for the domestic currency and supporting the peg. This sacrifices monetary policy independence to maintain the rate — a key drawback.

3

Capital Controls

Restricting capital flows reduces exchange rate pressure. Countries may limit how much domestic currency can be converted or transferred abroad — e.g. China's historically restricted capital account reduces pressure on the yuan.

4

Fiscal Policy Adjustment

Austerity reduces domestic demand, cutting imports and easing pressure on the exchange rate — an "internal devaluation" that substitutes for nominal exchange rate adjustment, but at the cost of growth and employment.

5

Sterilisation Operations

Intervention affects the domestic money supply. Sterilisation offsets this: if the CB sells domestic currency (increasing money supply), it simultaneously sells government bonds to absorb excess liquidity, keeping monetary conditions stable while still managing the rate.

6

Credibility & Communication

The more credible the commitment to the peg, the less intervention is needed. Hong Kong has maintained its USD peg since 1983 — over 40 years — building extraordinary credibility through consistent defence, institutional backing, and adequate reserves.

Benefits & Drawbacks — Click to Expand

✓

Benefits

Fixed rates eliminate exchange rate risk for exporters and importers, reducing hedging costs and making long-term contracts predictable. Businesses can plan with confidence, potentially increasing international trade and investment volumes.

Pegging to a low-inflation currency forces domestic monetary policy to remain compatible. Countries with poor inflation track records can "import" credibility — as the Eurozone embodies for its member states, who cannot inflate away their debts independently.

Reduced exchange rate uncertainty makes long-term investment decisions easier for multinational firms, potentially increasing FDI flows — particularly relevant for smaller, developing economies seeking to attract foreign capital.

With a fixed rate, firms don't need to spend money on currency hedging instruments (forwards, options). This is particularly valuable for small and medium enterprises that lack sophisticated treasury functions and cannot easily manage forex exposure.

✗

Drawbacks

Interest rates must serve the exchange rate target, not domestic objectives. The "Impossible Trinity" (Mundell-Fleming) states you cannot simultaneously have a fixed rate, free capital flows, and independent monetary policy — a fixed rate forces sacrifice of monetary independence.

Defending the peg requires sufficient foreign reserves to withstand market pressure. Depleting reserves signals weakness, inviting further speculation — a dangerous spiral that cost the UK over £3 billion on Black Wednesday (1992) before forcing ERM exit.

A fixed rate provides a clear target for speculators. If markets believe the peg is unsustainable, a one-way bet emerges. The 1997 Asian Financial Crisis saw coordinated attacks on Thai baht, Indonesian rupiah, and Korean won pegs, causing devastating recessions.

Fixed rates prevent gradual adjustment, allowing imbalances to build until the peg becomes unsustainable. When it breaks, the correction is sudden and severe — Argentina's 2001–02 crisis saw the peso collapse and GDP fall ~10.9%, causing mass unemployment and bank runs.

✏️

Exam Tip — Fixed Rates

The Mundell-Fleming "Impossible Trinity" is a powerful evaluation framework. When evaluating devaluation as a policy tool, always apply the Marshall-Lerner condition (a devaluation only improves the current account if the sum of export and import price elasticities of demand exceeds 1) and the J-curve effect (in the short run, the current account may worsen before it improves, as existing contracts mean volumes don't adjust immediately). Cite Black Wednesday 1992 (UK ERM exit) as a classic fixed rate failure, and contrast with Hong Kong's HKD peg (maintained since 1983) as evidence that credibility and reserve adequacy can sustain a fixed rate long-term.

Managed Float (Semi-Fixed)

Key Definition

A managed float (also called a dirty float or semi-fixed system) is a hybrid approach where exchange rates are generally determined by market forces but the central bank intervenes occasionally to influence the rate or limit volatility. It attempts to combine the flexibility of floating with the stability elements of fixed rate systems.

The Currency Band System

1

The Band

Many managed floats operate with an implicit or explicit band. The currency floats freely between an upper limit (ceiling) and a lower limit (floor). No intervention occurs while the rate stays within the band — e.g. a central bank allows trading between $1.20 and $1.40.

2

Currency Ceiling (Upper Limit)

When market pressure pushes toward the ceiling, the central bank sells domestic currency and buys foreign currency — increasing supply of domestic currency, preventing further appreciation. This protects export competitiveness.

3

Currency Floor (Lower Limit)

When market pressure pushes toward the floor, the central bank buys domestic currency using foreign reserves — increasing demand, preventing further depreciation. Guards against inflation from more expensive imports.

4

Free Float Within the Band

Between ceiling and floor, the rate fluctuates freely based on supply and demand. This provides day-to-day adjustment benefits while the band limits extreme volatility. Band width determines the balance between flexibility and stability.

5

Adjusting the Band

Central banks can periodically shift the band in response to changing economic fundamentals. If the economy strengthens sustainably, the band may be shifted upward — more flexible than a rigid fixed rate while still maintaining directional control.

6

Discretionary Intervention

Some managed floats have no explicit band — the central bank intervenes discretionarily when it judges volatility is excessive or the rate deviates significantly from fundamentals. China's PBOC sets a daily reference rate with a ±2% daily trading band as a key example.

Benefits & Drawbacks — Click to Expand

✓

Benefits

The band allows automatic adjustment to economic conditions while limiting extreme volatility. Businesses get more predictability than pure floating while the economy retains adjustment flexibility — often described as combining the "best of both worlds."

Unlike fixed rates, central banks only need to intervene periodically, preserving the ability to set interest rates for domestic objectives most of the time. This was particularly valuable during COVID-19 when emergency monetary measures were required globally.

Because managed floats don't commit to a single rigid rate, they're less vulnerable to one-way speculative bets. If pressure intensifies, the rate can partially adjust, reducing the all-or-nothing dynamics that make fixed rates crisis-prone.

Intervention can smooth short-term volatility driven by speculation or temporary shocks, protecting the real economy from disruptive swings — "leaning against the wind" — without preventing necessary long-run adjustment to fundamentals.

✗

Drawbacks

Managed floats lack the transparency of purely floating or rigidly fixed rates. If intervention rules are unclear or undisclosed, businesses cannot reliably hedge, and market uncertainty can actually increase compared to either clear alternative.

Central banks may intervene at inappropriate times due to political pressure or attachment to outdated targets. A government might defend an overvalued currency to keep import prices low — wasting reserves defending an unsustainable rate while delaying necessary adjustment.

The band can create false stability, encouraging businesses and investors to take unhedged positions assuming central bank protection. When the band eventually shifts or is overwhelmed, the resulting adjustment can be sudden and catch unprepared actors by surprise.

Managed floats can facilitate currency manipulation — deliberately keeping a currency undervalued to maintain export competitiveness. The discretionary nature makes it hard to distinguish legitimate smoothing from deliberate manipulation, creating international tension (e.g. US–China trade disputes).

✏️

Exam Tip — Managed Float

Use China's PBOC system as your primary case study — well-documented and examiners respond well to it. The key evaluative point: does a managed float actually deliver "the best of both worlds," or does it simply inherit the disadvantages of both? Singapore's MAS approach (managing via the exchange rate rather than interest rates) is a distinctive and impressive additional example.

Essay Structure — "Evaluate the case for fixed exchange rates"

Para 1: Define and explain the mechanism (CB intervention, reserves, interest rates). Para 2: Key benefit — trade certainty and anti-inflation discipline (Hong Kong case study). Para 3: Key drawback — loss of monetary independence / speculative attacks (Black Wednesday). Para 4: Evaluation — effectiveness depends on trade openness, reserve adequacy, anchor currency choice, and whether the rate is set appropriately. Conclusion: Fixed rates work best for small, very open economies tightly linked to one major trading partner — but are dangerous for large economies with divergent business cycles.

🔺

The Mundell-Fleming "Impossible Trinity"

A country cannot simultaneously achieve: (1) a fixed exchange rate, (2) free capital flows, and (3) independent monetary policy. It must sacrifice one. Floating rates sacrifice stability; fixed rates sacrifice monetary independence; capital controls sacrifice financial integration. Use this as an evaluative framework across all three systems — it consistently earns marks in 25-mark essays.
