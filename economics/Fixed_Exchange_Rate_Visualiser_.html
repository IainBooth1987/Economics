Fixed & Managed Exchange Rates

Interactive Visualiser · Crisis Simulator · Case Studies

Core Theory
Key Definition

A fixed exchange rate (pegged exchange rate) is where a country's central bank maintains the value of its currency at a predetermined rate against another currency or basket. Unlike a floating rate determined by market forces, the central bank actively intervenes to keep the rate at the target level — buying or selling domestic currency as required.

The Impossible Trinity (Mundell-Fleming)
🔒
Fixed Exchange Rate
Stable rate for trade and investment certainty
🌊
Free Capital Flows
Unrestricted movement of money across borders
🎯
Independent Monetary Policy
Set interest rates for domestic objectives

The Trilemma

A country cannot have all three simultaneously. It must sacrifice one:
• Fixed rate + Free capital → Must sacrifice monetary independence (e.g. Hong Kong)
• Fixed rate + Monetary independence → Must impose capital controls (e.g. China historically)
• Free capital + Monetary independence → Must sacrifice fixed rate (e.g. UK post-1992)

Marshall-Lerner Condition & the J-Curve

Marshall-Lerner Condition

A devaluation/depreciation will only improve the current account if the sum of the price elasticities of demand for exports and imports (PEDX + PEDM) exceeds 1. If elasticities are low (e.g. inelastic demand for oil imports), devaluation may worsen the trade balance.

J-Curve Effect

Even if the Marshall-Lerner condition holds, there is a short-run worsening of the current account before improvement. Existing contracts are fulfilled at old prices — volumes don't adjust immediately. The trade balance dips before rising, tracing a J shape over time.

Policy Tools for Maintaining a Fixed Rate
1
Direct FOREX Intervention

Buy domestic currency (spending reserves) to prevent depreciation. Sell domestic currency (accumulating reserves) to prevent appreciation. Immediate but depletes reserves.

2
Interest Rate Policy

Raise rates to attract capital inflows (hot money), increasing demand for domestic currency. But this risks recession — sacrifices monetary policy independence.

3
Capital Controls

Restrict currency outflows — limit how much domestic currency can be converted abroad. Reduces pressure on reserves. But may reduce FDI and signal economic weakness.

4
Fiscal Austerity (Internal Devaluation)

Cut government spending / raise taxes to reduce aggregate demand and imports, easing pressure on the exchange rate. Painful — requires actual falls in wages and prices rather than a simple currency adjustment.

5
Sterilisation

FOREX intervention changes the domestic money supply. Sterilisation offsets this — e.g. if selling domestic currency (expansionary), simultaneously sell government bonds to mop up liquidity. Prevents unintended inflation.

✏️

Essay Evaluation Framework

The success of a fixed rate depends on: (1) whether the rate is set at a realistic level aligned with fundamentals, (2) adequacy of reserves, (3) fiscal discipline, (4) credibility — is the commitment to defend believed? Contrast Hong Kong (all four present → 40+ year success) with Argentina 2002 (fundamental misalignment → collapse). Use the Marshall-Lerner condition when evaluating devaluation as an exit strategy.

Exam Tip — Reading the Diagram

When demand shifts LEFT of the fixed rate line, the market equilibrium rate would be higher than the peg — the central bank must buy domestic currency (spending reserves) to maintain it. When demand shifts RIGHT, the peg is below market equilibrium — the CB sells domestic currency (accumulating reserves). Always label the fixed rate line, the market equilibrium, and the intervention arrow.

Ceiling vs Floor — What Happens?
🔴 At the Ceiling

Demand is very high — the currency wants to appreciate above the ceiling. The central bank must sell domestic currency (increasing supply) to prevent it. Reserves accumulate — this is the "good problem." But it can cause imported inflation.

🟢 At the Floor

Demand is very low — the currency wants to depreciate below the floor. The central bank must buy domestic currency (spending reserves) to support it. Reserves deplete — the dangerous scenario. If reserves run out, the floor collapses.

✏️

Exam Tip — Band System

Note the asymmetry: defending the floor is much more dangerous than defending the ceiling. A central bank can print unlimited domestic currency to sell (ceiling defence), but cannot print foreign currency to buy (floor defence) — reserves are finite. This is why currency crises almost always involve currencies hitting the floor, not the ceiling.

Historical Case Studies

Five defining moments in fixed exchange rate history — from catastrophic failures to remarkable successes. Know these for your essays.

🇬🇧
Black Wednesday
UK · ERM · 1992
Failure
Peg2.78–3.13 DEM/£
Cost£3.3bn
Drop−15%
The CrisisPound maintained in ERM despite UK being in recession. George Soros and others shorted £10bn of sterling, betting the peg was unsustainable.
The ResponseInterest rates raised from 10% to 15% in a single day. Over £27bn (half of reserves) spent buying pounds. Failed within hours.
OutcomePeg abandoned 16 September 1992. Pound fell 15% against DEM. Ironically, UK economy recovered strongly afterwards as lower rates and weaker pound boosted growth.
Lesson: No amount of reserves can defend a peg that is fundamentally misaligned with economic conditions. Markets will win when the peg is unsustainable.
🌏
Asian Financial Crisis
Thailand, Indonesia, Korea · 1997–98
Failure
Thai GDP−10.5%
Indonesia−13.1%
FX Fall50–80%
Root CauseDollar pegs + large foreign currency debt + current account deficits. Pegs became overvalued as dollar strengthened through 1996–97.
The Death SpiralCurrency devaluation made foreign debt more expensive in local currency → corporate bankruptcies → banking collapse → deeper recession → more devaluation pressure.
ContagionCrisis spread from Thailand to Indonesia, Korea, Malaysia as investors panicked about similar vulnerabilities across the region.
Lesson: Fixed rates amplify crises when countries have large foreign currency debts. Devaluation doesn't just fix competitiveness — it can detonate a debt bomb.
🇦🇷
Argentina Currency Board
Argentina · 1991–2002
Failure
Peg1:1 Peso/USD
GDP fall−28%
Poverty57%
The Setup1991: Argentina introduced currency board — peso fully backed by USD at 1:1. Initially hugely successful in ending hyperinflation.
The ProblemAs US dollar strengthened in late 1990s, peso became massively overvalued. Argentina in deep recession but couldn't devalue or cut rates. Government borrowed heavily in dollars.
Collapse2001: Bank runs, capital flight, IMF bailout failed. January 2002: peg abandoned. Peso fell 75%. Largest sovereign default in history at the time.
Lesson: Currency boards require fiscal discipline and economic flexibility. Without the ability to use monetary policy, a recession under a peg can spiral into catastrophe.
🇨🇭
Swiss Franc Floor
SNB · 2011–2015
Mixed
Floor1.20 CHF/EUR
Reserve growth50→500bn
Drop on exit−30%
Background2011: Safe-haven flows during Eurozone crisis caused CHF to surge, threatening Swiss exporters. SNB set 1.20 floor against Euro and defended it aggressively.
The ScaleReserves grew from CHF50bn to CHF500bn — larger than Swiss GDP — as SNB printed francs to buy euros. SNB became one of world's largest sovereign investors.
The ShockJanuary 15, 2015: SNB abandoned floor without warning. CHF appreciated 30% within minutes. Enormous losses for currency traders and Swiss exporters.
Lesson: Even wealthy central banks have limits. The asymmetry of ceiling defence (can print unlimited CHF) was sustainable technically but created growing balance sheet risks that eventually became unacceptable.
🇭🇰
Hong Kong Dollar Peg
HKMA · 1983–present
Success
Peg7.75–7.85/USD
Reserves$430bn+
Duration40+ years
The SystemCurrency board: every HKD in circulation is 100% backed by USD in the Exchange Fund. No discretion — intervention is automatic and rule-based.
Tests SurvivedAsian Financial Crisis (1997), Global Financial Crisis (2008), COVID-19, 2019 political unrest. Peg held through all of them.
Why It WorksEnormous reserves (>100% of monetary base), strict fiscal discipline (HK rarely runs deficits), automatic interest rate mechanism, and deep political commitment to maintaining the peg.
Lesson: Fixed rates CAN work long-term with: (1) adequate reserves, (2) fiscal discipline, (3) credible automatic mechanisms, and (4) economic conditions compatible with the anchor currency.
⚠️ Warning Signs of Peg Failure
📉Persistent current account deficits — more currency flowing out than in
🏦Rapid reserve depletion from frequent interventions
💸Large foreign currency debt that devaluation would make more expensive
📊Economic divergence from the anchor currency country
🗳️Political pressure to devalue or abandon peg
🐆Speculative positioning — traders shorting the currency
