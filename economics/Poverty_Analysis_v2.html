Understanding Poverty: Core Concepts

What is Poverty?

Poverty describes a condition in which individuals or households lack the resources to meet basic needs or maintain a socially acceptable standard of living. Economists distinguish between two main forms:

Absolute Poverty

Definition: A condition in which individuals lack the minimum resources required to survive — food, shelter, clean water and basic healthcare.

📌 Key Concept
  • Fixed threshold — does not change with average incomes
  • Based on a defined minimum caloric / resource standard
  • Can be eliminated as a society grows richer
  • Most relevant in developing economies

Relative Poverty

Definition: A condition in which individuals have significantly less income than is typical in their society, preventing full participation in normal social life.

📌 Key Concept
  • Measured relative to median income — rises with living standards
  • Focuses on inequality and social exclusion
  • Context-dependent: the threshold shifts across countries and time
  • Persistent even in rich nations

Critical Distinction: Why Both Matter

If everyone's income doubles, absolute poverty falls but relative poverty stays the same — the distribution of income is unchanged. This is why policymakers need both perspectives simultaneously:

  • Absolute measures track whether basic needs are being met.
  • Relative measures track inequality and social inclusion.
💡 Evaluation Point

A government could claim success on absolute poverty while relative poverty worsens — good analysts always interrogate which measure is being reported.

Measures of Absolute Poverty

1. International Poverty Line (World Bank: $2.15/day)

The most widely used global measure. Set at $2.15/day (2017 PPP), representing the typical poverty line of the world's poorest countries.

Scale of Progress: Using this measure, extreme global poverty fell from 36% (1990) to around 8.5% (2019) — over 1 billion people lifted out of poverty, primarily driven by China and South-East Asia.
💡 Evaluation Point

Limitation: The $2.15 threshold is extremely low and may mask significant deprivation. PPP conversions are imperfect and cannot capture access to public services (healthcare, education). Progress may be overstated if many people are clustered just above the line.

2. National Poverty Lines

Countries set their own lines based on local costs and needs:

  • USA: ~$15,000/year for individuals (3× minimum food costs, 1960s methodology — widely criticised as outdated)
  • India: ₹47/day rural, ₹56/day urban (Rangarajan Committee, 2014)
  • China: Declared elimination of absolute poverty in 2020 using ¥4,000/year standard
💡 Evaluation Point

National lines are not comparable across countries and are often politically sensitive — governments may set thresholds low to show success.

3. Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI)

Developed by UNDP and the Oxford Poverty & Human Development Initiative. Measures deprivation across three dimensions with 10 indicators — someone deprived in 33%+ of weighted indicators is counted as multidimensionally poor.

Health (1/3 weight)

  • Nutrition
  • Child mortality

Education (1/3 weight)

  • Years of schooling
  • School attendance

Living Standards (1/3 weight)

  • Electricity, clean water, sanitation
  • Cooking fuel, housing quality, household assets
🔍 Analysis

The MPI captures what income measures miss: two households with identical incomes may face very different levels of deprivation in health or education access. However, it is more data-intensive and harder to compute consistently across countries.

Measures of Relative Poverty

1. Percentage of Median Income

The standard approach: poverty line set at 50%, 60%, or 70% of median equivalised household income. The EU uses 60%; the OECD uses 50%.

Example — UK 2023:
Median income (after housing costs): £28,400  |  60% threshold: £17,040
Result: 22% of population (~14.4 million people) in relative poverty

Why median not mean? The mean is pulled upward by very high incomes, making it a poor reflection of typical living standards. The median is resistant to extreme values.

2. The Lorenz Curve & Gini Coefficient

Reading the Lorenz Curve

The Lorenz curve plots the cumulative share of income received by the bottom x% of the population. The further the curve bows away from the diagonal line of perfect equality, the greater the inequality.

The Gini coefficient = Area A ÷ (Area A + Area B). It ranges from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (one person owns everything).

🔍 Analysis
0.25–0.30Low inequality (Nordic countries)
0.30–0.40Moderate (most of Europe)
0.40–0.50High (USA, UK, China)
0.50+Very high (South Africa, Brazil)
💡 Evaluation Point

The Gini coefficient summarises the Lorenz curve in a single number — but two countries can have identical Gini values with very different distributions (e.g. one where the middle class is squeezed vs one where the very top is enriched).

3. Material Deprivation Indices

Measures whether households can afford items considered normal in their society — a direct test of social inclusion rather than just income.

EU measures 13 items including:
  • One week's annual holiday away from home
  • Keeping the home adequately warm
  • Replacing worn furniture or clothing
  • An internet connection
  • Regular social activities and leisure

Unable to afford 5+ items = materially deprived.

↔ Compare & Contrast

Material deprivation indices capture non-monetary dimensions of poverty that income measures miss, but the choice of items is inherently subjective and changes as societal norms evolve.

Causes of Changes in Poverty

Structural Economic Factors

  • Economic Growth: The most powerful driver of absolute poverty reduction — raises incomes, creates employment and expands the fiscal capacity to fund social programmes
  • Labour Markets: Wage levels, employment quality and collective bargaining arrangements critically shape poverty outcomes
  • Globalisation: Can reduce absolute poverty through growth and job creation, but may increase inequality — winners and losers vary by sector and skill level
  • Demographics: Ageing populations, household composition and youth unemployment all affect poverty rates
  • Inflation: Disproportionately harms the poor, who spend a higher share of income on food, energy and housing

Real-World Policy Evidence

🇧🇷 Brazil: Bolsa Família (1995–Present)

What is it?

Conditional Cash Transfer (CCT) Programme

  • Monthly payments to poor families (~$50/month)
  • Conditions: Children must attend school (85%+ attendance) and receive health check-ups
  • Reaches 50 million people — approximately 25% of the population
  • Cost: ~0.5% of GDP

Impact on Absolute Poverty

  • Extreme poverty fell from 14% (2001) to 4.5% (2014)
  • Lifted approximately 36 million Brazilians out of extreme poverty
  • Accounted for 15–20% of total poverty reduction

Impact on Relative Poverty & Inequality

  • Gini coefficient fell from 0.59 to 0.52 (2001–2014)
  • Income of poorest 20% grew 138% vs 16% for richest 20%
  • Relative poverty fell from 28% to 19%

Long-Term Effects

  • School enrolment rose to 98%; dropout rates fell 36%
  • Infant mortality declined 19% faster in high-coverage areas
  • No evidence of work disincentive — labour force participation remained stable
  • Women's empowerment increased (transfers paid directly to mothers)
💡 Evaluation Point
Key Lesson: Well-designed CCTs can significantly reduce both forms of poverty when adequately funded. The conditions attached create long-run human capital benefits — but success also depended on broader macroeconomic growth during this period. CCTs alone are not sufficient without wider economic context.

🇨🇳 China: Economic Reform & Poverty Alleviation (1978–2020)

The Greatest Poverty Reduction in History

~800 million people lifted out of poverty

  • Extreme poverty: 88% (1981) → 0.6% (2019)
  • Official elimination of absolute poverty declared 2020
  • Accounted for >70% of global poverty reduction since 1990

Key Mechanisms

  • Agricultural reforms (1978–84): Household Responsibility System replaced communes; productivity surged ~34% in six years
  • Export-led manufacturing: Special Economic Zones created hundreds of millions of factory jobs
  • Internal migration: 290 million rural workers moved to cities for higher wages
  • Infrastructure: Massive investment connected poor regions to markets
  • Targeted alleviation (2013–20): $246 billion invested; 3 million officials deployed to 128,000 poor villages

The Inequality Paradox

Despite dramatic absolute poverty reduction, inequality rose sharply:

  • Gini coefficient: 0.30 (1980) → 0.47 (2008)
  • Urban incomes now 3.2× rural incomes (was 2.5× in 1990)
  • Top 10% income share rose from 25% to 40%
💡 Evaluation Point

Core Insight: Rapid growth eliminated absolute poverty while simultaneously increasing relative poverty. This illustrates that growth alone does not reduce inequality — specific redistributive policies are required.

🇬🇧 UK: Welfare Reform & Austerity (2010–Present)

Policy Changes

  • Universal Credit: Merged six benefits into one (rolled out 2013–2018)
  • Austerity: Benefit freeze (2016–20), benefit cap, two-child limit
  • Goal: Reduce the fiscal deficit following the 2008 financial crisis

Impact on Poverty

  • Relative poverty (after housing costs) rose from 21% to 22% (2010–2020)
  • Child poverty increased: 27% → 31% (4.3 million children)
  • In-work poverty increased: 16% of working families now below the poverty line
  • Food bank usage increased approximately 100-fold over the period

COVID Response: A Natural Experiment

In 2020, Universal Credit was increased by £20/week (a ~20% boost):

  • Estimated to have prevented 700,000 people falling into poverty during the pandemic
  • When removed in October 2021 despite widespread opposition, pushed an estimated 500,000+ into poverty
📊 Evidence

This episode demonstrated that benefit adequacy directly drives poverty outcomes — and that the cuts of 2010–2019 had real effects on living standards.

🇸🇪 Sweden: The Nordic Model

Key Features

  • Universal welfare state: free education, healthcare, heavily subsidised childcare
  • Generous social insurance (unemployment, sickness, parental leave)
  • Active labour market policies — retraining and job-search support
  • High taxation (~45% of GDP)
  • High union membership (~70%), extensive collective bargaining

Outcomes

  • Absolute poverty essentially eliminated (<1%)
  • Gini coefficient: 0.28 — among the world's most equal economies
  • High productivity despite high wages and taxes
  • Material deprivation: 2–3% (lowest in Europe)

Recent Challenges

Despite its successes, Sweden has seen rising inequality since the 1990s:

  • Relative poverty: 6% (1990) → 16% (2020)
  • Driven by benefit reforms, integration challenges and globalisation pressures
  • Recent immigrants face a 45% poverty rate
💡 Evaluation Point

Even the strongest welfare states face pressures from global trends. The Nordic model is not immune — it requires sustained political commitment and fiscal capacity to maintain outcomes.

🇺🇸 USA: Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

How It Works

  • A refundable tax credit for low-income working families
  • Phases in with earnings, reaches a maximum, then phases out
  • Maximum credit: ~$7,400 for families with three or more children
  • Covers 25 million households at a cost of approximately $70 billion per year

Measured Impact

  • Lifts 5.6 million people (including 3 million children) above the poverty line annually
  • Increased single mother employment by 6–8 percentage points
  • Long-run: children exposed to the EITC earn ~$3,000/year more as adults; higher college enrolment rates

Limitations

  • Only helps working families — childless adults in poverty receive negligible support
  • Evidence suggests ~25% of EITC value is captured by employers through lower wage offers
  • Combined with a stagnant federal minimum wage ($7.25 since 2009), contributes to persistent in-work poverty
↔ Compare & Contrast

The EITC illustrates a tension in in-work benefit design: effective at reducing poverty, but may subsidise low-wage employers rather than requiring them to pay living wages.

Cross-Cutting Themes

What Works for Absolute Poverty

  • Economic growth (China)
  • Direct cash transfers (Brazil)
  • Universal services (Sweden)
  • Employment creation
  • Human capital investment

What Works for Relative Poverty

  • Progressive taxation (Sweden)
  • Generous transfer payments (Brazil, Sweden)
  • Wage policies — unions, minimum wages
  • Universal services (reduces private spending needs)
  • Active labour market policies
💡 Evaluation Point

Critical Insight: Policies that reduce absolute poverty do not automatically reduce relative poverty. Growth can increase inequality even as it lifts people above a fixed threshold (China). Reducing relative poverty requires deliberate redistribution beyond growth alone.

Policy Scenarios: Testing Your Analysis

These scenarios present realistic policy dilemmas. Click each option to reveal likely outcomes based on economic theory and real-world evidence. For each option, consider: What is the likely short-run effect? What are the long-run implications? What are the key trade-offs?

Scenario 1: Post-Pandemic Recovery

Context: Middle-income country recovering from a pandemic shock. GDP fell 6%; unemployment rose from 5% to 12%. Absolute poverty increased from 15% to 22%. Budget deficit is 8% of GDP.

Tension: International creditors are pressing for spending cuts. Domestic pressure is growing to support struggling households. Resources are constrained.

Option 1: Immediate Austerity

▼

End emergency transfers, cut public wages 10%, reduce unemployment benefits. Target 4% deficit within one year.

Likely Outcomes:
  • Poverty rises sharply to 27–30% as support disappears
  • Deeper recession likely (negative multiplier effect) — GDP could contract a further 2–3%
  • Deficit only falls to ~6% as the weaker economy reduces tax revenues
  • Social costs: increased crime, health deterioration, school dropout rates
  • Political instability risk
Real-World Parallels:
  • Greece 2010–15: Severe austerity deepened the recession; poverty rose from 28% to 36%
  • IMF research: Fiscal consolidation during downturns has larger negative effects (~1.5 multiplier) than previously expected
💡 Evaluation Point
Analysis: This prioritises fiscal credibility over social protection. Evidence suggests it is often counterproductive — slower growth means lower tax revenues, potentially worsening the fiscal position. Poverty increases are virtually certain in the short run. The key debate is whether short-term pain produces long-run gain, or whether it generates permanent scarring effects (especially for children).

Option 2: Maintain Support, Gradual Consolidation

▼

Continue emergency transfers for another year, begin gradual adjustment. Allow 7–8% deficit in year 1, target 5% by year 3.

Likely Outcomes:
  • Poverty stabilises at 22–23% — prevented from rising further
  • Economy recovers ~3–4% as aggregate demand is supported
  • Unemployment begins to fall; automatic stabilisers work in reverse
  • Deficit remains elevated initially but improves naturally as the economy recovers
Medium-Term (2–3 Years):
  • Poverty gradually falls to 18–19% as employment rises
  • Stronger recovery than under austerity path
  • Higher interest costs (~0.5% GDP more), but a better overall fiscal position
Real-World Parallels:
  • US 2009–12: Maintained stimulus longer than most of Europe; achieved a faster recovery
  • COVID responses: Countries with stronger support packages (Germany, Denmark) saw faster recoveries
🔍 Analysis
Analysis: The mainstream Keynesian approach — support aggregate demand during downturns and adjust during recoveries. Higher debt but a faster recovery means a better fiscal position within 3–4 years. Consistent with modern macroeconomic evidence on the size of fiscal multipliers during recessions.

Option 3: Expand Support + Active Labour Market Programmes

▼

Maintain transfers AND invest in large-scale job training, public employment, apprenticeships and wage subsidies. Finance through borrowing and progressive taxation. Deficit rises to 10%.

Year 1 Outcomes:
  • Poverty falls to 19–20% as expanded support reaches more households
  • Significant job creation — unemployment falls to 9–10%
  • Skills investment begins building future productive capacity
Years 2–3 Outcomes:
  • Workers with training secure better employment and higher wages
  • Poverty falls to 14–15% — below pre-pandemic levels
  • Productivity gains boost long-run potential growth
Risks:
  • Implementation capacity: Quality programmes are difficult to scale rapidly
  • Deadweight loss: Some subsidies go to employers who would have hired anyway
  • 10% deficit may concern financial markets and raise borrowing costs
  • Poorly designed training programmes have weak evidence bases
Real-World Parallels:
  • Sweden's ALMPs: Consistently reduce poverty and frictional unemployment; cost ~2% GDP in normal times
  • South Korea post-1997: Massive training investment helped achieve rapid recovery
  • Caveat: Success requires strong institutions and high programme quality
💡 Evaluation Point
Analysis: The most ambitious option — addresses both immediate poverty and structural employment quality. Highest upfront costs but potentially the best long-run outcomes. Success depends critically on institutional capacity to implement effectively. Risky if delivery systems are weak.

Option 4: Targeted Transformation

▼

End universal transfers. Create deeper support for specific groups: families with children, elderly, disabled. Reduce coverage from 30% to 20% of population but increase benefit levels 50%. Job-search conditionality for able-bodied adults.

Likely Outcomes:
  • Clear winners and losers: families with children better off; childless adults and informal workers worse off
  • Overall poverty stabilises at 21–22% — modest net improvement
  • Modest fiscal savings — deficit falls to ~6.5%
  • High administrative burden from means-testing
  • Take-up problems — approximately 25% of eligible households may not claim
Medium-Term Issues:
  • Poverty traps — cliff-edge benefit withdrawal can discourage earnings increases
  • Stigma effects reduce take-up among those who most need support
  • Political vulnerability — narrow programmes for the poor are often politically weak
Real-World Parallels:
  • UK Universal Credit: Targeting saved money but created hardship through design flaws (5-week wait, cliff effects)
  • US TANF: Strict conditionality reduced caseloads but did not reduce poverty — many fell through the cracks
  • Typical non-take-up rates for means-tested benefits: 20–40%
↔ Compare & Contrast
Analysis: Reflects the "targeting vs universality" debate. Concentrates resources on those deemed most needy, which appears efficient, but the real-world costs of means-testing — administrative overhead, exclusion errors, stigma, poverty traps, and political vulnerability — often erode this apparent efficiency advantage.

Scenario 2: Rising In-Work Poverty in a Developed Economy

Context: A wealthy country with 3.5% unemployment and 2% annual growth. Yet in-work poverty has risen from 8% to 13% over a decade. Median wages grew 15% but bottom wages were flat. Housing costs have risen to 40–50% of income for the bottom quintile. Gig and zero-hours contracts are growing. The minimum wage stands at 45% of the median.

Option 1: Substantial Minimum Wage Increase

▼

Raise the minimum wage from 45% to 60% of median over three years (~33% increase). Extend coverage to gig workers.

Positive Outcomes:
  • 15–20% of workers receive wage increases
  • In-work poverty falls from 13% to approximately 9–10%
  • Spillover effect: wages above the minimum also tend to rise
  • Reduced inequality: Gini falls ~0.02
  • Boost to consumer spending at lower income levels
Potential Negatives:
  • Job losses estimated at 2–5% for affected workers
  • Some employers cut hours rather than headline wages
  • May accelerate automation in labour-intensive sectors
  • Regional variation — appropriate for cities but potentially damaging in low-cost regions
The Minimum Wage Debate — Evidence:
  • Seattle $15: Some hours reduction but total earnings increased for most affected workers
  • UK National Living Wage 2016–23: £6.70 → £10.42 with minimal measurable employment effects
  • Card & Krueger (1994): Landmark finding of no employment loss following a state-level increase
  • Dube (2019) meta-analysis: Increases up to 60% of median have "small to zero" employment effects
🔍 Analysis
Analysis: Modern evidence suggests moderate increases (to 50–60% of median) have smaller employment effects than traditional competitive models predict. Key explanations: employer monopsony power, efficiency wage effects, reduced turnover costs. Very high minimums (>65% of median) or very rapid implementation are more likely to cause job losses.

Option 2: Expand In-Work Benefits + Universal Childcare

▼

Increase in-work benefits by 50% and extend eligibility. Introduce universal subsidised childcare (80% funded for low-income families). Cost: 1.5% of GDP, financed by progressive taxation.

Positive Outcomes:
  • In-work poverty falls from 13% to approximately 8–9%
  • No employment loss — in-work benefits actually increase work incentives
  • Childcare access raises female labour force participation by 3–5 percentage points
  • Long-term: quality early childcare significantly improves children's educational and health outcomes
  • Business-friendly — imposes no direct cost on employers
Potential Negatives:
  • Expensive: ~1.5% of GDP as an ongoing fiscal commitment
  • Effectively subsidises low-wage employers with public money — treats symptoms not causes
  • Childless adults excluded — single workers receive little benefit
  • Complexity: high effective marginal tax rates (60–70%) in phase-out range
  • Take-up: 20–30% of eligible households may not claim
Evidence:
  • Quebec universal childcare ($7/day, 1997): Maternal employment +13 percentage points; programme essentially paid for itself in tax revenues and reduced benefit costs
  • US EITC 1990s: Single mother employment +7 percentage points; significant poverty reduction
  • UK Tax Credits (1999–2010): Child poverty 34% → 18%; single parent employment 45% → 57%
💡 Evaluation Point
Analysis: Addresses both income gaps and employment barriers simultaneously. Strong evidence base. But raises a fundamental normative question: should the state subsidise low-wage employers rather than requiring them to pay adequate wages? Works best as part of a broader package combining wage policy with income support.

Option 3: Strengthen Worker Bargaining Power

▼

Reform labour laws to promote unionisation, sectoral collective bargaining, gig worker organising rights and worker board representation. Combine with a moderate minimum wage increase to 55% of median.

Positive Outcomes:
  • Union premium: 10–15% wage growth at the bottom
  • Compressed wage distribution — reduced inequality
  • Better non-wage working conditions: hours, scheduling, safety, benefits
  • Spillover: even non-union employers improve conditions to remain competitive
  • Addresses root causes of wage stagnation rather than symptoms
Potential Negatives:
  • Employment effects: higher labour costs may reduce jobs in competitive traded sectors
  • Insider-outsider problem: unions may protect existing workers at the expense of the unemployed
  • Slow impact — takes years for union density to increase significantly
  • Strong business opposition; risk of capital flight
  • Path dependency — very hard to build from a low base
Evidence:
  • Nordic countries: 70%+ bargaining coverage, low inequality (Gini ~0.26), high productivity
  • Germany: Co-determination associated with lower inequality and productive labour relations
  • US union decline: Fall from 35% to 10% coverage accounts for an estimated 20–30% of the observed rise in inequality
  • OECD: Coordinated bargaining associated with lower inequality without higher unemployment
💡 Evaluation Point
Analysis: A "pre-distribution" approach — changing the distribution of market incomes before redistribution occurs. Nordic evidence shows high coverage is compatible with strong economic performance. But achieving this from a low base requires: sectoral bargaining legislation, coordination between unions, business acceptance and sustained political support. Best understood as a long-term institutional reform rather than a short-run fix.

Option 4: Housing Cost Intervention

▼

Tackle the housing cost driver directly: build 100,000 social housing units per year for five years, introduce rent controls, increase housing benefit, tighten rental regulation. Finance through borrowing and a land value tax.

Positive Outcomes:
  • Housing costs fall from 40–50% to 25–30% of income — equivalent in practical terms to a large income increase
  • After-housing-cost poverty falls an estimated 4–5 percentage points
  • Permanent affordable housing stock created
  • Construction creates 200,000+ jobs
  • Stable housing improves children's educational and health outcomes
  • Affordable housing in high-wage cities enables greater labour market mobility
Challenges:
  • Major upfront cost — estimated £75 billion over 5 years, plus ongoing maintenance
  • Rent control risks: may reduce new private supply, decrease quality and create shortages over time
  • Planning, land availability and local opposition constraints
  • 3–5 year lag before completed units provide relief
  • Allocation challenges — criteria for social housing access are politically contentious
Evidence:
  • Vienna: 60% in social/subsidised housing; rents 20–30% below market; poverty rates noticeably lower than comparable Western European cities
  • Singapore: 80% in government flats, 90% homeownership, low housing cost burden
  • UK Right to Buy: Social housing stock fell from 30% to 17% without replacement — a major contributing factor to the current affordability crisis
  • Finland Housing First: Providing secure housing reduced homelessness by 35% and proved cost-effective overall
💡 Evaluation Point
Analysis: Housing costs are often overlooked but now consume 40–50% of low-income budgets (up from 20–25% in the 1980s). Addressing the structural cause of in-work poverty rather than compensating for it through benefits. Vienna and Singapore show it works at scale — but requires massive upfront investment, long time horizons and sustained political will in the face of significant homeowner opposition.

Synthesis: Which Approach is Best?

Each option addresses a different dimension of in-work poverty:

  • Minimum wage: Direct and impactful, but employment effects remain contested at very high levels
  • In-work benefits + childcare: Proven effectiveness, no job losses, but expensive and arguably subsidises low-wage employers
  • Collective bargaining: Addresses the power imbalance at root; long-term solution, but very slow to build and requires institutional foundations
  • Housing: Tackles the biggest cost driver, but very expensive and slow to deliver

Optimal policy: Most economists recommend combining multiple approaches — a moderate minimum wage, expanded in-work benefits, affordable childcare and a housing supply programme. Single-instrument solutions are insufficient given the multidimensional nature of in-work poverty.

Key Lessons Across Scenarios

What These Scenarios Teach Us

  • Context matters — policies work differently in different institutional settings
  • Trade-offs are real — every policy choice has costs and benefits
  • Time horizons differ — some policies have immediate effects, others take years
  • Implementation capacity is often the binding constraint
  • Multi-dimensional strategies consistently outperform single-instrument approaches

Evidence-Based Principles

  • Direct income support works — cash transfers are effective when adequately funded
  • Human capital investment pays off in the long run
  • Labour market institutions are central — wages and employment quality matter
  • Universal provision often outperforms targeted approaches on coverage and political durability
  • Fiscal consolidation during downturns is counterproductive — timing matters
💡 Evaluation Point
Final Reflection: Poverty is fundamentally a political economy question, not merely a technical one. Evidence shows what works, but implementation requires political will, adequate resources and sustained commitment. The "best" policy depends on values, institutions and context — good analysis requires integrating economics, politics, history and ethics.
