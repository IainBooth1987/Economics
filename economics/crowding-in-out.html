Crowding In & Crowding Out

The debate between monetarist and Keynesian economists over the effects of government spending on private sector investment.

When governments increase spending, they interact with the private sector in ways that are fiercely contested. Monetarists warn of crowding out — government intervention displacing private activity. Keynesians argue for crowding in — government spending stimulating private investment. Understanding both views is essential for evaluating fiscal policy.

The Monetarist Argument

"In the short run, expansionary fiscal policy may raise output, but in the long run it simply crowds out private investment, leaving total output unchanged."

Milton Friedman & the Monetarist critique of fiscal policy

Monetarists identify three distinct channels through which government spending displaces private sector activity. Crucially, the problem goes beyond the loanable funds market — the expansion of the state itself crowds out private enterprise.

Channel 1 — Financial

Loanable Funds & Rising Interest Rates

To finance a deficit, governments borrow in capital markets, increasing demand for loanable funds. This drives up the real interest rate. Higher borrowing costs deter private investment — firms cancel capital projects whose expected return no longer exceeds the cost of borrowing. The IS curve shifts right but the interest rate rise offsets the multiplier effect.

Channel 2 — Resource

Labour Market & Public Sector Employment

When the state expands, it competes directly with the private sector for skilled workers. Government jobs — often offering pension security and stable pay — attract workers away from private firms. This reduces the private labour supply, raises private sector wages, squeezes margins, and reduces incentives for private entrepreneurs to expand. The state "hollows out" the private talent pool.

Channel 3 — Structural

State Dependency & Enterprise Displacement

A growing public sector can crowd out private enterprise even without direct financial competition. If government provides services free at the point of use (healthcare, education, broadband), private suppliers exit those markets. This reduces competitive pressure, innovation, and allocative efficiency. Long-run potential output may fall as the entrepreneurial sector shrinks.

Channel 4 — Expectations

Ricardian Equivalence & Future Tax Burden

Robert Barro extended the argument: rational consumers anticipate that today's deficit means higher future taxes. They increase saving today to prepare for the tax bill, reducing current consumption and private investment. Government borrowing is thus offset by private saving — the fiscal multiplier approaches zero in this extreme case.

Government Allocation: The Waste Problem

At the heart of the crowding out debate is a deeper question: even if government spending does not raise interest rates, does it allocate resources as productively as the private sector would? Monetarists argue that government spending is systematically prone to waste — absorbing resources without generating commensurate output or value. This is a qualitative crowding out, not just a financial one.

Example — UK HS2

Cost Overruns & Sunk Costs

HS2 was originally budgeted at £32bn. Costs escalated to over £100bn before the northern legs were cancelled in 2023. No private investor would commit to a project with such uncontrolled cost escalation — capital markets impose discipline through the threat of loss. Government faces no such constraint: it can borrow or tax to cover overruns indefinitely, meaning capital is locked into failing projects rather than redirected.

Example — NHS IT Programme

The £12bn NPfIT Failure

The National Programme for IT in the NHS was abandoned in 2011 having spent approximately £12bn with little to show for it. A private firm facing comparable failure would have gone bankrupt or replaced its leadership within months. Public sector contracts lack the profit-and-loss discipline that forces private firms to innovate, adapt, or exit. Resources continued to flow into a failing project because no mechanism enforced accountability.

Example — Procurement

Bureaucratic Procurement & Rent-Seeking

Government procurement processes are frequently captured by suppliers who learn to navigate the tender system rather than compete on price or quality. Defence contracts illustrate this: the UK's Ajax armoured vehicle programme spent over £3.5bn yet delivered vehicles unfit for service. Contractors know that governments rarely cancel large contracts — the political cost of abandonment is too high — creating an incentive to underbid then overcharge once the contract is awarded.

Example — Labour Markets

Featherbedding & Overstaffing

Public sector employment often reflects political and social objectives rather than productive need. During the 1970s, nationalised industries in the UK employed significantly more workers per unit of output than comparable private firms. British Steel, British Rail, and British Telecom all shed hundreds of thousands of jobs rapidly upon privatisation — revealing that the public sector had been absorbing labour that generated minimal output. The wages paid crowded out private sector hiring without generating equivalent value.

Example — Fiscal Policy

Political Business Cycles & Pork-Barrel Spending

Government spending decisions are distorted by electoral incentives. Politicians increase spending before elections and in marginal constituencies regardless of economic merit — "pork-barrel" politics. The UK's post-2008 stimulus included significant spending on schemes later found to have minimal productivity impact. Private investment, by contrast, follows returns rather than votes. Capital flows where it is most productive, not where it generates most political support.

Example — Subsidies

Supporting Sunset Industries

Governments routinely subsidise declining industries to protect employment in politically sensitive regions. UK coal subsidies in the 1970s and 1980s kept open mines that were economically unviable, absorbing capital and labour that could have been redeployed into higher-value sectors. The same pattern is visible in EU agricultural subsidies, which direct capital towards low-productivity farming rather than allowing reallocation to manufacturing or services. Creative destruction — the engine of growth — is suppressed.

Private Sector Dynamism: Why Markets Allocate Better

The monetarist concern is not merely that government wastes resources — it is that those resources, if left with the private sector, would be allocated far more productively. Private markets possess mechanisms for discovery, discipline, and adaptation that the public sector structurally cannot replicate.

Mechanism — Price Signals

The Price System as Information Processor

Friedrich Hayek argued that the price system aggregates dispersed knowledge that no central planner can possess. When private investors allocate capital, they respond to price signals — rising prices indicating where scarcity exists and investment is needed; falling prices revealing where capacity is surplus. Government spending bypasses this mechanism: civil servants cannot know whether a pound spent on hospital X generates more value than the same pound in start-up Y. Prices do know, and private investors follow them.

Mechanism — Profit & Loss

Discipline Through the Threat of Failure

Private firms that misallocate resources make losses and eventually fail. This is not a flaw of markets — it is the essential feedback loop that continuously redirects capital away from low-value uses. Amazon's early logistics investments generated losses for years before proving their worth; countless competitors who made worse decisions simply closed. Government has no equivalent discipline: a failing programme generates calls for more funding, not closure. Capital is trapped rather than reallocated.

Mechanism — Innovation

Competition Drives Productivity Growth

Private firms compete for customers, and competition forces continuous innovation. The smartphone, fracking, cloud computing, and mRNA vaccines all emerged from competitive private markets where firms invested heavily in R&D because the returns accrued to the innovator. Government R&D spending, by contrast, is often directed by incumbent interests or safe political priorities. The private sector generates Schumpeterian creative destruction; the public sector tends to protect the status quo.

Mechanism — Incentives

Ownership, Equity & Skin in the Game

Private investors risk their own capital. Entrepreneurs who back bad ideas lose their wealth; those who back good ideas gain it. This alignment of incentives between effort and reward encourages rigorous due diligence and rapid adaptation when projects underperform. A civil servant allocating a public investment budget faces no personal downside from a bad decision — the incentive to be merely defensible rather than genuinely productive is structurally embedded in public allocation.

Mechanism — Adaptability

Speed of Response to Changing Conditions

Private firms can pivot rapidly when circumstances change: a retailer can shift inventory, a bank can reprice loans, a manufacturer can retool production within months. Government spending programmes are embedded in multi-year budgets, subject to legislative approval, and protected by employment law and political sensitivity. During the 2008 crisis, private banks recapitalised and restructured within years; equivalent restructuring of public spending programmes took a decade of political negotiation. Agility is a private sector advantage that crowded-out firms cannot exercise.

Mechanism — Scale & Specialisation

Deep Capital Markets & Venture Financing

Private capital markets allow specialised investors — venture capitalists, private equity, bond markets — to match capital to risk profiles that government cannot assess. A venture fund backing 20 start-ups expects 15 to fail and one to generate returns that cover the rest. This portfolio logic funds genuinely risky innovation that no government would sanction given accountability norms. When government crowds out private capital markets, it removes not just funds but the expertise, networks, and risk-tolerance that make those funds productive.

Monetarist Policy Conclusions

  • Fiscal policy is ineffective at full employment
  • Monetary policy is the superior stabilisation tool
  • Government should run balanced budgets over the cycle
  • State should be reduced to allow private sector to thrive
  • Supply-side reforms outperform demand management
  • Automatic stabilisers acceptable; discretionary spending risky
The Keynesian Argument

"The difficulty lies not in the new ideas, but in escaping from the old ones — in a recession, government must spend to compensate for the collapse of private demand."

J.M. Keynes — The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money (1936)

Keynesians argue that the monetarist critique only applies at full employment. When there is a negative output gap, idle resources mean government spending does not displace private activity — it activates it. The crowding-in effect can amplify government stimulus beyond the initial injection.

Channel 1 — Multiplier

The Keynesian Multiplier Effect

An injection of government spending sets off a chain of expenditure. Workers paid by government projects spend their incomes at local businesses, who in turn hire and invest. Each round of spending creates further income. If the marginal propensity to consume (MPC) is 0.8, the multiplier is 5: £1bn of government spending generates £5bn of GDP. This is the core Keynesian case for fiscal stimulus.

Channel 2 — Accelerator

Crowding In via the Accelerator

When government spending raises national income, firms respond to the higher demand by increasing their own investment to expand capacity. This accelerator effect means private investment rises alongside government spending — the opposite of crowding out. Public investment in infrastructure can be particularly powerful: roads, rail, and broadband reduce private sector costs and unlock investment that would not otherwise occur.

Channel 3 — Interest Rates

Liquidity Trap & Low Interest Sensitivity

In a recession, interest rates may already be near zero (the zero lower bound). Businesses are not investing because of pessimistic expectations, not because rates are too high. More investment would not occur even if rates fell further. Government borrowing therefore does not compete for scarce capital — it uses idle savings. The loanable funds crowding out mechanism simply does not operate.

Channel 4 — Complementarity

Public Goods as Private Sector Complements

Public investment can make private investment more productive rather than replacing it. A new motorway or broadband network reduces logistics costs and opens new markets for private firms. Educated workers (a product of public spending) raise private sector productivity. In this view, public and private investment are complements, not substitutes — the state enables the market.

Transmission Mechanism: Crowding-In with a Negative Output Gap

Economy below full employment (Y < Yfe)
→

↓

→

Keynesian Policy Conclusions

  • Fiscal policy is most effective in recession / at zero lower bound
  • Government should borrow to invest when private sector retreats
  • Austerity in a recession deepens and prolongs the downturn
  • Infrastructure investment has especially high fiscal multipliers
  • Employment protection sustains consumer confidence and MPC
  • The deficit should be allowed to rise automatically in downturns
Evaluation: When Does Crowding In Work Best?

Conditions Required for Effective Crowding In

Keynesian crowding in depends critically on context. It is most effective when: (1) there is a significant negative output gap with idle resources; (2) interest rates are at or near zero; (3) business confidence is low so private investment is not forthcoming; (4) the government spends on high-multiplier activities (infrastructure, transfer payments to high-MPC households); (5) the economy is closed or has a low marginal propensity to import (otherwise the multiplier leaks abroad). Critics note that even Keynesians accept the monetarist critique applies at or near full employment.
