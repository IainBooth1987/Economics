Welcome to Northbridge Mortgages

A-Level Economics / Business — market failure, asymmetric information & the principal-agent problem. Each team runs a regional mortgage branch for a fictional bank, Northbridge, across the boom years. Head Office (you, the teacher) sets the rules each round — and what your branch chooses in Round 1 comes back to find you by Round 4. Set up your teams below — names and scores save automatically in this browser.

How the game runs

Hand out the workbooks. Each round: project the Head Office brief → branches complete the worksheet task → press Reveal for what actually happened → award points → Save. This is a connected story, not four separate scenarios — Round 2's brief explicitly references what happened in Round 1, Round 3 references Rounds 1–2, and Round 4 ("The Crash") is where every branch's earlier choices catch up with them. Remind teams to keep their Round 1–3 worksheet answers in front of them for Round 4.

Debrief hook: the whole game is one long worked example of the timing distinction examiners love: choices made before a contract (which borrowers apply, which risks get sold on) are adverse selection; behaviour that changes after a contract is signed (lending recklessly because of an implicit guarantee) is moral hazard. Branches that mixed the two up in Round 4's pitch should lose marks even with a good policy idea — precision on timing is the A-Level differentiator.

Starter — Spot the Mismatch

Five real situations where one person's pay doesn't match what they're actually being trusted to do. Every one of these is a documented finding. In pairs: rank them from biggest behaviour gap to smallest — how much does the mismatch actually change what people do? Two minutes. Gut instinct only.

?
Selling your own house vs a client's
US estate agents selling their OWN home vs a client's home, holding house quality constant
Agents leave their own home on the market 9.5 days longer and sell it for 3–4% more than an equivalent client home — because a client's agent is paid the same modest commission whether the sale takes one extra week or one extra month, so rushing a client's sale costs the agent almost nothing (Levitt & Syverson, 2008, Review of Economics and Statistics)
?
Auditors paid by the company they audit
Arthur Andersen was both Enron's auditor AND a highly-paid consultant to Enron, 2001
Andersen signed off Enron's accounts for years while earning millions in consulting fees from the same client — when the fraud collapsed, Andersen itself was criminally convicted and dissolved, and the scandal directly produced the Sarbanes-Oxley Act's rules separating audit from consulting work
?
Brokers paid only on commission per sale
Mortgage brokers paid purely on volume sold, not on whether the loan is ever repaid
Widely documented in the run-up to the 2007–08 financial crisis: commission-only brokers had every incentive to approve as many mortgages as possible, regardless of the borrower's ability to repay — exactly the mechanic in today's Round 1
?
No-claims bonus on car insurance
A deliberate FIX, not a problem — insurers reward drivers who don't claim
This is the rare example built to close the gap rather than open one: by giving the driver a financial stake in their own careful behaviour, insurers reduce the moral hazard that "being insured" would otherwise create
?
Waiting staff paid tips vs a flat wage
Restaurant servers whose pay depends on the customer's discretionary tip
Tipped servers are consistently found to offer more attentive service and upsell more — a small, everyday version of the same principle: tie pay to an outcome the principal (the customer, here) actually values, and behaviour shifts toward it
02:00
Notice the one outlier: the no-claims bonus is the only card on this list designed to close the gap rather than open one — that's exactly the kind of fix Northbridge will need to find by the end of today's game. Every other card is a real, documented case of pay and trust pulling in different directions — and today, you're going to live through one from the inside.

Before you reveal: ask each branch to total up, from their own Round 1–3 answers, (a) how many risky loans they wrote, (b) whether they paid for independent ratings or used the cheap in-house ones, and (c) whether they lent aggressively in Round 3 expecting a bailout. The branches that stacked up "risky + inflated rating + aggressive lending" are about to lose the most.

NORTHBRIDGE'S ANNUAL REPORT · ONE YEAR ON

No single fix — a toolkit, used together

The branches that survived the crash best weren't the ones that picked the single "best" tool — they were the ones whose Round 4 pitch combined an incentive fix (so managers want to do the right thing) with an information fix (so Head Office and investors can actually check). A contract alone doesn't work if no one can verify what happened; a rating alone doesn't work if managers are still paid to take risks.

Teams: write one sentence each identifying which TWO tools from the table they'd combine for Northbridge, and why one alone wouldn't have stopped their own branch's Round 1–3 story.

✍ The essay this lesson was secretly building

AQA / EDEXCEL STYLE · MARKET FAILURE & REGULATION · HOMEWORK OR NEXT LESSON

"Evaluate the view that regulation, rather than incentive contracts, is the best way to address agency problems and asymmetric information in financial markets." (25 marks)

KAA from today: principal-agent problem · moral hazard (hidden action, Round 1) · adverse selection (hidden information, Round 2) · systemic moral hazard / "too big to fail" (Round 3) · signalling and screening as private-sector fixes · regulation (capital requirements, affordability rules) as a public-sector fix.

Evaluation from today: the timing distinction (before vs after the contract) · why a fix that targets only one branch's incentives (contracts) won't catch a system-wide guarantee problem · the cost of regulation (credit rationing, compliance cost) vs the cost of doing nothing (the crash itself) · the strongest answers will use Round 4's lesson: it's a toolkit, not a single answer.

How the story connected — the whole game in one timeline

Flat salary, no oversight STARTER

Effort fades when the principal can't observe the agent — the seed of every round that follows.

Sales targets & commission ROUND 1

Pay tied to volume, not loan quality — hidden action moral hazard. Whatever pay structure you chose became Northbridge's policy for every branch.

Package and sell the risk ROUND 2

Securitisation spreads Round 1's hidden risk further down the chain — adverse selection, made worse by a conflict of interest in ratings.

The implicit guarantee ROUND 3

Believing in a bailout changes behaviour after the fact — systemic moral hazard, now bank-wide rather than branch-wide.

The bill arrives 💥 THE CRASH

Every round's choices are now visible in one number — this is why economists care about agency problems: they compound.

Fix it, properly EPILOGUE

Combine an incentive fix with an information fix — a toolkit, not a single silver bullet.
