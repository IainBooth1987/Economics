What Is the Balance of Payments?

The Balance of Payments (BoP) is a complete record of all financial transactions between a country's residents and the rest of the world over a given period, usually a year or a quarter. Every transaction — buying a foreign car, a foreign investor purchasing UK gilts, a UK company opening a factory abroad, a migrant worker sending money home — is captured somewhere within it.

The BoP is divided into two main accounts, each covered fully in the next two sections: the current account (trade in goods and services, plus income flows) and the capital and financial account (flows of investment and capital). As Section 4 explains, these two accounts are mechanically linked — because every pound spent buying something from abroad has to come from somewhere, the BoP as a whole always balances to zero.

Why This Topic Matters: the balance of payments is one of AQA's four core macroeconomic objectives (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Macroeconomic Objective Conflicts guide), and is central to understanding why a booming domestic economy doesn't automatically mean a healthy external position — a country can have excellent growth, low unemployment, and low inflation, and still be steadily accumulating foreign debt to pay for it.

The Current Account — Trade, Primary & Secondary Income

The current account records the flow of money from trade in goods and services, plus international income flows, between a country and the rest of the world. It is the balance most commonly referred to in the news as simply "the trade deficit" or "the trade surplus," although it actually covers considerably more than trade alone.

Fig 1: The four components of the current account. Trade in goods and trade in services together make up the "trade balance"; primary and secondary income are added to give the full current account balance.
Trade in Goods
The value of physical, tangible exports minus imports — cars, machinery, food, oil, clothing, electronics. The UK has run a persistent, large deficit here for decades (see Section 6).
Trade in Services
Exports minus imports of intangible services — financial services, insurance, consulting, tourism, education, IT and software, transport. The UK runs a substantial surplus here, driven heavily by the City of London's financial and professional services.
The Trade Balance
Trade in Goods + Trade in Services combined. This is the figure most often quoted in day-to-day news reporting, but it is not the same as the full current account balance.
Primary Income
Cross-border flows of income earned from providing a factor of production — interest, profits, and dividends earned on foreign investments (e.g. a UK pension fund's dividends from US shares, or a foreign multinational's profits repatriated from its UK factory), plus compensation of cross-border employees.
Secondary Income
Transfers where nothing is provided in direct economic return — international aid payments, remittances sent home by migrant workers, and government transfers (e.g. contributions to international organisations).
Current Account Balance = (Exports − Imports of Goods) + (Exports − Imports of Services) + Net Primary Income + Net Secondary Income
★ REAL WORLD: China's Current Account, 2024 — A Full Worked Breakdown
China's 2024 current account surplus of $422.0 billion was made up of a $767.9 billion surplus on trade in goods (China's vast manufacturing export base), a $228.8 billion deficit on trade in services (partly reflecting Chinese tourists' spending abroad), a $131.9 billion deficit on primary income (reflecting profits earned by foreign firms operating in China being repatriated abroad), and a $14.8 billion surplus on secondary income. Adding these four components together — 767.9 − 228.8 − 131.9 + 14.8 — gives the overall $422.0 billion current account surplus: a clean, real-world illustration of exactly how the four components combine.

The Capital & Financial Account

The capital and financial account records cross-border flows of capital and ownership of assets — money moving between countries not to pay for goods or services, but to buy, sell, lend, or invest.

The Capital Account
A relatively small component recording capital transfers (e.g. debt forgiveness) and the acquisition/disposal of non-produced, non-financial assets (e.g. the sale of a patent or trademark across borders). Usually tiny relative to the financial account.
The Financial Account
The much larger component, recording cross-border flows of investment: Foreign Direct Investment (FDI — building or buying a lasting stake in a foreign business, e.g. a foreign car manufacturer opening a UK factory), portfolio investment (buying foreign shares, bonds, and gilts without seeking control), and other investment (bank loans, trade credit, and reserve assets held by the central bank).
Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)
Investment intended to establish a lasting interest and degree of control in a foreign enterprise (typically a 10%+ ownership stake) — building a new factory, acquiring a controlling stake in a foreign firm, or reinvesting profits in an existing overseas subsidiary.
Portfolio Investment
Cross-border purchases of financial assets — shares, bonds, gilts — made purely for a financial return, without seeking any operational control over the issuer.
The Key Intuition: if the current account measures money flowing in and out for goods, services, and income, the financial account measures the money flowing in and out to fund that — a country running a current account deficit must be attracting a matching inflow of foreign capital (financial account surplus) to pay for the gap, exactly as Section 4 explains in full.

Why the Balance of Payments Always Balances — Errors & Omissions

A common source of confusion: students often ask "how can the balance of payments always equal zero, when countries clearly run current account deficits and surpluses?" The answer lies in the accounting relationship between the two accounts.

Current Account Balance + Capital & Financial Account Balance + Net Errors & Omissions = 0

Why a Current Account Deficit Means a Financial Account Surplus

If a country's residents spend more buying foreign goods, services, and assets than foreigners spend buying that country's goods, services, and assets (a current account deficit), the difference has to be paid for somehow. In practice, this happens because foreigners are willing to sell the deficit country their own currency's assets in return — foreign investors buy that country's gilts, shares, property, and businesses, or foreign banks lend to it. This inflow of foreign capital is recorded as a financial account surplus, and by construction it must exactly offset the current account deficit (net of errors and omissions).

A country imports more than it exports (current account moves into deficit) → residents need foreign currency to pay for those imports, so they supply their own currency in the FOREX market and demand foreign currency → simultaneously, foreign residents, businesses, and governments must be willing to acquire assets denominated in the deficit country's currency (its gilts, shares, property, bank deposits) to the same value → this capital inflow is recorded as a financial account surplus → current account deficit and financial account surplus are two sides of the same set of transactions, and must net to (approximately) zero.
Net Errors & Omissions
A balancing/residual item included specifically because real-world data collection is imperfect — trade invoices, investment flows, and currency movements are recorded by different agencies using different methods and timings, and small measurement discrepancies are inevitable. Errors and omissions simply "plugs the gap" so that the accounts balance exactly to zero as they must in theory, without implying anything is economically wrong.

Worked Example — A Hypothetical Country's Full Balance of Payments

Suppose a hypothetical country, "Ruritania," records the following flows over a year, in £ billions:

AccountComponent£bn
Current AccountTrade in goods−40.0
Trade in services+15.0
Primary income−5.0
Secondary income−2.0
Current Account Balance−32.0
Capital & Financial AccountCapital account (transfers)+0.5
Financial account: FDI+10.0
Financial account: portfolio + other investment+23.0
Capital & Financial Account Balance+33.5
Current Account (−32.0) + Capital & Financial Account (+33.5) = +1.5

In theory, these two figures should sum to exactly zero — the current account deficit should be exactly financed by the capital and financial account surplus. In practice, Ruritania's statisticians have recorded a discrepancy of +£1.5bn, most likely because some cross-border transactions (perhaps informal remittances, under-reported trade invoicing, or timing differences between when a transaction is recorded on each side of the border) were not captured identically by both sets of data. To force the accounts to balance to zero exactly, as the accounting identity requires, a Net Errors & Omissions entry of −£1.5bn is added:

−32.0 + 33.5 + (−1.5) = 0
Reading This Correctly: the −£1.5bn Errors & Omissions figure does not mean £1.5bn has vanished from the economy, or that anything sinister has occurred — it simply means the true, unmeasured current account deficit and financial account surplus were probably slightly different from the recorded £32.0bn and £33.5bn (most likely somewhat smaller, since the recorded financial account surplus slightly overstates what was needed to fund the recorded deficit). In large, complex modern economies with millions of daily cross-border transactions, an error and omissions term of this size is entirely normal and expected — persistent, unusually large errors and omissions figures over many years are what would actually raise a statistician's eyebrow, since they can suggest large-scale unrecorded capital flight or mismeasured trade.
★ REAL WORLD: China's Mirror-Image Accounts, 2024
China's $422.0 billion current account surplus in 2024 was matched by a capital and financial account deficit of $434 billion (including net errors and omissions) — almost exactly offsetting, as the accounting identity requires. In China's case, this reflects the fact that a large surplus economy is a net exporter of capital: rather than needing to attract foreign investment to fund a deficit, it is China's own residents, firms, and state investment vehicles acquiring foreign assets (buying US Treasury bonds, investing abroad, building foreign reserves) — the mirror image of a deficit country like the UK, which must attract foreign capital inflows to fund its current account deficit.

Countries With Current Account Surpluses — How & What It Means

A number of major economies have run large, persistent current account surpluses for decades, typically achieved through a deliberate combination of policy choices and structural characteristics rather than accident.

CountryHow the Surplus Was AchievedWhat It Has Meant
Germany (5.8% of GDP, 2024 — largest in the G7)Decades of sustained investment in globally competitive, export-oriented advanced manufacturing (the "Mittelstand" of mid-sized exporters), a strong vocational training system feeding skilled labour into industry, and historically high domestic savings rates relative to investment.Germany has accumulated enormous net foreign assets and become a major creditor nation, but has faced criticism (including from the IMF and the US) for relying on external demand rather than boosting its own domestic consumption and investment, and for contributing to persistent imbalances within the Eurozone (see the BIZ-OMICS Exchange Rates guide's currency union section).
China ($422bn surplus, 2024; a record $735bn in 2025)An export-oriented industrialisation strategy, historically a managed/undervalued exchange rate to support export competitiveness, high domestic savings rates, and — more recently — a persistent gap between rapidly growing manufacturing capacity and weaker domestic consumption demand.China has built vast foreign currency reserves and become the world's largest holder of US Treasury bonds, giving it significant financial leverage internationally, but its reliance on export-led growth leaves it exposed to trade tensions with major partners (see Section 7) and has fuelled persistent complaints of global "imbalances."
Norway (large, oil-driven surplus)North Sea oil and gas exports generate a large, resource-based trade surplus. Crucially, Norway has systematically saved this windfall via its sovereign wealth fund (the Government Pension Fund Global) rather than spending it all on current consumption.Norway avoided the "resource curse" seen in some oil-exporting economies by investing surplus revenue abroad for future generations, insulating domestic demand from the volatility of oil prices and building one of the largest sovereign wealth funds in the world.

Dumping — An Artificial Route to a Surplus, and a Source of Friction

Not every current account surplus reflects genuine competitiveness of the kind built by Germany's manufacturing base or South Korea's decades of supply-side investment (Section 12). Dumping — selling exports into a foreign market at a price below their cost of production, or below the price charged in the home market — is a more controversial route to boosting exports (and therefore the trade and current account balance), often made possible by state subsidies, cheap state-directed credit, or a firm's willingness to sustain losses in order to gain market share and eliminate foreign competitors.

A country's firms produce far more of a good (e.g. steel or solar panels) than domestic demand can absorb, often encouraged by state subsidies or cheap credit → rather than cutting output, firms export the surplus at prices below cost or below their home-market price → this artificially boosts the exporting country's trade and current account balance, since exports rise regardless of genuine underlying competitiveness → in the importing country, domestic producers of the same good cannot profitably compete with prices that don't reflect real production costs, and may be forced to cut output, lay off workers, or exit the market entirely → the importing country's trade balance in that sector worsens, and its own industrial capacity is damaged — creating exactly the kind of trade imbalance and political friction explored in Section 7.
Why This Differs From "Fair" Competitiveness: a surplus built on dumping is fundamentally different from one built on genuine productivity, innovation, or skills advantages (as in Sections 5's Germany and South Korea examples) — it relies on prices that do not reflect real economic costs, and is widely judged unsustainable and unfair by the importing countries affected, which is precisely why the World Trade Organization (WTO) permits members to impose anti-dumping duties — tariffs specifically calculated to offset the gap between the dumped export price and the good's "normal value" — as a legitimate, rules-based response, distinct from ordinary protectionism.
★ REAL WORLD: Chinese Steel Overcapacity & Anti-Dumping Duties
Following a period of massive state-backed expansion in Chinese steel production capacity — which came to substantially exceed both Chinese and global demand — large volumes of Chinese steel were exported at prices well below those charged by European and American producers. Both the EU and US imposed anti-dumping duties on a range of Chinese steel products through the 2010s and 2020s in response, arguing that the low prices reflected state subsidy and overcapacity rather than genuine efficiency, and that they were causing serious harm to domestic steel industries and jobs. Similar anti-dumping disputes have recurred over Chinese solar panels, illustrating how dumping-driven surpluses in specific sectors can generate exactly the kind of sustained trade friction, tariff retaliation, and anti-globalisation political pressure explored in Section 7 — even between countries with an otherwise broadly cooperative trading relationship.

The UK's Balance of Payments History

The UK provides one of the clearest, longest-running real-world case studies of a persistent, structural current account deficit among major developed economies.

★ REAL WORLD: The UK's Persistent Deficit Since the Mid-1980s
The UK has run a current account deficit in almost every year since the mid-1980s, standing at 2.2% of GDP in 2024 and 2.4% in 2025. This is driven by a substantial and long-standing trade in goods deficit (7.3% of GDP in 2024) — a direct legacy of the deindustrialisation that accelerated from the early 1980s, as the UK's manufacturing export base shrank and the country became a persistent net importer of manufactured goods (cars, electronics, machinery), only partly offset by a trade in services surplus (6.4% of GDP), led overwhelmingly by financial and business services centred on the City of London.

What This Has Meant for the UK: the persistent deficit means the UK has had to attract a continuous, large inflow of foreign capital (a financial account surplus — see Section 4) to fund it, historically through a combination of foreign direct investment, foreign purchases of UK gilts and shares, and inflows into London's property and financial markets. This has left the UK with a large negative net international investment position (it owes more to the rest of the world than it owns abroad) and makes the UK's currency and asset markets more sensitive to shifts in foreign investor sentiment than a more balanced economy would be — a vulnerability highlighted by the market reaction to the September 2022 mini-budget (see the BIZ-OMICS Fiscal Policy guide), when a sudden loss of investor confidence contributed to a sharp fall in the pound and a spike in gilt yields.

The USA, Trump & the Trade Imbalance — Anti-Globalisation

The United States has run the largest current account deficit in the G7 for decades (4.0% of GDP in 2024), and under President Trump this deficit was elevated to the status of a declared national economic emergency — with consequences that have reshaped global trade policy and fuelled a broader anti-globalisation political movement.

1
"Liberation Day" — 2 April 2025

President Trump signed Executive Order 14257, declaring a national emergency over the United States' "large and persistent" trade deficit and invoking the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) to impose sweeping tariffs — a baseline 10% on virtually all imports from 5 April, with higher country-specific "reciprocal" rates from 9 April, calculated by dividing each country's bilateral goods trade deficit with the US by the value of US imports from that country.

2
Tariffs spike to century-high levels

The effective US tariff rate — which had sat around 2% for years — spiked to a peak of roughly 21–22.5% in the days following Liberation Day, the highest level since the 1930s Smoot-Hawley era. Tariffs on China rose as high as 145% at one point in April 2025, prompting Chinese retaliation.

3
Market and economic reaction

Financial markets fell sharply in the days following the announcement, prompting a 90-day pause on the higher country-specific rates (though the 10% baseline remained). US manufacturing employment fell by around 89,000 jobs over the following ten months, even as the administration pointed to the tariffs as a tool to revive domestic industry.

4
Bilateral deals and legal challenges

Through 2025, the administration negotiated a series of bilateral "Agreements on Reciprocal Trade" with partners including the UK, EU, Japan, and South Korea, typically securing tariff rates below the original Liberation Day levels in exchange for market access and investment commitments. Separately, US courts — including the Supreme Court — ruled that the administration had exceeded its legal authority under IEEPA, forcing it to seek alternative legal bases to maintain the tariff regime.

5
The trade deficit persisted regardless

Despite the scale of the tariffs, China ended 2025 with a record $1.2 trillion overall trade surplus, having rerouted exports to other markets — illustrating how difficult it is for tariffs alone to eliminate a large, structurally embedded trade imbalance between two economies with very different savings and investment patterns.

Linking to Anti-Globalisation: the Trump tariff agenda did not emerge in isolation — it reflected, and further energised, a broader political movement across many developed economies pushing back against decades of trade liberalisation and globalisation. Supporters argue that globalisation, while raising aggregate GDP, allowed manufacturing jobs to migrate to lower-cost economies (particularly China after its 2001 WTO accession), hollowing out industrial communities and contributing to wage stagnation for manufacturing workers even as consumers benefited from cheaper imported goods — a distributional conflict directly connected to the growth-vs-inequality tension explored in the BIZ-OMICS Macroeconomic Objective Conflicts guide. Critics of the tariff response counter that tariffs raise costs for domestic consumers and firms reliant on imported inputs (evidenced by the 2.8% rise in US food prices following the 2025 tariffs), and that the underlying trade deficit is driven more by the US's low domestic savings rate relative to investment than by "unfair" foreign trade practices — meaning tariffs address a symptom rather than the root macroeconomic cause.

Benefits & Drawbacks of a Current Account Deficit

✓ Benefits of a Current Account Deficit

Higher living standards in the short run

A deficit means a country is consuming more goods and services than it produces — residents enjoy access to a wider range of cheaper imported goods than domestic production alone could provide.

Access to foreign capital and investment

The financial account inflows that fund a deficit can bring valuable foreign direct investment, technology transfer, and expertise into the domestic economy, rather than the country needing to rely purely on domestic savings to fund investment.

Can reflect strong domestic demand

A deficit driven by strong consumer and business confidence and high investment can be a by-product of a genuinely healthy, growing economy (see Conflict 4 in the BIZ-OMICS Macroeconomic Objective Conflicts guide) rather than a sign of weakness in itself.

May make very little difference if modest and well-financed

A small, easily-financed deficit in a country with a credible currency and deep, liquid financial markets (as the UK and US have enjoyed for decades) may simply reflect the ordinary workings of global capital markets rather than signal any genuine problem — many economists argue the size of a deficit matters far less than its financing and sustainability.

✗ Drawbacks of a Current Account Deficit

Dependence on foreign capital inflows

A persistent deficit must be continuously financed by attracting foreign capital; if investor sentiment shifts (e.g. due to a loss of confidence in economic management), inflows can dry up or reverse suddenly.

Currency and financial instability risk

A sudden loss of confidence can trigger a sharp currency depreciation and a spike in borrowing costs, as seen in the UK's September 2022 mini-budget episode, forcing a disruptive, unplanned correction.

Rising external debt

A persistent deficit accumulates into a growing negative net international investment position — the country owes progressively more to the rest of the world, with future income needed to service that debt.

Can signal deeper competitiveness problems

A large, persistent deficit driven by a shrinking export base and deindustrialisation (as in the UK's case) may reflect a genuine, structural loss of international competitiveness rather than simply strong domestic demand.

Why "Bad" Depends Heavily on Context: the honest answer to whether a current account deficit is a problem depends critically on why it exists and how it is financed — precisely the cyclical/structural distinction explored next in Section 9, and the central question tackled in full in Section 13's evaluation.

Cyclical vs Structural Current Account Deficits

Cyclical Current Account Deficit
A deficit that widens and narrows with the domestic economic cycle — worsening during a boom (as strong domestic demand pulls in more imports) and improving automatically during a slowdown (as weaker demand reduces import spending), without any change in the economy's underlying competitiveness. This component should self-correct as the cycle turns, requiring no policy action.
Structural Current Account Deficit
A deficit that persists even when the economy is operating at a normal point in its cycle, reflecting a genuine, long-run mismatch between the economy's productive/export capacity and its appetite for imports — typically rooted in deindustrialisation, weak investment, or a persistently uncompetitive exchange rate. A structural deficit will not correct itself through the economic cycle alone and requires deliberate policy action (Sections 11–12) to address.
Why the Distinction Matters: exactly as with the cyclical/structural distinction for budget deficits (see the BIZ-OMICS Fiscal Policy guide), policymakers must correctly diagnose which type of current account deficit they are facing before reaching for a policy response. Treating a purely cyclical deficit (which will narrow naturally as the economy cools) as if it were structural risks unnecessary and premature intervention (e.g. deflationary policies that needlessly damage growth); but treating a genuinely structural deficit as merely cyclical — assuming it will resolve itself — risks allowing a real competitiveness problem to persist and compound indefinitely, exactly as has occurred with the UK's deficit since the 1980s (Section 6).

Benefits & Risks of a Current Account Surplus

✓ Benefits of a Current Account Surplus

Net creditor status

A surplus country accumulates foreign assets over time (as in Norway's sovereign wealth fund or China's foreign reserves), building a stock of wealth and financial leverage that can be drawn on in future.

Currency and financial stability

A surplus country is not dependent on attracting foreign capital inflows to fund its external position, insulating it from the kind of sudden confidence-driven crisis that can hit deficit countries.

Reflects strong export competitiveness

A sustained surplus often reflects a genuinely strong, competitive export sector (as with German manufacturing) — a sign of underlying economic strength rather than weakness.

✗ Risks of a Current Account Surplus

Excessive reliance on external demand

A very large surplus can indicate an economy relying too heavily on foreign demand rather than developing its own domestic consumption and investment, leaving it exposed if trading partners reduce demand or impose trade barriers (see Section 7).

Contributes to global imbalances

Persistent, very large surpluses in some countries mechanically require persistent deficits elsewhere (since the world's current accounts must sum to roughly zero) — critics argue Germany's large surplus, for example, has compounded the difficulty other Eurozone members face in achieving external balance (see the BIZ-OMICS Exchange Rates guide's currency union section).

Trade friction and political risk

Very large bilateral surpluses (e.g. China's surplus with the US) can trigger protectionist retaliation from trading partners, as seen directly in Section 7 — potentially disrupting the very trade relationships the surplus depends on.

Foregone domestic consumption

A surplus, by definition, means a country is producing more than it consumes — in extreme or prolonged cases (as some argue of China), this can mean households are foregoing consumption and living standards that a more domestically-balanced growth model could otherwise support.

Correcting a Current Account Deficit

Policymakers have two broad families of tools for correcting a persistent current account deficit, each working through a different mechanism. But before reaching for any deliberate policy tool, it is essential to understand that the exchange rate regime a country operates under fundamentally changes how — and how automatically — a deficit gets corrected at all.

The Role of the Exchange Rate Regime

As covered in full in the BIZ-OMICS Exchange Rates guide, a floating exchange rate offers a current account deficit a genuine automatic adjustment mechanism that a fixed rate does not.

A country runs a current account deficit → this means more of its currency is being supplied (to buy foreign currency to pay for imports) than is being demanded (by foreigners buying its exports) → under a floating exchange rate, this excess supply causes the currency to depreciate automatically, with no policy action required → the depreciation makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive → assuming the Marshall-Lerner condition holds, export volumes rise and import volumes fall over time (after the J-curve) → the current account deficit narrows, entirely through the ordinary working of the FOREX market.

Floating Exchange Rate — Automatic Correction

Because the currency is free to depreciate in response to a deficit, a floating regime provides a built-in, self-correcting mechanism that requires no deliberate government or central bank intervention — one of the strongest arguments for floating rates covered in the BIZ-OMICS Exchange Rates guide. This is a key reason economists are often more relaxed about a deficit in a country with a freely floating, credible currency (such as the UK or US) than in a country whose exchange rate cannot adjust.

Fixed Exchange Rate — No Automatic Correction

Under a fixed exchange rate, the rate cannot adjust automatically to a deficit — the government or central bank must either defend the fixed rate by running down foreign currency reserves (unsustainable indefinitely), deliberately devalue the currency (a discrete policy choice, not an automatic market process — see the BIZ-OMICS Exchange Rates guide's devaluation section), or force an "internal devaluation" through painful domestic wage and price deflation instead. A structural deficit under a fixed exchange rate can therefore persist and compound for far longer than under a floating regime, precisely because the normal market adjustment channel has been switched off.

Why This Matters for the UK Specifically: the UK's persistent post-1980s current account deficit (Section 6) has occurred entirely under a floating pound — meaning the automatic depreciation mechanism described above has, in principle, been operating throughout. The fact that the deficit has nonetheless persisted for decades is itself informative: it suggests the UK's underlying loss of export competitiveness (deindustrialisation, a shrinking manufacturing base) has been severe and structural enough that even a floating, adjusting exchange rate has not been sufficient to fully correct it — reinforcing why supply-side policy (Section 12), not just relying on the exchange rate, is often argued to be necessary for a genuine, lasting correction.
Expenditure-Switching Policies
Policies designed to switch spending away from imports and towards domestically-produced goods, without necessarily reducing total spending. Key tools: devaluation/depreciation (making exports cheaper and imports more expensive — see the BIZ-OMICS Exchange Rates guide's devaluation section and the Marshall-Lerner condition) and protectionism (tariffs, quotas, or other trade barriers directly restricting imports, as seen in Section 7's Trump tariffs).
Expenditure-Reducing Policies
Contractionary fiscal or monetary policy (higher taxes, lower government spending, higher interest rates) that reduces total domestic spending (AD) across the board — since imports are a function of domestic income and spending, reducing AD directly reduces import spending, narrowing the deficit as a side effect of demand management rather than through any change in relative prices.

Issues With Expenditure-Switching

Devaluation's Marshall-Lerner & J-curve problems

As covered in full in the BIZ-OMICS Exchange Rates guide, a devaluation only improves the trade balance if the Marshall-Lerner condition holds, and even then typically worsens the balance in the short run (the J-curve) before improving it — and it directly raises import prices, risking cost-push inflation.

Protectionism's retaliation risk

Tariffs and quotas invite retaliatory measures from trading partners (exactly as seen in the US-China tariff exchanges of 2025), potentially triggering a damaging trade war that leaves all parties worse off, and raise costs for domestic firms and consumers reliant on imported inputs.

Issues With Expenditure-Reducing Policies

Directly conflicts with growth and employment

Contractionary policy narrows the deficit precisely by weakening domestic demand — reducing growth and raising unemployment, a direct real-world illustration of the growth-vs-balance-of-payments conflict explored in the BIZ-OMICS Macroeconomic Objective Conflicts guide.

A blunt, economy-wide tool

Unlike expenditure-switching, which targets the trade balance directly, expenditure-reducing policy squeezes the entire economy to achieve a narrower deficit — a considerable price to pay if the underlying problem is genuinely structural (Section 9) rather than simply excess demand.

Supply-Side Policy & International Competitiveness

Because a structural current account deficit reflects a genuine, long-run loss of competitiveness, the most durable correction is supply-side policy that directly rebuilds a country's ability to produce and export competitively — rather than the blunter, more disruptive expenditure-switching and expenditure-reducing tools of Section 11.

Investment in R&D & Non-Price Competitiveness
Raises the quality, innovation, and branding of exports, allowing firms to compete on more than just price — reducing the need for currency depreciation to stay competitive.
Education & Skills Investment
Raises labour productivity, lowering unit labour costs and improving the competitiveness of domestically-produced goods relative to imports over time.
Infrastructure Investment
Improves ports, digital connectivity, and logistics, lowering the cost of getting domestically-produced goods to foreign markets.
Targeted Industrial Strategy
Deliberate state coordination — subsidised credit, tax incentives, and backing for strategically chosen export industries — to build competitiveness in sectors a purely free market might under-invest in from a low starting base.
★ REAL WORLD: South Korea's Export-Led Industrialisation
From the 1960s, South Korea's government pursued a deliberately interventionist supply-side strategy to build export competitiveness from a position of severe underdevelopment: directing subsidised credit and tax incentives towards targeted export industries (starting with textiles, then progressing to shipbuilding, steel, electronics, and automobiles), backing large conglomerates (chaebols such as Samsung and Hyundai) as national champions, and investing heavily in the ports, education, and infrastructure needed to support export growth — combined with a competitive exchange rate policy. This transformed South Korea from one of the poorest countries in the world into a major current-account-surplus exporting economy within a few decades, illustrating how sustained, targeted supply-side investment — rather than currency devaluation or demand suppression alone — can durably correct a country's international competitiveness and balance of payments position (see also the BIZ-OMICS Unemployment and Fiscal Policy guides, which cover this example from the employment and growth angles respectively).
Why Supply-Side Correction Is Superior in the Long Run: unlike devaluation (which can be undone by inflation eroding the competitive gain) or contractionary demand policy (which sacrifices growth and employment to narrow the deficit), successful supply-side policy raises genuine international competitiveness — meaning the current account can improve alongside growth and employment, rather than at their expense. The trade-off is time: supply-side reforms of this kind typically take years or decades to show results, exactly as the BIZ-OMICS Macroeconomic Objective Conflicts guide notes of supply-side policy in general.

Is a Current Account Deficit Always Bad? — Evaluation

This is the single most common essay-style question on this topic, and a strong answer resists giving a simple yes/no answer.

The Case That It Can Be Genuinely Concerning

A large, structural (not merely cyclical) deficit, driven by a genuinely uncompetitive, shrinking export base, financed increasingly by short-term or "hot money" capital inflows rather than stable long-term FDI, in a country whose currency lacks the deep credibility of a reserve currency, is a real warning sign — it signals accumulating external debt and vulnerability to a sudden, disruptive loss of investor confidence.

The Case That It May Make Little Difference

A modest deficit that is cyclical rather than structural, financed by stable long-term FDI rather than volatile short-term flows, in a large, credible economy with deep and liquid financial markets (as the UK and US have both enjoyed for decades despite persistent deficits) may simply reflect the ordinary, sustainable functioning of global capital markets — attracting foreign investment because the economy is an attractive place to invest, not because it is in crisis.

The Balanced Conclusion: the size of a current account deficit alone tells you relatively little — the crucial questions are (1) is it cyclical or structural? (2) how is it being financed (stable FDI vs volatile short-term flows)? (3) is the country's currency and government credible enough to keep attracting that financing indefinitely? A large German-style surplus is not automatically "better" than a UK-style deficit either — as Section 10 shows, sustained surpluses carry their own risks and criticisms. The strongest essays reach a context-dependent judgement rather than treating "deficit = bad, surplus = good" as a fixed rule.
Evaluation Checklist for 25-Mark Questions: 1. Define the current account and distinguish it clearly from the trade balance. 2. Establish whether the deficit/surplus in question is cyclical or structural. 3. Consider how it is being financed. 4. Weigh genuine risks against the case that it may be sustainable or even benign. 5. Bring in a specific real-world example (UK, Germany, US, China, South Korea). 6. Reach a final, context-specific judgement rather than a blanket rule.

Exam Technique & Model Chains of Reasoning

The Golden Chain: How a Current Account Deficit Is Financed

A country's residents spend more on imports of goods, services, and foreign assets than foreigners spend on that country's goods, services, and assets → the current account moves into deficit → the shortfall must be paid for using foreign currency → residents supply their own currency and demand foreign currency in the FOREX market → for this to balance, foreign investors must be willing to acquire an equivalent value of assets denominated in the deficit country's currency (gilts, shares, property, bank deposits) → this capital inflow is recorded as a financial account surplus → the current account deficit and financial account surplus offset one another (net of errors and omissions), and the balance of payments as a whole sums to zero.

The Golden Chain: Correcting a Deficit via Devaluation (Expenditure-Switching)

Government judges the current account deficit to be structural and unsustainable → the currency is devalued (or allowed to depreciate) → exports become cheaper in foreign currency terms and imports more expensive in domestic currency terms → in the short run, volumes barely respond (contracts already signed, consumers slow to switch), so the trade balance may briefly worsen (the J-curve) → over 12–18 months, assuming the Marshall-Lerner condition holds, export volumes rise and import volumes fall → the trade balance improves, narrowing the current account deficit — though imported inflation and the risk of retaliatory devaluation are genuine costs of this approach.

The Golden Chain: Correcting a Deficit via Supply-Side Policy

Government identifies a structural loss of export competitiveness as the root cause of a persistent current account deficit → supply-side policy is introduced — investment in R&D, education and skills, infrastructure, and/or targeted industrial strategy for key export sectors → over a period of years, the productivity, quality, and non-price competitiveness of domestically-produced goods improves → export volumes rise and import substitution occurs without needing a currency devaluation or a contraction in domestic demand → the current account deficit narrows durably, alongside — rather than at the expense of — continued growth and employment.

Key Exam Tips

1

Always distinguish the current account from the trade balance precisely — the trade balance (goods + services) is only part of the current account, which also includes primary and secondary income.

2

Never treat "deficit = bad, surplus = good" as a fixed rule — always establish whether the imbalance is cyclical or structural, and how it is being financed, before reaching a judgement.

3

Distinguish expenditure-switching from expenditure-reducing policy precisely: switching changes relative prices/availability (devaluation, protectionism); reducing cuts total domestic spending (contractionary fiscal/monetary policy).

4

Use the Marshall-Lerner condition and J-curve whenever discussing devaluation as a correction tool — never assume the effect on the trade balance is immediate or guaranteed.

5

Bring in real-world context for maximum marks: Germany's 5.8% surplus vs the UK's 2.2–2.4% deficit, China's $422bn–$735bn surplus and its 2024 composition breakdown, Norway's sovereign wealth fund, the 2025 "Liberation Day" tariffs, and South Korea's export-led industrialisation are all precise, high-value examples.

6

Evaluate — never just describe. For 25-mark questions on whether a deficit is "always bad," structure your answer around the cyclical/structural distinction and the financing question, and reach a final, context-specific judgement.
