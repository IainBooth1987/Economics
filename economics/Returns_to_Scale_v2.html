Returns to Scale & Diminishing Returns

A complete guide to short-run and long-run production concepts

Increasing Returns to Scale (IRS)

Definition: Increasing returns to scale occurs when a proportionate increase in all inputs leads to a more than proportionate increase in output. It is a long-run phenomenon — all factors of production are variable.

The Three Types of Returns to Scale

When all inputs are scaled proportionately in the long run, there are three possible outcomes:

IRS: Double all inputs → output more than doubles (e.g. × 2 inputs → × 2.5 output)

CRS: Double all inputs → output exactly doubles (e.g. × 2 inputs → × 2 output)

DRS: Double all inputs → output less than doubles (e.g. × 2 inputs → × 1.7 output)
Common Misconception — Productivity vs Returns to Scale:
Productivity = output per unit of input. It can rise through training, technology or better management without changing scale.
Returns to Scale = what happens when you proportionately increase all inputs simultaneously. Think: working smarter (productivity) vs getting bigger (returns to scale).

Why Does IRS Occur?

1. Specialisation and Division of Labour

As firms grow, they can employ specialists for each task. Workers become more skilled through repetition, reduce time lost switching activities, and increase output per hour. Adam Smith's pin factory remains the classic illustration.

2. Technical Economies (Indivisibilities)

Some capital equipment only becomes economically viable at large scale — it cannot be bought in fractions. A blast furnace, automated assembly line, or server farm must be built at a certain minimum size. Large firms can justify this investment and spread its cost across many more units of output.

Example: Pharmaceutical Manufacturing

A drug manufacturing facility may cost hundreds of millions to build. A firm producing 10 million tablets spreads that fixed cost far more effectively than one producing 1 million. IRS allows average costs to fall dramatically as scale increases.

3. Purchasing Economies

Large firms buying inputs in bulk negotiate lower unit prices from suppliers. This reduces the cost per unit of output, contributing to falling average costs.

4. Network Effects

In digital industries especially, the value of a product increases as more users join. Each new user adds to the network at near-zero marginal cost while increasing the product's value for all existing users.

Example: Payment Networks

Visa and Mastercard become more valuable as more merchants and cardholders join. The infrastructure cost grows slowly, but the utility of the network grows rapidly — a powerful source of IRS.

IRS and the LRAC Curve

IRS is the production-side concept that directly causes economies of scale on the cost side. When a firm achieves IRS, its long-run average cost (LRAC) falls as output expands — the firm moves along the downward-sloping portion of its LRAC curve.

The link: IRS (production) → Economies of Scale (cost) → Falling LRAC

IRS and Market Structure

Where IRS is persistent and significant, industries tend toward concentration:

  • Natural Monopoly: IRS across the entire range of demand means one firm can supply at lower cost than two — utilities (water, electricity grids) are the classic case.
  • Oligopoly: Significant but not unlimited IRS leads to a small number of large firms dominating (automotive, aerospace, semiconductor fabrication).
  • Barriers to Entry: New entrants face much higher average costs than incumbents unless they can immediately achieve large-scale production — requiring huge capital and carrying substantial risk.

Example: Commercial Aircraft (Airbus & Boeing)

Developing a new aircraft model costs tens of billions. These R&D and tooling costs must be spread over hundreds of aircraft to be viable. This IRS dynamic is why only two manufacturers can compete globally in wide-body aircraft — and why new entrants have failed to break through for decades.

Cobb-Douglas Production Function (Advanced)

Q = A × Lα × Kβ

Where Q = output, L = labour, K = capital, A = total factor productivity, α and β = output elasticities. The sum α + β determines returns to scale:

  • α + β > 1 → Increasing Returns to Scale
  • α + β = 1 → Constant Returns to Scale
  • α + β < 1 → Decreasing Returns to Scale

Decreasing Returns to Scale (DRS)

Definition: Decreasing returns to scale occurs when a proportionate increase in all inputs leads to a less than proportionate increase in output. Like all returns to scale concepts, it is a long-run phenomenon — all factors of production are variable.
Critical Distinction — DRS vs Diminishing Returns:
These are two of the most frequently confused concepts in production theory.

Decreasing Returns to Scale = long run; all inputs increase proportionately; output rises by a smaller proportion.
Diminishing (Marginal) Returns = short run; at least one input is fixed; adding more of a variable input eventually yields smaller and smaller additions to output.

They can both be present, but they are conceptually independent. A firm can experience diminishing returns to labour in the short run while having constant returns to scale in the long run, and vice versa.

Why Does DRS Occur?

Decreasing returns to scale arise when the management and coordination problems of large organisations outweigh the efficiency gains from size. Key causes include:

1. Management and Coordination Difficulties

As organisations grow very large, coordinating activities across divisions, regions, and layers of management becomes increasingly costly and error-prone. Communication slows, decisions take longer, and bureaucratic inefficiency grows faster than output.

Example: Large Bureaucracies

A government agency or very large corporation may find that doubling its staff and resources does not double its output — because an increasing proportion of time is spent on internal coordination, reporting, and compliance rather than productive activity.

2. Principal-Agent Problems at Scale

As firms expand, owners (principals) become increasingly unable to monitor the behaviour of managers and workers (agents). This misalignment of incentives reduces productivity at the margin — effort and accountability diminish as organisations grow.

3. Resource Constraints

As a firm expands very rapidly, it may face constraints in acquiring suitably skilled labour, specialist inputs, or managerial talent. Competing internally for limited resources creates inefficiency and raises costs per unit.

4. Diminishing Marginal Returns to Management

Management itself is a factor of production subject to diminishing returns in a sense — adding more managers to coordinate a process eventually adds less value per manager, pushing up average costs and contributing to DRS.

DRS and the LRAC Curve

DRS is the production-side concept that causes diseconomies of scale on the cost side. When a firm experiences DRS, its long-run average cost (LRAC) rises as output expands — the firm moves along the upward-sloping portion of its LRAC curve.

The link: DRS (production) → Diseconomies of Scale (cost) → Rising LRAC

The U-Shaped LRAC Curve: Combining All Three

In practice, most firms experience all three types of returns to scale at different output levels, producing the characteristic U-shaped long-run average cost curve:

Low output → IRS → Falling LRAC
Optimal scale → CRS → Flat LRAC (Minimum Efficient Scale)
Very large scale → DRS → Rising LRAC
Minimum Efficient Scale (MES): The lowest level of output at which the firm fully exploits all available economies of scale. At MES, the LRAC curve reaches its minimum. Firms producing below MES face a cost disadvantage relative to larger competitors.

Example: Retail Banking

A small bank experiences IRS as it grows — spreading IT and branch infrastructure costs over more customers reduces average cost. At a certain size (MES), further growth yields CRS. Very large multinational banks may experience DRS due to regulatory complexity, coordination failures across global divisions, and management diseconomies — explaining why some mega-banks have periodically restructured and downsized.

Real-World Examples of DRS

Historical Case: Vertical Integration Failures

When firms integrate across many stages of production — from raw material extraction to retail — the coordination burden can become overwhelming. Several large conglomerates of the 1970s and 1980s broke up not because their individual divisions were unviable, but because managing them together produced diseconomies: the whole was less efficient than the sum of its parts.

The "Too Big to Succeed" Problem

In some industries, beyond a certain scale, productivity measurably falls. Research has found that very large hospital systems, some large universities, and certain government-run enterprises exhibit DRS — doubling inputs consistently produces less than double the output, and often considerably less.

Policy Implications

DRS has important implications for industrial policy and competition regulation:

  • Merger policy: Regulators may permit mergers where IRS gains are expected to benefit consumers, but scrutinise very large mergers where DRS and reduced competition could harm welfare.
  • Privatisation debates: State-owned enterprises may face DRS if they are very large; however, privatised natural monopolies with IRS may require regulation.
  • Firm strategy: Firms recognising they have reached or exceeded MES may choose to divide, spin off divisions, or restructure to recover efficiency.

The Law of Diminishing (Marginal) Returns

Definition: As additional units of a variable input are added to one or more fixed inputs, beyond some point the marginal product of the variable input will begin to decline. This is a short-run phenomenon — at least one factor of production is fixed.

Short Run vs Long Run

The distinction is not about calendar time — it is about whether all factors can be varied:

  • Short run: At least one factor is fixed (typically capital — land, buildings, machinery). Labour and raw materials can be varied.
  • Long run: All factors of production are variable. The firm can adjust factory size, machinery, and workforce proportionately.

The "short run" differs by industry. For a coffee shop, it might be weeks. For a nuclear power station, it could be a decade.

The Three Measures of Product

Total Product (TP)

Total output produced with a given quantity of variable input (e.g. number of workers), holding fixed inputs constant.

Marginal Product (MP)

MP = ΔTP / ΔL

The additional output from employing one more unit of the variable input. Diminishing returns sets in when MP begins to fall — not when it becomes negative.

Average Product (AP)

AP = TP / L

Output per unit of the variable input. AP rises when MP > AP and falls when MP < AP. MP intersects AP at AP's maximum — a mathematical necessity.

Why Diminishing Returns is Short-Run Only

Diminishing returns depends entirely on the presence of a fixed factor acting as a constraint. As more labour is added to a fixed factory:

  • Initially workers are very productive — capital is underutilised and specialisation is possible.
  • As more workers are added, the fixed capital becomes a bottleneck — workers share equipment, compete for workspace, wait for machines.
  • In the long run, the firm can expand its capital too, eliminating the constraint. Diminishing returns cannot occur when all inputs are fully flexible.

Classic Example: Restaurant Kitchen

A fixed kitchen (capital) with limited worktops and ovens:

  • 1 chef: 20 meals/hour — kitchen severely underutilised
  • 2 chefs: 50 meals/hour (MP = 30) — specialisation benefits
  • 3 chefs: 72 meals/hour (MP = 22) — kitchen busier
  • 4 chefs: 88 meals/hour (MP = 16) — crowding begins
  • 5 chefs: 96 meals/hour (MP = 8) — severe congestion; workers obstructing each other

Diminishing marginal returns set in at the 3rd chef. Total product still rises, but at a decreasing rate. In the long run the kitchen could be expanded, resolving the constraint.

Diminishing Returns and Short-Run Cost Curves

Diminishing returns in production directly explains the shape of every short-run cost curve.

MC = W / MPL

Since the wage rate (W) is fixed in the short run, when MP falls (diminishing returns), MC must rise. This is the fundamental reason MC curves are U-shaped:

  • MP rising → MC falling
  • MP at maximum → MC at minimum
  • MP falling (diminishing returns) → MC rising

The same logic extends to AVC and ATC — both are U-shaped, and MC intersects each at its respective minimum point (a mathematical necessity, not a coincidence).

Real-World Applications

Agriculture

The classic textbook case: a fixed area of land with variable labour and fertiliser inputs. Excessive fertiliser application is the modern demonstration — early applications increase yield substantially, but at high application rates, additional fertiliser adds less and less to output, and beyond a threshold can even reduce it (negative marginal product).

Office and Knowledge Work

Adding programmers to a late software project is a well-documented example (sometimes called Brooks' Law). Beyond an optimal team size, communication overhead, merge conflicts, and coordination costs mean each additional programmer contributes progressively less — and may even reduce output.

Service Sector

A call centre with a fixed number of telephone lines and server capacity: adding more agents initially increases total calls handled, but as agent numbers exceed line capacity, waiting time rises and productivity per agent falls — diminishing returns in a modern services context.

Exam Technique

These principles apply across all contexts where production and costs appear in examinations.

01

State the time horizon immediately. Your first sentence should always establish whether you are discussing the short run or the long run. "In the short run, with at least one fixed factor..." signals conceptual precision before you write another word.

02

Name the concept explicitly before applying it. Don't write around a concept hoping the examiner infers it. State "This illustrates the law of diminishing marginal returns because..." and explain the mechanism clearly.

03

Link production to cost curves. Higher-mark responses connect MP/AP to MC/AVC/ATC. When MP falls (DR), MC rises — explain why using MC = W/MP, not just "costs go up."

04

Use a real-world example. Anchoring your argument in a specific industry — agricultural inputs, software teams, pharmaceutical manufacturing — demonstrates conceptual understanding rather than rote recall.

05

Reference or describe a diagram. Even without drawing one, writing "as shown by the inverted-U shape of the MP curve" or "the U-shaped LRAC" signals diagram literacy and adds analytical weight.

06

Distinguish DRS from diminishing returns when both appear. If a question mentions "increasing the scale of production" but also "adding more workers to a fixed factory," recognise these as different concepts and address each separately.

Worked Paragraph Structure

Topic sentence → Mechanism → Diagram reference → Real example → Implication

"In the short run, a firm adding workers to a fixed factory will eventually experience diminishing marginal returns. As each additional worker contributes less to total output, the marginal product of labour falls. Since marginal cost equals the wage rate divided by marginal product (MC = W/MPL), a falling MP directly causes MC to rise — explaining the upward-sloping portion of the short-run MC curve. A bakery adding its fifth and sixth bakers to a fixed set of ovens illustrates this: each baker produces fewer additional loaves than the last. This rising MC means the firm must charge higher prices to profitably supply greater output in the short run."
