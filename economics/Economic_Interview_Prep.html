The Diamond-Water Paradox
Water is essential for life yet cheap; diamonds are decorative yet expensive. How do economists resolve this apparent paradox?
HintThink about the difference between total utility and marginal utility at current levels of consumption.
Key ConceptsMarginal utility; diminishing marginal utility; consumer surplus; exchange value vs use value; Jevons, Menger, Walras marginal revolution.
Model AnswerThe paradox dissolves once we distinguish total utility from marginal utility. Water has enormous total utility but because it is abundant, its marginal utility — the value of one extra unit — is low. Diamonds are scarce; their marginal utility is high. Price reflects the margin, not the total. This insight, formalised by the 1870s marginalist revolution, replaced the classical labour theory of value and forms the foundation of modern demand theory. A strong answer would note that in water-scarce economies (e.g. parts of the Middle East) the paradox largely disappears, illustrating that scarcity, not intrinsic value, drives price.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can distinguish total utility from marginal utility — one of the most important analytical distinctions in economics. They want to see you identify the paradox as a problem of framing before resolving it, and ideally name the marginalist revolution. Avoid simply saying 'scarcity' — that's a conclusion, not an explanation.

Opportunity Cost in Everyday Decisions
A talented surgeon earns £200,000 a year. She decides to mow her own lawn rather than pay a gardener £50. Is this economically rational?
HintWhat is the surgeon's opportunity cost per hour? Are there non-monetary factors that might still make mowing rational?
Key ConceptsOpportunity cost; comparative advantage; non-pecuniary benefits; homo economicus; bounded rationality.
Model AnswerThe standard economic answer is no — at £100/hr, one hour mowing costs her £100 in foregone earnings to save £50, a net loss of £50. This illustrates comparative advantage: even if she mows faster than the gardener, she has a higher opportunity cost in surgery. However, a nuanced answer recognises that this assumes she can always convert free time into earnings (she may not be able to bill extra hours), ignores non-monetary utility from physical activity or autonomy, and that the homo economicus model may not capture real preferences. Interviewers value candidates who identify the assumption before critiquing it.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you automatically think in opportunity costs rather than just accounting costs. They also want to see intellectual honesty: can you identify the assumptions that make the standard answer correct, then question those assumptions? A candidate who gives only the textbook answer misses the point; one who identifies the limits of the model impresses.

The Sunk Cost Trap
A student has paid £200 for a non-refundable concert ticket. On the night, she feels ill. Should the £200 affect her decision to go? Most people say yes — economists say no. Why, and what does this reveal about how humans actually make decisions?
HintThe £200 is already spent regardless of what she decides tonight. What should the decision actually depend on?
Key ConceptsSunk cost fallacy; rational decision-making at the margin; prospect theory; loss aversion; Kahneman and Tversky.
Model AnswerRationally, the £200 is irretrievable whether she goes or not — it should not enter her marginal calculation. The decision should rest solely on whether the marginal benefit of attending (enjoyment despite illness) exceeds the marginal cost (discomfort, potential worsening). The sunk cost fallacy — letting irrecoverable past costs influence forward decisions — is one of the most robust findings in behavioural economics. Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory explains it through loss aversion: not going "confirms" the loss, whereas going feels like partial recovery. This has wide policy implications: governments continuing failed projects (Concorde, HS2 debate) may be committing institutional sunk cost fallacies.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can identify irrational behaviour, name the concept, and connect it to a broader theoretical framework (prospect theory, loss aversion). They want to see you move beyond 'people are irrational' to explaining why this particular irrationality is systematic and predictable. Bonus marks for a policy application.
Thought ExperimentIntermediate
Why Do Supermarkets Put Milk at the Back?
Supermarkets consistently place everyday essentials — milk, bread, eggs — as far from the entrance as possible. Is this profit-maximising behaviour, irrational design, or something else? What economic concept explains it?
HintThink about what happens to a shopper who walks the length of the store. Is this a nudge, a loss leader strategy, or both?
Key ConceptsLoss leader pricing; nudge theory; behavioural retail economics; footfall and impulse purchasing; store layout as price discrimination.
Model AnswerThis is deliberate profit-maximising behaviour, not irrationality. Essentials like milk have highly inelastic demand — customers will buy them regardless — but low margins. By placing them at the back, supermarkets maximise footfall past high-margin impulse items. This is a form of nudge architecture: the store layout exploits the predictable irrationality of shoppers who pick up unplanned purchases en route. It also acts as price discrimination: time-poor shoppers who don't want to walk the store may shop elsewhere, while bargain-hunters accept the walk for competitive essential prices. A strong answer connects this to Thaler and Sunstein's nudge theory and the broader behavioural design of commercial environments.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can apply economic reasoning to everyday observations — a core Oxbridge interview skill. They want to see that you don't accept surface explanations ('it's just design') and instead identify the economic mechanism (footfall, impulse purchasing, nudge). The best answers connect retail design to behavioural economics theory.

The Volunteer's Dilemma
Someone needs an ambulance. The more bystanders present, the less likely any individual is to call. How does this differ from a standard Prisoner's Dilemma, and what does it imply about the voluntary provision of public goods?
HintIn a Prisoner's Dilemma both players defecting is the dominant strategy. In the Volunteer's Dilemma, someone calling is the best collective outcome — but who bears the cost?
Key ConceptsBystander effect; diffusion of responsibility; free rider problem; public goods; mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium; Kitty Genovese case.
Model AnswerIn a standard Prisoner's Dilemma, mutual defection is the dominant strategy Nash equilibrium. The Volunteer's Dilemma differs: here, someone calling is socially optimal and individually costly, but if no one calls the outcome is worst for all. The game has no pure-strategy Nash equilibrium — it resolves in mixed strategies, where each bystander calls with some probability. Crucially, as group size rises, each individual's probability of calling falls, and the probability that no one calls can actually rise. This is a precise model of the bystander effect documented by Darley and Latané (1968). For public goods provision, it implies that larger communities may systematically underprovide voluntary public goods — a counterintuitive finding that challenges naive notions that more people means more resources.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can distinguish between different types of strategic interaction and reason carefully about equilibrium. They want to see that you understand mixed-strategy Nash equilibria intuitively (not necessarily formally) and can derive the counterintuitive result that more bystanders can reduce the probability of help. This tests both game theory and public goods logic.

Marginal Utility Paradoxes
If diamonds have higher marginal utility than water at current consumption levels, why don't we observe people substituting water consumption for diamond purchases until marginal utilities equalize?
HintConsider the equimarginal principle: consumers equalise marginal utility per pound spent, not marginal utility in absolute terms.
Key ConceptsEquimarginal principle; utility maximisation; budget constraints; income effects; non-substitutability of necessities.
Model AnswerThe equimarginal principle states that consumers maximise utility by equating marginal utility per pound spent across all goods (MU_x/P_x = MU_y/P_y), not marginal utility in absolute terms. Diamonds are expensive; water is cheap. If MU_diamond/P_diamond = MU_water/P_water at equilibrium, the raw marginal utility of a diamond can exceed that of water while still satisfying optimality. Furthermore, water has a physiological minimum consumption below which survival is impossible — it is not continuously substitutable with diamonds. This non-linearity is outside standard utility theory's usual domain and reveals that the theory's assumptions break down at survival thresholds, a point worth raising for a genuinely impressive answer.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you understand the equimarginal principle precisely — not just 'marginal utility' but MU per pound. They want to see you handle the formal condition MU_x/P_x = MU_y/P_y before noting the deeper problem: physiological survival constraints make water non-substitutable at the margin. A candidate who spots this limitation of standard theory stands out.
Thought ExperimentAdvanced
Multi-Agent Moral Hazard in Education
A school implements performance pay for teachers based on test scores. Students can observe teacher effort. How might this create a three-way moral hazard problem between students, teachers, and school administration?
HintThink about how each agent can adjust behaviour once they know the incentive structure. Does the presence of observable effort by one party change another's incentive to free-ride?
Key ConceptsMoral hazard; principal-agent problem; multi-task incentive problem; Holmström and Milgrom; Goodhart's Law; gaming metrics.
Model AnswerClassic moral hazard involves one principal and one agent. Here we have three interacting agents. Teachers may focus effort narrowly on testable content ("teaching to the test"), reducing broader educational value — Holmström and Milgrom's multi-task problem. Students, observing increased teacher effort, may free-ride — reducing their own study effort since the teacher's input substitutes for theirs. Administrators, rewarding test scores, may face gaming: schools select easier exam boards or exclude weaker students from cohorts. The result is that the metric (test scores) becomes detached from the underlying goal (education quality) — a direct application of Goodhart's Law. A superb answer would note that this is why education economists increasingly favour value-added measures over raw scores, and still find gaming behaviour.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can extend a standard two-party principal-agent problem to a multi-agent setting — a sign of analytical depth. They want to see you identify each agent's altered incentive, name the multi-task problem, and connect to Goodhart's Law. The ability to layer multiple concepts onto one scenario is exactly what Oxbridge tutors are looking for.
Thought ExperimentAdvanced
Negative Prices
In April 2020, oil futures briefly traded at −$37 per barrel — sellers paid buyers to take delivery. How can a price be negative? What does this reveal about storage costs, derivatives markets, and the limits of standard supply-demand analysis?
HintA futures contract requires physical delivery. If storage capacity is exhausted, what is the cost of being forced to take delivery of oil you cannot store?
Key ConceptsFutures markets; storage costs; convenience yield; COVID demand shock; market microstructure; physical vs financial commodity markets.
Model AnswerStandard supply-demand models implicitly assume goods can be stored or disposed of costlessly. Oil futures require physical delivery, and in April 2020 — with COVID having collapsed demand and storage tanks near capacity — the cost of disposing of oil (renting tankers, finding buyers) exceeded the value of the oil itself. The "price" was therefore the negative disposal cost. This reveals three important limits of standard analysis: first, storage capacity creates non-linearities invisible in textbook models; second, financial derivatives markets can disconnect from physical market fundamentals in extremis; third, the convenience yield (the value of holding inventory) can become a negative liability when storage is exhausted. This episode is a powerful illustration of how real commodity markets differ from the frictionless abstraction of standard theory.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you understand the assumptions that underpin standard supply-demand models and can identify which one breaks down. The key insight — that standard analysis assumes costless disposal — is not in most A-level textbooks. They also want to see comfort with real-world market complexity (futures, derivatives, storage) rather than just textbook abstraction.

Why Don't Employers Just Pay Everyone What They're Worth?
If wages reflect marginal product, why do firms use rigid pay scales, seniority systems, and internal labour markets rather than continuously repricing each worker? What problems would continuous repricing solve — and create?
HintThink about information asymmetries, morale effects, and tournament theory. What happens to cooperation if workers know they are continuously assessed against each other?
Key ConceptsEfficiency wage theory; internal labour markets; tournament theory; implicit contracts; morale and fairness; adverse selection in labour markets.
Model AnswerContinuous repricing would require accurate, costless measurement of each worker's marginal product — both practically infeasible and destructive of cooperation. Rigid pay scales and seniority systems serve several functions: they reduce measurement costs; create implicit long-term contracts that encourage firm-specific human capital investment; and avoid the morale destruction of constant relative comparison. Lazear and Rosen's tournament theory explains seniority wage profiles as incentive structures — workers accept below-MP wages early in exchange for above-MP wages later, which also reduces turnover. Efficiency wage theory (Shapiro-Stiglitz) shows firms may deliberately pay above market to elicit effort through the threat of unemployment. Continuous repricing would maximise short-run allocative efficiency but destroy the relational contracts that underpin long-run productivity.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can explain an apparent puzzle using multiple competing economic theories — tournament theory, efficiency wages, implicit contracts — and synthesise them. They want to see that you understand firms as institutions, not just price-takers in a labour market, and that you can identify trade-offs between short-run efficiency and long-run productivity.
Thought ExperimentAdvanced
The Market for Lemons and Used Textbooks
Second-hand economics textbooks sell for a fraction of new ones, yet used cars (Akerlof's classic example) suffer from severe adverse selection. Why might the used textbook market function better than the used car market? What does this reveal about the conditions under which information asymmetry matters?
HintIs quality verifiable for textbooks in a way it is not for cars? Can buyers inspect the good before purchase? Does the range of quality variation matter?
Key ConceptsAdverse selection; information asymmetry; Akerlof's lemons model; credence goods vs search goods vs experience goods; signalling; inspection feasibility.
Model AnswerAkerlof's model predicts market unravelling when sellers know quality and buyers don't. The key conditions are: quality is unverifiable pre-purchase, quality variation is large, and the cost of misrepresentation is low. Textbooks satisfy fewer of these conditions: quality is largely observable before purchase (you can read a page), quality variation is narrower (highlighting is visible and priced in), and condition is standardised. Used cars have hidden mechanical defects invisible to buyers, large quality variance, and high inspection costs. The textbook market also benefits from competitive online platforms (eBay, Amazon) that aggregate seller history — a form of reputation signalling that partially solves the information problem. This reveals that Akerlof's result is sensitive to inspection feasibility and market institution design, not just information asymmetry per se.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you know Akerlof's model precisely enough to identify when it applies and when it doesn't. They want to see you use the model's conditions analytically rather than just cite it as a fact. The ability to reason about why two superficially similar markets behave differently — using the same theoretical framework — is exactly the kind of comparative analysis Oxbridge rewards.

UBI and Labour Market Dynamics
If UBI reduces work incentives, why might it simultaneously increase entrepreneurship? Could this create a scenario where aggregate productivity rises despite fewer people in traditional employment?
HintConsider the role of income security as insurance against entrepreneurial failure. Who currently doesn't start businesses because the downside risk is too high?
Key ConceptsIncome effect vs substitution effect; risk and insurance; option value; creative destruction; Schumpeterian growth; heterogeneous agents.
Model AnswerThe apparent paradox dissolves when we separate income from substitution effects. UBI raises non-labour income, creating an income effect that reduces labour supply (leisure is a normal good). But simultaneously, UBI acts as a universal insurance policy against entrepreneurial failure — the biggest barrier to new business formation, especially for risk-averse or credit-constrained individuals. If the marginal entrepreneurs unlocked by UBI are more productive than the marginal employed workers who exit traditional employment, aggregate productivity could rise. This is essentially a Schumpeterian argument: creative destruction requires that resources — including human capital — move from low to high productivity uses. The net effect is theoretically ambiguous and empirically contested; pilots like Finland's 2017-2018 UBI experiment showed wellbeing gains but modest labour supply effects, leaving the productivity question open.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can decompose a seemingly paradoxical result into income and substitution effects — a fundamental analytical tool. They want to see you identify who the marginal entrepreneur is (credit-constrained, risk-averse) and why UBI changes their decision. The best answers acknowledge empirical uncertainty and cite relevant evidence rather than asserting the result as certain.

Gaming Environmental Subsidies
A government subsidises firms based on pollution reductions from a baseline. Why might rational firms increase pollution before the baseline is measured? What mechanism design principle does this violate?
HintIf the subsidy is based on reduction from a baseline, the firm's incentive is to make the baseline as high as possible. This is a form of strategic behaviour — what general principle describes incentive design failures like this?
Key ConceptsMechanism design; incentive compatibility; baseline gaming; Goodhart's Law; ratchet effect; perverse incentives in environmental policy.
Model AnswerRational firms will increase pre-baseline pollution to inflate the reference point, maximising the subsidy achievable from a given actual emissions level. This violates incentive compatibility — the foundational mechanism design principle that an optimal mechanism should make truthful revelation of private information the dominant strategy. The subsidy scheme creates a ratchet effect: the baseline becomes a strategic variable rather than an objective measure. This is why economists generally prefer Pigouvian taxes or cap-and-trade systems with absolute (not relative) targets — they remove the gaming incentive entirely by taxing every unit of pollution rather than rewarding reductions from a manipulable reference point. The same logic explains why grandfathering emissions permits in the EU ETS rewarded historical polluters.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can spot the incentive structure in a policy before evaluating it. They want to see you identify the baseline manipulation incentive immediately, name the mechanism design principle violated (incentive compatibility), and contrast with better-designed alternatives. Spotting perverse incentives quickly is a hallmark of good economic thinking.

Goodhart's Law and GDP
"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Apply this to GDP: if governments optimise for GDP growth, in what ways might they systematically distort the economy in unmeasured directions? Is there a better target — and would targeting that create the same problem?
HintWhat does GDP not capture? Think about sustainability, inequality, leisure, unpaid work, and wellbeing. Then ask: if we targeted those instead, what would be gamed?
Key ConceptsGoodhart's Law; GDP limitations; Kuznets' original warning; natural capital; inequality; OECD Better Life Index; Bhutan GNH; measurement and policy.
Model AnswerGDP-targeting creates systematic distortions: it incentivises market transactions over non-market activity (childcare becomes GDP when outsourced; parental care is invisible); it ignores natural capital depletion (resource extraction raises GDP today, destroying wealth tomorrow); it is distribution-blind (GDP can rise while median welfare falls); and it ignores negative externalities. Kuznets himself warned against using GDP as a welfare measure when he developed it in 1934. Alternatives like the OECD Better Life Index, genuine savings, or happiness indices partly address these gaps — but Goodhart's Law applies recursively: if governments target happiness scores, citizens game survey responses; if they target genuine savings, firms reclassify depreciation. The deeper insight is that any single summary statistic of a complex multidimensional phenomenon will be incomplete, and optimising for it will exploit the incompleteness. This points to using dashboards of indicators rather than single targets — but then the problem becomes aggregation weights.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can engage critically with a meta-level question about economic measurement — not just apply theory, but question the tools economists use. They want to see you generate multiple concrete examples of GDP distortion, engage seriously with alternatives, and then apply Goodhart's Law recursively to those alternatives. Intellectual self-awareness about the limits of economics impresses tutors.
Thought ExperimentExpert
The Cobra Effect
The British colonial government in India offered a bounty for dead cobras to reduce the snake population. Cobra breeders emerged to farm snakes for the bounty. When the bounty was cancelled, the farmed snakes were released — worsening the problem. What general principle does this illustrate, and how does it apply to modern policy?
HintThe bounty created an incentive to supply the very thing being targeted. What does this say about the relationship between incentives and unintended consequences?
Key ConceptsPerverse incentives; unintended consequences; mechanism design failure; Campbell's Law; Peltzman effect; second-order effects in policy.
Model AnswerThe Cobra Effect illustrates that poorly designed incentives can create the problem they intend to solve. It is the supply-side analogue of the free-rider problem: the bounty commodified cobras, creating a market for the pest. Modern parallels are abundant: UK hospitals given waiting time targets saw patients held in ambulances outside A&E to avoid the clock starting; No Child Left Behind in the US saw schools narrow curricula to testable subjects; PFI contracts incentivised hospital builders to design buildings minimising cleaning staff needs. The general principle — related to both Goodhart's Law and Campbell's Law — is that any incentive strong enough to change behaviour will attract strategic gaming that undermines the target. Robust mechanism design therefore requires anticipating second-order supply responses, not just demand effects.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can generalise from a historical anecdote to a precise economic principle, and then apply that principle to modern examples without prompting. They want to see you name the mechanism (perverse incentives, strategic gaming), relate it to Goodhart and Campbell's laws, and generate your own contemporary applications. Original examples you generate yourself are more impressive than ones from the reading list.

Monopsony in Plain Sight
Textbooks treat labour market monopsony as rare. Yet nurses, teachers, and care workers all face employers with significant wage-setting power despite competitive product markets. Why does monopsony persist in skilled public-sector labour markets, and what are the implications for minimum wage analysis?
HintWhat are the switching costs for a nurse or teacher? Are there geographic, regulatory, or professional barriers that limit labour mobility even when there are nominally many employers?
Key ConceptsMonopsony; labour mobility; switching costs; oligopsony; Manning's monopsony model; minimum wage in monopsony; Card and Krueger.
Model AnswerMonopsony does not require a single employer — it requires that workers face upward-sloping labour supply curves to individual firms, meaning wages must rise to attract additional workers. In public-sector professions, this arises through geographic immobility (a nurse cannot easily move county), professional registration requirements that limit sector-switching, and psychic attachment to vocation ("calling occupations"). Alan Manning's dynamic monopsony model shows that even modest search frictions create monopsonistic wage-setting power. The policy implication is significant: in monopsonistic labour markets, a minimum wage set between the monopsonist's optimal wage and the competitive wage can simultaneously raise wages and increase employment — the famous Card-Krueger result (1994). This directly contradicts the standard competitive model where any minimum wage above the market rate reduces employment, and explains why empirical evidence on minimum wages is far more mixed than textbook theory predicts.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can challenge a textbook simplification using real-world evidence — a hallmark of sophisticated economic reasoning. They want to see you explain why monopsony arises even without a single employer (search frictions, mobility barriers), and crucially, derive the policy implication: minimum wages can raise employment in monopsonistic markets. This directly contradicts standard undergraduate teaching and demonstrates genuine depth.

Price Elasticity and Revenue
Why does a firm facing inelastic demand benefit from raising price, while a firm facing elastic demand does not? What does this imply about pharmaceutical companies setting drug prices?
HintIf |PED| < 1, a 10% price rise causes less than 10% fall in quantity. What happens to total revenue = P × Q?
Key ConceptsPrice elasticity of demand; total revenue test; monopoly pricing; patent protection; deadweight loss; access to medicines debate.
Model AnswerWhen demand is inelastic (|PED| < 1), a price increase raises revenue because the proportional fall in quantity is smaller than the proportional rise in price. The reverse applies for elastic demand. Pharmaceutical companies exploit this by patenting drugs: patents grant temporary monopoly, but the inelasticity of demand for life-saving medication means the monopolist can set prices far above marginal cost, maximising profit. This creates a tension between dynamic efficiency (patents incentivise R&D) and static efficiency (monopoly pricing excludes patients who would benefit). The debate over compulsory licensing in developing countries — allowing generic production of patented AIDS drugs — is a direct policy manifestation of this tension.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you understand the total revenue test intuitively before they test your ability to apply it to a real industry. They want to see you move quickly from the abstract principle to the pharmaceutical example, identifying why inelastic demand arises (necessity, patent monopoly, lack of substitutes) and the welfare implications. The best answers raise the dynamic efficiency trade-off with R&D incentives.

Dynamic Elasticity and Network Effects
Train fares exhibit low short-run elasticity, but if network effects make railways more valuable as ridership increases, could price cuts that reduce short-term revenue actually maximise long-term profits?
HintConsider the difference between short-run and long-run elasticity, and how network externalities create positive feedback loops between price, ridership, and willingness-to-pay.
Key ConceptsShort vs long-run elasticity; network externalities; two-sided markets; dynamic pricing; platform economics; Metcalfe's Law.
Model AnswerShort-run elasticity for train travel is low because alternatives are limited in the immediate term. Long-run elasticity is higher as commuters relocate and businesses adjust. If the rail network exhibits positive network externalities — more passengers increases frequency viability, attracts more commercial investment near stations, and raises the convenience of the network — then price cuts can trigger a virtuous cycle: more passengers → higher network value → higher willingness to pay → demand shifts outward. This is essentially a platform economics argument: railways as two-sided markets connecting passengers and destinations. The profit-maximising price path would involve below-cost pricing to build the network, followed by price recovery once lock-in is achieved — the logic used by loss-making tech platforms. Whether public railways should exploit this for profit or pass it on as social surplus is a normative question.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can reason about dynamic feedback loops — not just static comparative statics. They want to see you identify the distinction between short-run and long-run elasticity, understand network externalities as a demand-side scale effect, and reason about intertemporal profit maximisation. Connecting this to platform economics theory shows impressive breadth.
MicroeconomicsAdvanced
Strategic Complementarity in Crime
In some neighbourhoods, individual decisions to commit crime exhibit strategic complementarity — my incentive to commit crime increases if others do too. How does this change optimal policing strategies?
HintIf the crime rate is self-reinforcing, what happens with a small reduction in policing? Think about tipping points and multiple equilibria.
Key ConceptsStrategic complementarity; multiple equilibria; tipping points; Schelling segregation models; hot-spot policing; social norms.
Model AnswerWhen crime decisions are strategic complements, the crime rate exhibits multiple equilibria: a low-crime steady state (where few others offend, lowering my return to crime) and a high-crime steady state (where widespread crime reduces detection probability and social stigma). Standard policing models treat each offender independently; strategic complementarity means marginal deterrence effects are amplified — reducing crime below a tipping point can cause a cascade to the low equilibrium. This implies concentrated policing (hot-spot strategies) may be more effective than uniform deployment, as it targets the threshold crossing. It also implies that community-level interventions that shift social norms — Broken Windows policing, community co-production — can be rational from a mechanism design perspective even if they seem indirect, because they shift which equilibrium the system selects.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can apply game theory beyond simple two-player settings to complex social equilibria. They want to see you identify multiple equilibria and tipping points, derive the implication that small interventions can have large non-linear effects, and connect to optimal policing strategy. The ability to move from abstract game-theoretic logic to concrete policy design is highly valued.

Coasean Lighthouse Paradox
Coase argued that lighthouses were historically funded privately, contradicting standard public goods theory. If lighthouses can exclude ships through port fees, are they still public goods?
HintThe two properties of public goods are non-rivalry and non-excludability. If port authorities can charge ships that use a lighthouse's signal, which property is violated?
Key ConceptsPublic goods; excludability; non-rivalry; Coase theorem; club goods; Van Zandt's critique of Coase's lighthouse; market failure and institutional design.
Model AnswerA lighthouse signal is non-rival — one ship using it does not diminish availability to others. But Van Zandt's historical analysis (and Coase's own) shows that exclusion was achieved indirectly through port fees: ships that never entered port could benefit from the lighthouse without paying, but most ships did enter port and could be charged there. This makes lighthouse services a club good (excludable) in practice, not a pure public good. Coase's insight is that public goods theory often conflates technological non-excludability with institutional non-excludability: the former is inherent to the good; the latter depends on institutional design. This has broader implications — many goods labelled public goods (spectrum, broadcast TV, GPS) are technically non-excludable only in the absence of appropriate institutions, and private provision through bundling and indirect pricing is often feasible.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can apply the two properties of public goods precisely enough to question a textbook classification. They want to see you identify that excludability is institutional rather than purely technological, and draw the broader implication: many goods we treat as requiring public provision may be privately providable with the right institutional design. This tests both knowledge and willingness to challenge received wisdom.
MicroeconomicsExpert
Hyperbolic Discounting and Commitment
If people have hyperbolic discount functions, when might they rationally choose to restrict their own future choices? How does this affect market design for commitment devices?
HintHyperbolic discounters are time-inconsistent — their preferences tomorrow differ from what they want today. A sophisticated hyperbolic discounter knows this. What would they rationally do about it?
Key ConceptsHyperbolic discounting; time inconsistency; present bias; sophisticated vs naive agents; commitment devices; Strotz (1956); Laibson; pension auto-enrolment.
Model AnswerExponential discounters have time-consistent preferences — what they prefer to do in the future matches what they want today. Hyperbolic discounters exhibit present bias: they discount the near future more steeply than the far future, leading to dynamic inconsistency. A sophisticated hyperbolic discounter (Strotz 1956; formalised by Laibson) recognises that their future self will make decisions their current self regrets. Rationally, they demand commitment devices: mechanisms that restrict future choice to align with current preferences. Examples: Christmas savings clubs (lock in savings against future impulse spending); pension auto-enrolment with barriers to withdrawal; gym contracts with cancellation fees; Ulysses tying himself to the mast. For market designers, this creates a profitable opportunity: sell commitment mechanisms to sophisticated agents. It also supports paternalistic policy — auto-enrolment in pensions is welfare-improving for sophisticated hyperbolic discounters even though it restricts choice.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you understand the difference between time consistency and time inconsistency — a concept central to modern monetary and behavioural economics. They want to see you distinguish naive from sophisticated hyperbolic discounters, explain why commitment devices are demanded, and derive the policy implication for pension design. This question rewards candidates who have read beyond the standard A-level syllabus.

Signalling vs Human Capital in Education
If education is pure signalling, should society subsidise it? If it's human capital, why can't firms test skills directly rather than relying on degrees?
HintIn Spence's signalling model, education conveys no productive value — it is a costly signal that separates high from low ability workers. If this is true, the private return is positive (firms pay graduates more) but the social return may be zero. What does this imply for subsidy?
Key ConceptsSpence's signalling model; human capital theory (Becker); credentialism; screening vs selection; direct testing costs; credential inflation.
Model AnswerIf education is pure signalling (Spence 1973), its social return is zero — it redistributes rather than creates value. Subsidising it would be wasteful: we are paying for a sorting device, not productivity. The optimal policy might be to find cheaper signals — aptitude tests, work trials. But direct testing is itself costly: it requires firms to design, administer and validate tests for every applicant, while a degree is a pre-packaged signal that amortises testing costs across thousands of graduates and across time. The credentialist equilibrium persists because no firm individually gains from unilaterally abandoning degree requirements — a coordination failure. If the truth is a mixture of signalling and genuine human capital (the empirical consensus), subsidy is justified to the extent that human capital externalities exist (social returns exceeding private returns), but should be calibrated to the human capital component. What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can hold two competing theories in tension and reason about their different policy implications, rather than simply choosing one as 'correct'. Why Do Auctions Exist?

HintAuctions are information revelation mechanisms. Think about when the seller does not know buyers' valuations, and when buyers' valuations are correlated with each other.
Key ConceptsVickrey-Clarke-Groves mechanism; revenue equivalence theorem; winner's curse; common vs private values; Milgrom and Wilson; spectrum auctions.
Model AnswerAuctions are optimal mechanisms when sellers face uncertainty about buyers' valuations — they are truth-revelation devices. The Multiplier Effect

Key ConceptsKeynesian multiplier; marginal propensity to consume; leakages; crowding out; Ricardian equivalence; fiscal policy effectiveness.

Expectations-Augmented Phillips Curve

Model AnswerFriedman and Phelps showed the long-run Phillips curve is vertical at the NAIRU: any attempt to exploit the trade-off shifts expectations until only surprise inflation moves unemployment. This connects macroeconomic stabilisation policy to distributional outcomes in ways that representative-agent models entirely miss.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you understand the fallacy of composition — that aggregate outcomes can differ from the sum of individual rational decisions. Fiscal Sustainability and Endogenous Growth

Prisoner's Dilemma — Nash Equilibrium
Two firms can either compete (low price) or collude (high price). If both collude, each earns £10m. If both compete, each earns £4m. If one competes while the other colludes, the competitor earns £14m and the colluder earns £1m. Find the Nash equilibrium. Is it Pareto optimal?
Solution
Working
  1. Firm A: if B colludes → compete gives £14m vs £10m (compete better). If B competes → compete gives £4m vs £1m (compete better). Compete is dominant for A.
  2. By symmetry, compete is dominant for B.
  3. Nash Equilibrium: (Compete, Compete) → both earn £4m.
  4. Pareto optimal? No — (Collude, Collude) gives both £10m. The NE is Pareto dominated.
Full ExplanationThis is the classic Prisoner's Dilemma. Compete is a strictly dominant strategy because it yields a higher payoff regardless of the rival's choice. The Nash equilibrium is individually rational but collectively suboptimal — the social optimum (mutual collusion) is not self-enforcing. This is why competition authorities exist: without enforcement, the equilibrium is wasteful competition rather than consumer-harming collusion. In repeated games (infinite horizon), cooperation can be sustained through trigger strategies (Grim Trigger, Tit-for-Tat) — a key insight for understanding why oligopolies can sustain tacit collusion without explicit agreement.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can construct a payoff matrix, identify dominant strategies systematically, and state the Nash equilibrium with precision. They then want you to identify the welfare implication — the NE is Pareto dominated — and connect it to real competition policy. Showing you understand the repeated game extension (cooperative equilibrium is possible) demonstrates depth beyond the one-shot result.

Elasticity and Tax Incidence
Demand: Q = 100 − 2P. Supply: Q = 3P − 20. A tax of £5 per unit is imposed on producers. Calculate the new equilibrium price paid by consumers, price received by producers, quantity traded, and the split of the tax burden.
Solution
Working
  1. Pre-tax equilibrium: 100 − 2P = 3P − 20 → 5P = 120 → P* = 24, Q* = 52.
  2. Tax shifts supply: producers receive Ps = Pc − 5. Supply becomes Q = 3(Pc − 5) − 20 = 3Pc − 35.
  3. New equilibrium: 100 − 2Pc = 3Pc − 35 → 5Pc = 135 → Pc = 27, Ps = 22, Q = 46.
  4. Consumer burden: £27 − £24 = £3. Producer burden: £24 − £22 = £2. Total: £5 ✓
  5. Consumer bears 60% of tax; producer bears 40%.
Full ExplanationTax incidence depends on relative elasticities. |PED| = (dQ/dP)(P/Q) = −2 × (24/52) ≈ −0.92. PES = (dQ/dP)(P/Q) = 3 × (24/52) ≈ 1.38. Consumer share = PES/(|PED|+PES) = 1.38/(0.92+1.38) ≈ 60%. The less elastic side bears more tax — consumers (less elastic here) bear £3 and producers (more elastic) bear £2. This is a fundamental result: tax incidence is independent of which side the tax is legally imposed on — it depends entirely on relative elasticities.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can solve a system of equations under a tax constraint and correctly split the burden. Crucially, they want to see you connect the numerical result to the elasticity formula — the less elastic side bears more tax — and derive this as a general principle rather than a coincidence of the numbers. Mathematical competence combined with economic interpretation is what distinguishes excellent answers.

Monopoly Pricing and Welfare Loss

Solution
Working

  2. MR = MC: 120 − 2Q = 20 → Q_m = 50, P_m = 70.
  3. Competitive: P = MC → 120 − Q = 20 → Q_c = 100, P_c = 20.
  4. CS (monopoly): ½ × (120−70) × 50 = £1,250. PS: (70−20) × 50 = £2,500.
  5. DWL: ½ × (70−20) × (100−50) = £1,250.
Full ExplanationThe monopoly restricts output from 100 to 50 and raises price from £20 to £70. Consumer surplus falls from £5,000 to £1,250 — a loss of £3,750. The monopolist produces at the point where MR = MC, exploiting market power at the expense of allocative efficiency. Cournot Duopoly Equilibrium
Two firms face market demand P = 100 − Q where Q = q₁ + q₂. Both have MC = 10. Derive the Cournot Nash Equilibrium quantities, price, and compare to the monopoly and perfectly competitive outcomes.
Solution
Working
  1. Firm 1 profit: π₁ = (100 − q₁ − q₂ − 10)q₁. FOC: dπ₁/dq₁ = 90 − 2q₁ − q₂ = 0 → q₁ = (90−q₂)/2.
  2. By symmetry: q₂ = (90−q₁)/2. Solve simultaneously: q₁ = q₂ = 30.
  3. Q = 60, P = 100 − 60 = 40. Profit per firm = (40−10)×30 = £900.
  4. Monopoly: MR=MC → 90−2Q=0 → Q=45, P=55. Duopoly output higher, price lower.
  5. Perfect competition: P=MC → Q=90, P=10. Cournot is between monopoly and competition.
Full ExplanationThe Cournot model assumes firms simultaneously choose quantities, taking rivals' output as given. Each firm's best response (reaction function) slopes downward — if the rival produces more, the optimal response is to produce less. The Nash equilibrium is the intersection of reaction functions. With n firms, the Cournot price converges to competitive as n→∞: this is the Cournot convergence theorem. The model predicts an intermediate outcome — better for consumers than monopoly, worse than perfect competition — which describes many real oligopoly markets well. The Bertrand model (price competition) gives the competitive outcome with just 2 firms, illustrating that market structure predictions are highly sensitive to the mode of competition assumed.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can derive reaction functions from first-order conditions, solve a simultaneous system, and interpret the result. Solow Growth Model — Steady State
An economy has production function Y = K^0.5 L^0.5, savings rate s = 0.3, depreciation δ = 0.05, population growth n = 0.02, and technological growth g = 0.03. Find the steady-state capital per effective worker. What is the Golden Rule savings rate?
Solution
Working
  1. In effective labour units (Ã = AL): ỹ = k̃^0.5 where k̃ = K/AL.
  2. Steady state: s·ỹ = (δ+n+g)k̃ → 0.3·k̃^0.5 = 0.1·k̃ → k̃^0.5 = 3 → k̃* = 9.
  3. ỹ* = 9^0.5 = 3. Check: s·ỹ* = 0.9 = (δ+n+g)·k̃* = 0.1×9 = 0.9 ✓
  4. Golden Rule: maximise c̃ = ỹ − (δ+n+g)k̃. FOC: MPK = δ+n+g. For Cobb-Douglas: 0.5k̃^{-0.5} = 0.1 → k̃_GR = 25, s_GR = 0.5.
Full ExplanationAt steady state, investment per effective worker exactly offsets depreciation and dilution from population and technological growth. With s = 0.3 and (δ+n+g) = 0.1, k̃* = 9 — below the Golden Rule of 25. This economy is dynamically efficient (undersaving) — increasing s would raise consumption per worker in the long run. At the Golden Rule, the marginal product of capital equals the break-even investment rate, maximising steady-state consumption. If k̃* > k̃_GR, the economy is dynamically inefficient (oversaving) — a situation empirically unlikely but theoretically important as it justifies government borrowing (OLG models).
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you can work with a model in per-effective-worker terms — a non-trivial transformation — and correctly apply the steady-state condition. They want to see precise derivation of both the actual steady state and the Golden Rule, with correct interpretation of whether the economy is dynamically efficient. Mistakes in the algebra are less important than demonstrating you understand what you are solving for and why.

Climate Economics and Intergenerational Equity
How should we discount future climate damages when the stakes are potentially catastrophic? Could standard cost-benefit analysis be fundamentally inadequate for irreversible environmental changes?
HintStern used a near-zero discount rate; Nordhaus used a market-based rate. The difference in their policy conclusions is enormous. Who is right — and is the choice of discount rate a technical or ethical decision?
Key ConceptsSocial discount rate; Ramsey formula; pure time preference; Stern Review; Nordhaus; fat-tailed distributions; Weitzman uncertainty; precautionary principle; irreversibility.
Model AnswerThe Ramsey formula decomposes the social discount rate: r = δ + ηg, where δ is pure time preference (how much we value the future less than the present for its own sake), η is the elasticity of marginal utility, and g is consumption growth. Stern set δ ≈ 0 on ethical grounds (future generations matter equally), yielding low r ≈ 1.4% and strong action. Nordhaus used market rates (δ ≈ 1.5%), yielding r ≈ 6% and gradual action. This is not a technical dispute — it is a normative debate about intergenerational ethics. Furthermore, Weitzman showed that fat-tailed uncertainty about catastrophic outcomes (low-probability, civilisation-ending scenarios) makes standard expected value CBA break down: the expected damage can be infinite regardless of the discount rate. This argues for treating climate change through insurance logic (avoiding catastrophic tail risks) rather than CBA optimisation. The irreversibility of mass extinction events further undermines CBA, since you cannot compensate future generations for outcomes that prevent them from existing.
What Interviewers Are TestingInterviewers are testing whether you understand that the choice of discount rate is fundamentally an ethical decision, not a technical one — and whether you can engage with the Stern-Nordhaus debate at that level.
