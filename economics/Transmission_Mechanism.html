Welcome to the Transmission Mechanism

The monetary policy transmission mechanism shows how changes in the Bank Rate flow through the financial system and real economy to affect output and inflation. Click any node above to explore its role in this chain.

⚠️ Limitations & Breakdowns of the Mechanism

The transmission mechanism assumes functioning financial markets and solvent, willing-to-lend banks. When these conditions fail, rate changes may transmit weakly or not at all — the most important evaluation points for A-Level essays.

🏦 Bank Liquidity Constraints & the Credit Channel Breakdown

The entire money-and-credit channel assumes commercial banks can and will transmit rate cuts into new lending. This breaks down when banks face a liquidity or solvency crisis. If interbank lending markets freeze — as in 2008 — banks cannot fund new loans even when the Bank Rate falls to near zero.

During a balance sheet recession, banks prioritise rebuilding capital ratios (deleveraging) over expanding lending. The Bank Rate may fall but bank credit actually contracts — the transmission channel goes into reverse. Broad money (M4) can shrink even as base money expands.

Key distinction: base rate vs broad money. The BoE controls the price of reserves in the overnight market. It does not directly control the volume of commercial bank lending. The two can diverge dramatically when banks are impaired.

🔎 2008–09: The Bank Rate fell from 5% to 0.5% in six months. Yet net lending to businesses fell sharply. Banks facing insolvency and regulatory pressure to hold more capital could not — and did not — pass cheap base rates into new credit.

📉 The Zero Lower Bound (ZLB) & Liquidity Trap

Nominal interest rates cannot (in practice) fall significantly below zero — depositors would withdraw cash rather than pay a bank to hold it. This is the zero lower bound. Once the Bank Rate reaches near-zero (0.1% in 2020), the conventional rate-cut mechanism is exhausted.

At the ZLB, the economy may enter a liquidity trap (Keynes): even at zero rates, firms and households prefer cash hoarding over spending and investment, because expectations of deflation or depression make holding money more attractive than spending it. Rate cuts lose traction entirely.

This is why the BoE turned to Quantitative Easing (QE) from 2009 onwards — buying gilts and corporate bonds to push down long-term yields and inject liquidity directly, bypassing the broken credit channel.

🔎 The BoE accumulated £895bn in asset purchases by 2021. The transmission from QE to inflation proved weaker and slower than expected — suggesting the credit channel breakdown was at least partially structural, not merely a function of the price of credit.

🏦 Impaired Bank Capital & the Capital Channel

Banks must hold capital against their loan books (Basel III: minimum CET1 ratio ≈ 4.5%, with buffers up to 10%+). When asset prices fall — as in 2008 — bank capital is eroded. Impaired capital means banks cannot expand lending without breaching regulatory limits, regardless of what the Bank Rate is doing.

This creates a pro-cyclical feedback loop: rate rises → asset prices fall → bank capital falls → banks tighten credit → further falls in asset prices → further capital erosion. Monetary tightening can inadvertently trigger a credit crunch that goes beyond the intended demand reduction.

The macroprudential toolkit (FPC at the BoE) was created post-2008 to interrupt this loop — tools like the countercyclical capital buffer allow regulators to release capital requirements during downturns, supporting bank lending even when interest rate policy is constrained.

🔎 The FPC released the countercyclical capital buffer from 1% to 0% in March 2020 (COVID-19), freeing up an estimated £190bn of bank lending capacity — a macroprudential tool operating alongside Bank Rate cuts.

📊 Time Lags & Policy Errors

The 18–24 month transmission lag creates a fundamental information problem: the MPC must act on forecast inflation, not current inflation. This requires predicting shocks — energy prices, global supply chains, pandemics — that are inherently unpredictable. A policy that looks correct today may look wrong in 18 months.

Overshooting risk: If the MPC raises rates aggressively in response to supply-side inflation (e.g. energy price spikes), it risks generating a deep recession once the supply shock reverses — because the demand-reducing effect of rate rises arrives just as the supply shock is fading.

Variable lags: Milton Friedman's "long and variable lags" — different channels transmit at different speeds. The exchange rate channel operates within weeks; the credit channel takes months; the full real economy effect takes 1–2 years. This makes calibration extremely difficult.

🔎 UK 2023: Some economists argued the BoE overtightened — raising rates to 5.25% to address inflation that was primarily supply-driven (energy). The resulting near-zero growth and mortgage arrears spike suggested demand was already cooling before the highest rates took effect.

💱 Exchange Rate Uncertainty & Uncovered Interest Parity

The exchange rate channel relies on the UIP (Uncovered Interest Parity) mechanism: higher UK rates → capital inflows → sterling appreciation → cheaper imports. In practice, exchange rates are driven by many factors beyond interest rate differentials — geopolitical risk, current account dynamics, global risk appetite — and can move in unexpected directions.

The "carry trade" complication: International capital flows seeking yield can overshoot — pushing sterling above equilibrium, harming exporters more than intended, and creating financial stability risks when the carry trade unwinds rapidly.

Pass-through limitations: Even when sterling appreciates, import prices may not fall immediately — importers may absorb exchange rate movements in margins rather than cutting prices, especially if they expect the move to be temporary.

🔎 After the September 2022 mini-budget, sterling fell to $1.07 despite the Bank Rate already being above US levels — demonstrating that UK-specific risk premia can overwhelm the interest rate differential in the exchange rate channel.

🏠 Structural Features That Blunt Transmission

Fixed-rate mortgage prevalence: As the UK shifted toward longer fixed-rate deals (2 and 5-year fixes), the share of mortgagors on variable rates fell. Rate rises take 2–4 years to fully feed through to household budgets — weakening the consumption channel and forcing the MPC to raise rates further to achieve the same effect.

Corporate debt structure: Many large firms issued long-dated fixed-rate bonds during the 2010s at ultra-low rates. Rate rises do not affect their borrowing costs until refinancing — potentially years away. The investment channel is slower than it would be in a floating-rate environment.

Supply-side inflation: Monetary policy works through the demand side. It cannot fix supply-chain disruptions, commodity price spikes, or post-pandemic labour shortages. Raising rates in response to cost-push inflation causes demand destruction but may do little to address the underlying supply shock — imposing recessionary costs for limited inflationary gain.

🔎 The BoE's own research (2023) estimated that the shift toward fixed-rate mortgages reduced the peak impact of rate rises on consumer spending by roughly 20–25% compared with the pre-2010 mortgage market.
⚖️ Essay evaluation framework: The transmission mechanism is most effective when (1) banks are solvent and willing to lend, (2) the economy is away from the ZLB, (3) the dominant inflation is demand-pull rather than cost-push, (4) firms and households are on variable-rate debt, and (5) expectations are well-anchored. When any of these conditions breaks down, the mechanism weakens — and policymakers must consider unconventional tools (QE, forward guidance, macroprudential policy) or coordinate with fiscal policy to compensate.

⚖️ Key Evaluation Point — Time Lags
Both policy types face the same fundamental constraint: the full effect on CPI takes 18–24 months to materialise. This means the MPC must be forward-looking, acting on forecast inflation rather than current data. Raising rates too late risks an entrenched wage-price spiral; raising too aggressively risks an unnecessary recession. The 2022–24 UK episode illustrates both dangers — rates possibly held high too long even after inflation was clearly falling.
