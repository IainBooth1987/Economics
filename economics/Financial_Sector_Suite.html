Profit & Liquidity: Bank Asset Rankings

Bank Asset Management

Banks hold a portfolio of assets that differ in how profitable they are and how quickly they can be converted to cash. Understanding this trade-off is fundamental to banking regulation and financial stability.

Profitability

The Return Spectrum

Profitability measures the return generated by an asset. Loans to customers earn the highest interest rates, but carry default risk. Cash earns almost nothing but is always available. Banks must balance the desire for profit with the need for safety.

Liquidity

Speed of Conversion

Liquidity refers to how quickly and cheaply an asset can be converted into cash without losing value. A bank needs liquid assets to meet unexpected withdrawals. Cash is perfectly liquid; loans to customers may take 30+ days to recall.

The Trade-off

Profit vs. Liquidity

There is a fundamental inverse relationship: the most profitable assets (customer loans) are the least liquid, while the most liquid assets (cash) are the least profitable. This is the central tension in commercial banking.

Regulation

Liquidity Coverage Ratio

Following the 2008 financial crisis, Basel III regulations introduced the Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR), requiring banks to hold enough high-quality liquid assets to survive 30 days of stressed outflows.

The Principal-Agent Problem in Financial Markets

The Principal-Agent Problem

A principal-agent problem arises when one party (the agent) is hired to act on behalf of another (the principal), but has different incentives or information. In financial markets, this misalignment was a central cause of the 2008 crisis.

Principal-Agent

The Core Problem

The agent has more information than the principal (information asymmetry) and may act in their own interest rather than the principal's. In banking: borrowers know more than brokers; brokers know more than the banks packaging loans into securities.

Moral Hazard

Risk Without Consequence

Moral hazard occurs when an agent takes on more risk because they do not bear the full cost of that risk. Mortgage brokers paid per loan had no incentive to check creditworthiness — they earned commission regardless of whether the loan was repaid.

Information Asymmetry

Unequal Knowledge

Asymmetric information means one party has better information than the other. Borrowers knew their true income; brokers often did not verify it; banks buying securitised loans had almost no visibility into the underlying credit quality.

Securitisation

Spreading — and Hiding — Risk

Securitisation bundles individual loans into tradeable securities. This allowed banks to remove risky loans from their balance sheets, reducing their incentive for due diligence. When loans defaulted en masse in 2007–08, banks worldwide were exposed.

The Investment Bank — Securitisation

Banks buy bundles of mortgages from brokers and package them into mortgage-backed securities (MBS) to sell on to investors. This process — securitisation — passes the risk to someone else. Once the risk is gone, the bank has no incentive to check whether the underlying loans are good.

🔄

Bank Run Simulator — Key Concepts

Fractional Reserve Banking & Bank Runs

Commercial banks do not hold all deposited money in reserve. They lend out the majority, keeping only a fraction available for withdrawals. This creates systemic vulnerability when depositors lose confidence simultaneously.

Fractional Reserve

How Banks Create Money

A bank receiving £1,000 in deposits might hold £100 in reserve and lend £900 out. That £900 is deposited elsewhere, creating further loans. This multiplier effect creates broad money. The reserve ratio determines the money multiplier: 1 ÷ reserve ratio.

Bank Run

Self-Fulfilling Panic

A bank run occurs when many depositors simultaneously attempt to withdraw their funds, fearing the bank will become insolvent. Because banks cannot immediately liquidate all their assets, even a solvent bank can fail if the panic is severe enough. It is a self-fulfilling prophecy.

Lender of Last Resort

Central Bank Role

The Bank of England acts as lender of last resort, providing emergency liquidity to solvent banks facing temporary shortfalls. This backstop, established after the 1866 Overend crisis, is designed to break the self-fulfilling nature of bank runs — but creates moral hazard.

Deposit Insurance

Removing the Incentive to Panic

The UK's FSCS insures deposits up to £85,000 per bank. If depositors know their money is guaranteed, they have no reason to queue at the first sign of trouble. This removes the coordination problem at the heart of bank runs — but may encourage banks to take excessive risks.

Historical Bank Runs
1929–1933
Great Depression Bank Runs

Over 9,000 American banks failed as depositor panic spread. Led to the creation of FDIC deposit insurance in 1933 — fundamentally changing banking stability.

2007
Northern Rock (UK)

The first bank run in the UK since 1866. Northern Rock relied heavily on short-term wholesale funding; when that market froze, queues formed outside branches before the Bank of England stepped in.

2008
Washington Mutual

Depositors withdrew $16.7bn in 10 days — the largest bank failure in US history. WaMu's heavy exposure to subprime mortgages undermined depositor confidence irreversibly.

2013
Cyprus Banking Crisis

Capital controls — a direct restriction on withdrawals — were imposed for the first time in a eurozone country. Depositors above €100,000 faced a "bail-in," losing a portion of their savings.

2023
Silicon Valley Bank

A social-media-accelerated bank run. $42bn was withdrawn in a single day — driven partly by VC firms coordinating on messaging platforms. Demonstrated how digital banking has compressed the timescale of collapse.

Commercial vs Investment Banking

Not all banks do the same thing. Commercial banks serve individuals and businesses with everyday financial services. Investment banks operate in wholesale financial markets, advising corporations and trading securities. Understanding this distinction is essential to understanding why ringfencing was introduced after 2008.

Commercial Bank

Retail & Business Banking

Takes deposits from the public, makes loans to individuals and businesses, operates the payments system. Funded primarily by customer deposits. Profits from the interest rate spread — borrowing cheaply, lending at higher rates.

Investment Bank

Wholesale & Capital Markets

Raises capital for corporations, advises on mergers and acquisitions, trades securities on its own account (proprietary trading), structures complex financial products. Does not take retail deposits. Higher risk, higher fee income.

Universal Bank

Both Under One Roof

Pre-2008, many large banks — RBS, Barclays, HSBC — combined both functions. This meant that risky investment banking activities sat alongside protected retail deposits, creating systemic risk. A failure in the trading division could threaten ordinary depositors' savings.

Key Difference

Who Bears the Risk?

Commercial banking risk falls on depositors and regulators. Investment banking risk falls on shareholders and counterparties. When these are combined, taxpayers effectively subsidise investment banking risk — because the government cannot allow the retail bank to fail.

How Banks Create Credit

One of the most misunderstood aspects of banking: banks do not simply lend out money that has been deposited. When a bank makes a loan, it creates new money — new deposits — in the process. This is credit creation, and it is how the broad money supply expands.

Regulating the Financial Sector

Banks are not left to operate freely. Because the failure of a major bank can destabilise the entire economy, the UK has a layered regulatory architecture: macroprudential oversight of systemic risk, microprudential supervision of individual firms, and conduct regulation to protect consumers. Each regulator has a distinct mandate.

Bank of England

The UK's central bank and the apex of the regulatory architecture. Acts as lender of last resort, sets monetary policy, and houses the Prudential Regulation Authority and Financial Policy Committee.

Oversees systemic stability through the FPC

🛡️
Prudential Regulation Authority
PRA — Est. 2013
▼

A subsidiary of the Bank of England. Responsible for the microprudential supervision of around 1,500 banks, building societies, insurers and investment firms.

Sets minimum capital and liquidity requirements (Basel III implementation)
Authorises banks to accept deposits — no bank can operate without PRA approval
Conducts stress tests — simulates severe economic shocks to test resilience
Has power to remove directors and wind up failing institutions
Created from the FSA's split in 2013 after the FSA was criticised for failing to prevent the 2008 crisis
📊
Financial Policy Committee
FPC — Est. 2013
▼

The macroprudential regulator — responsible for identifying and addressing risks to the stability of the entire UK financial system, rather than individual firms.

Sets the Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB) — requiring banks to hold extra capital during booms
Publishes the Financial Stability Report (twice yearly) identifying systemic risks
Can direct the PRA and FCA to take action to address systemic risks
Monitors leverage ratios, credit growth, and asset price bubbles
Created because no single regulator focused on system-wide risk before 2008 — a key lesson of the crisis
⚖️
Financial Conduct Authority
FCA — Est. 2013
▼

Regulates the conduct of financial firms — how they treat customers, the integrity of markets, and competition. Entirely separate from the BoE.

Regulates over 50,000 financial services firms including banks, insurers, and investment advisers
Protects consumers from mis-selling, fraud, and unfair practices
Oversees market integrity — prevents insider trading, manipulation
Can fine firms, ban individuals, and impose consumer redress requirements
Example: Fined Barclays £72m for poor financial crime controls (2015); PPI mis-selling led to £38bn in consumer refunds

Formula
LCR = High Quality Liquid Assets ÷ Net 30-day Cash Outflows × 100
Requires banks to hold enough liquid assets to survive 30 days of stressed withdrawals without external support. Introduced by Basel III after Northern Rock demonstrated that even solvent banks could fail through illiquidity.

Post-Crisis Regulatory Reform Timeline

08
2008
Global Financial Crisis — RBS & Lloyds Bailout
The UK government injects £65bn into RBS and Lloyds TSB, taking majority stakes. The FSA is widely criticised for light-touch supervision. Emergency bank guarantee schemes introduced.
10
2010
Independent Commission on Banking (Vickers Report)
Sir John Vickers chairs a review recommending ringfencing of retail banking from investment banking, and higher capital requirements. Foundation of the Banking Reform Act 2013.
11
2011–13
Basel III — International Capital & Liquidity Standards
The Basel Committee introduces new global standards: higher minimum capital ratios, the Liquidity Coverage Ratio (30-day buffer), Net Stable Funding Ratio, and the countercyclical capital buffer. Phased in through 2019.
13
2013
Regulatory Architecture Reform
FSA abolished. PRA (microprudential) and FCA (conduct) created. Financial Policy Committee established within the Bank of England for macroprudential oversight. The "twin peaks" model replacing single-regulator approach.
19
2019
Ringfencing Fully Implemented
UK banks with retail deposits over £25bn legally required to separate core retail banking from investment banking. Barclays, HSBC, Lloyds, NatWest all implement structural separation. Debate continues about whether this goes far enough.
23
2023
SVB & Credit Suisse — New Stress Tests
Silicon Valley Bank collapses in 36 hours; Credit Suisse rescued by UBS merger. UK government reviews ringfencing rules. PRA and BoE both conduct rapid system-wide stress assessments. Basel III "endgame" rules debated globally.
