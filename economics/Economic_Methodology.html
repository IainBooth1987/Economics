Economic Methodology

How economists think, reason and argue — from observation to theory, from facts to values.

What is Economic Methodology?

Economics isn't just about money — it's a discipline with its own rules of reasoning. Economic methodology explores how economists build knowledge: what counts as evidence, what makes a claim scientific, and where value judgements enter the picture.

Core Concept

Economic methodology is the study of how economists generate, test, and apply theories to understand real-world behaviour — combining elements of the natural sciences, social analysis, and philosophical reasoning.

1

Observation

Economists observe patterns in real data — prices, wages, trade flows — and ask: what's driving this?

2

Hypothesis

A testable prediction is formed. "When X increases, Y will fall" is a hypothesis — specific and falsifiable.

3

Testing

Statistical analysis, natural experiments, and historical data are used to stress-test the hypothesis.

4

Theory

Hypotheses that consistently survive testing evolve into theories — accepted frameworks for economic analysis.

Why Methodology Matters

Good economic reasoning requires understanding not just what economists conclude, but how they reached those conclusions — and crucially, whether a claim is a testable fact or a value judgement dressed up as one.

🔍 A Classic Confusion "The minimum wage should be raised because workers deserve a living wage" feels factual — but the word "should" gives it away as normative. The methodological skill is learning to spot the difference instantly.

Limitations of Economic Analysis

  • Economies are complex systems — controlled experiments are rarely possible
  • Simplifying assumptions (like ceteris paribus) may not hold in practice
  • Data can be interpreted differently by economists with different values
  • Predictions about human behaviour carry inherent uncertainty

Economic Theory & Method

Economic theories are not guesses — they are carefully constructed frameworks built through a systematic process. Understanding that process helps you evaluate how robust any given theory really is.

The Scientific Method in Economics

1

Observation

Real-world economic phenomena are observed and recorded. Changes in consumer spending, unemployment, exchange rates, inflation.

2

Hypothesis Formation

A specific, testable prediction is proposed about the relationship between variables — grounded in the observations.

3

Testing & Analysis

Statistical tools, econometric models, and natural experiments are used to assess whether the evidence supports or challenges the hypothesis.

4

Theory Development

Hypotheses that withstand rigorous testing become part of accepted economic theory, used to inform predictions and policy.

Economic Models

Models are deliberate simplifications of reality. They isolate key variables to reveal underlying relationships. The supply-demand model, for example, holds all other factors constant — a technique economists call ceteris paribus.

🗺️ The Map Analogy A map is not the territory — it omits detail to be useful. Economic models work the same way. Their power lies in what they leave out, not just what they include.

Core Assumptions

🧠 Rational Behaviour

Classical economics assumes agents maximise utility or profit. Behavioural economics challenges this, showing how real decisions often deviate from the rational model.

⚖️ Ceteris Paribus

"All else equal." This assumption isolates the effect of one variable — essential for building clear models, but rarely true in the real world.

Key Insight

A theory's usefulness is not judged by whether its assumptions are perfectly realistic — it's judged by whether its predictions are accurate. A simplified model can produce powerful, testable insights.

Positive vs Normative

One of the most important distinctions in economics. Confusing the two is how economic debates get muddled — and how politicians exploit the language of facts to smuggle in value judgements.

✓ Positive Economics

Describes what is or what will be. Objective, testable, value-free.

  • Based on facts and data
  • Can be proven true or false
  • No value judgements
  • Uses cause-and-effect language
⚠ Normative Economics

Expresses what ought to be. Subjective, opinion-based, value-laden.

  • Based on values and beliefs
  • Cannot be proven true or false
  • Contains value judgements
  • Uses "should", "ought", "fair"
Positive Examples
  • The unemployment rate is currently 4.2%
  • A rise in interest rates reduces household borrowing
  • Higher tobacco taxes lower consumption among young smokers
  • GDP contracted by 0.3% in the last quarter
Normative Examples
  • The government should increase the minimum wage
  • Income inequality is unfair and must be reduced
  • Economic growth ought to be prioritised over environmental concerns
  • Free healthcare should be a universal right
⚡ The Trick Question "Unemployment is too high." — Looks like a fact. But "too high" is a value judgement. The claim can't be verified without agreeing on what level counts as acceptable — and that's a normative question.

Why the Distinction Matters

  • It separates evidence-based analysis from personal opinion
  • Positive economics can inform normative policy decisions — but it can't determine them
  • Policy debates often blend both types — spotting which is which is a critical skill
  • Economists can disagree on policy (normative) while agreeing on the facts (positive)
