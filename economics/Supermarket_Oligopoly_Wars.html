UK Supermarket Oligopoly Wars — Theory Notes

📚 Oligopoly Theory

What is an Oligopoly?

An oligopoly is a market structure dominated by a small number of large firms. The key feature is interdependence — each firm's decisions about price, output, and strategy are directly influenced by what it expects rivals to do. This creates a fundamentally different logic from competitive or monopoly markets.

In the UK grocery market, the Big Four (Tesco, Sainsbury's, ASDA, Morrisons) have traditionally dominated, though Aldi and Lidl have significantly eroded their combined share over the past decade — now holding around 19% between them (Kantar, March 2025).

Five-Firm Concentration Ratio (CR5)

Using March 2025 Kantar data: Tesco (27.9%) + Sainsbury's (15.2%) + ASDA (12.5%) + Aldi (11.0%) + Morrisons (8.5%) = CR5 ≈ 75.1%. This confirms a highly concentrated oligopolistic market. Note that Morrisons has now been displaced from the Big Four by Aldi in share terms.

The Kinked Demand Curve

The kinked demand curve model (Sweezy, 1939) attempts to explain price rigidity in oligopolies. It rests on two asymmetric assumptions about rival behaviour:

If you raise price...

Rivals do not follow. Customers switch away. Demand is price elastic above the current price — revenue falls.

If you cut price...

Rivals match the cut to avoid losing market share. All firms gain little — demand is price inelastic below the current price.

The Implication: Price Rigidity

Neither raising nor cutting price improves revenue, so oligopolists tend to leave prices stable. This is consistent with supermarkets competing primarily through loyalty schemes, product range, and non-price factors rather than constant price changes.

UK Example — Price Matching Guarantees

Tesco's "Price Promise" and ASDA's "Price Guarantee" are institutional responses to the kinked demand logic — they reassure consumers without initiating destructive price wars, since any cut will be matched immediately.

Game Theory and Strategic Interdependence

Game theory formalises the strategic thinking between rivals. The classic framework is the Prisoner's Dilemma, which illustrates why individually rational decisions can lead to collectively worse outcomes.

Price War as a Prisoner's Dilemma

If Tesco and ASDA both cut prices, both earn lower profits but neither gains market share. If one cuts and the other holds, the cutter gains share — but if both anticipate this, both cut, landing in a worse equilibrium. The Nash equilibrium is often the high-price outcome if firms can credibly commit to it — or tacit collusion.

Nash Equilibrium

A Nash equilibrium exists when no player can improve their outcome by changing strategy, given what others are doing. In repeated games (as in real supermarket competition), firms can learn to cooperate tacitly — matching rivals' prices without explicit agreement, which avoids the costs of a price war.

Non-Price Competition

Because price competition is potentially destructive (as the kinked demand curve predicts), oligopolists often compete through non-price means. In supermarkets, this includes:

Loyalty Schemes

Clubcard, Nectar, and MyMorrisons lock in customers and generate data for targeted pricing — a competitive advantage hard to replicate.

Own-Label Ranges

Premium own-label (e.g. Tesco Finest) allows quality differentiation without triggering price wars. Record own-label penetration in 2025.

Format Innovation

Express/convenience formats (Tesco Express, Sainsbury's Local) expand reach. Rivals followed rapidly — a classic oligopolistic reaction.

Online Delivery

Ocado's 15% growth (Dec 2025) and expanded home delivery services reflect how digital formats are the new front in non-price competition.

The Discounter Disruption

Aldi and Lidl's rise represents a form of market contestability — new entrants (operating on very different cost structures) have forced the Big Four to respond. Between 2012 and 2025, Aldi grew from approximately 2% to 11% market share. This challenges traditional oligopoly models which assume high barriers to entry.

Evaluative Point for Essays

The growth of Aldi and Lidl demonstrates that the UK grocery market may be more contestable than a simple oligopoly model suggests. The threat of discounter expansion has disciplined Big Four pricing — a market outcome closer to competition than a static oligopoly model would predict.

Regulation and Competition Policy

The Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) monitors grocery oligopoly behaviour for:

Tacit Collusion

Coordinated price behaviour without explicit agreements — technically legal but harmful to consumers.

Shrinkflation

The CMA investigated hidden price rises through reduced product sizes — a non-transparent form of price increase.

Merger Control

The CMA blocked the proposed Sainsbury's/ASDA merger in 2019, citing harm to consumer welfare — a direct application of oligopoly theory.

Supplier Relations

Supermarkets' buyer power over suppliers has been investigated via the Groceries Supply Code of Practice.

UK Supermarket Oligopoly Wars — Debrief Questions

✍️ Debrief Questions

Use these questions for class discussion or written work. Hints can be revealed on screen or will appear automatically when printed.

Question 1 — Interdependence
When you cut prices in the simulation, rival supermarkets quickly matched your move. Using the concept of interdependence, explain why oligopolists feel compelled to react to a rival's price cut rather than hold their own price.
Key concepts to include: Interdependence means that each firm's decisions directly affect rivals' revenues and market share. If a rival cuts price and attracts customers, the other firms risk losing sales if they don't respond. This creates a strong incentive to match — even if the price cut ultimately reduces industry profits — because not reacting risks losing market share permanently. Consider linking to the kinked demand curve: the elastic section above current price means rivals won't follow a price rise, but the inelastic section below means they will follow a cut (to protect their own share).
Question 2 — Kinked Demand Curve
The kinked demand curve model predicts that price wars are self-defeating in oligopolistic markets. Evaluate whether your experience in the simulation supports or challenges this prediction.
Structure your evaluation: First explain the model — the kink at the current price, the break in the MR curve, and the resulting price rigidity. Then assess: did market share gains from price cuts prove temporary once rivals matched? Did the discounters (Aldi/Lidl) growing regardless suggest limits to the model? Consider weaknesses of the kinked demand model — it doesn't explain how the original price was set, it assumes asymmetric behaviour without fully explaining why, and it doesn't account for collusion. A strong answer will note both supporting evidence from the simulation and reasons to question the model's completeness.
Question 3 — Game Theory and Nash Equilibrium
Using the Prisoner's Dilemma framework, explain why supermarkets might maintain high prices rather than continuously undercutting each other — even without any explicit agreement.
Key ideas: In a one-shot Prisoner's Dilemma, both firms have a dominant strategy to cut price, leading to a low-profit Nash equilibrium (both cut). But in a repeated game — which real supermarket competition is — firms can adopt strategies like "tit-for-tat": cooperate (hold price) unless the rival defects (cuts), then retaliate. Over time this can sustain a high-price equilibrium without explicit collusion. This is known as tacit collusion. Loyalty schemes, price-matching guarantees, and public price announcements may all facilitate this coordination.
Question 4 — Non-Price Competition
In the simulation, promotions and expansion moves triggered less immediate market share change than price cuts. Why might oligopolists prefer non-price competition, and what are its advantages and limitations for consumers?
Arguments for non-price competition: It avoids the destructive spiral of a price war; it builds brand loyalty and differentiation; it increases switching costs for consumers; it can be more profitable long-run. Consumer advantages: Innovation, quality improvements, wider product ranges, convenience (delivery, formats). Consumer limitations: May disguise underlying high prices; loyalty schemes raise switching costs and reduce price sensitivity; advertising costs are ultimately passed on; premium own-label may displace choice.
Question 5 — Contestability and the Discounter Threat
Aldi and Lidl grew automatically in the simulation, reducing the Big Four's combined share each round. Using the concept of market contestability, assess the extent to which Aldi and Lidl's growth challenges the view that the UK grocery market is a classic oligopoly.
Structure: Define contestability — markets are contestable if entry/exit is relatively easy and sunk costs are low. Aldi and Lidl's entry from the 1990s–2010s suggests barriers were not insurmountable. Their combined ~19% share (Kantar, March 2025) has forced Big Four price investment (Price Promise, etc.) — consistent with contestability theory's prediction that the threat of entry disciplines incumbent pricing. Counter-argument: Sunk costs (store fit-out, supply chains) are still significant; Aldi and Lidl took decades to scale — this suggests barriers are lower than a pure oligopoly model assumes, but not negligible. A strong answer evaluates the degree of contestability rather than treating it as binary.
Question 6 — Evaluation: Oligopoly and Consumer Welfare
Overall, to what extent does the UK supermarket oligopoly operate in consumers' interests? Draw on evidence from the simulation, your own knowledge, and economic theory in your answer.
This is a broad evaluative question — structure with both sides:
Case that it serves consumers: Intense competition (especially from discounters) has kept prices relatively low; loyalty schemes offer genuine savings; product innovation and range are extensive; price wars (when they occur) directly benefit shoppers.
Case that it doesn't: Prices remain higher than under perfect competition; tacit collusion and price rigidity may mean prices are stickier than in competitive markets; shrinkflation investigations suggest hidden price rises; supplier power is exploited; advertising and switching costs reduce consumer sovereignty.
Conclusion: Consider which factors are most significant, and whether regulation (CMA) provides adequate protection. A strong answer avoids a binary conclusion and weighs the evidence.
