The Phillips Machine (MONIAC) — Historical Context

Bill Phillips (1914–1975) was a New Zealand economist who studied at the London School of Economics. In 1949, while still a student, he built the Monetary National Income Analogue Computer (MONIAC) — a 2-metre tall hydraulic machine that used coloured water flowing through transparent pipes to represent money circulating through the economy.

The machine was revolutionary: it made abstract macroeconomic relationships visible and physical. Policymakers could physically manipulate valves representing tax rates and interest rates, and watch how the water (money) redistributed itself across tanks representing consumption, investment, government spending, and exports.

🗺️ Where are MONIACs today?

Approximately 14 original machines were built. Surviving examples are held at: the Science Museum (London), the Reserve Bank of New Zealand, Leeds University, Cambridge, and the Ford Motor Company.

⚙️ How it worked

Water flowed from a top reservoir through valves (representing policy levers) into tanks for each GDP component. Overflow pipes modelled saving, taxation, and imports — the economy's leakages. The water level in the GDP tank showed total output.

📡 Why it mattered

Pre-dating computers, the MONIAC could solve simultaneous equations for the whole economy in real time. The LSE used it for teaching for decades; the Reserve Bank of New Zealand used it for actual policy simulations.

Aggregate Demand: AD = C + I + G + (X−M)

Aggregate demand is the total demand for goods and services produced in an economy at a given price level over a given time period. Each component has distinct determinants that are reflected in the water tanks of this simulator.

🟢 Consumption (C) — largest component (~60–70% of UK GDP)

Determinants: Disposable income (income after tax), interest rates (cost of borrowing), consumer confidence, wealth effects, availability of credit. The MPC (Marginal Propensity to Consume) determines how much of each extra £1 of income is spent.

🟡 Investment (I) — most volatile component

Determinants: Interest rates (cost of borrowing capital), corporate profitability (influenced by corporate tax), business confidence, accelerator effect, technological change. Investment is critical for long-run productive capacity.

🔵 Government Spending (G) — direct injection

Determinants: Government policy decisions, automatic stabilisers (e.g. unemployment benefits rise in recession), fiscal rules, national debt levels. G is an injection into the circular flow — a policy lever for demand management.

🟣 Net Exports (X−M) — trade balance

Determinants: Exchange rate (stronger £ → dearer exports, cheaper imports), relative inflation rates, income levels abroad, trade barriers. A trade deficit (M > X) is a leakage from AD; a surplus is an injection.

💡 Examiner's Note — The Multiplier Effect

When G increases by £1B, the final increase in GDP is greater than £1B — this is the multiplier. The size depends on the MPC: Multiplier = 1 ÷ (1 − MPC). With MPC = 0.6, multiplier = 2.5. Watch for the multiplier ripple animation on the GDP tank when you increase government spending significantly.

Fiscal Policy — Government Spending & Taxation

Fiscal policy is the use of government spending (G) and taxation (T) to influence aggregate demand, income distribution, and resource allocation.

📈 Expansionary Fiscal Policy

↑ G or ↓ T → ↑ AD → ↑ real GDP (short run) → ↓ unemployment. Risk: may cause ↑ inflation if economy near full capacity. Creates a budget deficit (T < G). Used in recessions (e.g. UK 2008–2010 stimulus).

📉 Contractionary Fiscal Policy

↓ G or ↑ T → ↓ AD → ↓ inflationary pressure. Risk: may cause ↑ unemployment, ↓ growth. Creates a budget surplus. Used to control inflation or reduce national debt (e.g. UK austerity 2010–2016).

⚠️ Crowding Out

When government borrowing to fund higher G pushes up interest rates, this can reduce private sector investment (I). If crowding out is complete, ↑G is offset by ↓I and GDP does not rise — a key argument against fiscal stimulus.

🕐 Time Lags

Fiscal policy suffers from: recognition lag (identifying the problem), implementation lag (parliamentary process), and impact lag (time for spending to reach the economy). Total lag can be 12–24 months.

💡 Examiner's Note — Budget Balance vs Debt

The budget balance is the annual difference (T − G). National debt is the cumulative stock of past deficits. A government can run a deficit while reducing the debt-to-GDP ratio if growth is fast enough — as shown in the debt-to-GDP indicator in this simulator.

Monetary Policy — Interest Rates & the Bank of England

Monetary policy is the use of interest rates (and other tools) to control the money supply and influence inflation, output, and employment. In the UK, it is set by the Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) of the Bank of England, which has an inflation target of 2% CPI.

The Transmission Mechanism of Monetary Policy

A change in the base rate works through multiple channels:

  • Consumer spending: ↑ rates → ↑ mortgage costs → ↓ disposable income → ↓ C
  • Business investment: ↑ rates → ↑ cost of borrowing capital → ↓ I (very sensitive)
  • Exchange rate: ↑ rates → ↑ hot money inflows → ↑ £ → ↓ export competitiveness → ↓ X, ↑ M → ↓ NX
  • Asset prices: ↑ rates → ↓ house/equity prices → negative wealth effect → ↓ C
  • Expectations: Signalling credible commitment to low inflation can anchor expectations

🏦 Quantitative Easing (QE)

When rates near zero, the Bank of England can create money to buy government bonds — pushing down long-term rates and increasing the money supply. Used extensively 2009–2021. Risk: asset price inflation, inequality.

⚙️ Limitations of Monetary Policy

Works with long lags (6–18 months). May be ineffective in liquidity trap (near-zero rates). Cannot target specific sectors. Independent MPC may conflict with government fiscal priorities. Cannot solve supply-side problems.

💡 Examiner's Note — Conflicts & Trade-offs

Higher interest rates to control inflation may worsen unemployment. Lower rates to stimulate growth may fuel inflation. This is the core tension shown in the Phillips Curve — and why policymakers rarely have a single clear answer.

The Phillips Curve — Inflation vs Unemployment

In 1958, Bill Phillips published research showing a statistical relationship between wage inflation and unemployment in the UK over nearly a century. This became the basis of the Phillips Curve — suggesting a trade-off: policymakers could choose lower unemployment at the cost of higher inflation.

📊 Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC)

Shows inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment in the short run. Moving along the SRPC: expansionary policy → lower unemployment, higher inflation. Contractionary policy → higher unemployment, lower inflation.

📐 Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC)

Friedman & Phelps (1968): in the long run, the SRPC shifts up as expectations adjust. The LRPC is vertical at the NAIRU (Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment) — there is no long-run trade-off.

🌪️ Stagflation (1970s)

The 1973 oil shock caused both high inflation AND high unemployment simultaneously — the SRPC shifted outward. This contradicted the simple Phillips Curve and supported expectations-augmented models. Unemployment: 5.7%, Inflation: 24% (UK, 1975).

🎯 NAIRU & Expectations

The NAIRU is the unemployment rate consistent with stable inflation. Attempts to push unemployment below NAIRU through demand management cause accelerating inflation as workers demand higher wages to offset expected price rises.

💡 Critical Thinking — Is the Phillips Curve dead?

Post-2008 the relationship appeared to break down — UK unemployment fell below 4% by 2019 yet inflation remained below target. Explanations include: weaker wage bargaining power, globalisation suppressing prices, anchored expectations from credible inflation targeting. This remains a live debate in macroeconomics.

ℹ️ About the MONIAC

Historical Background

The Phillips Machine — formally the Monetary National Income Analogue Computer (MONIAC) — was built in 1949 by New Zealand economist Bill Phillips while a student at the London School of Economics. Standing nearly 2 metres tall, it used coloured water flowing through transparent pipes to demonstrate macroeconomic relationships physically.

The Phillips Curve Connection

Bill Phillips is doubly famous: for the MONIAC (1949) and for his 1958 paper establishing the empirical relationship between wage inflation and unemployment — the Phillips Curve. This simulator brings both contributions together.

The Original Machine Today

Around 14 original machines were built. Working examples survive at the Science Museum (London), Leeds University, Cambridge, the Reserve Bank of New Zealand, and the Ford Motor Company. The Leeds machine is particularly notable given GSAL's connection to the city.
