Welcome to The Cartel

A-Level Economics — oligopoly, game theory & competition policy. Each team runs an independent firm in the UK roofing materials market — four major suppliers control 85% of sales between them. Every round is a pricing decision, made the way real rival firms make them: secretly, and watching everyone else. What you choose early doesn't just affect this round's profit — it's evidence, and the CMA's Dawn Raid in the climax round will use your whole history against you. Set up your teams below — names and scores save automatically in this browser.

How the game runs

Hand out the workbooks. Each round: project the brief → firms complete the worksheet task → press Reveal for what actually happened → award points → Save. This is a connected story, not four separate scenarios — Round 2's invitation only matters because of how firms behave afterwards, Round 3 tests whether an agreement actually holds, and the 🚨 Dawn Raid is where every firm's Round 2–3 choices catch up with them at once. Remind teams to keep their Round 1–3 worksheet answers in front of them for Round 4.

Debrief hook: the whole game is one long worked example of why concentrated markets are tempted into collusion and why competition law targets the moment of agreement, not the outcome. Round 1 shows that undercutting is the individually rational dominant strategy even though it leaves every firm worse off than if they'd held price — the classic prisoner's dilemma. Rounds 2–3 show how that trap gets "solved" illegally, and why the solution is unstable. The Dawn Raid shows real CMA tools (leniency, fines up to 10% of turnover) in miniature. Firms that say "parallel pricing = automatic proof of collusion" anywhere in Round 4 should lose marks — that distinction is the A-Level differentiator.

Starter — Spot the Cartel Signal

Five real situations where rivals end up charging suspiciously similar prices. Only some of them are illegal. In pairs: rank them from strongest evidence of explicit, illegal collusion to weakest — competition lawyers do this for a living. Two minutes. Gut instinct only.

?
Identical "cover price" bids, hand-delivered minutes apart
Real UK construction tenders: rival firms phoning each other for a deliberately uncompetitive "cover" bid before submitting
This requires direct contact between rivals about a specific price before it's submitted — there is no innocent explanation. In a real 2009 OFT case, over 100 UK construction firms were fined for exactly this practice on public sector tenders, including schools and hospitals
?
Pricing software that auto-matches a rival's price
Two online sellers each used algorithms instructing them not to undercut the other below a floor price
No human ever picked up a phone — the algorithms did the coordinating. The CMA still found this unlawful price-fixing in a real 2016 case (Trod Ltd & GB eye Ltd, online poster sellers): automating an agreement doesn't make it legal
?
A trade body's quarterly lunch, "industry challenges" on the agenda
Major firms in a sector meet regularly through a legitimate trade association
Trade bodies are completely legal — until price gets discussed off the record. Real competition cases have used exactly this kind of meeting as the evidence trail, because minutes, calendar invites and expense claims put rivals in the same room at the same time
?
Every major firm raises prices by the same % in the same week
No announcement, no explanation, prices just move together
This is called parallel pricing — and on its own, it is not illegal. Firms in a concentrated market can rationally watch and match each other without ever communicating. Telling this apart from real collusion is exactly today's hardest skill
?
Two firms both raise prices after a raw-material cost shock
A key input gets more expensive for everyone, industry-wide, at the same time
This is just ordinary competitive behaviour — every firm faces the same higher cost and responds independently. If a regulator ever raided this market, this card alone would never be enough to charge anyone
02:00
The line that matters: cards 1 and 2 are explicit collusion — direct or software-mediated contact about price. Card 3 is the grey zone every real cartel hides in. Cards 4 and 5 are completely legal, however suspicious they look. Today, your own choices will walk that exact line — and by the end, a regulator is going to judge which side of it you landed on.

The Dawn Raid — Live Results

C

"This is the Competition and Markets Authority. We have reasonable grounds to suspect price-fixing in this market. No one leaves the building. Phones on the table."

Before you reveal: ask each firm to total up, from their own Round 2–3 answers, (a) did they ATTEND the trade body meeting, and (b) did they HONOUR the agreed price. Then ask the room directly: does anyone want to confess, right now, before the reveal? The CMA's real leniency programme gives full immunity to the first firm to confess and a reduced fine to the second — after the reveal, it's too late.

CARTEL CONFIRMED — DAWN RAID UNDERWAY
Scoring guide for the Dawn Raid: declined the Round 2 meeting throughout = CLEAN, no penalty. Attended Round 2 AND honoured the price in Round 3 = FULL EXPOSURE, apply a −6 point penalty to Round 4. Attended Round 2 but secretly undercut in Round 3 = PARTIAL EXPOSURE, apply a −3 point penalty — attending the meeting is enough evidence on its own, but a shorter, less stable cartel typically draws a smaller fine. Leniency: the first exposed firm to confess before you reveal has their penalty wiped to 0 entirely (full immunity); the second to confess has their penalty halved. Run the "does anyone want to confess?" moment live and let it breathe — the tension is the lesson, more than any single number.

CMA MARKET STUDY · ONE YEAR ON

A market built for collusion doesn't need a meeting to behave collusively

Even with the cartel broken and the fines paid, four firms in one concentrated market can land on the same prices simply by watching each other and following a price leader — perfectly legally. Competition law punishes explicit collusion (the agreement, the meeting, the algorithm built to coordinate) relatively easily. Tacit collusion that emerges naturally from market structure — few firms, a similar product, high barriers to entry — is far harder to prove, and arguably impossible to fully eliminate without breaking up the market itself.

AQA / EDEXCEL STYLE · OLIGOPOLY & COMPETITION POLICY · HOMEWORK OR NEXT LESSON

"Evaluate the view that breaking up a price-fixing cartel guarantees a genuinely competitive market." (25 marks)

KAA from today: oligopoly & concentration ratios · game theory, dominant strategy & Nash equilibrium (Round 1) · explicit vs tacit collusion (Rounds 2–3) · cartel instability / incentive to cheat (Round 3) · CMA powers — fines up to 10% of turnover, leniency, the criminal cartel offence (Dawn Raid) · price leadership & market concentration (Epilogue).

Evaluation from today: tacit collusion can reproduce cartel-like outcomes without anyone breaking the law · market structure (concentration, barriers to entry, a similar product) makes coordination easy regardless of legality · fines as a deterrent vs the cost of compliance · whether genuine competition needs structural remedies (breaking up firms), not just punishing the cartel that got caught.
