What Are Financial Markets?

Financial markets are markets in which financial assets — money, shares, bonds, currencies, and derivatives — are bought and sold, bringing together those with surplus funds (savers, investors) and those who need funds (borrowers, firms, governments) to allocate capital across the economy.

Why This Topic Matters: financial markets are not a peripheral part of the economy — they are the mechanism through which savings are channelled into productive investment, businesses raise the capital needed to grow, and governments and central banks implement fiscal and monetary policy (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Fiscal Policy and Monetary Policy guides). When financial markets malfunction, as in 2008, the damage spreads rapidly to the whole real economy — the central theme of Sections 18–22.

The Characteristics & Functions of Money

The Functions of Money

Medium of Exchange
Money is widely accepted in exchange for goods and services, eliminating the need for a "double coincidence of wants" that a barter economy would require.
Store of Value
Money retains its purchasing power over time (at least when inflation is low and stable — see the companion BIZ-OMICS Inflation guide), allowing people to save now and spend later.
Measure of Value (Unit of Account)
Money provides a common measure in which the prices of all other goods and services can be expressed and compared.
Standard of Deferred Payment
Money allows debts and future payments to be specified in a way both parties understand and accept — e.g. a loan agreement specifying repayment in a given currency.

The Characteristics of Money

Acceptability
Money must be widely trusted and accepted by others in exchange for goods and services — without this, none of its other functions can operate at all.
Valuable (Holding Value)
Money must hold its value reasonably well over time in order to function as a store of value — a currency losing value rapidly (e.g. during hyperinflation, see the companion BIZ-OMICS Inflation guide) undermines confidence in using it at all.
Portability
Money must be convenient to carry and transfer, so it can be readily used in everyday transactions.
Durability
Money needs to last — physically robust enough to be used repeatedly over time without degrading or falling apart.
Divisibility
Money can be broken down into smaller denominations, allowing transactions of any size to be made precisely, from a coffee to a car.
Hard to Counterfeit
Money must be difficult to reproduce illegitimately — if it could be easily faked, its scarcity (and therefore its value and acceptability) would collapse.

Measuring the Money Supply — Narrow & Broad Money

Narrow Money
The most liquid forms of money — physical cash (notes and coins) in circulation, plus balances readily usable for everyday transactions (e.g. current account deposits). Narrow money measures (such as M0 or M1) capture money that can be spent immediately.
Broad Money
A much wider measure including narrow money plus less liquid forms of savings — time deposits, savings accounts, and other assets that can be converted into cash relatively quickly but are not used for day-to-day spending. The UK's headline broad money measure is M4.
Why the Distinction Matters: the growth rate of broad money is closely watched by monetarist economists (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide's Quantity Theory of Money section) as a leading indicator of future inflation, since M4 captures the full stock of money potentially available to be spent across the economy, not just physical cash in circulation.
4

Types of Financial Market — Money, Capital & Foreign Exchange

Money Markets
Markets for short-term borrowing and lending, typically with maturities under one year — Treasury bills, interbank lending, and short-term commercial paper. Used by banks, firms, and governments to manage short-term liquidity needs.
Capital Markets
Markets for longer-term finance — shares (equities) and bonds with maturities of a year or more — used by firms and governments to fund long-term investment (Section 5). Capital markets are further split into primary and secondary markets (Section 6).
Foreign Exchange (FOREX) Markets
The market in which currencies are bought and sold, determining exchange rates (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Exchange Rates guide's full treatment of the FOREX market, its participants, and how exchange rates are determined).

Primary Markets vs Secondary Markets

Primary Market
Where a financial security is issued for the very first time — a company selling new shares via an IPO, or a government auctioning newly-issued gilts (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide's bonds section). Funds raised here go directly to the issuer.
Secondary Market
Where previously-issued securities are subsequently bought and sold between investors — the London Stock Exchange for shares, or the gilt secondary market where the Bank of England conducts its QE and QT operations. The issuer receives no funds from secondary market trades; it is investors trading with each other.
Why the Distinction Matters: a liquid, well-functioning secondary market is what makes the primary market attractive in the first place — investors are far more willing to buy newly-issued shares or bonds if they know they can sell them on easily later, rather than being locked in until maturity or a company's eventual sale.

Commercial Banks vs Investment Banks

Commercial BanksInvestment Banks
Core BusinessTaking deposits from, and making loans to, households and businesses (Section 8) — the everyday high-street banking most people interact with.Advising on and arranging capital raising (share and bond issues), mergers and acquisitions, and trading securities on their own account or for clients.
Customer BaseIndividual savers, households, and businesses of all sizes.Large corporations, governments, and institutional investors (pension funds, hedge funds).
Risk ProfileGenerally lower-risk, relationship-based lending, though still exposed to the maturity transformation risks explored in Section 18.Typically higher-risk trading and underwriting activity, directly exposed to financial market volatility.
ExampleNatWest, Lloyds, Barclays' high-street operations, Nationwide.Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Barclays' investment banking division.
Why This Distinction Became Politically Important: before 2008, many large banking groups (including UK banks like RBS and Barclays) combined both commercial and investment banking activities within the same institution — meaning losses from risky investment banking trading could threaten the deposits of ordinary retail customers. This is precisely the concern the UK's post-crisis "ring-fencing" reform (Section 20) was designed to address.

The Main Functions of a Commercial Bank

1
Accepting deposits

Providing a safe place for households and firms to hold their money, typically paying interest in return.

2
Providing loans and credit

Lending to households (mortgages, personal loans) and businesses (working capital, investment finance), earning interest income in return — and, in the process, creating new money (Section 11).

3
Facilitating payments

Operating the payments infrastructure (debit cards, standing orders, transfers) that allows money to move between accounts and between banks efficiently.

4
Maturity transformation

Taking in short-term, instantly-withdrawable deposits and transforming them into longer-term loans and mortgages — a core banking function, but also the central source of bank fragility explored fully in Section 18.

5
Risk management services

Offering products (e.g. insurance, hedging instruments) that allow customers to manage financial risk.

The Structure of a Commercial Bank's Balance Sheet

A bank's balance sheet records what it owns (assets) and what it owes (liabilities), plus shareholders' equity — the difference between the two.

Assets (What the Bank Owns / Is Owed)Liabilities (What the Bank Owes)
Cash and reserves held at the central bankCustomer deposits (repayable on demand or short notice)
Loans to customers (mortgages, business loans) — the bank's largest asset by value, but the least liquidWholesale/interbank borrowing from other financial institutions
Government and corporate bonds held as investmentsBonds issued by the bank itself
Shareholders' Equity — the owners' capital stake, acting as a buffer absorbing losses before depositors or other creditors are affected

Worked Example — A Simplified Bank Balance Sheet, Before & After a Financial Crisis

Before Crisis (£bn)After Crisis (£bn)
Total Assets (loans, bonds, cash)10085
Liabilities (deposits, borrowing)9595
Shareholders' Equity (Assets − Liabilities)5−10
Leverage Ratio (Assets ÷ Equity)20:1Insolvent

In this simplified example, a bank starting with just £5bn of equity supporting £100bn of assets (a leverage ratio of 20:1 — common before 2008) only needs asset values to fall by 5% for its equity to be completely wiped out. If loan losses and falling asset values (e.g. mortgage-backed securities losing value) reduce total assets to £85bn while liabilities remain unchanged at £95bn, the bank's equity turns negative — it is technically insolvent, owing more than it owns, precisely the scenario that forced government recapitalisation of UK banks in 2008 (Section 21).

Why This Matters for Regulation: this simple arithmetic is exactly why post-crisis regulation (Section 20) has focused so heavily on requiring banks to hold significantly more equity capital relative to their assets — a bank with a lower leverage ratio (more equity per pound of assets) can absorb much larger losses before becoming insolvent.

Commercial Bank Objectives — Liquidity, Profitability & Security

Liquidity
Holding enough readily-available cash and easily-sellable assets to meet depositors' withdrawal demands at any time, without being forced to sell illiquid assets (like long-term loans) at a loss.
Profitability
Generating sufficient returns for shareholders — primarily through the interest rate spread between what the bank pays depositors and what it charges borrowers, plus fees.
Security (Safety/Solvency)
Maintaining a strong enough capital buffer (Section 18) and prudent lending standards to remain solvent even if a significant share of loans turn bad.
The Central Conflict — The Liquidity-Profitability Trade-Off: these three objectives frequently pull against each other, and the most fundamental tension is specifically known as the liquidity-profitability trade-off. Holding more liquid assets (cash, short-term government bonds) improves liquidity and security, but these assets typically earn a lower return than long-term loans — directly reducing profitability. Chasing higher profitability by lending aggressively to riskier borrowers at higher interest rates, or tying up more funds in long-term illiquid loans, increases returns, but reduces both liquidity (loans cannot be quickly converted back to cash) and security (higher default risk). A bank cannot simultaneously maximise liquidity and maximise profitability — holding an extra pound in cash to improve liquidity is a pound not out earning interest as a loan. Banks must constantly balance this trade-off alongside their security objective, and the 2008 crisis is widely seen as a case where profitability was pursued at the expense of both liquidity and security across much of the global banking system.

How Banks Create Credit

Commercial banks do not simply lend out pre-existing deposits one-for-one — the modern understanding, confirmed by the Bank of England itself, is that banks create new money when they make a loan.

1
A bank approves a loan

Rather than transferring money from an existing depositor's account, the bank simply credits the borrower's account with a new deposit — simultaneously creating a new asset (the loan, an amount owed to the bank) and a new liability (the deposit, an amount owed to the customer) on its balance sheet.

2
The money supply expands

Because the new deposit did not exist before the loan was made, the broad money supply (Section 3) has genuinely increased as a direct result of the bank's lending decision.

3
The borrower spends the new money

As the borrower spends the loan (e.g. paying a builder, buying a car), that money circulates through the economy and typically ends up as a deposit in another bank.

4
Regulation, not reserves, is the binding constraint

Rather than being constrained by needing to hold reserves equal to a fixed fraction of deposits before lending (the traditional "money multiplier" textbook model), modern banks are primarily constrained by regulatory capital and liquidity requirements (Section 18) and by their own assessment of profitable, creditworthy lending opportunities.

Why This Matters: this explains why the Bank of England's Quantitative Easing (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide) does not automatically translate into a proportional rise in bank lending — QE increases bank reserves, but reserves are not the binding constraint on how much banks actually choose to lend, which instead depends on capital requirements, risk appetite, and demand for credit.

The Role of Financial Markets in the Wider Economy

1
Channelling savings into investment

Financial markets connect households and firms with surplus funds to those needing capital, allowing productive investment that would not otherwise be possible from a firm's own retained profits alone.

2
Price discovery

Share and bond prices continuously aggregate information about firms' and governments' prospects, providing signals that guide the efficient allocation of capital across the economy.

3
Facilitating monetary policy transmission

The Bank of England's Bank Rate and QE operations work specifically through financial markets — bond yields, bank lending rates, and asset prices — to influence the wider economy (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide).

4
Enabling risk management

Financial markets allow firms and individuals to insure against and hedge various risks (interest rate changes, currency movements, commodity price swings), supporting more confident long-term investment decisions.

5
Transmitting shocks to the real economy

The same interconnectedness that allows financial markets to perform these positive functions also means problems within them — a credit crunch, a bank failure — can transmit rapidly and severely to the real economy, exactly the systemic risk explored in Section 19.

Bonds, Coupons, Maturity & Yield — Full Numerical Treatment

Coupon
The fixed annual interest payment a bond pays, expressed as a percentage of its face value — set at issue and never changing over the bond's life.
Maturity
The date on which the bond's face value (the original amount borrowed) is repaid in full to whoever holds the bond at that time.
Yield
The effective annual return an investor earns based on the bond's current market price, not its original face value.
Yield = (Annual Coupon Payment ÷ Current Market Price) × 100

Worked Example 1 — Calculating a Basic Yield

A UK government bond (gilt) has a face value of £1,000 and a coupon rate of 4% (paying £40 per year). If it currently trades at its face value of £1,000:

Yield = (£40 ÷ £1,000) × 100 = 4.0%

Worked Example 2 — How Falling Confidence Raises Yields

Suppose investors become worried about the UK government's fiscal position — perhaps following a poorly-received Budget (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Fiscal Policy guide's coverage of the September 2022 mini-budget) — and demand a higher return to compensate for the perceived extra risk of holding UK debt. Investors sell the bond, pushing its market price down to £800:

New Yield = (£40 ÷ £800) × 100 = 5.0%

The coupon payment itself has not changed — bondholders still receive exactly £40 per year — but because the price investors are willing to pay has fallen, the effective yield for anyone buying at the new lower price has risen from 4.0% to 5.0%. This is precisely the mechanism behind headlines describing gilt yields "spiking" during a loss of confidence: it reflects falling bond prices, not a change in the underlying coupon.

Worked Example 3 — Rising Confidence Lowering Yields

Conversely, if the same bond becomes more attractive — for example, if the Bank of England begins large-scale gilt purchases under Quantitative Easing (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide) — increased demand could push its price up to £1,250:

New Yield = (£40 ÷ £1,250) × 100 = 3.2%

Higher demand pushes the price up and the yield down — precisely the mechanism through which QE lowers long-term borrowing costs across the economy.

Why This Matters for Government Finances: a government must pay the prevailing market yield on any newly-issued debt. If confidence deteriorates and yields rise across the board, the government's future borrowing becomes structurally more expensive — directly linking bond market sentiment to the sustainability of government finances (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Balance of Payments guide's discussion of bond yields and national debt).

Why Bond Prices & Interest Rates Move Inversely

Section 14's worked examples illustrate the mechanics; this section explains why a change in prevailing interest rates moves bond prices in the opposite direction.

Market interest rates rise (e.g. the Bank Rate increases, or new government bonds are issued with higher coupons) → newly-issued bonds now offer a more attractive coupon than older, existing bonds paying a lower fixed rate → investors no longer wish to hold the older, lower-coupon bonds at their original price, since they could earn more buying new bonds instead → investors sell the older bonds, increasing their supply on the secondary market (Section 6) → with more sellers than buyers at the old price, the old bonds' market price falls → the old bond's price keeps falling until its yield (coupon ÷ price) rises enough to become competitive with the new, higher-coupon bonds → equilibrium is restored, but only because the old bond's price has fallen — demonstrating the inverse relationship directly.
The Reverse Case: exactly the same logic runs in reverse when interest rates fall — existing higher-coupon bonds become relatively more attractive than newly-issued lower-coupon bonds, so investors bid their price up, which mechanically lowers their yield until it again becomes consistent with prevailing market rates.

Regulating the Financial System — Why It's Needed

Why Financial Markets Require Regulation

Information asymmetry

Depositors and investors typically know far less about a bank's true risk exposure than the bank's own management — regulation aims to protect less-informed customers from excessive risk-taking they cannot properly assess themselves.

Systemic risk

Because banks are deeply interconnected (Section 19), the failure of one institution can threaten the stability of the entire financial system and real economy — a cost that extends far beyond the failing bank's own shareholders and management, justifying regulation in the wider public interest.

Moral hazard

Without robust regulation, banks may take excessive risks knowing they are likely to be rescued if things go wrong (Section 19) — regulation aims to constrain this risk-taking directly, rather than relying purely on the threat of failure as a deterrent.

Consumer protection

Ensuring products are sold fairly, fees are transparent, and customers are treated appropriately — separate from purely prudential (safety and soundness) concerns.

17

UK Financial Regulation — The Bank of England, PRA, FPC & FCA

Following the 2008 crisis, the UK fundamentally restructured its financial regulatory framework via the Financial Services Act 2012, moving away from the pre-crisis Financial Services Authority (FSA) model widely judged to have failed.

The Bank of England
Sits at the centre of the UK's regulatory framework, housing both the PRA and the FPC, alongside its traditional monetary policy role (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide).
The Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA)
Responsible for the "microprudential" regulation of individual banks, insurers, and major investment firms — supervising each firm's own safety and soundness, capital adequacy, and risk management (Section 18).
The Financial Policy Committee (FPC)
Responsible for "macroprudential" regulation — monitoring and addressing risks to the stability of the financial system as a whole, rather than any single firm, with the power to set system-wide requirements (e.g. mortgage lending limits, or the countercyclical capital buffer requiring banks to hold more capital during a credit boom).
The Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)
Responsible for conduct regulation — ensuring firms treat customers fairly, markets function with integrity, and competition works well for consumers — covering the full range of financial firms, not just banks.
Micro vs Macroprudential — The Key Distinction: the PRA asks "is this individual bank safe?"; the FPC asks "is the financial system as a whole safe?" — a bank can look safe in isolation while still contributing to a dangerous system-wide bubble (e.g. if many banks simultaneously expand mortgage lending during a housing boom), which is precisely the kind of risk the FPC's macroprudential remit was created to catch, having been entirely absent from the pre-2008 regulatory framework.

Why Banks Fail — Maturity Transformation & Liquidity/Capital Ratios

The core structural vulnerability of banking lies in maturity transformation (Section 8) — borrowing short-term (deposits, withdrawable on demand) to lend long-term (mortgages, business loans, which cannot be called back quickly).

A bank funds long-term loans and investments using short-term, instantly-withdrawable deposits → this works fine under normal conditions, since not all depositors withdraw their money simultaneously → but if depositors lose confidence and demand their money back all at once (a bank run) → the bank cannot instantly convert its long-term loans back into cash without selling them at a loss (since buyers know the seller is desperate) → the bank may become unable to meet withdrawal demands even though its underlying loan book might eventually have been repaid in full → the bank fails not because its assets were worthless, but because of a mismatch between the timing of its assets and liabilities — precisely what happened to Silicon Valley Bank (Section 22).
Cash Ratio
The proportion of a bank's short-term liabilities covered specifically by pure cash and central bank reserves (its most liquid assets of all) — the higher the cash ratio, the more liquid the bank is, since cash can meet withdrawal demands instantly with no need to sell anything.
Liquidity Ratio
The proportion of a bank's assets held in cash or easily-sellable, high-quality liquid assets relative to its short-term liabilities — a higher ratio means the bank can meet a larger volume of sudden withdrawals without needing to sell illiquid assets at a loss.
Capital Ratio
The proportion of a bank's assets funded by shareholders' equity (Section 9) rather than debt/deposits — a higher capital ratio means the bank can absorb larger losses on its loans before becoming insolvent, since losses are borne by shareholders first.
Debt-to-Equity Ratio
Total debt (deposits plus other borrowing) divided by shareholders' equity — this one runs in the opposite direction to the other three: a lower debt-to-equity ratio is safer, since it means the bank relies less on debt and more on its own loss-absorbing equity cushion for every pound of funding. A high debt-to-equity ratio signals a highly leveraged, less resilient bank.

Worked Example — Calculating All Four Ratios

Consider a simplified bank with the following balance sheet: total assets of £200bn, of which £10bn is held in pure cash/central bank reserves and a further £15bn in easily-sellable high-quality liquid assets (giving £25bn in total liquid assets); short-term liabilities of £150bn; shareholders' equity of £16bn; and total debt (deposits plus other borrowing) of £184bn.

RatioFormulaCalculationResult
Cash RatioCash ÷ Short-Term Liabilities£10bn ÷ £150bn6.7%
Liquidity RatioTotal Liquid Assets ÷ Short-Term Liabilities£25bn ÷ £150bn16.7%
Capital RatioShareholders' Equity ÷ Total Assets£16bn ÷ £200bn8.0%
Debt-to-Equity RatioTotal Debt ÷ Shareholders' Equity£184bn ÷ £16bn11.5 (i.e. 11.5:1)
Interpreting the Result: this bank's 8% capital ratio and 11.5:1 debt-to-equity ratio mean it is funded overwhelmingly by debt rather than equity — for every £1 of its own shareholders' money, it has borrowed £11.50 from depositors and other creditors. Its 16.7% liquidity ratio means it could meet withdrawal demands equal to roughly one-sixth of its short-term liabilities purely from liquid assets without needing to sell any long-term loans at a loss — comfortable under normal conditions, but a severe enough bank run (Section 22's Silicon Valley Bank case saw depositors demand $42bn in a single day) could still exceed this buffer.

Worked Example — Comparing a "Safer" and "Riskier" Bank

RatioBank A ("Safer")Bank B ("Riskier")
Cash Ratio10.0%3.0%
Liquidity Ratio25.0%8.0%
Capital Ratio14.0%4.0%
Debt-to-Equity Ratio6.1:124:1

Bank A holds far more cash and liquid assets relative to its short-term liabilities (higher cash and liquidity ratios), and funds a much larger share of its assets with loss-absorbing equity rather than debt (a higher capital ratio and, consistently, a much lower debt-to-equity ratio of 6.1:1 rather than 24:1). Bank B's position mirrors the pre-2008 balance sheets many banks actually held (Section 9's worked example used a comparable 20:1 leverage ratio) — highly profitable in good times, since more of its funding comes from cheap debt rather than equity, but acutely vulnerable to even a modest shock to asset values or a moderate deposit withdrawal, precisely the liquidity-profitability trade-off (Section 10) in action at the level of the whole balance sheet.

Liquidity vs Capital — Two Different Risks: a bank can be insolvent (liabilities exceed assets — a capital problem, reflected in a low capital ratio and high debt-to-equity ratio) without being illiquid (unable to meet immediate withdrawal demands, reflected in the cash and liquidity ratios), and vice versa — a fundamentally sound bank can still fail purely from a liquidity crisis if depositors panic simultaneously (see the BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide's Lender of Last Resort section on Northern Rock, precisely this kind of liquidity-driven failure). Robust ratios across all four measures, enforced by the PRA (Section 17), are designed to protect against both risks simultaneously.

Moral Hazard & Systemic Risk

Moral Hazard
The risk that a bank takes on excessive, imprudent risk because it expects to be rescued (by the central bank as Lender of Last Resort, or by a government bailout) if things go badly wrong — insulating decision-makers from the full consequences of their own risk-taking.
"Too Big to Fail"
The specific concern that the largest, most interconnected banks are implicitly guaranteed rescue because their disorderly failure would be too damaging to the wider financial system to allow — a particularly acute form of moral hazard, since these institutions may reasonably expect the most certain rescue.
Systemic Risk
The risk that problems at one financial institution spread to others and to the wider real economy, because financial institutions are deeply interconnected through interbank lending, shared exposures, and confidence effects — the reason financial sector regulation is treated as a matter of public interest, not just a concern for each firm's own shareholders.

How Financial Sector Problems Transmit to the Real Economy

1
A bank (or several banks) suffers major losses

E.g. from a fall in asset prices, loan defaults, or a sudden liquidity crisis (Section 18).

2
Banks become reluctant to lend (a "credit crunch")

To preserve capital and liquidity, banks tighten lending standards sharply, even to otherwise creditworthy borrowers — see the companion BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide's credit crunch chain.

3
Firms and households cannot access credit

Businesses cannot fund working capital or investment; households cannot access mortgages or consumer credit — spending and investment fall directly as a result.

4
Aggregate demand falls, deepening a recession

What began as a financial sector problem becomes a real economy problem — falling output, rising unemployment — precisely the transmission mechanism behind the severity of the 2008–09 recession.

Post-Crisis Regulatory Reforms

Basel III Reserve/Capital Requirements
An international regulatory framework significantly raising the minimum quality and quantity of capital banks must hold relative to their risk-weighted assets, alongside new minimum liquidity requirements — directly targeting the leverage and liquidity weaknesses exposed in 2008 (Section 9).
UK Ring-Fencing
Following the 2011 Vickers Report (Independent Commission on Banking), UK banks with retail deposits above a threshold have, since 2019, been required to legally separate ("ring-fence") their everyday retail banking operations from their riskier investment banking activities within the same corporate group — directly addressing the commercial/investment banking blurring identified in Section 7, so that losses in investment banking cannot threaten ordinary depositors' money.
"Bail-In, Not Bail-Out"
Under the UK's post-crisis resolution regime (built on the Banking Act 2009 and EU Bank Recovery and Resolution Directive), a failing bank's shareholders and certain bondholders can now be forced to absorb losses ("bailed in") to recapitalise the bank, before any taxpayer money is used — directly reversing the 2008 experience where shareholders and management were largely protected while taxpayers bore the cost (Section 21).
Why "Bail-In" Directly Addresses Moral Hazard: if shareholders and bondholders know they will bear losses first in a future failure, they have a much stronger incentive to monitor and constrain a bank's risk-taking themselves, rather than assuming taxpayers will ultimately cover the cost — directly targeting the moral hazard problem identified in Section 19.

The Cost of Intervention — Bailouts in the UK

★ REAL WORLD: The Scale & Cost of the UK's 2008 Bank Bailout
Facing the near-collapse of its banking system, the UK government injected a total of £45.8 billion into RBS alone (taking public ownership as high as 84%), while Lloyds and HBOS together raised £17 billion in government-backed capital. Beyond direct capital injections, the government also provided financial guarantees — on bank debt, deposits, and troubled assets — that the National Audit Office estimated reached over £1 trillion at their peak, though guarantees only cost the taxpayer if they are actually called upon. As of the Office for Budget Responsibility's final assessment in early 2018, the net cost of the entire package of interventions to the public finances was estimated at around £23 billion — reflecting a £27 billion loss on the RBS rescue specifically (shares were sold for far less than the government paid for them), broadly offset by the government roughly recovering, or in some cases profiting from, its investments in Lloyds and other schemes.

Weighing the Cost of Intervention

Critics point to the direct £23bn net fiscal cost, the vastly larger headline sums (£45.8bn to RBS alone, guarantees peaking above £1 trillion) that had to be committed and risked, and the contribution rising public debt from the bailout made to the subsequent austerity programme (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Fiscal Policy guide). Supporters counter that the alternative — allowing the banking system to collapse entirely — would very likely have produced a far deeper depression, mass unemployment, and a much larger ultimate cost to the public finances and the real economy than the bailout itself; on this view, even the £23bn net cost represents remarkably good value against the scale of disaster averted.

Recent Financial Collapses — Silicon Valley Bank

The March 2023 collapse of Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) — the largest US bank failure since 2008 — is a textbook real-world illustration of the maturity transformation risk explored in Section 18, playing out in a modern, social-media-accelerated context.

1
Deposits surged during the pandemic tech boom

SVB's deposits, overwhelmingly from technology start-ups and venture capital-backed firms, roughly doubled from $62 billion in March 2020 to $124 billion in March 2021.

2
SVB invested heavily in long-dated bonds

Seeking higher returns than short-term instruments offered, SVB placed a large share of these deposits into long-term US Treasury bonds — creating exactly the maturity mismatch (short-term deposits funding long-term assets) described in Section 18.

3
Rising interest rates crushed the value of those bonds

As the Federal Reserve raised rates sharply to combat the 2021–23 inflation surge (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Inflation guide), the market value of SVB's existing, lower-yielding bonds fell sharply (Section 15) — by the end of 2022, SVB had over $15 billion in unrealised losses on its bond portfolio.

4
Rising rates also squeezed tech start-ups, forcing deposit withdrawals

Higher rates made venture capital funding scarcer, so SVB's start-up clients began drawing down their deposits to fund operations, forcing SVB to sell some of its bonds to raise cash.

5
Crystallising losses triggered a panic

On 8 March 2023, SVB announced it had sold over $21 billion of securities at a realised loss of $1.8 billion and would need to raise fresh capital — instead of reassuring markets, this announcement, amplified rapidly through venture capital networks and social media, triggered a classic bank run.

6
The bank run overwhelmed the bank within 48 hours

Depositors attempted to withdraw a staggering $42 billion in a single day (9 March) — with over 85% of SVB's deposits uninsured (above the $250,000 US federal deposit insurance limit), depositors had every incentive to withdraw first and ask questions later. Regulators closed the bank on 10 March 2023.

★ REAL WORLD: The Resolution — US and UK Responses
US authorities took the unusual step of guaranteeing all SVB deposits in full — including those above the standard $250,000 insurance limit — explicitly to prevent contagion spreading to other regional banks, at an estimated cost to the FDIC's Deposit Insurance Fund of around $20 billion, though officials stressed no ordinary taxpayer funds were used (the Fund is financed by levies on banks themselves). In the UK, the Bank of England and Treasury facilitated the sale of Silicon Valley Bank UK — which held around £6.7 billion in deposits, mostly from UK tech firms — to HSBC for a nominal £1, protecting all UK depositors in full with no cost to UK taxpayers, and demonstrating the UK's post-2008 resolution regime (Section 20) functioning as designed: an orderly transfer of a failing bank's business to a stronger institution, avoiding both a disorderly collapse and a taxpayer-funded bailout.
The Exam-Relevant Lesson: SVB is a rare case where a bank failed not primarily due to bad loans or fraud, but from a textbook maturity mismatch (Section 18) combined with an extremely concentrated, uninsured depositor base uniquely prone to panic — and where the eventual UK resolution shows the post-2008 reforms (Section 20) working precisely as intended, resolving a bank failure without a taxpayer bailout.

Exam Technique & Model Chains of Reasoning

The Golden Chain: How a Loss of Confidence Raises Bond Yields

Investors lose confidence in a government's fiscal position (e.g. following an unfunded tax-cutting Budget) → investors sell existing government bonds, fearing future default risk or excessive future debt issuance → increased selling raises the supply of bonds on the secondary market relative to demand → the bond's market price falls → because the coupon payment is fixed, the yield (coupon ÷ price) rises → the government must now pay this higher prevailing yield on any new debt it issues → the government's borrowing costs rise across the board, potentially forcing a change in fiscal policy to restore investor confidence.

The Golden Chain: A Bank Run (Maturity Transformation Failure)

A bank funds long-term assets (loans, long-dated bonds) using short-term, instantly-withdrawable deposits → a shock (rising interest rates devaluing bond holdings, or a loss of confidence) prompts depositors to fear the bank cannot meet its obligations → depositors rush to withdraw their funds before others do (a bank run) → to raise cash quickly, the bank is forced to sell its long-term assets at a loss, since buyers know the seller is desperate → crystallising these losses further damages confidence and accelerates withdrawals → the bank becomes unable to meet withdrawal demands and fails, even if its underlying assets might eventually have been worth their full value if held to maturity.

Key Exam Tips

1

Always use the yield formula explicitly (Coupon ÷ Price) with a worked numerical example — demonstrating the mechanics earns more credit than simply asserting the inverse relationship.

2

Distinguish microprudential (PRA) from macroprudential (FPC) regulation precisely — a common source of confusion and lost marks.

3

Never conflate liquidity risk with solvency risk — a bank can be liquid but insolvent, or solvent but illiquid; both are genuine, distinct causes of bank failure.

4

Bring in real-world context for maximum marks: the 2008 UK bailout figures (£45.8bn to RBS, £23bn net cost), UK ring-fencing and bail-in reforms, and the 2023 Silicon Valley Bank collapse are all precise, high-value examples.

5

Link moral hazard explicitly to specific policy responses — bail-in, ring-fencing, and higher capital requirements are all direct regulatory attempts to address moral hazard, not simply abstract concepts in isolation.

6

Evaluate — never just describe. For questions on the cost of bailouts or the case for regulation, weigh the direct fiscal cost against the (larger, harder to quantify) cost of allowing systemic collapse, and reach a balanced judgement.
