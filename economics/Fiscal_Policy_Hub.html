Fiscal Policy & Government Spending

Why governments spend, how automatic stabilisers work, and what happens when you control the budget — all in one place.

Demand-Side Reasons for Government Spending

Demand-side fiscal policy focuses on managing aggregate demand to achieve macroeconomic objectives. Government spending is a key tool of expansionary fiscal policy used to stimulate activity during recessions or periods of low growth.

The Keynesian Perspective

Keynes argued that during recessions, private sector demand is insufficient to maintain full employment. He advocated active government intervention through increased public spending to compensate for falling private consumption and investment — directly challenging the classical view that markets automatically self-correct.

The mechanism: ↑ G → ↑ AD → ↑ Real GDP → ↓ Unemployment

The Fiscal Multiplier

An initial injection of government spending creates a chain of further spending as recipients re-spend their income. The multiplier (k) = 1 / MPW, where MPW is the marginal propensity to withdraw (save + tax + import).

  • Transfer payments to low-income households: multiplier ~1.4–1.6 (high MPC)
  • Tax cuts for higher earners: multiplier ~0.4–0.6 (proportion saved)
  • Infrastructure capital spending: multiplier ~1.5–1.8 (long-term supply-side effect)

Counter-Cyclical vs Pro-Cyclical Policy

Counter-cyclical (Keynesian): Increase spending in recessions; reduce in booms. This moderates the business cycle and stabilises output and employment.

Pro-cyclical (austerity view): Cut spending during recessions to reduce deficits. Critics argue this amplifies downturns by reducing aggregate demand precisely when it is already weak — the 2010-15 UK experience.

Limitations of Demand-Side Spending

  • Time lags: Recognition, decision, and implementation lags mean spending may arrive after recovery has begun
  • Crowding out: Government borrowing may raise interest rates, reducing private investment
  • Import leakage: Some stimulus is spent on imports, reducing domestic multiplier
  • Debt accumulation: Persistent deficits raise debt-to-GDP ratio, constraining future fiscal space
  • Inflation risk: Near full employment, demand stimulus generates inflation rather than real output growth

UK Evidence: Demand-Side Spending in Practice

2008–09 crisis response: VAT cut 17.5%→15% (cost £12.4bn); accelerated capital allowances; stamp duty holiday. Limited multiplier evidence — savings rates rose as confidence fell, reducing spending pass-through.

COVID-19 (2020): Furlough scheme (£70bn+), business support grants, SEISS — direct income replacement to maintain aggregate demand. More targeted and effective than 2008 VAT cut.

Lesson: Cash transfers to constrained households (high MPC) generate larger multipliers than broad tax cuts. The composition of demand stimulus matters as much as its size.

✅ Advantages of Demand-Side Spending

  • Fast impact — transfer payments and public sector wages reach economy quickly
  • Targeted support for most vulnerable during recessions
  • Multiplier effects amplify initial injection
  • Can prevent hysteresis — permanent damage from prolonged unemployment
  • Provides automatic stabilisation via benefits system

❌ Disadvantages of Demand-Side Spending

  • Risk of inflation if economy near capacity
  • Increases national debt — future interest payments crowd out spending
  • Crowding out may partially offset stimulus
  • Political difficulty reversing once implemented
  • Multiplier uncertain — depends heavily on MPC and openness

Supply-Side Reasons for Government Spending

Supply-side government spending aims to increase the productive capacity of the economy — shifting the LRAS curve rightward and expanding the Production Possibility Frontier. Unlike demand-side policy, well-designed supply-side investment expands output without generating inflation.

The LRAS / PPF Mechanism

LRAS is determined by the quantity and quality of factors of production. Supply-side spending shifts LRAS outward by: increasing capital stock (infrastructure); improving human capital quality (education, health); enhancing total factor productivity (R&D, digital infrastructure); and reducing inefficiencies (housing, transport).

✅ Advantages

  • Non-inflationary growth — expands capacity rather than just demand
  • Permanent benefits — infrastructure creates lasting improvements
  • Corrects market failure — addresses under-provision of public goods and positive externalities
  • Improves international competitiveness

❌ Limitations

  • Long time lags — benefits may take decades (HS2: 2030s at earliest)
  • Opportunity costs — resources committed cannot be used elsewhere
  • Cost overruns: Crossrail £4bn over; HS2 costs doubled to £100bn+
  • Political short-termism favours projects with visible short-term gains
  • Crowding out private investment if funded by borrowing

UK Productivity Gap

UK productivity (output per hour) is approximately 15–20% below the G7 average. Decades of infrastructure underinvestment — public investment fell from 3.5% of GDP (2009) to 2.5% (2018), one of the lowest in the OECD — is a significant explanatory factor. Strategic supply-side spending is therefore both an economic and a competitiveness necessity.

Addressing Market Failures Through Government Spending

Market failures occur when free markets fail to allocate resources efficiently, resulting in a net welfare loss. Government spending corrects these failures by providing goods markets under-provide or fail to provide at all.

Non-excludability + Non-rivalry = Free rider problem. Rational individuals refuse to pay for goods they can consume for free, so private firms cannot profitably supply them. Government provision funded by taxation is the only solution.

National Defence (£53.1bn, 2024/25): The quintessential public good. Everyone is protected equally; one person's protection does not reduce another's. No private market could charge individuals — free-riding would be universal.

Street lighting, flood defences, public health surveillance, basic scientific research: All share non-excludability and non-rivalry to varying degrees, justifying public provision.

Evaluation: Determining which goods are truly public (vs quasi-public) is contested. Some argue national parks, roads, and broadcasting are public goods; others disagree. Misclassification leads to government failure.

Merit goods are goods that generate positive externalities and are under-consumed at market prices because individuals undervalue their social benefit (information asymmetry, myopia, or bounded rationality).

Healthcare (NHS, ~£183bn/yr): Without public provision, under-consumption of healthcare would reduce workforce productivity, increase inequality, and generate negative externalities (communicable disease spread). The NHS treats healthcare as a right, not a commodity, preventing the welfare loss from pure market provision.

Education (~£102bn/yr): Private market would under-provide education because individuals cannot borrow against future human capital, and because social returns (reduced crime, civic participation, technological spillovers) exceed private returns. Free compulsory education corrects this.

Early Years Investment: Every £1 of early childhood education generates £7–10 in long-term benefits — the highest return of any education spending. Private markets severely under-invest due to inability to capture these diffuse social returns.

Market income distribution reflects factor endowments, luck, and inherited advantage — not social preference. Left uncorrected, markets generate inequality that: reduces social cohesion; impairs social mobility; constrains aggregate demand (lower MPC at top); and may violate widely-held fairness norms.

Universal Credit (~£80bn/yr): Means-tested income support that expands automatically in recessions (acting as an automatic stabiliser) and maintains consumption among constrained households.

State Pension (~£110bn/yr): Insurance against old-age poverty — individuals systematically undersave for retirement due to myopia and present bias. Mandatory state provision corrects this market failure.

Evaluation: Generous benefits may create moral hazard (reduced work incentive), but evidence suggests modest effects for most benefits. The UC taper rate (55%) is designed to preserve work incentives while maintaining support.

Climate spending (£30bn, 2021–26): Carbon emissions generate negative externalities not reflected in market prices. Government spending on renewable energy subsidies (CfD scheme), green R&D, and adaptation corrects this under-investment.

Environment Agency (£1.2bn/yr): Monitoring and enforcement prevents firms externalising pollution costs onto society — a direct correction of negative production externalities.

Public health campaigns (£500m/yr): Individuals underestimate health risks due to optimism bias and incomplete information. Government provision of accurate health information corrects information failures — a type of merit good intervention.

Evaluation — Government failure risk: Every intervention creates the risk of government failure — bureaucracy, political interference, and misaligned incentives may generate new inefficiencies that exceed the market failure being corrected.

Debt Servicing: The Opportunity Cost of Past Borrowing

Debt servicing — interest payments on accumulated national debt — is the fastest-growing component of UK government expenditure. It represents a direct opportunity cost of past borrowing decisions, constraining current spending choices.

The Scale of the Problem (2024/25)

National debt: ~£2,700bn (~98% of GDP), up from 35% in 2007/08

Annual debt interest: ~£110bn — exceeding the entire education budget and second only to health and social protection

Trajectory: Debt interest has more than doubled in real terms since 2010, driven by rising debt stock, higher interest rates (Bank Rate peaked at 5.25% in 2023), and ~25% of debt being index-linked (inflated by 2021–23 inflation surge)

Short-Run Constraints from Debt Servicing

  • Crowding out productive spending — since 2010, real-terms cuts to public services partly reflected rising debt interest absorbing revenue
  • Reduced fiscal space — high debt limited COVID support compared to what might otherwise have been possible
  • Political constraints — public concern over debt forces prioritisation of deficit reduction over optimal spending
  • Vulnerability to interest rate rises — 2022–23 rate cycle added ~£20bn annually to debt interest costs

Long-Run Arguments for Maintaining Debt Payments

  • Fiscal credibility: Market confidence keeps borrowing costs low — lost credibility (Greece 2010–12, UK mini-budget 2022) triggers sudden spike in gilt yields
  • Intergenerational equity: Defaulting transfers costs to future generations who had no say in spending decisions
  • Preserves fiscal space: Sustainable debt path enables borrowing for future crises
  • Reserve currency status: UK's creditor relationship with international investors supports sterling and financing costs

The Austerity Debate

Pro-austerity (monetarist/OBR view): Rapid deficit reduction restores confidence, reduces risk premia, and avoids debt spiral. "Expansionary fiscal contractions" may be possible if credibility effects dominate.

Anti-austerity (Keynesian view): Premature tightening prolongs recessions when multipliers are high. Post-2010 UK austerity coincided with sluggish growth vs the US fiscal expansion. Low interest rates made debt servicing affordable; the priority should have been growth-enhancing investment.

Evidence: IMF research (Blanchard & Leigh, 2013) found fiscal multipliers were significantly underestimated in 2010–12 austerity projections, suggesting austerity damaged growth more than predicted. Optimal policy is likely context-dependent: borrow at the zero lower bound; consolidate during expansion.

Fiscal Balance Analysis

How Automatic Stabilisers Work

Automatic stabilisers are features of fiscal policy that moderate economic fluctuations without requiring new legislation or policy decisions. They work through the tax and benefit system to provide counter-cyclical support — automatically.

The Counter-Cyclical Mechanism

During recessions: Tax revenues fall automatically (less income = less tax) while government spending rises automatically (more unemployment claims, more means-tested support). The fiscal balance worsens — injecting demand into the economy.

During booms: Tax revenues rise automatically (more income = more tax) while government spending falls (fewer unemployment claims). The fiscal balance improves — withdrawing demand, cooling the economy.

Key UK Automatic Stabilisers

  • Progressive Income Tax: Higher earners pay proportionally more — tax revenues are highly sensitive to GDP changes as earners move between brackets
  • Universal Credit: Means-tested support expands as incomes fall in recessions
  • Jobseeker's Allowance: Rises automatically as unemployment increases
  • VAT & Corporation Tax: Consumption and profit taxes fall during downturns
  • Working Tax Credits: Expand during economic stress

Why They Are Valuable

  • Speed: Activate immediately — no parliamentary approval required
  • Predictability: Economic agents can anticipate support, maintaining confidence
  • No political lag: Avoid recognition, decision, and implementation delays
  • Counter-cyclical by design: Expand when needed, contract when less needed
  • Universal reach: Every eligible person receives support automatically

Limitations

  • Cannot fully offset severe shocks — COVID-19 required massive discretionary support on top
  • Size is fixed by existing tax/benefit structure — cannot be calibrated to the scale of shock
  • Coverage gaps revealed by COVID: newly self-employed, gig workers, company directors excluded
  • Generous benefits may reduce work incentives (moral hazard) at the margin
  • Increase deficit during downturns — raises debt-to-GDP ratio

Automatic vs Discretionary: The Key Distinction

Automatic stabilisers: Built into the system; no new legislation required; activate immediately; examples: progressive income tax, unemployment benefits, means-tested welfare.

Discretionary fiscal policy: Requires deliberate government decisions and parliamentary approval; examples: VAT rate changes, emergency spending packages, fiscal stimulus. Subject to policy lags but can be scaled to match the crisis — as seen in COVID-19's £400bn+ support package.

Key Economic Concepts

The Fiscal Balance

Fiscal Balance = Tax Revenue − Government Expenditure

  • Surplus (balance > 0): Revenues exceed spending — government is a net saver
  • Deficit (balance < 0): Spending exceeds revenues — government is borrowing
  • Cyclically-adjusted balance: Removes the automatic effect to reveal the underlying structural position

Automatic stabilisers cause the balance to worsen in recessions (desirable — counter-cyclical) and improve in booms. This is economically beneficial even if politically uncomfortable.

The Output Gap

The difference between actual and potential GDP:

  • Negative output gap: Economy below capacity — unemployment, deflationary pressure. Automatic stabilisers inject demand.
  • Positive output gap: Economy above capacity — inflationary pressure. Automatic stabilisers withdraw demand.

Automatic stabilisers are most effective when output gaps are large. Near full capacity, they may be less necessary — and spending may generate more inflation than output.

Debt-to-GDP Ratio

Government debt as % of annual GDP — the key fiscal sustainability indicator:

  • Below 60%: Generally sustainable (Maastricht criterion)
  • 60–90%: Moderate risk; manageable with strong growth
  • Above 90%: High risk; may constrain future policy options (IMF research: growth drag at high debt)

UK trajectory: 35% (2007) → 65% (2010) → 85% (2019) → 100%+ (2021) → ~98% (2024)

Tax Elasticity & Stabiliser Strength

A progressive income tax system is a stronger automatic stabiliser than a flat tax because:

  • As incomes fall, earners drop into lower brackets — effective tax rate falls faster than income
  • Tax revenue is more sensitive to GDP changes (elasticity > 1)
  • The simulator uses a tax elasticity of 1.6 — each 1% fall in GDP generates a 1.6% fall in tax revenue

This is why a flat tax (proportional) would be a weaker automatic stabiliser — revenue would fall in direct proportion to income, not more than proportionately.
