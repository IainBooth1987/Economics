The Cartel
Oligopoly & Collusion

A-Level Economics · Game theory, oligopoly & competition policy

Welcome to the roofing materials market. You run an independent firm. Across this lesson you'll make four connected pricing decisions — and by the end, the consequences of your earlier choices come back to find you, live, in a regulator's raid. Keep every page in front of you; you'll need to refer back to your own answers.

Throughout, watch for two key terms: tacit collusion (similar prices reached without any agreement — completely legal) and explicit collusion (an actual agreement on price — a cartel, and illegal). Mixing these up costs marks at A-Level.

1ROUND 1

Set Your Price

Max 15 points

Jargon check

In an oligopoly, every firm's profit depends on what rivals do too — this is interdependence. A payoff matrix shows the outcome for every combination of choices. The strategy that earns you the most whatever your rival does is your dominant strategy.

"Every firm in this market watches everyone else's price like a hawk. Whatever you choose, it had better make sense no matter what THEY do."
Task

Choose your price for this quarter: UNDERCUT or HOLD. Your rivals choose at the same time, in secret. Use the payoff matrix to work out your best move.

The payoff matrix — quarterly profit (£000s)

  • Both firms HOLD → both earn £40k
  • You UNDERCUT, rival HOLDS → you earn £70k, rival earns £5k
  • Both firms UNDERCUT → both earn £15k

1Your choice

  • UNDERCUT — price below the going market rate
  • HOLD — price at the going market rate

2ROUND 2

The Trade Body Invitation

Max 15 points

Jargon check

Lawful price-watching across firms can still produce similar prices — tacit collusion. The moment rivals actually discuss or agree a price together, it becomes explicit collusion — a cartel — and is illegal, regardless of whether anything is ever written down.

"An invitation has arrived: 'The Roofing Suppliers Forum invites you to a private dinner to discuss industry sustainability and shared challenges.' Everyone who's anyone will be there."
Task

Decide: ATTEND the dinner (where, off the record, firms are expected to agree a price) or DECLINE and stay independent. This decision is recorded for the rest of the game.

1Your choice

  • ATTEND — join the informal price agreement
  • DECLINE — stay independent, set your own price

3ROUND 3

Hold the Line or Break Ranks

Max 15 points

Jargon check

Cartels are inherently unstable. Every member has a private incentive to secretly undercut the agreed price while everyone else holds it. This is one reason real cartels collapse, get reported by an insider, or are exposed by someone applying for leniency — confessing for a reduced (or zero) fine.

"The agreed price is holding... for now. But you've done the maths: if everyone else sticks to it and you quietly undercut by 5%, you take their customers and no one notices for a quarter."
Task

If you attended Round 2: decide whether to HONOUR the agreed price or SECRETLY UNDERCUT it anyway. If you declined Round 2: just price independently, as in Round 1.

1If you attended Round 2

  • HONOUR — keep to the agreed price, the cartel stays intact
  • SECRETLY UNDERCUT — break the agreement quietly for short-term gain

4ROUND 4

The Compliance Pitch

Max 20 points

Jargon check

Real CMA fines reach 10% of annual UK turnover, plus director disqualification, and a criminal cartel offence carrying up to 5 years in prison for individuals. The CMA's leniency programme gives full immunity to the first cartel member to confess.

"The raid is over. The fines are coming. The board wants one thing from you: a policy that would have kept us out of this room — not a generic essay, OUR story."
Task

Pitch one policy fix, explicitly referencing your own Round 1–3 choices. Use the correct vocabulary: cartel, collusion, leniency, dominant strategy / Nash equilibrium, parallel pricing.

★Policy menu — choose one

  • Refuse all future trade body "off-agenda" discussions — fixes Round 2
  • Compliance training + a competition-law sign-off before any pricing meeting — fixes Round 2
  • An internal whistleblowing line — addresses Round 3's instability
  • Apply for leniency proactively next time, rather than waiting to be caught
  • Accept that competing hard (Round 1's UNDERCUT) is the only fully legal strategy

One Year On

★ Bonus — max 5 points

Task

Pick TWO reasons from the list below why tacit collusion is so hard to stop, even after a successful raid.

Mechanisms that can recreate cartel-like prices, legally

  • Price leadership — one dominant firm moves first, others quietly follow
  • Pricing algorithms that auto-match a rival's price
  • Market concentration itself — just 3–4 dominant firms
  • Mandatory price transparency rules — can backfire by helping rivals monitor each other
Essay hook: "Evaluate the view that breaking up a price-fixing cartel guarantees a genuinely competitive market." (25 marks) — use today's KAA and the tacit vs explicit collusion distinction in your evaluation.
