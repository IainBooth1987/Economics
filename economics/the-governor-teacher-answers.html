Step-by-Step Walkthrough

Follow this exactly the first time — the mark schemes later in this book assume you've read this
Before the lesson (5 minutes, the night before)
  1. Open the-governor-teacher.html in a browser on the board/projector computer. This is the only file you actively click through during the lesson.
  2. On the opening Setup screen, choose your number of teams from the dropdown and type in team names (or leave as "Team 1", "Team 2" etc. if you're short on time).
  3. Print or share the-governor-student.html — one per team. This is the only thing students need; they never touch the console.
  4. Skim this answer book once, end to end, so the credibility-test trap in Round 4 doesn't catch you out either.
During the lesson — the loop you repeat for every round

Every round (Starter, R1–R4, Bonus) follows the exact same four-step loop on the console. Once you've done it once, the rest repeat themselves:

  1. Click the round in the side nav. The brief, jargon box and options are already projected — just talk through them with the class.
  2. Start the timer (button above the options) and let teams work on their workbook page.
  3. Press the gold "Reveal" button. The verdict, explanation and mark scheme appear on screen — read the decision aloud, then use the mark scheme box to mark.
  4. Enter scores in the grid at the bottom and press "Save." Most boxes auto-fill from a dropdown — you are mostly checking and topping up, not calculating from scratch.

The one exception is Round 3: there's a fifth step in the middle — after teams commit to a tool, scroll down and press "Run the simulation" before pressing Reveal. Let the bars animate, give students a minute to calculate the index moves, then press "Reveal the index scores," then press Reveal for the Governor's verdict.

After the lesson
  • Click "🏆 League Table" in the nav to show the final standings, then "Reveal final podium" for a satisfying finish.
  • Scores save automatically in this browser. If you're using the same computer next lesson, everything will still be there — use the red "Reset game" button only when you want to start completely fresh (e.g. a new class).
  • If you want a clean answer reference to read on paper instead of on screen, that's this document — use the round-by-round sections below.
If something looks wrong
  • A round looks already "revealed" when you open it: this is leftover saved progress from a previous run on this computer. Press "Reset game" in the header to clear it.
  • The Student Workbook / Teacher Answer Booklet buttons don't open anything: all three files need to be in the same folder — on your website, or on the computer if testing locally.
  • You're not sure what a score should be: every round's mark scheme appears automatically underneath the Reveal button on the console itself — you don't need to flip back to this book during the lesson, only to prepare beforehand.
Sequencing Guide

Running the Game in Order

Read this before the lesson
Before you start
  • Hand out student workbooks. Ask teams to read the Briefing carefully. Do not say it contains clues — that is the trap.
  • Run the Starter first — unscored warm-up, 2 mins + reveal.
  • Round 1 is two parts in one round: Part A (liquidity/solvency diagnosis) then Part B (the Bagehot ladder) — score them separately, then combine into one Round 1 total (max 20).
  • Run Round 2 next — pricing the stimulus informs the recovery maths later.
  • Round 3 runs its market-reaction simulation live, inline, in the same round — no separate tab to switch to: teams commit to a tool, you run the 🔬 simulation widget on the console (same screen), students calculate the index moves, then you press Reveal for the Governor's verdict.
  • Run Round 4 last — the credibility trap only works if students have not been warned. Any team that re-read the Briefing carefully will catch C and D.
  • The Bonus works in the same lesson or as homework.
The through-line

Briefing → diagnosis and positioning (R1) → pricing the stimulus (R2) → design and test (R3, market reaction included) → recovery and credibility (R4) → the exit problem (Bonus). The deepest lesson: the most "effective" option (the credibility trap in R4) is not the right one — and that is simultaneously an economics lesson and a credibility lesson.

Starter — Predict the Crisis

Correct Order and Figures

Unscored warm-up  ·  2 minutes + reveal
Correct ranking — biggest sum first
  1. A decade of printing money — £895bn. The Bank of England's total QE stock, peaking by November 2020/end of 2021 — equivalent to over 40% of annual UK GDP (Bank of England Independent Evaluation Office, 2021).
  2. The national rescue plan — £500bn. The UK's October 2008 bank rescue package: £50bn recapitalisation fund, £200bn Special Liquidity Scheme, £250bn Credit Guarantee Scheme (announced 8 Oct 2008 by Brown and Darling).
  3. The opening shot — £75bn. The Bank of England's first Quantitative Easing announcement, March 2009.
  4. Saving one bank — £45.8bn. Total UK government investment to rescue RBS across the October and November 2008 injections, peaking at 84% public ownership.
  5. Savers panic and withdraw — £4.6bn. Estimated Northern Rock deposit withdrawals within days of 14 September 2007 — the UK's first bank run in over 150 years.
Teaching points after the reveal
  • The order tells the story. A small bank run (£4.6bn) forced a single-bank rescue (£45.8bn), which forced a national rescue package (£500bn), which forced an entirely new monetary tool (£75bn) — which then got used again and again, ballooning to £895bn over a decade.
  • "Temporary emergency measures" tend to become permanent. QE was introduced as a crisis tool in 2009 and was still being expanded in 2020–21, in response to a completely different crisis.
  • Ask: "Why did a savings panic at one small bank end up costing the country £500bn?" → connects to contagion and counterparty risk — the diagnosis for Round 1.

Round 1 — Diagnose the Crunch & the Bagehot Line

The Governor's Verdict

Max 20 pts (10 + 10)·8 minutes

Part A — Diagnose the Crunch

Quote-matching key
"One needed cash for a few weeks… just briefly illiquid."B
"Nobody knows which of our banks is sitting on toxic assets…"E
"Every bank in this room is now treating every other bank as a suspect…"C
"The other is a different story: its balance sheet does not add up…" (decoy)A
"A central bank that bails out recklessness without cost…" (decoy)D
Correct ranking (from the three non-decoy matches)
  1. B — Liquidity risk. A genuinely healthy bank can still die for lack of overnight cash.
  2. E — Adverse selection. When lenders can't tell good banks from bad ones, the safest move is to lend to none of them.
  3. C — Counterparty risk & contagion. The same uncertainty about who is exposed makes every bank afraid of every other bank — trust collapses faster than the underlying problems justify.

Why the decoys don't fit:

  • A (solvency risk) is the real issue for the second bank in the Briefing, but it isn't what froze the whole interbank market at the start — that bank's specific problem doesn't explain why healthy banks also stopped lending to each other.
  • D (moral hazard) is a consequence of how you respond to the freeze, not a cause of the freeze itself — a common confusion.
Mark scheme — Part A
MarksCriterion
10 ptsFinal ranking is B, E, C in exactly that order
7 ptsAll three correct letters (B, E, C) but wrong order
4 ptsTwo of the three correct
1 ptZero or one correct
Maximum 10 for Part A — one comparison against the key above, no need to check each quote match individually
Common misconceptions
  • Solvency risk first: students have confused the bank that is genuinely insolvent with the general cause of the market-wide freeze. Ask: "What does the Briefing actually say is stopping banks lending to each other, specifically?"
  • Moral hazard in top 3: plausible-sounding but it describes a risk of future behaviour, not the cause of today's freeze. Strong answers separate cause from consequence.
  • Correct order with no quotations scores 6, not 10. Push for Briefing evidence.
Debrief hook

"Which of your three diagnosed concepts connects most directly to choosing an unconventional tool later?" → Liquidity risk and adverse selection both point toward why direct intervention (QE) works better than just talking (forward guidance) in Round 3. This is the lesson: diagnosis before design.

Part B — The Bagehot Ladder

Max 10 pts·4 minutes
Correct answer: 4 or 5 — Bagehot's dictum

Evidence pulling DOWN the ladder:

  • "A central bank that bails out recklessness without cost teaches every bank to take the same risks again."

Evidence pulling UP from zero:

  • "A central bank that does nothing while a solvent bank dies for lack of overnight cash has failed at the only job it has."

The synthesis: "I will lend freely, against good collateral, at a rate that punishes lateness." That's Bagehot's dictum word for word: lend freely, against good collateral, at a penalty rate. 4–5 on the ladder.

This is a genuine 1873 principle from economist Walter Bagehot that still anchors central banking today. Teams at 8+ heard "rescue the banks" and stopped reading; teams at 1–2 heard "don't reward recklessness" and did the same.

Common misconceptions
  • Defaulting to extremes: going to 8–10 ("save every bank, whatever it takes") or 0–1 ("let the market discipline itself") without reading the Briefing both ways. The question is about the Governor's position, not the student's own opinion.
  • Students sometimes choose a rung while describing a different one. Clarify: the task is placing the Governor's philosophy, not designing a specific rescue.
A-Level concept: Bagehot's dictum

After the reveal, name it explicitly: Bagehot's dictum — "lend freely, against good collateral, at a high rate" (Walter Bagehot, Lombard Street, 1873). Ask: "Why does a penalty rate matter if the goal is just to stop the panic?" — this is the moral hazard trade-off, and it's the live debate worth seeding for the essay.

Teaching point: the transmission mechanism — and its limit

"One more cut is worth £1.5bn a month." That's the case for cutting again — and it's a genuine cost-benefit argument, the same skill examiners reward in monetary policy essays. But flag the trap ahead: as the Bank Rate approaches zero, the same 1.5-point cut delivers a shrinking return, because banks would rather hoard cash than lend it out at near-zero margins. Ask: "If cutting always worked this well, why would a central bank ever need anything except interest rates?" → sets up the zero lower bound and the liquidity trap for Round 3.

Round 3 — Hit the Floor, Choose Your Tool

Market Reaction Results and Marking

Max 20 pts·Commit → simulation → reveal

Mechanism matching key — note the M-codes are deliberately NOT in A,B,C,D order

Common misconceptions
  • Choosing D on "more aggressive = more effective": students haven't separated a forceful-sounding tool from an effective mechanism. Negative rates sound dramatic but the real-world evidence (ECB, BoJ, SNB) shows only marginal extra stimulus.
  • Choosing C students reasoned well — don't penalise harshly. C is the second-strongest and its logic is well-grounded in the diagnosis.
  • Size vs effectiveness: B is also the biggest balance-sheet commitment and the hardest to reverse — that trade-off becomes central in Round 4 and the Bonus. Worth flagging now.
Why government bond QE won

Nobody could have known B would beat C without testing — being confidently wrong is the default human condition, and that's exactly why central banks watch market reactions so closely. And the deeper point: the Bank of England's own Independent Evaluation Office found QE's effect on long-term borrowing costs was largest when buying government bonds during stressed markets — precisely the pattern simulated here. Forward guidance's muted real-world effect (without backing action) is also well documented in the academic literature — exactly the kind of intuition a market-test exists to check.

Common misconceptions
  • Choosing C: "emergency liquidity support is standard central banking." True — but legitimate support has conditions and a review date. "No conditions or public review" is not that. Briefing line: "we will never quietly extend support without a review date attached."
  • Choosing D: "a confident promise builds market confidence." No — an open-ended promise with no exit is a credibility risk, not a confidence boost. Ask: "Would this survive a select committee asking 'until when, exactly?'"
  • Choosing F: credible, mathematically poor. £1.5bn for an output-index of 30 versus £1.4bn for 300 with A+B+E. Students who choose F often haven't calculated the cost per index point.
The hardest question in the game

Ask teams who failed the credibility test: "Did the Briefing tell you this would happen?" Yes — the line "we will never quietly extend support without a review date" was on page 1, before Round 1. The trap was always readable.

For clean-portfolio teams: "ABE reached an output-index of 300 — exactly what C promised. Did you lose anything by passing the credibility test?" In this case, no. But make them argue it.

Was it worth it? — strong answer points
  • The counterfactual case (for): without the emergency cuts and QE, the 2008 recession (and the 2020 lockdown shock) would very plausibly have been deeper and longer — most independent assessments credit QE with helping prevent a repeat of the 1930s Great Depression.
  • The cost that followed (against): ultra-loose policy for over a decade, plus huge fiscal spending and energy-price shocks, contributed to the inflation surge of 2021–23 — though economists still argue over how much was monetary policy versus supply shocks no interest rate could have prevented.
  • Distributional cost (against): QE inflates asset prices. Research shows older, wealthier households — who own more assets — gained disproportionately, even though the policy was designed to help the whole economy.
  • Counter-case (strong evaluation): the tools that save you from one crisis can create the conditions for the next one — a central bank's job doesn't end when the recession does, it ends when the unwind is finished too.
Essay connection

Every piece of evidence from the game — the liquidity trap, Bagehot's dictum, QE mechanics and scale (£75bn → £895bn), forward guidance, the credibility test — is KAA for the 25-marker: "Evaluate the effectiveness of unconventional monetary policy, such as quantitative easing, in tackling a liquidity trap." Strongest answers use the exit as the evaluative hinge: the policy isn't judged on the rescue alone, but on the rescue and the unwind together.

Key Terms — Model Definitions

Glossary Answers

Exam-appropriate language. Students should have filled these in during the game.

Liquidity risk

The risk that a solvent institution cannot meet short-term obligations because it cannot access cash quickly enough, even though its assets exceed its liabilities overall.

Solvency risk

The risk that an institution's liabilities exceed its assets — it is genuinely insolvent, and no amount of short-term lending fixes that.

Counterparty risk & contagion

The risk that a financial institution suffers losses because another institution it deals with fails. Contagion is the spread of this fear and the resulting loss of trust across the whole system.

Adverse selection

A situation where one side of a transaction has more information than the other, leading the less-informed side to withdraw from the market entirely rather than risk dealing with a "bad" counterparty.

E.g. banks refuse to lend to all other banks because they cannot tell which ones hold toxic assets.

Moral hazard

The tendency for an economic agent to take on more risk because the cost of that risk is borne by someone else — e.g. a bank that expects to be bailed out takes bigger risks than it otherwise would.

Bagehot's dictum

The classic principle of central bank crisis lending (Walter Bagehot, 1873): lend freely, against good collateral, at a penalty rate. Gentle enough to stop a panic, harsh enough to discourage recklessness.

Lender of last resort

The role of a central bank in providing emergency liquidity to solvent financial institutions facing short-term funding problems, when no other source of funding is available.

Transmission mechanism

The process by which a change in interest rates (or other monetary policy tools) affects spending, output, employment and inflation in the wider economy.

Liquidity trap

A situation where interest rates are so low that conventional monetary policy loses its power to stimulate the economy — people prefer to hold cash rather than spend or invest, however cheap borrowing becomes.

Zero lower bound

The point at which a central bank's policy interest rate is at or near zero, limiting its ability to cut rates further to stimulate the economy through conventional means.

Quantitative easing (QE)

A central bank creates new money electronically to buy financial assets (usually government bonds), pushing up their price and lowering long-term interest rates, to stimulate spending when conventional rate cuts are exhausted.

E.g. Bank of England, £75bn first announced March 2009, peaking at £895bn by 2021.

Forward guidance

A central bank communicates its intentions about future monetary policy (e.g. "rates will stay low until X happens") to influence expectations and behaviour today.

Negative interest rates

A policy where a central bank charges commercial banks for holding reserves with it, intended to push banks to lend rather than hoard cash. Used by the ECB, Bank of Japan and Swiss National Bank with limited extra stimulus.

Quantitative tightening (QT)

The reverse of QE — a central bank reduces its holdings of assets bought during QE, either by letting bonds mature or by actively selling them, which can push long-term borrowing costs back up.

Credibility (of a central bank)

The degree to which markets and the public believe a central bank will act consistently with its stated rules and promises. Once lost, credibility is very costly to rebuild, because future promises are discounted.

Credit crunch

A sharp reduction in the general availability of loans or credit, typically caused by a sudden unwillingness of banks to lend to each other or to households and firms.

Debrief, Extension and Exam Links

The Briefing clue trail — what students were meant to find
  • Round 1 (Part A): "solvent the whole time, just briefly illiquid" (liquidity risk); "nobody knows which of our banks is sitting on toxic assets" (adverse selection); the fear "of who else is exposed" (counterparty risk/contagion).
  • Round 1 (Part B): "a central bank that bails out recklessness without cost…" (down) vs "a central bank that does nothing… has failed at the only job it has" (up) + "I will lend freely, against good collateral, at a rate that punishes lateness" (synthesis — Bagehot's dictum).
  • Round 2: £150,000 / 1.5 points / 8,000,000 / £4.5bn — all in the Briefing.
  • Round 4 disqualifier: "we will never quietly extend support without a review date attached" — one sentence that detonates on options C and D. This was on page 1 before the game started.
Extension tasks for high-attaining students
  • Calculate cost per output-index point for each individual Round 4 option: A≈£4.2m · B=£5m · C≈£0.7m (fails credibility) · D≈£2.7m (fails credibility) · E=£5m · F=£50m.
  • Why might QE's effect fade in calmer markets even when the programme size stays the same? What does this tell us about the conditions unconventional policy needs to work?
  • Redesign option C to make it pass the credibility test. What changes, and what is the cost to its effectiveness?
  • Read: Bank of England Independent Evaluation Office (2021), "IEO evaluation of the Bank of England's approach to quantitative easing" — the real assessment this round is anchored to.
Generic exam links
  • Monetary policy: the transmission mechanism, the zero lower bound, and unconventional tools (QE, forward guidance, negative rates) are directly assessed across UK A-Level economics specifications.
  • Market failure / information failure: adverse selection in the interbank market (Round 1) is a textbook information failure with real-world consequences.
  • Government and central bank intervention: the Bagehot ladder maps directly to the spectrum of intervention most specifications cover for financial markets.
  • Evaluation skills: the exit problem, the distributional effect of QE, moral hazard versus stability, and credibility are all genuine evaluative arguments for 25-mark essays.
