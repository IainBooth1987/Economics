📖 Theory Reference

🏛️ Interest Rate Parity (IRP)
When a central bank raises interest rates, its currency typically strengthens. Higher rates offer better returns on assets denominated in that currency, attracting foreign capital inflows.
↑ Interest rates → ↑ Capital inflows → ↑ Currency demand → ↑ Exchange rate
🇺🇸 Example: When the Fed raised rates from 0% to 5.25% in 2022–23, the US Dollar Index surged ~15%, hitting 20-year highs against most major currencies.
🕊️ Hawkish vs Dovish Policy
Hawkish: central bank signals rate rises or inflation-fighting stance → currency strengthens.

Dovish: central bank signals rate cuts or stimulus → currency weakens.
💬 Key phrase to watch: "We are prepared to raise rates further" = hawkish. "We stand ready to ease policy" = dovish.
🔄 Carry Trade
Borrow in a low-rate currency (e.g. JPY at 0.1%) and invest in a high-rate currency (e.g. AUD at 4.5%). Profit = interest rate differential, minus any currency movement losses.
Carry Trade Return = (r_high − r_low) ± currency change
⚠️ Risk: If the low-rate currency suddenly strengthens (e.g. JPY spike), carry trade positions unwind rapidly, causing volatile moves.
📊 What Drives Currency Demand?
Currencies are demanded for three main reasons:

1. Trade — importers must buy foreign currency to pay for goods.

2. Investment — investors buy foreign assets (stocks, bonds, property).

3. Speculation — traders bet on future currency movements.
Strong exports → ↑ foreign demand for domestic currency → ↑ exchange rate
🛡️ Safe Haven Currencies
During crises, investors flee to perceived "safe" currencies. These typically have large current account surpluses and stable institutions.
🇯🇵 JPY — Japan is the world's largest creditor nation
🇨🇭 CHF — Switzerland's political neutrality and sound banking
🇺🇸 USD — global reserve currency status
⛏️ Commodity Currencies
Some currencies move closely with commodity prices because their economies are major exporters of raw materials.
🇦🇺 AUD → iron ore, coal (China demand-driven)
🇨🇦 CAD → oil prices (WTI crude correlation ~0.8)
🇳🇿 NZD → dairy and agricultural products
📉 Current Account & Trade Balance
A country with a current account surplus (exports > imports) sees net inflows of foreign currency — supporting its exchange rate. A deficit means more currency flows out, potentially weakening it.
Trade surplus → ↑ Demand for domestic currency → Appreciation pressure
⚖️ Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)
In the long run, exchange rates should equalise the price of a identical basket of goods across countries. If UK inflation is higher than US inflation, £ should weaken against $ over time.
E = P_domestic / P_foreign
🍔 The Big Mac Index (The Economist): compares burger prices globally to identify over/undervalued currencies. A Big Mac costing £4 in UK vs $5 in US implies GBP/USD should be ~0.80.
📐 Uncovered Interest Rate Parity (UIRP)
If a currency has higher interest rates, the market expects it to depreciate over time by the rate differential — so that returns are equalised. In practice, this "forward premium puzzle" often doesn't hold in the short run.
Expected depreciation ≈ domestic rate − foreign rate
💡 Why it matters: Markets often overshoot — higher-rate currencies can keep appreciating before eventually correcting, creating momentum trading opportunities.
📏 Real vs Nominal Exchange Rates
The nominal rate is what you see quoted (e.g. GBP/USD = 1.27). The real rate adjusts for inflation differences — it tells you about actual purchasing power and trade competitiveness.
Real rate = Nominal rate × (P_foreign / P_domestic)
📊 GDP Growth & Currencies
Strong economic growth generally attracts foreign investment, increasing demand for the domestic currency. It also signals future interest rate rises, further boosting the currency.
⚡ Watch for: GDP beat vs. expectations matters more than the absolute number. A 2.5% print when 1.8% was expected moves markets more than a 3% print when 3% was expected.
💼 Employment & Labour Markets
Low unemployment signals economic strength and wage growth, which can drive inflation — prompting central banks to raise rates. Non-Farm Payrolls (US) is one of the most-watched monthly releases globally.
Strong jobs → Wage growth → Inflation risk → Rate hike expectations → ↑ Currency
🌍 Political Risk & Uncertainty
Elections, referenda, geopolitical conflicts, and policy instability increase currency volatility. Markets dislike uncertainty — currencies of politically unstable countries typically weaken as capital flees to safer alternatives.
📉 Case study: GBP fell 15% in the weeks around the 2016 Brexit referendum result, as uncertainty about future trade relationships spooked investors.
📈 Inflation & Exchange Rates
High inflation erodes a currency's purchasing power (bad for the currency long-term). But short-term, high inflation can strengthen a currency if it means the central bank will raise rates to combat it.
Short run: High inflation → Rate hike expectations → ↑ Currency
Long run: High inflation → PPP depreciation → ↓ Currency
