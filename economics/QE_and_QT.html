Quantitative Easing

How central banks create money electronically, purchase assets, and stimulate a stalling economy — and why it doesn't always work as intended.

📖 What Is Quantitative Easing?

Quantitative Easing (QE) is an unconventional monetary policy tool deployed when conventional interest rate cuts are no longer effective — typically when the base rate has already been reduced to near zero (the "zero lower bound"). The central bank electronically creates new money and uses it to purchase financial assets — predominantly government bonds (gilts) — from commercial banks and institutional investors.

No physical banknotes are printed. The new money exists purely as accounting entries on the Bank of England's balance sheet — a credit to the seller's reserve account. The BoE first deployed QE in March 2009 with an initial purchase of £75bn. By 2021, cumulative purchases had reached £895bn — approximately 40% of UK GDP.

💡 Not "Printing Money"

QE creates electronic central bank reserves — not physical currency. The distinction matters: it affects bank balance sheets directly rather than putting cash in circulation.

🎯 Targets Long Rates

Unlike conventional policy (which targets the short-term base rate), QE primarily aims to reduce long-term interest rates — those that affect mortgages, corporate bonds, and capital investment.

📐 Scale Matters

The UK's £895bn programme represented ~40% of GDP. Scale and credibility are critical — a small, hesitant programme may fail to shift expectations or yields meaningfully.

🔧 Tool of Last Resort

QE is deployed when rates are at or near zero. It is inherently a crisis-era tool — its use signals that conventional levers have been exhausted.

⚙️ Transmission Mechanism — Step by Step
1

Asset Purchase

The BoE's Asset Purchase Facility (APF) buys gilts and corporate bonds from commercial banks and pension funds/insurers. It pays by crediting the sellers' reserve accounts at the Bank of England — creating new central bank money electronically.

2

Portfolio Rebalancing

Sellers now hold cash (reserves) instead of bonds. Since reserves earn little return, sellers seek higher-yielding alternatives — equities, corporate bonds, property. This portfolio rebalancing drives up asset prices and compresses yields across financial markets.

3

Lower Long-term Interest Rates

Increased demand for gilts raises their price, which inversely reduces their yield. Gilt yields act as a benchmark — falling gilt yields drag down mortgage rates, corporate borrowing rates, and other long-term rates throughout the economy.

4

Bank Lending Expands

Commercial banks receive reserves. In theory, improved balance sheet liquidity reduces their cost of funding and encourages greater credit extension to households and businesses at lower rates. The money multiplier can amplify the initial injection.

5

Wealth Effect

Rising equity and property prices increase household wealth. Those who feel wealthier are more likely to spend (the wealth effect), boosting consumption and aggregate demand. This is sometimes called the asset price channel.

6

Exchange Rate Depreciation

Lower UK interest rates make sterling assets relatively less attractive to foreign investors, reducing demand for the pound. A weaker sterling makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive — potentially stimulating net exports and raising imported inflation.

7

Inflation Returns to Target

Higher aggregate demand — from increased lending, wealth effects, and net exports — puts upward pressure on the price level, helping the BoE return CPI inflation toward its 2% target and supporting output and employment.

⚖️ Evaluation

✅ Arguments For QE

  • Prevents deflationary spirals at the zero lower bound — provides policy space that conventional tools cannot.
  • Empirically credited with stabilising financial markets in 2008–09 and preventing a deeper recession.
  • Lowers borrowing costs for households and firms, stimulating investment and consumption.
  • Supports government financing — lower gilt yields reduce the cost of public debt.
  • Forward guidance element: signals commitment to loose monetary policy, anchoring expectations.

❌ Arguments Against QE

  • Distributional inequality: Primarily benefits asset-holders (wealthier households) — critics call it "trickle up" economics.
  • Liquidity trap: Banks may hoard excess reserves rather than lending ("pushing on a string").
  • Inflation risk: Large-scale money creation can become inflationary — arguably contributed to the 2021–23 price surge.
  • Zombie firms: Artificially low rates keep unproductive firms alive, reducing long-run productivity growth.
  • Exit problem: Reversing QE (QT) is complex and can trigger financial market volatility.
Examiner tip: QE is most effective when combined with fiscal stimulus and when banks are willing to lend. On its own, QE may be insufficient if households and firms are deleveraging regardless of interest rates — the "Keynesian liquidity trap." Always contextualise: the 2009 and 2020 programmes operated in very different economic conditions.

Quantitative Tightening

The deliberate reversal of QE — how central banks drain liquidity, raise long-term rates, and fight inflation — and why it is harder to unwind than it was to deploy.

📖 What Is Quantitative Tightening?

Quantitative Tightening (QT) is the deliberate reduction of a central bank's balance sheet by selling government bonds back into the market or allowing them to mature without reinvestment. It is designed to withdraw excess liquidity from the financial system, raise long-term interest rates, and reduce inflationary pressure.

QT is a relatively untested and politically sensitive policy. The US Federal Reserve attempted QT in 2018 but reversed course in 2019. The Bank of England began active gilt sales in late 2022 as part of its response to the post-Covid inflation surge, which peaked at 11.1% in October 2022 — the highest since 1981.

⚠️ Asymmetric Risk

QT is generally considered more disruptive than QE. Markets are more sensitive to the withdrawal of liquidity than its injection — the 2022 LDI crisis illustrates the potential for sudden market dysfunction.

🔄 Two Approaches

Passive QT: allow bonds to mature without reinvestment. Active QT: sell bonds directly into the market — more immediate but carries greater risk of market disruption.

🧪 Limited History

QT is genuinely new territory — few central banks have successfully completed a full QT cycle. This makes it harder to model effects and communicate credibly to markets.

📉 Balance Sheet Losses

The BoE bought gilts at high prices (low yields); it sells/holds them at lower prices (higher yields). Realised losses are underwritten by HM Treasury — a fiscal consequence of monetary policy.

⚙️ Transmission Mechanism — Step by Step
1

Asset Sales or Maturities

The BoE sells gilts from its Asset Purchase Facility back to the private sector (pension funds, insurers, banks), or allows maturing bonds to expire without reinvesting the proceeds. Both approaches shrink the BoE's balance sheet.

2

Reserve Drain

When buyers purchase gilts, they pay by transferring reserves from their BoE accounts. This directly reduces the stock of reserves in the banking system — the precise reverse of QE. The money supply contracts.

3

Rising Long-term Yields

Increased supply of gilts in the market pushes their prices down, raising their yield. Higher gilt yields act as a benchmark — pulling up mortgage rates, corporate bond rates, and all other long-term borrowing costs.

4

Credit Tightens

Higher long-term rates and reduced bank reserves make credit more expensive and less available. Banks face higher funding costs and tighten lending standards — reducing the flow of credit to households and businesses.

5

Negative Wealth Effect

Higher yields make bonds relatively more attractive, causing investors to shift from equities and property into bonds. Asset prices fall — reducing household wealth, dampening the wealth effect, and cutting consumer confidence and spending.

6

Sterling Appreciates

Higher UK interest rates attract foreign capital seeking better returns. Increased demand for sterling causes the pound to appreciate — making imports cheaper (reducing imported inflation) and exports more expensive.

7

Inflation Falls to Target

Reduced spending (from higher borrowing costs, negative wealth effects, tighter credit), combined with lower import prices, puts downward pressure on inflation — ultimately returning CPI toward the 2% target, with long and variable lags.

⚖️ Evaluation of QT

✅ Arguments For QT

  • Directly addresses inflationary excess liquidity built up during successive QE rounds — a necessary policy normalisation.
  • Reduces the BoE's exposure to interest rate risk on its large bond portfolio.
  • Restores conventional monetary policy space — rebuilds "ammunition" for the next downturn.
  • Higher yields improve returns for savers and pension funds — a redistributive benefit compared to QE.
  • Reduces the perception that monetary and fiscal policy have dangerously merged.

❌ Arguments Against QT

  • Market disruption: Rapid gilt sales can destabilise bond markets — as in the September 2022 LDI pension fund crisis.
  • Amplification risk: When combined with rate hikes, QT may overtighten — risking recession.
  • Balance sheet losses: BoE sold gilts at a loss in 2022–23, with losses underwritten by HM Treasury.
  • Lag effects: QT operates with long and variable lags — risks overshooting and undershooting the inflation target.
  • Limited evidence base: Very few central banks have completed full QT cycles — significant uncertainty around the size of effects.
Examiner tip: A common error is to treat QT as simply "QE in reverse" with symmetrical effects. In practice QT is asymmetric — markets react more strongly to liquidity withdrawal, and the BoE must balance the pace of QT against financial stability risk. The September 2022 LDI crisis is an excellent real-world example. Also note: the BoE was simultaneously raising the base rate and selling gilts — an unusually aggressive dual tightening.
