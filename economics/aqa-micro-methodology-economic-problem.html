Economics as a Social Science

Economics is classified as a social science: it applies a scientific approach — observation, theory, and testing — to the study of human behaviour in the production, distribution, and consumption of goods and services. Like other social sciences (psychology, sociology, political science), it studies people, who are far less predictable than the physical particles or chemical reactions studied by the natural sciences.

Social Science
A discipline that studies human society and the relationships between individuals within it, using systematic, evidence-based methods — but applied to subjects (people, institutions, markets) that behave with far more variability and free will than the natural world.
Economic Model
A simplified representation of economic reality — such as the circular flow of income or a demand curve — built on a set of assumptions, used to isolate the effect of one variable at a time and to make predictions that can then be tested against real-world data.
Why "Science" at All? Economics earns the label "science" because it follows the same broad method as physics or biology: economists observe a phenomenon (e.g. rising prices), construct a theory to explain it (e.g. the quantity theory of money), derive testable predictions from that theory, and then test those predictions against real data — revising or discarding the theory if the evidence contradicts it. What separates it from the natural sciences is not the method itself, but the subject matter it is applied to.

Economic Methodology vs the Natural Sciences

Economics shares its underlying logical structure with the natural sciences, but the practical differences in how that method can be applied are substantial — chiefly because economists cannot isolate their subject in a laboratory the way a chemist or physicist can.

FeatureNatural Sciences (e.g. Physics, Chemistry)Economics
Controlled experimentsVariables can usually be isolated and controlled directly in a laboratory setting.Rarely possible on a whole economy — economists mostly rely on real-world data, natural experiments, and models built on the ceteris paribus assumption (holding other things equal).
Predictability of the subjectAtoms and chemical compounds behave according to fixed physical laws.Human beings have free will, imperfect information, and are influenced by psychology, emotion, and expectations — behaviour is far less uniform and can change in response to being studied.
RepeatabilityExperiments can typically be repeated under near-identical conditions.Historical economic events (e.g. a specific recession) are unique and cannot be exactly re-run to test a theory.
Value-freedomFindings are not usually contested on moral grounds (e.g. gravity is not "unfair").Economic findings and policies are frequently bound up with value judgements about fairness, rights, and priorities (see Section 3).
Use of models & assumptionsModels simplify reality but assumptions (e.g. frictionless surfaces) are usually uncontroversial.Models rely on simplifying assumptions about human behaviour (e.g. rational, self-interested "economic man") that are more open to challenge and debate.
Underlying methodObserve → theorise → predict → test → revise.Observe → theorise → predict → test → revise — the same basic scientific method, applied with more caveats.
Key Exam Point: Do not simply state "economics is not a real science." AQA rewards a balanced answer: economics does follow scientific method, but the complexity, unpredictability, and value-laden nature of human behaviour make its predictions inherently less precise and more contested than those of the natural sciences.

Positive & Normative Statements

Almost every statement an economist makes can be classified as either positive or normative. Confusing the two is one of the most common ways marks are lost at A-Level, so the distinction is worth learning precisely.

Positive Statement
A statement of fact that can, in principle, be tested and shown to be true or false by reference to evidence — it describes "what is," "what was," or "what will be," without any judgement about whether that is good or bad.
Normative Statement
A statement that contains a value judgement about what ought to happen — it cannot be tested purely by evidence, because it depends on the values, ethics, or priorities of the person making it.
StatementClassificationWhy
"UK inflation was 3.2% last month."PositiveA verifiable fact — it can be checked against ONS data and shown to be true or false.
"The government should raise the minimum wage."NormativeContains the word "should" and depends on a value judgement about the priority given to low-paid workers versus, say, business costs.
"A rise in interest rates will reduce consumer spending."PositiveA testable prediction based on economic theory — evidence can support or refute it, regardless of anyone's opinion on whether it is desirable.
"It is unfair that the top 1% own so much wealth."Normative"Unfair" is a value judgement — reasonable people can disagree even if they agree on the underlying facts about wealth distribution.
The Word-Spotting Trap: looking for words like "should," "ought," "fair," or "unfair" is a useful first check, but is not foolproof — a statement can smuggle in a value judgement without using any of these words (e.g. "This policy will make society better off" implicitly assumes a particular, contestable definition of "better off"). Always ask: could this be settled by evidence alone, or does it ultimately rest on someone's values?
4

Positive & Normative Statements in Famous Speeches

Famous political and economic speeches almost always blend positive claims (facts and predictions) with normative claims (what ought to be done) in the same breath — precisely why they are persuasive. Below are two well-known examples, each broken into its positive and normative components.

Margaret Thatcher, Conservative Party Conference, 1980

Responding to critics who urged a change of economic direction during a deep recession, Thatcher famously said:

"To those waiting with bated breath for that favourite media catchphrase, the 'U-turn', I have only one thing to say: you turn if you want to. The lady's not for turning."

Normative The statement itself is a declaration of political will and resolve — it asserts what policy ought to be pursued (continuing tight monetary policy and spending restraint) regardless of short-term pain, reflecting a value judgement that curbing inflation mattered more than avoiding immediate unemployment.

Positive The underlying economic claim implicit in her argument — that a "U-turn" (reflationary spending) would re-ignite inflation — is a testable, positive prediction about cause and effect in the economy, separate from whether avoiding that outcome was the right priority.

Franklin D. Roosevelt, First Inaugural Address, 1933

Addressing the nation during the Great Depression, Roosevelt argued for decisive government intervention:

"This nation asks for action, and action now... Our greatest primary task is to put people to work... It can be accomplished in part by direct recruiting by the Government itself."

Positive The description of mass unemployment and idle productive capacity during the Depression was a positive, factual claim — millions were out of work and factories stood idle, a state of affairs that could be measured and verified.

Normative The claim that government should directly recruit and employ people is a normative policy conclusion — it reflects a value judgement that the state has a duty to intervene, which economists (and citizens) with different values could reasonably reject in favour of, for example, relying on markets to self-correct.

Exam Application: when a question gives you an extract from a speech or article and asks you to identify positive and normative statements, look sentence by sentence rather than judging the whole passage at once — most real speeches interleave both, and examiners reward precise identification of exactly which clause is which.

Value Judgements in Economic Decision-Making

Even where economists agree entirely on the positive facts and predictions, they can still reach different conclusions about the best policy, because policy choices require weighing up whose gains and losses matter most — a question that positive economics alone cannot answer.

1
Positive analysis establishes the facts

Economic theory and evidence can establish, for example, that a minimum wage rise will raise the incomes of low-paid workers who keep their jobs, while increasing the risk of job losses for some others and raising costs for firms.

2
But policy requires a judgement about priorities

Deciding whether the wage gains for those who keep their jobs outweigh the costs to firms and the risk to those who lose theirs is not a question science can settle — it depends on how much weight is placed on equity for low-paid workers versus efficiency and employment.

3
Value judgements therefore shape the final decision

Two policymakers who accept exactly the same evidence can reach opposite conclusions on the minimum wage, because they hold different values about fairness, the role of the state, or the relative importance of different groups in society.

Government Policy Is Rarely Value-Free: almost every major policy area in economics — taxation, welfare, healthcare funding, environmental regulation — combines positive economic analysis (what the effects will be) with normative choices (which effects matter most, and to whom). Recognising this distinction is central to evaluating economic policy at A-Level.

Positive Consequences vs Moral & Political Judgements

When people judge which policy option is "best," their view is shaped by two distinct inputs working together: their assessment of the positive consequences of each option, and their own moral and political judgements about which of those consequences matter most.

Positive Consequences (Facts & Predictions)

  • What will actually happen to GDP, employment, inflation, or inequality under each option.
  • Based on economic theory, modelling, and empirical evidence.
  • Can, in principle, be debated and tested using data — even if forecasts carry uncertainty.

Moral & Political Judgements

  • Whether those consequences are seen as fair, desirable, or acceptable.
  • Reflects an individual's political ideology, ethical beliefs, or personal circumstances.
  • Cannot be resolved by evidence alone — two people can agree on the facts and still disagree on the verdict.

For example, two voters might both accept the positive prediction that raising corporation tax will reduce business investment by a certain amount. One voter, valuing redistribution and public spending highly, may still support the tax rise; another, valuing enterprise and low taxation highly, may oppose it. The disagreement is not about the facts — it is about values.

Exam Application: in extended-response questions asking you to "evaluate" or "discuss the extent to which" a policy is desirable, always separate your positive analysis (the likely economic effects) from your normative judgement (weighing those effects against a stated criterion, such as equity, efficiency, or sustainability) — and be explicit that your final judgement depends on the weight given to competing priorities.

Thinking as an Economist

Studying economics involves developing a distinctive way of reasoning about problems, which differs in important respects from how other scientific disciplines approach enquiry.

Ceteris Paribus
Economists routinely reason "holding all other things equal" — isolating the effect of one variable (e.g. price) on another (e.g. quantity demanded) while assuming everything else in the economy stays constant, even though in reality many variables move simultaneously.
Marginal Thinking
Economists habitually think at the margin — asking not "should I do this activity at all?" but "should I do a little more or a little less of it?" — a mode of reasoning distinctive to economics and central to concepts such as marginal cost and marginal utility.
Opportunity Cost Reasoning
Economists are trained to see every choice as involving a forgone alternative (Section 13), a habit of mind less central to disciplines that do not organise their subject around scarcity.
Model-Based Abstraction
Economists deliberately simplify a complex reality into stylised models (e.g. supply and demand diagrams) in order to generate insight, accepting that the model is a simplification rather than a literal description of the world — a trade-off other sciences also make, but one economics relies on especially heavily given the impossibility of laboratory control.
How This Differs from Other Scientific Enquiry: a natural scientist typically seeks a single, universally-applicable causal law tested under controlled conditions. An economist instead builds a simplified model, reasons carefully at the margin, explicitly separates positive prediction from normative judgement, and treats conclusions as provisional and context-dependent — because the underlying subject (human behaviour in aggregate) is inherently more variable, reflexive, and value-laden than the natural world.

The Nature & Purpose of Economic Activity

The central purpose of all economic activity is the production of goods and services in order to satisfy needs and wants. Every factory, farm, office, and app is ultimately engaged in transforming scarce resources into something that satisfies human demand.

Needs
Things essential for survival and basic functioning — food, water, shelter, and clothing — which are finite in number.
Wants
Things people would like to have to improve their standard of living or enjoyment of life, beyond basic survival — from a smartphone upgrade to a holiday abroad — which are effectively unlimited, since satisfying one want typically generates new ones.
Goods
Physical, tangible items produced to satisfy needs and wants — e.g. bread, cars, furniture.
Services
Intangible economic activities produced to satisfy needs and wants — e.g. haircuts, healthcare, streaming subscriptions.
Why This Distinction Matters: the gap between finite needs and infinite wants is precisely what generates the fundamental economic problem explored in Section 12 — if wants were finite and could all eventually be satisfied, scarcity (and therefore economics as a discipline) would not exist.

The Three Key Economic Decisions

Because resources are scarce relative to wants (Section 12), every economic system — whatever its size or level of government involvement — has to answer the same three fundamental questions.

What to Produce?
Which goods and services should be produced with society's scarce resources, and in what quantities — e.g. should an economy prioritise producing more capital goods (factories, machinery) or more consumer goods (food, entertainment)?
How to Produce?
What combination of factors of production (Section 10) and which techniques should be used — e.g. a labour-intensive method using many workers and little machinery, or a capital-intensive method using automation and few workers.
For Whom to Produce?
Who ultimately receives and benefits from the goods and services produced — how output is distributed among the population, which depends heavily on the distribution of income and, in a market economy, on who can afford to pay.
Linking to Economic Systems: how a society answers these three questions defines its economic system — a free market economy leaves the answers largely to price signals and consumer demand, a planned economy has these decisions made centrally by government, and most real-world economies (including the UK) are mixed economies that blend both mechanisms.

Factors of Production

Economists classify all the scarce resources used to produce goods and services into four categories, known as the factors of production. Each factor is owned by households and supplied to firms in return for a specific factor payment.

FactorDefinitionPaymentExample
LandAll natural resources used in production — not just physical land, but minerals, water, and energy sources.RentFarmland, North Sea oil, forests.
LabourThe human effort, physical and mental, applied to production.WagesA nurse, a factory worker, a software engineer.
CapitalMan-made resources used to produce other goods and services — machinery, factories, and infrastructure.InterestA production line, a delivery van, a office building.
EnterpriseThe willingness to take risks, organise the other three factors, and innovate in pursuit of profit.ProfitAn entrepreneur starting a business and risking their own capital.
Factor Inputs vs Factor Productivity: the quantity of factors available in an economy (how much land, labour, capital, and enterprise it has) is distinct from factor productivity — how efficiently those factors are combined. Growth can come from increasing the quantity of factors (e.g. a larger workforce) or from raising the productivity of existing factors (e.g. better technology or training) — a distinction that underpins outward shifts of the PPF explored in Section 17.

The Environment as a Scarce Resource

The natural environment — clean air, a stable climate, biodiversity, fresh water, and fossil fuels — should itself be understood as a scarce economic resource, not an unlimited free gift, even though it is not always bought and sold in a market.

Environment as a Scarce Resource
The capacity of the natural world to supply raw materials and to absorb waste and pollution is finite — meaning environmental resources are subject to exactly the same problem of scarcity as land, labour, capital, or enterprise, and choices about their use carry an opportunity cost.
Renewable Resource
A resource that can replenish itself over time if managed sustainably (e.g. forests, fish stocks), but which becomes effectively non-renewable if extracted faster than it can regenerate.
Non-Renewable Resource
A resource that exists in a fixed, finite quantity and cannot be replaced once used (e.g. oil, coal, natural gas).
★ REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE: The Atmosphere as a Scarce Resource
The atmosphere's capacity to absorb greenhouse gases without causing dangerous climate change is a scarce resource in exactly the economic sense — there is a limited "carbon budget" the world can emit while keeping warming within internationally agreed limits. Because using this capacity for one purpose (e.g. industrial production in one country) reduces what is available for others, using it involves a genuine opportunity cost, even though the atmosphere has traditionally not been priced or owned by anyone — a classic example of a resource being scarce despite having no market price, discussed further in the companion BIZ-OMICS Market Failure & Externalities guide.
Exam Application: treating the environment as a scarce economic resource — rather than as something separate from "the economy" — allows you to apply the same tools (opportunity cost, PPFs, efficiency) to environmental questions, and is a distinction AQA increasingly expects at A-Level.

The Fundamental Economic Problem: Scarcity

The fundamental economic problem is scarcity: society has only limited resources (Section 10) with which to satisfy the unlimited wants of its population (Section 8). Because resources are finite and wants are not, every society is forced to make choices about how those resources are allocated.

Scarcity
The basic economic problem arising because resources are finite while human wants are infinite, meaning not everyone can have everything they want and choices must be made about resource allocation.
Choice
The necessary consequence of scarcity — since not all wants can be satisfied, individuals, firms, and governments must select which wants to satisfy and which to leave unmet.
Resource Allocation
The process of deciding how scarce resources are distributed between competing uses — for example, how much land is allocated to housing versus farming, or how much of the government budget is allocated to health versus defence.
Why Scarcity Is "Fundamental": scarcity is described as the fundamental problem because it is the single underlying condition from which almost every other concept in economics follows — the three key economic decisions (Section 9), opportunity cost (Section 13), the PPF (Section 15), and the existence of markets and prices as an allocation mechanism all exist only because resources are scarce relative to wants.
13

Choice & Opportunity Cost

Because scarcity forces choices to be made, every choice necessarily involves giving something up — this forgone alternative is known as the opportunity cost of a decision, and it is one of the single most important concepts in the whole of economics.

Opportunity Cost
The value of the next best alternative forgone when a choice is made — not simply "what you don't choose," but specifically the single best option among everything you gave up.
A Universal Concept: opportunity cost applies at every level of decision-making — an individual choosing how to spend a free evening, a firm choosing which product line to expand, and a government choosing between funding schools or hospitals are all facing an opportunity cost, even though no money may directly change hands in some of these decisions (e.g. the opportunity cost of an hour of leisure time is the wage that could have been earned working instead).
14

Calculating Opportunity Cost — Worked Examples

Opportunity cost questions at AQA typically ask you to identify or calculate the value of the next best alternative forgone, using either a simple choice scenario or a production possibility diagram (Section 15).

Worked Example 1 — Simple Choice Between Alternatives

A student has £20 and is deciding between three options for their evening: going to the cinema (£20), buying a takeaway (£15), or saving the money. They choose to go to the cinema.

Opportunity cost of going to the cinema = the next best alternative forgone = the takeaway (£15), not saving the money, since the takeaway was the higher-value alternative given up.

Key point: opportunity cost is not the sum of all forgone alternatives — it is specifically the single next best one.

Worked Example 2 — Using a Linear PPF (Bikes and Cars)

Using the PPF in Fig. 1 (Section 16), an economy currently producing at point C (2 cars, 6 bikes) decides to move to point D (3 cars, 4 bikes) in order to produce one more car.

Opportunity cost of the 1 extra car = 6 bikes − 4 bikes = 2 bikes forgone.

Key point: because this PPF is a straight line, the opportunity cost of each additional car is constant — moving from any point to the next along the frontier always costs exactly 2 bikes per car, since resources are equally suited to producing either good (see Section 16).

Worked Example 3 — Opportunity Cost as a Ratio (Comparative Advantage Style)

A country can use a unit of resource to produce either 10 units of wheat or 5 units of cloth.

Opportunity cost of 1 unit of cloth = 10 wheat ÷ 5 cloth = 2 units of wheat per unit of cloth.
Opportunity cost of 1 unit of wheat = 5 cloth ÷ 10 wheat = 0.5 units of cloth per unit of wheat.

Key point: expressing opportunity cost as a ratio like this is the essential first step in later analysis of comparative advantage and international trade (see the companion BIZ-OMICS International Trade guide).

Exam Tip: whenever a question gives you numbers (prices, output quantities, or PPF coordinates), always show the subtraction or ratio explicitly — AQA awards method marks for correctly identifying which two values are being compared, even if the final answer contains an arithmetic slip.

Production Possibility Frontiers — What They Show

A Production Possibility Frontier (PPF), sometimes called a Production Possibility Curve (PPC), is a diagram showing the maximum possible combinations of two types of output an economy can produce when using all its available resources fully and efficiently, given existing technology.

Fig 1: A linear PPF for Bikes and Cars. Every point on the line (A–F) is productively efficient. Moving between adjacent points always costs exactly 2 bikes per extra car — a constant opportunity cost, because resources transfer between the two goods at a fixed rate (see Section 16).
On the Frontier
Points exactly on the PPF line (e.g. all of A–F in Fig. 1, or point C in Fig. 1b) represent full and efficient use of all available resources — the economy cannot produce more of one good without producing less of the other.
Inside the Frontier
A point inside the PPF (e.g. point D in Fig. 1b) represents an economy that is not using its resources fully or efficiently — some resources are unemployed or underused, an underutilisation of resources explored further in Section 17.
Outside the Frontier — Unobtainable
A point beyond the PPF (e.g. point E in Fig. 1b) is currently unobtainable — it lies beyond what the economy's existing resources and technology can produce, however they are allocated. It could only be reached if the PPF itself shifted outward (see Section 17).
Fig 1b: A curved PPF between Capital Goods and Consumer Goods, showing all three types of point at once. C sits exactly on the frontier (productively efficient); D sits inside the frontier (resources underutilised — see Section 17); E sits outside the frontier and is currently unobtainable, however resources are reallocated, unless the whole PPF shifts outward (Section 17).
What a PPF Illustrates: a single PPF diagram can be used to show several distinct features of the fundamental economic problem at once — the choice of resource allocation between two goods (which point on the frontier to choose), the opportunity cost and trade-off involved in moving between points, the presence of unemployed resources if the economy sits inside the frontier, the unobtainability of any point beyond the frontier, and economic growth if the whole frontier shifts outward — each of which is explored in detail in Section 17.

Linear vs Curved PPFs

A PPF can be drawn as either a straight line or a bowed-out (concave) curve, depending on an assumption about how easily resources can be transferred between producing the two goods — this single modelling choice has significant consequences for the pattern of opportunity cost across the diagram.

Fig 2: A curved (concave) PPF between Capital Goods and Consumer Goods. Point X lies inside the frontier (unemployed or underused resources); point Y lies on the frontier and is productively efficient. The bowed-out shape reflects increasing opportunity cost as production of consumer goods expands.
Linear PPFCurved (Concave) PPF
Assumption about resourcesAll resources are equally well-suited (perfectly substitutable) between producing either good.Resources are better suited to producing one good than the other — some units of land, labour, or capital are more specialised.
Pattern of opportunity costConstant — the opportunity cost of an extra unit of one good is the same no matter where on the frontier you start (e.g. always 2 bikes per car in Fig. 1).Increasing — the opportunity cost of an extra unit of one good rises the more of that good is already being produced, since increasingly unsuitable resources must be redirected.
ShapeA straight line.Bowed outward (concave to the origin) — the standard shape used in most real-world macroeconomic PPF diagrams (capital goods vs consumer goods).
RealismA simplifying special case, useful for isolating the concept of constant opportunity cost.Regarded as more realistic, since real factors of production are rarely perfectly interchangeable between completely different types of output.
Why the Curved PPF Bows Outward: as an economy shifts more and more resources toward consumer goods, it must eventually redirect resources that are far better suited to capital goods production (e.g. specialist heavy machinery or highly trained engineers) — each additional unit of consumer goods therefore costs progressively more capital goods, producing the characteristic concave shape. This is why the curved PPF is steeper at each end than in the middle.

Movements, Shifts & Unemployment on the PPF

Beyond simply showing efficient combinations of output, the PPF is a versatile diagram that can illustrate several distinct economic scenarios depending on where a point lies and how it moves.

Movement Along the Frontier
A reallocation of existing resources between the two goods (e.g. Fig. 1's move from C to D) — this represents a choice about resource allocation and always carries an opportunity cost, but involves no change in the economy's total productive potential.
A Point Inside the Frontier (Unemployment of Resources)
Represents an economy not using its resources fully or efficiently — for example, point X in Fig. 2, where factors of production such as labour or capital sit idle or underused (e.g. during a recession), even though the economy's underlying potential (shown by the frontier itself) has not changed.
Movement Towards the Frontier (Economic Recovery)
The economy moves from a point inside the curve (like X) towards the frontier itself (like Y) — previously idle resources are put back to work, closing a negative output gap without needing any change in the economy's underlying capacity.
Outward Shift of the Frontier (Economic Growth)
The whole PPF shifts outward, meaning more of both goods can now be produced — caused by an increase in the quantity of factors of production available (e.g. a larger workforce or more capital) or a rise in factor productivity (e.g. better technology), previously unattainable combinations of output become achievable.
Inward Shift of the Frontier (Economic Decline)
The whole PPF shifts inward, meaning the economy's maximum potential output has fallen — caused, for example, by a natural disaster destroying capital stock, a shrinking working-age population, or the depletion of a natural resource.
Fig 3: PPF shifts inward and outward. PPF₁ is the original frontier (K₁, C₁). An increase in resources or an improvement in technology shifts the whole frontier outward to PPF₃ (K₃ > K₁, C₃ > C₁), making combinations previously unobtainable (like point E in Fig. 1b) newly achievable. A decrease in resources or a worsening of technology shifts the whole frontier inward to PPF₂ (K₂ < K₁, C₂ < C₁), meaning fewer of both goods can be produced than before.
★ UK EXAMPLE: The 2008–09 Recession on a PPF Diagram
The 2008–09 financial crisis pushed the UK economy well inside its PPF, as GDP contracted sharply while the underlying capital stock and workforce remained largely intact — unemployment rose from around 5.2% to a peak of 8.5%, illustrating factories, offices, and workers sitting idle rather than producing, exactly as point X illustrates in Fig. 2. The subsequent recovery represented a movement back towards the frontier, closing the gap between actual and potential output, without requiring any underlying growth in the economy's productive capacity.
Exam Trap to Avoid: a very common error is to confuse a movement towards the frontier (a recovery, absorbing spare capacity) with an outward shift of the frontier (genuine economic growth) — these represent two entirely different economic events and must never be described interchangeably.

Productive vs Allocative Efficiency

Every point exactly on the PPF boundary is productively efficient — but this does not mean every such point is also allocatively efficient. The two concepts of efficiency are related but distinct, and confusing them is a frequent source of lost marks.

Productive Efficiency
Occurs whenever an economy is producing on its PPF boundary — using all its available resources fully, with no waste, and producing the maximum possible output from those resources given current technology. Every single point on the frontier (A–F in Fig. 1, or Y in Fig. 2) satisfies this condition, regardless of which combination of goods is being produced.
Allocative Efficiency
Occurs only at the one specific point on the PPF boundary that reflects society's actual preferences — where the combination of goods being produced matches what consumers most value, so that resources are allocated to their most valued use overall.
Why Not All Boundary Points Are Allocatively Efficient: productive efficiency is a technical property of how output is produced (using all resources, wasting none) and is satisfied identically by every point on the frontier. Allocative efficiency additionally requires that the particular combination of output chosen matches what society actually wants — an economy could, for instance, be producing efficiently at point F in Fig. 1 (all cars, no bikes), fully using its resources, and yet be badly allocatively inefficient if consumers in fact place much higher value on bikes than cars. Only the specific point matching consumer preferences is allocatively as well as productively efficient.
Productive EfficiencyAllocative Efficiency
ConditionProducing on the PPF boundary (no resources wasted or unemployed).Producing the specific combination of goods on the boundary that matches consumer preferences.
How many points on the PPF satisfy it?Every single point on the boundary.Only one specific point on the boundary (in a simple two-good model).
AnalogyDoing things right — using resources without waste.Doing the right things — producing what people actually want most.

Exam Technique & Model Chains of Reasoning

The Golden Chain: Scarcity to Opportunity Cost

Resources (land, labour, capital, enterprise) are finite → human wants are effectively unlimited → this gap creates the fundamental economic problem of scarcity → because not every want can be satisfied, choices must be made about how scarce resources are allocated → every choice made means an alternative use of that resource is forgone → the value of the best forgone alternative is the opportunity cost of the choice.

The Golden Chain: Recession, Recovery, and Growth on a PPF

A negative demand shock (e.g. a financial crisis) causes firms to cut output and lay off workers → previously fully-employed resources become idle → the economy moves from a point on its PPF to a point inside it, representing unemployment of resources → as demand recovers, firms re-employ idle resources and output rises → the economy moves back towards its PPF boundary, closing the output gap → this recovery is entirely separate from genuine economic growth, which instead requires an increase in the quantity or productivity of factors of production and is shown as an outward shift of the entire frontier.

Key Exam Tips

1

Always classify a statement as positive or normative by asking whether it could, in principle, be tested against evidence alone — not simply by scanning for words like "should."

2

When calculating opportunity cost, always identify the single next-best alternative forgone — not a list of everything given up.

3

Distinguish clearly between a movement along the PPF (resource reallocation, always at a cost), a movement inside/towards the PPF (unemployment/recovery), and a shift of the PPF itself (change in productive potential) — never use these interchangeably.

4

Remember that every point on a PPF boundary is productively efficient, but only one point additionally matches consumer preferences and is therefore also allocatively efficient — state both conditions explicitly when asked to evaluate efficiency.

5

When asked to compare a linear and curved PPF, always link the shape directly to the assumption about resource substitutability, and describe the resulting pattern of opportunity cost (constant vs increasing) rather than just describing the shapes.

6

For extended-response questions on policy, always separate the positive economic analysis (predicted effects) from the normative judgement (whether those effects are desirable), and be explicit that your final conclusion depends on the value judgement applied.
