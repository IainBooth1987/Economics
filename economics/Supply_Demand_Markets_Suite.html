📊 The Laws of Demand

An Interactive Guide to Understanding Consumer Behavior

The Law of Demand

📚 Theoretical Foundation

The Law of Demand states that, ceteris paribus (all other factors remaining constant), there is an inverse relationship between the price of a good and the quantity demanded. As price increases, quantity demanded decreases, and vice versa.

Why Does This Inverse Relationship Exist?

1. The Substitution Effect: When the price of a good rises, consumers substitute it with relatively cheaper alternatives. For example, if beef prices increase, consumers may switch to chicken or pork.

2. The Income Effect: When prices rise, real purchasing power (real income) decreases. Consumers can afford to buy less with the same nominal income, leading to a reduction in quantity demanded.

3. Diminishing Marginal Utility: Each additional unit of a good consumed provides less satisfaction than the previous one. Consumers are willing to pay less for additional units, meaning higher prices lead to lower quantity demanded as consumers reach their utility maximization point earlier.

4. New Buyers Enter/Exit the Market: At lower prices, more consumers find the product affordable and enter the market. At higher prices, some consumers are priced out of the market entirely.

📈 Movements Along the Demand Curve

A change in price causes a movement along the existing demand curve, not a shift of the curve itself. This is crucial to understand:

  • Extension of Demand (Expansion): When price decreases, quantity demanded increases - movement down and to the right along the curve
  • Contraction of Demand: When price increases, quantity demanded decreases - movement up and to the left along the curve

⚠️ Exception: Giffen Goods

What Are Giffen Goods?

Giffen goods represent a fascinating exception to the Law of Demand. These are inferior goods for which the quantity demanded increases when the price rises, creating a positively-sloped demand curve.

Why Do Giffen Goods Violate the Law of Demand?

The Income Effect Dominates the Substitution Effect:

For a Giffen good to exist, three conditions must be met:

  • 1. The good must be inferior: As income rises, demand for the good falls
  • 2. The good must represent a large proportion of the consumer's budget: The income effect must be substantial
  • 3. There must be few or no close substitutes: The substitution effect must be small

The Mechanism:

When the price of a Giffen good rises:

  • Substitution Effect (normal): The higher price encourages substitution away from the good (reduces quantity demanded)
  • Income Effect (paradoxical): The price increase significantly reduces real income. Because the consumer is now poorer and the good is inferior, they actually consume MORE of it, as they can no longer afford superior alternatives
  • Net Result: The large negative income effect outweighs the small substitution effect, causing quantity demanded to increase with price

🌾 Historical Example: The Irish Potato Famine (1845-1852)

The classic historical example comes from potatoes in 19th-century Ireland. Potatoes were:

  • A staple food comprising a large portion of the poor's diet and budget
  • An inferior good (the poor would prefer meat or bread if they could afford it)
  • Subject to few affordable substitutes for the impoverished

When potato prices rose, the poor became even poorer (strong income effect). They couldn't afford meat or bread anymore, so they bought even MORE potatoes to survive, despite the higher price.

🍚 Modern Example: Staple Foods in Developing Economies

In some developing countries, rice or wheat might exhibit Giffen behavior for the poorest households:

  • When rice prices increase, very poor families can no longer afford any vegetables or protein
  • They respond by purchasing more rice (the only calories they can afford) despite its higher price
  • Rice consumption increases as price increases for the poorest segment

Important Note: Giffen goods are extremely rare in practice. They require very specific economic conditions and are primarily theoretical or confined to historical contexts and extreme poverty situations.

Shifts in Demand

📚 Understanding Demand Shifts

A shift in demand occurs when factors other than the good's own price change, causing the entire demand curve to move to a new position. This represents a change in demand at every price level.

Rightward Shift (Increase in Demand): At every price point, consumers are willing and able to buy more. The entire curve moves right.

Leftward Shift (Decrease in Demand): At every price point, consumers are willing and able to buy less. The entire curve moves left.

Key Distinction: Remember, price changes cause movements ALONG the curve, while non-price factors cause SHIFTS of the entire curve.

🎯 Non-Price Determinants of Demand

The following factors cause the demand curve to shift:

1️⃣ Income Levels

Normal Goods: Demand increases when income rises

Inferior Goods: Demand decreases when income rises

Real-World Example:

Normal Good - Organic Food: As household incomes rise, consumers shift toward organic produce. A 2023 study showed that a 10% increase in median income correlated with a 15% increase in organic food sales.

Inferior Good - Instant Ramen: During economic prosperity, instant ramen sales decline as consumers upgrade to fresh meals and restaurant dining.

2️⃣ Prices of Related Goods

Substitutes: If the price of a substitute rises, demand for this good increases

Complements: If the price of a complement rises, demand for this good decreases

Real-World Example:

Substitutes - Coffee vs Tea: When coffee prices surged 25% in 2021 due to Brazilian frost, tea sales increased by 12% in major markets as consumers substituted.

Complements - Cars and Gasoline: When gas prices spiked to $5/gallon in 2022, demand for large SUVs dropped 18%, while hybrid vehicle demand increased 35%.

3️⃣ Consumer Tastes & Preferences

Changes in fashion, trends, advertising, and social attitudes shift demand

Real-World Example:

Plant-Based Meat: Growing health and environmental consciousness increased demand for plant-based meat alternatives by 300% between 2019-2023, despite prices being higher than conventional meat.

Fast Fashion Decline: Negative publicity about environmental impact reduced demand for fast fashion brands by 20% among millennials and Gen-Z consumers (2020-2023).

4️⃣ Population & Demographics

Changes in population size, age distribution, or composition affect market demand

Real-World Example:

Aging Population - Healthcare: As baby boomers age, demand for healthcare services, retirement homes, and mobility aids has increased by 40% in the last decade.

Urbanization - Delivery Services: Migration to cities increased urban population density, boosting food delivery app usage by 250% (2018-2023).

5️⃣ Future Price Expectations

If consumers expect prices to rise, current demand increases (and vice versa)

Real-World Example:

Housing Market: In 2020-2021, expectations of rising interest rates and home prices led to a buying frenzy. Home sales increased 22% as buyers rushed to purchase before anticipated price increases.

Black Friday Sales: Announcements of upcoming sales cause consumers to delay purchases, shifting current demand leftward as they wait for lower prices.

6️⃣ Consumer Confidence

Optimism about the economy increases demand; pessimism decreases it

Real-World Example:

COVID-19 Pandemic (2020): Consumer confidence plummeted, causing demand for non-essential goods (luxury items, travel, dining out) to decrease by 40-60%, even among those who retained their income.

Post-Recession Recovery (2010-2012): As confidence returned, demand for durable goods like appliances and vehicles increased 30% despite stable income levels.

7️⃣ Seasonal Factors

Time of year affects demand for many goods

Real-World Example:

Ice Cream: Demand shifts rightward by 40-50% during summer months compared to winter, independent of price changes.

Heating Oil: Demand increases 300% in winter months in cold climates, causing the demand curve to shift significantly rightward from October to March.

8️⃣ Credit Availability

Easier access to credit increases demand for big-ticket items

Real-World Example:

Auto Loans (2010s): Relaxed lending standards and 84-month loan terms increased vehicle demand by 25%, enabling consumers to purchase more expensive vehicles.

Credit Card Limits: Studies show that a $1,000 increase in credit card limits increases consumer spending by $130-$190 on average, shifting demand for various goods.

🔍 Key Takeaway: The Ceteris Paribus Condition

The original demand curve is drawn assuming all non-price factors remain constant (ceteris paribus). When any of these factors change, we must draw a new demand curve in a different position.

This distinction is crucial for economic analysis:

  • Policy Implications: Price controls cause movements along the curve (shortages/surpluses), while policies affecting income or preferences shift the curve
  • Business Strategy: Companies can't shift their demand curve by changing price alone; they must invest in marketing, innovation, or target different demographics
  • Market Analysis: Understanding whether changes are movements or shifts helps predict market equilibrium outcomes

📈 Understanding Supply

An Interactive Guide to Supply Theory and Determinants

The Laws of Supply

The Law of Supply states that there is a positive (direct) relationship between price and quantity supplied, ceteris paribus (all other factors remaining constant). This means that as the price of a good increases, producers are willing and able to supply more of that good to the market.

🎯 Why Does This Positive Relationship Exist?

1. Profit Motive

Producers are motivated by profit maximization. When prices rise, the potential for greater profits increases, incentivizing producers to supply more goods. Higher prices mean higher revenue for each unit sold, making production more attractive.

2. Law of Diminishing Returns

As production increases in the short run (with at least one fixed factor), each additional unit becomes more costly to produce. The marginal cost of production rises. Therefore, producers need higher prices to justify producing additional units and to cover these increasing costs.

3. Opportunity Cost

Producers face opportunity costs when allocating resources. A higher price for a good makes it more worthwhile to dedicate resources to its production rather than alternative uses. The higher price compensates for the opportunity cost of not producing other goods.

4. Entry of New Firms

When prices are high and profits attractive, new firms are incentivized to enter the market, increasing overall market supply. Conversely, low prices may drive some firms out of the market.

5. Existing Producers Expand Capacity

Higher prices encourage existing producers to increase their productive capacity by utilizing more resources, extending working hours, or investing in additional capital equipment.

📊 Movement Along the Supply Curve

It's crucial to understand that changes in price cause movements along the supply curve, not shifts of the curve itself. There are two types of movements:

  • Extension of Supply (Expansion): An increase in quantity supplied caused by an increase in price - movement up and to the right along the supply curve
  • Contraction of Supply: A decrease in quantity supplied caused by a decrease in price - movement down and to the left along the supply curve

🔑 Key Concept

Remember: Price changes cause movements ALONG the supply curve. Non-price factors cause SHIFTS of the entire supply curve (covered in Tab 2).

🛢️ Real-World Example: Oil Market (2020-2022)

Extension of Supply: During 2021-2022, as oil prices rose from around £50 per barrel to over £80 per barrel, oil producers (particularly OPEC members and US shale producers) increased production significantly. Higher prices made it profitable to reopen previously capped wells and increase extraction rates, leading to an expansion in quantity supplied along the supply curve.

Contraction of Supply: In April 2020, when oil prices collapsed to below £20 per barrel (even briefly negative for some futures contracts), producers drastically cut production. Many wells were shut down as the low prices made extraction unprofitable, demonstrating a contraction in quantity supplied along the supply curve.

Shifts of Supply

While price changes cause movements along the supply curve, non-price determinants cause the entire supply curve to shift. A shift represents a change in supply at every price level.

🔄 Types of Shifts

  • Rightward Shift (Increase in Supply): More is supplied at every price level - the curve moves to the right
  • Leftward Shift (Decrease in Supply): Less is supplied at every price level - the curve moves to the left

📋 Non-Price Determinants of Supply (CREPT-G)

💰 Costs of Production

Increase in costs → Leftward shift (decreased supply)

Decrease in costs → Rightward shift (increased supply)

Includes wages, raw materials, energy, and rent

🔬 Related Goods Prices

Substitutes in production: If maize prices rise, farmers may reduce wheat supply to grow more maize

Complements in production: Beef and leather are produced together - more beef means more leather supply

🎯 Expectations

Future price rise expected → Current supply decreases (withhold stock)

Future price fall expected → Current supply increases (sell now)

🏭 Productivity & Technology

Improved technology → Rightward shift (increased efficiency and output)

Better productivity → Lower costs per unit, increased supply

📜 Taxes & Subsidies

Indirect taxes ↑ → Leftward shift (increased costs)

Subsidies ↑ → Rightward shift (decreased costs)

🌍 Other Factors

Number of sellers: More firms → increased supply

Weather/Natural events: Can increase or decrease agricultural supply

Government regulations: Can restrict or encourage supply

📈 Market Equilibrium

Understanding How Markets Find Balance

What is Market Equilibrium?

Market equilibrium occurs when the quantity of a good or service that consumers want to buy (demand) exactly equals the quantity that producers want to sell (supply). At this point, the market reaches a stable state where there is no pressure for the price or quantity to change.

Equilibrium Price: The price at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. This is also known as the market-clearing price The market-clearing price is the price level at which the market "clears" all goods with no surplus (excess supply) or shortage (excess demand). At this price, every willing buyer finds a willing seller. .

Why is it Called the Market-Clearing Price?

The equilibrium price is called the market-clearing price because at this price level, the market "clears" - meaning there is neither a shortage nor a surplus of goods.

Above Equilibrium (Surplus):

When the price is set above equilibrium, quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded. This creates a surplus, putting downward pressure on prices as suppliers compete to sell their excess inventory.

Below Equilibrium (Shortage):

When the price is set below equilibrium, quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied. This creates a shortage, putting upward pressure on prices as consumers compete for the limited supply.

Self-Correcting Mechanism: The beauty of market equilibrium is that the market naturally moves toward this clearing price through the forces of supply and demand. Surpluses cause prices to fall, while shortages cause prices to rise, until equilibrium is reached.

Allocative Efficiency

Market equilibrium demonstrates allocative efficiency Allocative efficiency occurs when resources are distributed in the best possible way to maximize total welfare. It happens when the price equals the marginal cost of production and the marginal benefit to consumers. - a situation where resources are distributed in a way that maximizes social welfare. At equilibrium, allocative efficiency is achieved because:

  • Consumer Satisfaction: Every consumer willing to pay the equilibrium price or higher gets the product, maximizing consumer surplus Consumer surplus is the difference between what consumers are willing to pay and what they actually pay. It represents the extra benefit consumers receive from a purchase. .
  • Producer Optimization: Every producer willing to supply at the equilibrium price or lower can sell their product, maximizing producer surplus Producer surplus is the difference between the price producers receive and the minimum price they would accept. It represents the profit or benefit producers gain from selling. .
  • No Waste: There are no willing buyers left without goods and no willing sellers left with unsold goods.
  • Optimal Resource Allocation: Resources are allocated to their most valued uses, as determined by what consumers are willing to pay and what producers are willing to accept.
Economic Welfare Maximization: At equilibrium, the combined value of consumer surplus (benefit to buyers) and producer surplus (benefit to sellers) is maximized. This represents the most efficient allocation of resources from society's perspective.

Key Characteristics of Market Equilibrium

🎯 Stability

Once reached, the market tends to remain at equilibrium unless external factors cause supply or demand to shift.

⚖️ Balance

The equilibrium point represents a perfect balance where market forces are in harmony, with no tendency for change.

💰 Price Discovery

The market equilibrium process reveals the true value of goods and services through the interaction of buyers and sellers.

⚠️ When Markets Don't Reach Efficient Equilibrium

While the theory of market equilibrium assumes perfect competition, in reality, many markets fail to achieve allocative efficiency due to various market failures and imperfections. Understanding these limitations is crucial for a complete picture of how markets actually function.

Market Power: Monopolies and Oligopolies

🏢 Monopoly Markets

In a monopoly A monopoly exists when a single firm dominates the market with no close substitutes, giving it significant control over price and output. , a single seller controls the market and can set prices above the competitive equilibrium level.

Why equilibrium fails:

  • Restricted Output: Monopolists produce less than the socially optimal quantity to maintain higher prices
  • Higher Prices: Prices are set above marginal cost, unlike in competitive markets
  • Deadweight Loss: The reduction in total welfare creates economic inefficiency
  • Allocative Inefficiency: Resources are not allocated to maximize social welfare

Real-world examples: Utility companies (electricity, water), pharmaceutical patents, local cable providers

🤝 Oligopoly Markets

In an oligopoly An oligopoly is a market structure with a few large firms that dominate the industry, leading to strategic interdependence. , a few large firms dominate and may collude to restrict competition.

Why equilibrium fails:

  • Price Leadership: Dominant firms set prices that others follow, preventing true competitive equilibrium
  • Tacit Collusion: Firms may avoid price competition without explicit agreements
  • Barriers to Entry: High entry costs prevent new competitors from entering the market
  • Strategic Behavior: Firms consider rivals' reactions rather than responding only to market forces

Real-world examples: Airlines, telecommunications, automotive industry, streaming services

Other Market Failures

Externalities: When production or consumption affects third parties not involved in the transaction, the market equilibrium doesn't reflect true social costs or benefits. For example, pollution (negative externality) leads to overproduction, while education (positive externality) leads to underproduction.
Public Goods: Goods that are non-excludable and non-rivalrous (like national defense or street lighting) cannot be efficiently provided by free markets, as the equilibrium would result in underproduction due to the free-rider problem The free-rider problem occurs when people can benefit from a good without paying for it, leading to underprovision by private markets. .
Information Asymmetry: When buyers and sellers have different information (such as in used car markets or insurance), market equilibrium may not be efficient. The seller knows more than the buyer, leading to adverse selection and potential market failure.
Merit and Demerit Goods: Governments may intervene when they believe consumers under-consume beneficial goods (merit goods like healthcare) or over-consume harmful goods (demerit goods like tobacco), as the free market equilibrium doesn't align with social welfare.

Government Intervention

When markets fail to achieve efficient equilibrium, governments often intervene through:

  • Price Controls: Minimum wages, rent controls (though these create their own inefficiencies)
  • Regulation: Anti-trust laws to prevent monopolies, environmental regulations for externalities
  • Taxation and Subsidies: To correct externalities or encourage/discourage consumption
  • Public Provision: Direct provision of public goods and merit goods
  • Information Campaigns: To reduce information asymmetry

⚖️ The Trade-Off

While perfect competition and market equilibrium provide a useful theoretical framework, policymakers must balance the efficiency of markets with their limitations. Understanding when markets work well and when they fail is essential for effective economic policy.

Changes to Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium is not static. When supply or demand shifts, a new equilibrium is formed at a different price and quantity. Understanding these shifts is crucial for analyzing how markets respond to real-world changes.

The Pricing Mechanism

The pricing mechanism has three key functions that help markets adjust to new equilibriums:

📡 Signal Function

Prices provide information to buyers and sellers about market conditions. A rising price signals scarcity or increased demand, while a falling price signals abundance or decreased demand.

🎁 Incentive Function

Prices create incentives for behavior. Higher prices incentivize producers to supply more and consumers to demand less. Lower prices incentivize consumers to buy more and producers to supply less.

📊 Rationing Function

Prices allocate scarce resources among competing uses. When demand exceeds supply, higher prices ration goods to those most willing and able to pay, preventing shortages from persisting.

Understanding Extensions and Contractions

Important Distinction: When a curve shifts, we say demand/supply "increases" or "decreases". When we move along a curve due to price changes, we say demand/supply "extends" or "contracts".

Extensions and Contractions:

  • Extension of Demand: Movement down the demand curve when price falls (quantity demanded increases)
  • Contraction of Demand: Movement up the demand curve when price rises (quantity demanded decreases)
  • Extension of Supply: Movement up the supply curve when price rises (quantity supplied increases)
  • Contraction of Supply: Movement down the supply curve when price falls (quantity supplied decreases)

Market Interrelationships

Understanding Joint Supply & Demand, Derived Demand, and Composite Demand

Joint Supply and Joint Demand

Theory: Joint Supply

Joint Supply occurs when the production of one good automatically results in the production of another good or by-product. These goods are produced together from a single production process, making it impossible to produce one without producing the other.

The supply of one good is intrinsically linked to the supply of the other(s). An increase in production of the primary good leads to an automatic increase in the supply of the by-product(s).

Theory: Joint Demand

Joint Demand (also called complementary demand) occurs when two or more goods are demanded together because they are consumed or used in conjunction with each other. The goods have little or no value when consumed separately.

An increase in demand for one good will lead to an increase in demand for the other good(s), as they are typically purchased together.

Key Differences

Aspect Joint Supply Joint Demand
Definition Multiple products produced simultaneously from a single process Multiple products consumed together
Side of Market Supply side Demand side
Relationship Production of one creates the other Consumption of one requires the other
Control Producer cannot easily separate outputs Consumer chooses to purchase together
Price Effect Increase in demand for one product affects price of by-product Increase in price of one reduces demand for both

Real World Examples

Joint Supply Example 1: Livestock

Primary product: Beef

By-products: Leather, bone meal, gelatin, pet food ingredients

When a cattle farmer increases production to meet higher beef demand, they automatically produce more leather as a by-product. This increases leather supply in the market, potentially lowering leather prices if demand remains constant.

Joint Supply Example 2: Petroleum Refining

Primary products: Petrol, diesel

By-products: Kerosene, lubricants, petroleum jelly, asphalt

Crude oil refining produces multiple products simultaneously. An increase in petrol production inevitably increases the supply of other petroleum products.

Joint Demand Example 1: Cars and Petrol

Cars and petrol are in joint demand. An increase in car sales leads to increased demand for petrol. Conversely, if petrol prices rise significantly, it may reduce demand for cars (especially fuel-inefficient ones).

Joint Demand Example 2: Printers and Ink Cartridges

Printers and ink cartridges are complementary goods. When printer sales increase, demand for ink cartridges rises. Printer manufacturers often sell printers cheaply but earn profits from cartridge sales.

Derived Demand

Theory

Derived Demand refers to demand for a good or service that arises not from direct consumption but because it is needed to produce another good or service. The demand is "derived" from the demand for the final product.

This concept is particularly important in factor markets (labour, capital, raw materials) where demand depends on the demand for the final goods these factors help produce.

Key characteristics:

  • Demand is indirect and depends on another market
  • Changes in final product demand directly affect derived demand
  • Price elasticity of derived demand relates to the final product's demand elasticity
  • Common in labour markets and raw material markets

Real World Examples

Example 1: Tourism and Seasonal Workers

Final product: Tourism services (holidays, experiences)

Derived demand: Seasonal workers (hotel staff, tour guides, waiters)

When tourism increases in a coastal resort, hotels and restaurants need more staff. The demand for seasonal workers is derived from tourist demand. If tourism declines, fewer workers are needed.

Example 2: Housing Construction and Building Materials

Final product: New houses

Derived demand: Timber, bricks, cement, construction workers

A housing boom increases demand for construction materials and skilled tradespeople. Their demand derives entirely from housing demand. When the housing market slows, construction material and labor demand falls.

Example 3: Smartphone Production and Rare Earth Metals

Final product: Smartphones

Derived demand: Lithium (batteries), cobalt, rare earth elements

The explosive growth in smartphone demand has created massive derived demand for lithium and cobalt. These materials have little consumer demand themselves but are essential for smartphone production.

Example 4: Film Industry and Camera Equipment

Final product: Movies and TV shows

Derived demand: Professional cameras, lighting equipment, sound engineers

When streaming services increase film production, demand for professional camera equipment and skilled crew members rises accordingly.

Step-by-Step Explanation:

1. Tourism demand increases (D₁ → D₂) - perhaps due to seasonal factors or destination popularity

2. Hotels, restaurants, and tour operators need more workers to serve increased tourists

3. Labour demand shifts right (DL₁ → DL₂) - this is derived demand

4. Wages increase (W₁ → W₂) and employment rises (L₁ → L₂)

5. If tourism falls (off-season), this process reverses - derived demand falls, wages decrease, workers laid off

Key Insight: Derived demand creates a direct link between final product markets and factor markets. Workers, materials, and capital goods are demanded not for themselves, but because they're needed to produce goods and services that consumers actually want. This makes factor markets highly sensitive to changes in final product demand.

Policy Implications

Understanding derived demand is crucial for:

  • Labour market policy: Supporting industries with high consumer demand creates employment opportunities
  • Resource planning: Anticipating raw material needs based on final product demand
  • Economic forecasting: Changes in consumer sectors ripple through supply chains
  • Business strategy: Suppliers must monitor their customers' markets, not just their immediate buyers

Composite Demand

Theory

Composite Demand (also called competitive demand) occurs when a single good or resource is demanded for multiple different uses. The resource must be allocated between competing uses, creating opportunity costs.

When demand increases in one use, it can restrict availability and increase prices for other uses, assuming supply is relatively fixed or slow to adjust.

Key characteristics:

  • Single resource with multiple applications
  • Competition between different uses for the same resource
  • Increased demand in one sector can crowd out other sectors
  • Creates opportunity costs between alternative uses
  • Price increases in one use tend to affect all uses

Real World Examples

Example 1: Milk - Food vs. Dairy Products

Single resource: Milk

Competing uses: Fresh drinking milk, cheese production, butter production, yogurt, ice cream

If demand for cheese rises significantly (perhaps due to pizza popularity), more milk is diverted to cheese production. This can reduce milk availability for other uses, potentially increasing prices for fresh milk, butter, and ice cream.

Example 2: Corn - Food, Fuel, and Feed

Single resource: Corn

Competing uses: Human food (corn flour, sweeteners), animal feed, biofuel (ethanol)

The rise of biofuel production created significant composite demand for corn. When governments mandated ethanol in petrol, corn demand for fuel increased dramatically, raising corn prices for food manufacturers and livestock farmers. This sparked the "food vs. fuel" debate.

Example 3: Land - Housing vs. Agriculture

Single resource: Land (especially near cities)

Competing uses: Residential housing, commercial development, agricultural production

Urban expansion increases demand for land for housing. This raises land prices, making farming on the urban fringe less profitable. Agricultural land gets converted to housing estates, reducing food production capacity near cities.

Example 4: Steel - Construction vs. Manufacturing

Single resource: Steel

Competing uses: Construction (buildings, bridges), automotive manufacturing, machinery, appliances

A construction boom increases steel demand for building projects. This can raise steel prices for car manufacturers and other industries, potentially forcing them to reduce production or seek alternatives.

Example 5: Water - Drinking vs. Agriculture vs. Industry

Single resource: Fresh water

Competing uses: Household consumption, agricultural irrigation, industrial processes, power generation

In drought conditions, competition for limited water intensifies. Allocating more water to cities can restrict agricultural irrigation, affecting crop yields and food prices.

Key Insight: Composite demand creates trade-offs between different uses of the same resource. When demand increases for one use, it can crowd out other uses by raising prices and reducing availability. This is especially significant when supply is relatively inelastic (fixed) in the short run.

Price Transmission in Composite Demand

How price changes spread across uses:

  1. Initial shock: Demand increases in one market (e.g., biofuel)
  2. Resource competition: More of the resource is allocated to the growing market
  3. Supply restriction: Less resource available for other uses
  4. Price rise: Prices increase across ALL uses of the resource
  5. Reallocation: Resource flows to highest-value uses

The strength of this effect depends on:

  • Supply elasticity: Can supply expand to meet all demands?
  • Substitutability: Can users switch to alternatives?
  • Relative demand sizes: Large shifts in small markets matter less
  • Time horizon: Long-run supply is more elastic than short-run

Economic and Policy Implications

Market Allocation Challenges

Composite demand raises important questions:

  • Should governments prioritize certain uses? (e.g., food over fuel, water for drinking over industry)
  • How do markets balance competing needs through price mechanisms?
  • What happens when one use is subsidized (creating unfair competition)?
  • Should there be quotas or restrictions on certain uses?

Example: The EU's biofuel mandates increased corn/rapeseed demand for ethanol/biodiesel, raising food prices. This contributed to the 2007-2008 food price crisis. Policymakers had to reconsider the balance between renewable energy goals and food security.
