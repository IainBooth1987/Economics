🔺 Price Ceilings — Maximum Prices

Rationale

⚡ UK Energy Price Guarantee (2022)

During the energy crisis, Ofgem's price cap was raised sharply. The government introduced an EPG limiting what households paid per unit. Rationale: energy is a near-necessity; rising prices disproportionately affected low-income households (equity failure). Result: reduced consumer bills but cost the Treasury ~£25bn in supplier subsidies.

Rationale

🏠 Rent Controls (Scotland 2022–2024)

Scotland introduced rent freeze powers for social and private tenants during the cost-of-living crisis. Rationale: housing is a merit good; market rents had risen 14–25% in cities. Risk: landlords exiting the market, reducing long-run supply.

Unintended Consequences

🇻🇪 Venezuela Food Price Caps

Caps on staple food prices led to chronic shortages, empty shelves, and black markets. A textbook example of government failure where price signals were suppressed entirely.

Evaluative point: Price ceilings may achieve short-run equity goals but risk long-run supply reductions and misallocation. The degree of elasticity matters: inelastic supply (e.g. housing) worsens shortages more severely.

🔻 Price Floors — Minimum Prices

Rationale

🥃 MUP on Alcohol — Scotland (2018)

Scotland set a minimum price of 50p per unit of alcohol — the first such policy in the UK. Rationale: alcohol is a demerit good (negative externalities, information failure). Market prices for cheap own-brand spirits/cider were well below social cost. Evidence shows alcohol-specific deaths fell 13.4% in the two years following implementation.

Rationale

💷 National Living Wage (UK, 2016–present)

The NLW is a price floor in the labour market. Rationale: monopsonistic employers (esp. in care, retail) pay below the competitive wage. The NLW corrects this. Research (Resolution Foundation) shows limited negative employment effects at current levels, though controversy continues at higher rates.

Unintended Consequences

🌾 EU Common Agricultural Policy (CAP)

Guaranteed prices above equilibrium generated massive overproduction: "grain mountains" and "butter mountains". EU taxpayers funded costly storage and disposal. Also distorted global trade, harming developing-country farmers.

Evaluative point: Price floors work best where there is a clear market failure rationale (externality, monopsony, demerit good). Setting the floor too far above equilibrium amplifies surplus and government failure. Context determines whether intervention improves or worsens welfare.
