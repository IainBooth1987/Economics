Case study — the trades reversal

Recent labour market data suggests trades wages have grown faster than the wider economy — a live example of demand and supply shifting at the same time. Source: ONS ASHE 2025; Total Skills UK trade wage analysis, 2026

Why is this happening? ↓
Evidence
A shortfall of roughly 104,000 electricians is projected by 2032, alongside a reported 227:1 ratio of vacancies to apprenticeship places. Source: UK Trade Skills Index; Total Skills UK, 2026
↓
Economic explanation
Labour supply for electricians is relatively inelastic in the short run because training (apprenticeship + licensing) takes several years. At the same time, demand (and therefore MRP) is rising, partly driven by EV charging and heat-pump installation work. A rightward demand shift meeting an inelastic supply curve produces a larger wage rise than it would in a market where supply could adjust quickly.
↓
Evaluation
Higher wages should eventually attract more entrants — and apprenticeship applications in construction do appear to be rising — but only with a multi-year lag, so the wage premium is likely to persist for some time before supply catches up. This is theory-consistent, but the exact size and duration of the effect isn't something a single data point can confirm.
Evidence
Government policy confirms no new gas boiler installations in new-build homes from 2025, with a full ban by 2035. Source: UK Government policy announcements, as reported by Total Skills UK, 2026
↓
Economic explanation
This is a demand shift that moves in opposite directions for two similar trades: it increases demand for electricians (heat pumps, EV infrastructure) while reducing long-run demand for plumbers whose work is tied to gas systems — illustrating that "trades" isn't a single labour market, but several distinct ones responding to different signals.
↓
Evaluation
Plumbers able to retrain into heat pump installation would likely offset much of this effect; the wage impact therefore depends heavily on how easily supply can shift between occupations, not just how it responds within one.
Evidence
Self-employed tradespeople reportedly out-earn employed tradespeople by £15,000+ a year on average. Source: ProCalculator UK trades earnings analysis, 2026
↓
Economic explanation
This gap is better explained as economic rent for risk-bearing than as extra human capital — self-employed workers forgo sick pay, employer pension contributions and guaranteed hours, so part of their higher headline pay compensates for that lost security.
↓
Evaluation
Comparing self-employed and employed pay directly can overstate the "true" premium once these non-wage costs are priced in — a fuller comparison would need to net off the value of benefits employed workers receive.

Case study — the gender pay gap

Equal pay ≠ the gender pay gap. Paying men and women differently for the same job has been illegal since the Equal Pay Act 1970. The ONS gender pay gap is a different, broader measure: the difference between men's and women's median earnings across all jobs in the economy — it captures who ends up in which jobs and grades, not whether any individual employer is breaking the law. Source: ONS, Gender pay gap in the UK, 2025
What explains the gap? ↓
Evidence
Skilled trades show the largest occupational gender pay gap reported (13.9%), and the overall gap for full-time employees roughly triples between the 30–39 and 40+ age bands (3.9% → 9.1%). Source: ONS, Gender pay gap in the UK, 2025
↓
Economic explanation
Occupational segregation (very few women working in trades at all) and human capital effects (career breaks around childcare interrupting pay progression) are the standard explanations economists reach for before assuming direct pay discrimination — both can produce a large aggregate gap without any employer ever paying a man and a woman differently for an identical role.
↓
Evaluation
Current data can't fully separate how much of the gap is segregation versus human capital versus residual discrimination — that decomposition would need more detailed longitudinal data than a headline occupation-level figure provides. Treat the split as indicative, not precise.
Evidence
The UK-wide gender pay gap for full-time employees has fallen by roughly a quarter over the past decade, but progress has reportedly slowed in recent years. Source: ONS, Gender pay gap in the UK, 2025
↓
Economic explanation
Becker's model predicts competitive markets should erode taste-based discrimination over time, which is broadly consistent with the long-run decline — but a slowing rate of decline suggests either that remaining causes are harder to compete away (structural segregation, human capital effects) or that discrimination was never the dominant cause in the first place.
↓
Evaluation
This is a genuinely open empirical question rather than a settled one — a stronger answer would acknowledge that economists disagree on how much of the residual gap reflects discrimination versus unmeasured segregation and human capital factors.
