How to Run This — Quick-Start Guide

Written for the version of you teaching this cold, five minutes before the bell.

Before the lesson (2 minutes)

Open Northbridge_Mortgages.html on the board/projector — this is the only screen students see, with its own Setup tab for team names and team count (3–5 branches works best for a class of 24–30). Print or hand out Northbridge_Mortgages_Student_Workbook.html, one per branch, not per student. Keep this answer booklet open or printed beside you — every reveal, rubric and "watch for" note below matches what's projected live in the console.

The shape of every round

  1. Brief. The console shows Head Office's task and the option cards. Read it aloud once.
  2. Work. Start the on-screen timer. Branches discuss and write on the matching workbook page. Circulate — don't reveal anything yet.
  3. Reveal. Click "Reveal" on the console. The decision and reasoning appear on screen — identical to what's printed in this booklet for that round.
  4. Score. Use this booklet's rubric to mark each branch's worksheet answer, type the score into the console's score grid — the league table updates itself.
  5. Move on. Click the next round in the console's nav bar.
This is a connected story, not four separate scenarios. Round 2's brief references Round 1's choices, Round 3 references Rounds 1–2, and Round 4 ("The Crash") is where every branch's earlier choices catch up with them — a −2 point penalty per "Yes" on the exposure check (max −6), applied before the pitch is scored. Tell branches at the start to keep their Round 1–3 worksheet answers in front of them; Round 4 is unmarkable without them.

★STARTER

Spot the Mismatch — answer

The real findings, ranked by scale of effect

  • 1. Estate agents selling their own home — leave it on the market 9.5 days longer and sell it for 3–4% more than an equivalent client home (Levitt & Syverson, 2008)
  • 2. Auditors paid by the company they audit — Arthur Andersen's conflict of interest at Enron led to its own criminal conviction and dissolution, and produced the Sarbanes-Oxley reforms
  • 3. Commission-only mortgage brokers — widely documented driver of looser lending standards before the 2007–08 crisis (today's Round 1 mechanic)
  • 4. No-claims bonus car insurance — the one deliberate FIX on the list: gives the driver a stake in their own careful behaviour, reducing moral hazard rather than creating it
  • 5. Tipped waiting staff — a modest, everyday version of the same principle: tie pay to an outcome the principal values, and behaviour shifts toward it
Teaching point

The no-claims bonus is the odd one out — it's the only card designed to close a principal-agent gap rather than open one. That's the type of fix Northbridge will need to find by the end of the game.

1ROUND 1

The Sales Targets — answer

Max 15 points

What happened

Commission-only branches sold the most — and to the riskiest borrowers — because pay depended on volume, never on whether the loan was ever repaid.

Why this is moral hazard, not bad luck

Hidden action: Head Office sees sales volume but not how carefully each loan was checked — the manager's true effort and honesty are invisible to the principal.

Misaligned incentives: under commission-only pay, the manager's personal best interest (sell more, earn more) stops matching the bank's real interest (only lend to people who'll repay).

The partial fix: commission + compulsory sign-off narrows the gap but doesn't close it — certifying a check happened isn't the same as it being done properly under time pressure.

Marking rubric — max 15

  • 4 pts — correctly identifying the chosen pay structure's effect on lending behaviour
  • 4 pts — explicitly naming the principal-agent problem and correctly identifying who is principal/agent
  • 4 pts — explaining why it's a hidden action / moral hazard issue specifically
  • +3 pts — explaining their risk card and how their checks decision interacted with it

Note for Round 4: flag which branches relaxed their checks here — it determines part of their Crash exposure penalty.

2ROUND 2

Package It Up — answer

Max 15 points

What happened

In-house-rated bonds sold for the same price as honestly-rated ones — investors couldn't tell the difference, because the rating label was the only information they had.

Why the information gap gets worse, not better

1. The chain of asymmetric information: the branch knew the true risk in Round 1. The investor here knows only the rating label — if inflated, the investor is now further from the truth than the original lender ever was.

2. Conflict of interest: a rating agency paid by the seller has a financial incentive to rate generously — the "independent check" stops being independent (structurally identical to a student marking their own exam).

3. Compounding risk: branches that relaxed checks in Round 1 AND chose the in-house rating have sold their hidden risk to someone with even less information than Head Office had.

Marking rubric — max 15

  • 5 pts — correctly explaining that asymmetric information compounds down a chain (not just "the investor doesn't know")
  • 5 pts — identifying the rating agency's conflict of interest specifically
  • 5 pts — correctly linking their own Round 1 decision to what they're now selling

3ROUND 3

The Safety Net — answer

Max 15 points

What happened

Branches that lent aggressively scored well this round — but only because the bailout hasn't actually been tested yet. That bill is coming in Round 4.

The same word, a different mechanism

Round 1's moral hazard was about a manager's hidden effort — a microeconomic, branch-level hidden action problem.

This round's moral hazard is systemic: the whole bank takes on more risk because the cost of failure is expected to fall on the taxpayer rather than the bank. The "agent" is arguably the whole bank; the "principal" is the government/taxpayer.

Both are "after the contract" problems — the implicit guarantee already exists, so behaviour changes because of it. Adverse selection happens before a deal is struck; moral hazard happens because of how people behave once one exists.

Marking rubric — max 15

  • 5 pts — correctly identifying "too big to fail" / implicit guarantee as the cause of riskier lending
  • 5 pts — distinguishing this systemic moral hazard from Round 1's branch-level moral hazard
  • 5 pts — correctly using the before/after timing test to justify moral hazard vs adverse selection

The Bill Arrives — marking guide

Exposure penalty

Apply a −2 point penalty per "Yes" from the exposure check (relaxed checks in R1 / in-house rating in R2 / lent aggressively in R3), max −6, to each branch's Round 4 score before adding pitch marks. A branch with three "Yes" answers should feel the full weight of the story it built for itself.

4ROUND 4

The Board Meeting — answer

Max 20 points (after exposure penalty)

What separates a strong pitch from a generic one

No single tool fixes all three rounds — branches that pitched ONE tool matching their own worst round scored highest, not the most ambitious-sounding policy.

Matching policy to problem

A branch that relaxed checks in Round 1 but pitches "capital requirements" hasn't fixed their own problem — capital rules limit how much a bank can lend, not who it lends to. The accurate fix for Round 1 is an incentive tool (profit-share, clawback) or mandatory checks.

A branch exposed mainly through Round 2's in-house rating needs an information fix — independent, investor-paid ratings.

A branch that lent aggressively in Round 3 needs the bailout guarantee itself addressed (ring-fencing, ending "too big to fail") — a rating or pay fix doesn't touch this.

Marking rubric — max 20 (after exposure penalty)

  • Exposure penalty: −2 pts per "Yes" from The Crash (max −6) applied first
  • 6 pts — policy genuinely matches the branch's own worst round
  • 6 pts — accurate use of correct vocabulary for that round's problem
  • 5 pts — explicitly references the branch's own Round 1–3 choices
  • +3 pts — acknowledges one limitation or cost of their chosen policy

📈EPILOGUE

What Changed at Northbridge — answer

★ Bonus — max 5 points

Model answer

The branches that survived the crash best combined an incentive fix (so managers want to do the right thing — e.g. profit-share/clawback) with an information fix (so Head Office and investors can actually check — e.g. independent ratings or affordability rules). A contract alone doesn't work if no one can verify what happened; a rating alone doesn't work if managers are still paid to take risks.

Bonus marking

Award up to 5 points for a well-justified two-tool combination that explains why one alone wouldn't have stopped their own branch's Round 1–3 story.

Essay model answer notes (25 marks): "Evaluate the view that regulation, rather than incentive contracts, is the best way to address agency problems and asymmetric information in financial markets."

KAA: principal-agent problem · moral hazard (hidden action, Round 1) · adverse selection (hidden information, Round 2) · systemic moral hazard / "too big to fail" (Round 3) · signalling/screening as private fixes · regulation (capital requirements, affordability rules) as a public fix.

Evaluation: the timing distinction (before vs after the contract) · why a fix targeting only one branch's incentives won't catch a system-wide guarantee problem · the cost of regulation (credit rationing, compliance cost) vs the cost of doing nothing (the crash itself) · strongest answers conclude it's a toolkit, not a single answer.

✓GLOSSARY

Key terms — model definitions

Principal-agent problem

A conflict of interest arising when one party (the agent) is expected to act in the best interests of another (the principal), but the agent's own incentives diverge from the principal's, and the principal cannot fully observe or enforce the agent's behaviour.

Moral hazard (hidden action)

A situation where one party takes on more risk, or behaves differently, because they know the other party cannot observe their actions or will not bear the full consequences — typically arises after a contract or arrangement exists.

Adverse selection (hidden information)

A situation where one party to a transaction has more information than the other before the deal is struck, leading to a higher proportion of low-quality or high-risk participants in the market.

Securitisation

The process of pooling various types of debt (e.g. mortgages) and selling the pooled debt as a tradeable bond to investors.

Conflict of interest

A situation where a party responsible for providing an impartial judgement (e.g. a credit rating agency) has a competing interest (e.g. being paid by the firm it is rating) that could compromise that judgement.

"Too big to fail"

A firm so large or interconnected that its failure would cause serious damage to the wider economy, leading markets to expect government intervention/bailout rather than allowing collapse.

Signalling vs screening

Signalling: the informed party takes a costly, credible action to demonstrate quality (e.g. a seller offering a warranty). Screening: the uninformed party takes action to obtain information (e.g. a buyer paying for an independent inspection or rating).
