The Economic (Business) Cycle — Stages & Definitions

The economic (or business) cycle describes the recurring pattern of fluctuations in real GDP around its long-run trend — periods of faster growth followed by periods of slower growth, stagnation, or outright contraction.

Fig 1: The economic cycle — actual real GDP (solid line) fluctuating around the economy's long-run trend growth rate (dashed line). The cycle moves through recurring phases of boom, slowdown/recession, and recovery, even as the underlying trend itself continues rising over time (Section 17).
Boom
The peak of the cycle — growth is rapid, unemployment is very low, and the economy is at or near full capacity. Inflationary pressure typically builds as demand strains against limited spare capacity (see Section 13).
Slowdown / Downturn
Growth decelerates from its peak rate — still positive, but weakening, often as rising inflation prompts tighter monetary policy (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide), or as confidence begins to fade.
Recession
The technical definition (used by the ONS and most statistical agencies) is two or more consecutive quarters of negative real GDP growth — output is actually shrinking, not just growing more slowly. Unemployment typically rises sharply (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Unemployment guide) as firms cut output and lay off workers.
Recovery
Growth turns positive again following a recession or slowdown, as spare capacity built up during the downturn is gradually absorbed — a movement back towards the economy's PPF (see the companion BIZ-OMICS PPF, Circular Flow & National Income guide's Section 4).

The Output Gap — Positive & Negative

The output gap measures the difference between an economy's actual real GDP and its potential (trend) output at any given time — the single clearest way of expressing exactly where the economy sits within the cycle described in Sections 1–2.

Positive Output Gap
Actual output exceeds potential output — the economy is temporarily operating above its sustainable long-run capacity (e.g. by using overtime, running down inventories, or drawing in workers who would not normally participate). This is inherently unsustainable and generates inflationary pressure, since demand is outstripping the economy's genuine capacity to supply.
Negative Output Gap
Actual output falls short of potential output — the economy has spare capacity (unemployed labour, idle machinery), exactly the "productively inefficient" point inside the PPF described in the companion BIZ-OMICS PPF, Circular Flow & National Income guide. This typically corresponds to the recession/slowdown phase of the cycle (Section 1).
Fig 3: A positive output gap shown on an AD/AS diagram. The vertical LRAS marks full-employment output (YFE) — the economy's sustainable potential. AD₁ intersects the upward-sloping SRAS at Y₁, to the right of YFE — actual output (Y₁) temporarily exceeds potential output (YFE), the positive output gap bracketed at the bottom. This is only possible in the short run; the excess demand pushing the price level up to P₁ is itself a symptom of the economy straining beyond its sustainable capacity.
Fig 4: The output gap over time, shown against the economy's sustainable (trend) growth path. Where actual growth (the red curve) sits above the sustainable growth line (between t and t′), the economy has a positive output gap; where it dips below (between t′ and t″), it has a negative output gap. This is precisely the same picture as Fig 1's economic cycle diagram, just with the gap between actual and trend output explicitly shaded and labelled.
Why the Output Gap Is Central to Macroeconomic Policy: policymakers use estimates of the output gap to judge whether the economy needs demand-side stimulus (to close a negative gap, Section 5) or restraint (to close a positive gap and prevent inflation from accelerating further) — directly informing the Bank of England's interest rate decisions (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide) and the Phillips Curve trade-offs explored in the companion BIZ-OMICS Macroeconomic Objective Conflicts guide.

Short-Run vs Long-Run Economic Growth

Short-Run (Actual) Growth
An increase in real GDP achieved by using existing spare capacity more fully — absorbing unemployed labour and idle capital, moving the economy from a point inside its PPF towards the frontier itself (see the companion BIZ-OMICS PPF, Circular Flow & National Income guide). This is primarily driven by changes in aggregate demand (Sections 5–7).
Long-Run (Potential) Growth
An increase in the economy's maximum productive potential itself — the PPF (and LRAS, Section 14) shifting outward, driven by an increase in the quantity or productivity of factors of production (Sections 14–16), independent of how fully existing capacity happens to be used at any given moment.
Why the Distinction Is Fundamental: short-run growth can occur even with no change at all in the economy's underlying potential — simply putting existing spare capacity to work. Long-run growth, by contrast, requires the economy's productive ceiling itself to rise. An economy experiencing rapid short-run growth as it recovers from a recession (Section 2) is not necessarily experiencing any long-run growth at all — it may simply be catching up to a potential that was already there.

Demand-Side vs Supply-Side Improvements

Demand-Side Improvements
Policies or changes that raise aggregate demand (Section 5) to close a negative output gap and absorb spare capacity — monetary policy (interest rate cuts, QE) and fiscal policy (tax cuts, higher government spending), covered fully in the companion BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy and Fiscal Policy guides. These primarily drive short-run growth (Section 4).
Supply-Side Improvements
Policies or changes that raise the economy's productive capacity itself — education and skills investment, infrastructure, deregulation, and R&D incentives, covered fully throughout the companion BIZ-OMICS series (particularly the Fiscal Policy and Macroeconomic Objective Conflicts guides' supply-side sections). These primarily drive long-run growth (Section 4), shifting the LRAS (Section 14) and PPF outward.
Why Both Are Needed: demand-side policy alone cannot generate sustained growth beyond an economy's productive potential without triggering inflation (Section 13) — it can only help the economy reach its existing ceiling faster. Supply-side policy alone, meanwhile, does nothing to guarantee that demand exists to actually make use of newly created capacity. A well-managed economy typically requires both: demand-side policy to keep actual output close to potential, and supply-side policy to keep raising that potential over time.

Aggregate Demand — Definition, Components & UK Examples

Aggregate Demand (AD) is the total demand for goods and services produced within an economy at a given price level, over a given period.

AD = C + I + G + (X − M)
ComponentDefinitionUK Example / Scale
C — ConsumptionHousehold spending on goods and services.The largest component of UK AD, typically around 58–65% of GDP.
I — InvestmentFirms' spending on capital goods (Gross Fixed Capital Formation — see the companion BIZ-OMICS PPF guide's gross/net investment distinction).Around 17% of UK GDP.
G — Government SpendingGovernment current and capital expenditure on goods and services (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Fiscal Policy guide).Around 20% of UK GDP.
X − M — Net TradeExports minus imports (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Balance of Payments guide).The UK typically runs a negative net trade position (around −2% of GDP), reflecting its persistent current account deficit.

The AD Curve — Why It Slopes Downward & Causes of Shifts

Why AD Slopes Downward (The "Law" of AD)
As the general price level falls, real AD rises, for three main reasons: the real balance effect (falling prices raise the real value/purchasing power of existing savings, encouraging more spending), the interest rate effect (lower prices reduce the demand for money, pushing down interest rates and encouraging borrowing/investment), and the international trade effect (lower domestic prices make exports more competitive and imports relatively less attractive, raising net trade).

Causes of Shifts in AD (Movements of the Whole Curve)

Component AffectedCause of Rightward Shift (AD Increases)
ConsumptionLower interest rates, higher consumer confidence, rising real wages, tax cuts, wealth effects (rising house/share prices).
InvestmentLower interest rates, higher business confidence, technological change creating new investment opportunities.
Government SpendingExpansionary fiscal policy (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Fiscal Policy guide).
Net TradeA weaker exchange rate (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Exchange Rates guide), stronger global growth raising demand for exports, or successful improvements in international competitiveness.

Each of these factors working in reverse (higher interest rates, falling confidence, contractionary fiscal policy, a stronger pound) shifts AD to the left, reducing aggregate demand.

Fig 6: A leftward shift of AD (from AD1 to AD2) — for example, following a fall in consumer confidence or higher interest rates — reduces both the price level (from PL down to PL2, passing through PL1) and real GDP (from Y down to Y2, passing through Y1). Notice the AS curve here is drawn with the full Keynesian shape (Section 14) — flat at low output, curving upward as capacity constraints bind — meaning the exact price and output impact of the AD shift depends on which section of the AS curve the economy is currently sitting on.
Fig 7: The reverse case — a rightward shift of AD (from AD1 to AD2), for example following an interest rate cut or expansionary fiscal policy, raises both the price level (P1 to P2) and real GDP (Y1 to Y2), again against a curved AS curve.

Contextual Examples of AD Shifts

★ REAL WORLD: The September 2022 Mini-Budget
The UK's September 2022 mini-budget (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Fiscal Policy guide) proposed large, unfunded tax cuts explicitly intended to boost consumption and investment — a deliberate rightward shift of AD. The market reaction (a sharp fall in the pound and a spike in gilt yields) forced a rapid reversal, illustrating both how fiscal policy is intended to shift AD, and how financial market confidence can constrain the government's ability to do so (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Financial Markets guide).
★ REAL WORLD: COVID-19 Furlough as an AD Support Measure
The UK's furlough scheme (2020–21) was explicitly designed to sustain household consumption during lockdowns by maintaining incomes even where output had collapsed — preventing an even larger leftward shift in AD than the direct effect of lockdown restrictions on output would otherwise have caused, at a substantial fiscal cost (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Financial Markets guide's bailout cost discussion).

The Multiplier Effect

The multiplier effect describes how an initial change in an injection (investment, government spending, or exports) generates a larger final change in national income, as the extra income is re-spent through successive rounds of the circular flow (see the companion BIZ-OMICS PPF, Circular Flow & National Income guide).

Multiplier (k) = 1 ÷ (1 − MPC) = 1 ÷ MPW
Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC)
The proportion of any extra pound of income a household spends, rather than saves, pays in tax, or spends on imports.
Marginal Propensity to Withdraw (MPW)
The proportion of any extra pound of income that leaks out of the circular flow — the sum of the marginal propensities to save, to pay tax, and to import (MPS + MPT + MPM). MPW = 1 − MPC.
Fig 5: The multiplier effect on an AD/AS diagram. An initial injection (e.g. a rise in government spending) shifts AD from AD1 to AD2 — the direct, first-round effect. But the income earned by those who received this initial spending is itself partly re-spent, shifting AD further out to AD3 — the larger, final rise in AD "after the multiplier," moving real output from Y1 to the ultimately larger Y3, and the price level from P1 to the higher P2.

Worked Example 1 — A Positive Multiplier

Suppose the marginal propensity to consume (MPC) is 0.8, meaning households spend 80p of every extra £1 of income (so MPW = 0.2):

Multiplier = 1 ÷ (1 − 0.8) = 1 ÷ 0.2 = 5

If the government increases spending by £10bn, the eventual total rise in national income is:

ΔY = ΔG × k = £10bn × 5 = £50bn

The initial £10bn of government spending becomes £50bn of extra national income once every round of re-spending is accounted for — a five-fold multiplier effect.

Worked Example 2 — A Negative Multiplier

The multiplier works identically in reverse for a fall in an injection. Suppose MPC is lower, at 0.5 (so MPW = 0.5):

Multiplier = 1 ÷ (1 − 0.5) = 1 ÷ 0.5 = 2

If business investment falls by £5bn (e.g. due to a collapse in confidence):

ΔY = ΔI × k = −£5bn × 2 = −£10bn

The initial £5bn fall in investment causes national income to fall by a larger £10bn once the negative effect ripples through successive rounds of reduced spending — precisely why a fall in investment (or any injection) can drag down the wider economy by more than its own direct size.

Worked Example 3 — Calculating the Multiplier From Its Components

Suppose a more detailed breakdown finds MPC = 0.60, MPS = 0.15, MPT = 0.15, and MPM = 0.10 (which sum to 1):

MPW = MPS + MPT + MPM = 0.15 + 0.15 + 0.10 = 0.40
Multiplier = 1 ÷ 0.40 = 2.5
Why the Size of the Multiplier Varies: the more that leaks out of the circular flow at each round (higher savings rates, higher tax rates, or a higher propensity to import), the smaller the multiplier — an economy with a large marginal propensity to import (like the UK) will typically see more of any initial stimulus "leak" abroad rather than being re-spent domestically, producing a smaller multiplier than a more closed economy with the same MPC.

The Accelerator Effect

The accelerator effect describes how the level of investment depends not on the level of demand or output, but on its rate of change — meaning investment can fall even while output is still growing, simply because it is growing more slowly than before.

Net Investment = Accelerator Coefficient × Change in Output

Worked Example — Why Investment Falls Even as Output Keeps Growing

Suppose a firm's accelerator coefficient (its capital-output ratio) is 3 — meaning it needs £3 of additional capital for every £1 increase in annual output:

YearGrowth in OutputNet Investment Required (Accelerator × Growth)
Year 1+£10m3 × £10m = £30m
Year 2+£5m (growth slows, but is still positive)3 × £5m = £15m
Year 3£0m (output stops growing, but does not fall)3 × £0m = £0m

Even though output never actually fell across these three years, net investment collapsed from £30m to £15m to £0m — purely because the rate of output growth slowed. This is the core insight of the accelerator: investment is highly sensitive to changes in the growth rate of demand, not just whether demand is rising or falling in absolute terms.

How the Accelerator Differs From the Multiplier
The multiplier (Section 9) shows how an initial change in any injection generates a larger final change in national income, via household consumption re-spending. The accelerator instead explains what drives the level of investment itself — it argues investment responds specifically to the rate of change of output/demand, not to income re-spending at all. The two effects are often combined in the "multiplier-accelerator model": a rise in demand triggers the multiplier (raising national income), which can itself trigger the accelerator (as firms invest to meet the new, higher level of demand), which further raises AD, feeding back into the multiplier again — helping explain why economic cycles (Section 1) can be self-reinforcing in both directions.
★ REAL WORLD: Investment Volatility During the 2008–09 Financial Crisis
Business investment is consistently the most volatile component of UK aggregate demand (Section 6) — falling far more sharply in percentage terms than consumption during the 2008–09 recession. This is widely explained by the accelerator effect: as the expected future growth rate of demand collapsed (not just the current level of demand), firms sharply cut back planned capital spending, since maintaining or expanding capacity no longer appeared justified against a much weaker growth outlook — precisely the mechanism illustrated in the worked example above, playing out at the scale of the whole economy.

Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)

Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)
The total quantity of goods and services firms are willing and able to supply at each price level, assuming the prices of factors of production (particularly wages) remain fixed in the short run.
Why SRAS Slopes Upward (The "Law" of SRAS)
Because wages and other input costs are assumed fixed in the short run, a higher output price allows firms to earn a greater profit margin on each unit sold, incentivising them to increase output — hence a positive relationship between the price level and quantity supplied in the short run.
Fig 10: Some textbooks draw a Keynesian SRAS with the same three-stage shape used for Keynesian LRAS in Section 14 (Fig 11) — a horizontal stage at low output (genuine spare capacity, no upward price pressure at all), an intermediate stage where prices begin rising as bottlenecks emerge, and a near-vertical stage once capacity constraints bind hard. Where a source draws the curve this way, note it is describing the same underlying Keynesian logic as the LRAS treatment in Section 14 — the labelling as "short-run" vs "long-run" varies between textbooks, since Keynesian economics does not always draw as sharp a distinction between the two timeframes as the Classical model does.

Causes of Shifts in SRAS

Cause of Leftward Shift (SRAS Decreases — Costs Rise)Cause of Rightward Shift (SRAS Increases — Costs Fall)
Rising wage costs (e.g. from a tight labour market)Falling wage costs, or slower wage growth
Rising commodity/raw material prices (e.g. oil price shocks)Falling commodity/raw material prices
A weaker exchange rate raising the cost of imported inputsA stronger exchange rate lowering the cost of imported inputs
Higher indirect taxes on firmsLower indirect taxes, or new subsidies to producers
Fig 8: A leftward shift of SRAS (from SRAS1 to SRAS2) — for example, following a rise in wage or commodity costs — raises the price level (PL1 to PL2) while lowering real GDP (Q1 to Q2), with AD held constant. This is the textbook diagram for cost-push inflation (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Inflation guide) combined with falling output — sometimes called stagflation.
Fig 9: The reverse case — a rightward shift of SRAS (from SRAS1 to SRAS2), for example following falling commodity prices or lower wage costs, lowers the price level (PL1 to PL2) while raising real GDP (Q1 to Q2), again with AD held constant — an unambiguously favourable outcome, unlike an AD-driven change in output (Section 7), which always trades off price level and output in the same direction.

Contextual Examples of SRAS Shifts

★ REAL WORLD: The 2022 Energy Price Shock
Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, global gas and oil prices surged, sharply raising energy costs for UK firms across almost every sector — a clear leftward shift of SRAS, directly contributing to the cost-push inflation that pushed UK CPI above 11% in October 2022 (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Inflation guide's cost-push section).
★ REAL WORLD: Sterling Depreciation Raising Import Costs
A weaker pound (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Exchange Rates guide) directly raises the cost of imported raw materials and components for UK firms, shifting SRAS to the left even with no change in global commodity prices at all — precisely why sterling's post-Brexit-referendum depreciation in 2016 was linked to rising domestic cost pressures in the following years.

How Demand-Side Changes Can Affect Productive Capacity

While AD shifts are usually treated as purely short-run phenomena (Section 4), sustained demand-side changes can, in specific circumstances, genuinely affect an economy's long-run productive capacity too.

Demand-Pull Inflation Eroding Competitiveness
A sustained positive output gap (excess demand — see the companion BIZ-OMICS Macroeconomic Objective Conflicts guide) can generate persistent demand-pull inflation. If domestic inflation consistently runs ahead of trading partners', UK exports become progressively less price-competitive, potentially causing a lasting loss of market share that damages long-run productive capacity in exposed industries — a rare case of a demand-side phenomenon (inflation) feeding back into supply-side, long-run outcomes.
Hysteresis — A Demand-Side Recession Causing a Supply-Side Scar
A prolonged demand-deficient recession (Section 1) can cause structural unemployment to persist even after demand recovers (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Unemployment guide's hysteresis chain) — long-term unemployed workers lose skills and motivation, and firms that closed during the downturn do not automatically reopen once demand returns. This means a purely demand-side shock (a fall in AD) can leave a lasting scar on the economy's long-run productive capacity — precisely why prolonged recessions are taken so seriously by policymakers, beyond their immediate short-run cost.
Why This Blurs the SR/LR Distinction: Section 4 presented short-run and long-run growth as cleanly separate concepts, but hysteresis and sustained demand-pull inflation both show that a severe or prolonged enough demand-side shock can, in practice, permanently damage supply-side potential — meaning demand-side policy is not always neutral with respect to the economy's long-run trajectory, especially during deep recessions.

Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS) — Classical vs Keynesian

Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS) represents the economy's maximum sustainable output when all factor prices, including wages, are fully flexible and have adjusted — the economy's true productive capacity, directly linked to its PPF (Section 17).

Fig 2: Classical LRAS (left) is a single vertical line at full-employment output (Yf) — the Classical view holds that wages and prices are always fully flexible, so the economy self-corrects to full employment output regardless of the price level, meaning LRAS is unaffected by AD. Keynesian LRAS (right) has three distinct sections: a flat portion at low output levels (spare capacity means firms can expand output with no upward pressure on prices at all), a rising middle portion (as capacity constraints begin to bind and bottlenecks emerge, further output increases require rising prices), and a vertical portion at true full capacity (Yf) once genuinely no further output is possible regardless of price.
The Classical View
Assumes wages and prices are fully flexible in all markets, so the economy always self-corrects to full-employment output — LRAS is vertical at every price level, and demand-side policy can only affect the price level, not real output, in the long run (echoing Say's Law — "supply creates its own demand").
The Keynesian View
Argues wages, in particular, are "sticky downward" (resistant to falling even when demand is weak), meaning an economy can remain stuck below full employment for a prolonged period without self-correcting — hence LRAS is flat at low output (genuine spare capacity, no inflationary pressure from expanding output), curves upward as capacity constraints begin to bind, and only becomes vertical once true full capacity (Yf) is reached.
Fig 11: A rightward shift of the Classical LRAS, from LRAS to LRAS1 — representing exactly the same underlying causes as an outward PPF shift (Section 15, and the companion BIZ-OMICS PPF guide). With AD held constant, this rightward shift raises full-employment real output (Y to Y1) while lowering the equilibrium price level (P to P1) — since the same level of demand is now being met by a larger productive capacity. This is the Classical model's clean prediction for genuine long-run growth: more output and lower prices, in sharp contrast to an AD-driven expansion (Section 7), which always raises the price level alongside output.
Why the Distinction Matters for Policy: the Classical view implies demand-side stimulus is essentially pointless (or even harmful, purely inflationary) in the long run, since the economy will reach full employment regardless. The Keynesian view implies demand-side stimulus can be genuinely effective at raising real output — with no inflationary cost at all — provided the economy is operating on the flat section of the curve, but risks accelerating inflation once it moves onto the rising or vertical sections. This is precisely the theoretical basis for the output gap analysis in Section 3, and in the companion BIZ-OMICS Macroeconomic Objective Conflicts guide.

Causes of Shifts in LRAS

An Increase in the Quantity of Factor Inputs
A larger workforce (population growth, migration, higher participation rates), an increase in the capital stock via net investment (see the companion BIZ-OMICS PPF guide), or the discovery of new natural resources — all shift LRAS to the right.
An Improvement in Factor Productivity
Better technology, improved education and skills, more efficient management, or improved infrastructure allow more output to be produced from the same quantity of resources, shifting LRAS to the right.
Supply-Side Policy
Deliberate government policy targeting either of the above channels (Section 5) — education and training, infrastructure investment, deregulation, R&D incentives, and improved labour market flexibility (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Fiscal Policy and Unemployment guides).

Each of these factors working in reverse — a shrinking workforce, falling productivity, capital destruction, or damaging supply-side shocks — shifts LRAS to the left, exactly mirroring the inward PPF shift causes covered in the companion BIZ-OMICS PPF guide.

Contextual Examples of LRAS Shifts

★ REAL WORLD: South Korea's Supply-Side Transformation
South Korea's decades of targeted investment in education, infrastructure, and export-oriented industry (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Economic Development & Growth guide) represent one of history's most dramatic real-world rightward LRAS shifts — transforming the economy's productive capacity from one of the world's poorest to a high-income, advanced economy within a few decades.
★ REAL WORLD: Brexit as a (Contested) LRAS Shift
The OBR's estimate that Brexit will reduce long-run UK productivity by around 4% relative to EU membership (see the companion BIZ-OMICS International Trade guide) is, in AD/AS terms, an estimated leftward shift of LRAS — reduced trade openness lowering the economy's long-run productive capacity, distinct from any short-run AD effects Brexit may separately have caused.

The Link Between the PPF, LRAS & the Trend Rate of Growth

The PPF (see the companion BIZ-OMICS PPF, Circular Flow & National Income guide), LRAS, and the economy's trend rate of growth (Fig 1's dashed line) are three different representations of the exact same underlying concept: an economy's maximum sustainable productive potential.

ConceptWhat It ShowsHow It Moves
PPFMaximum combinations of two goods an economy can produce (a microeconomic-style diagram applied to the whole economy).Shifts outward/inward exactly as covered in Sections 5–6 of the companion PPF guide.
LRASMaximum total real output at full employment, plotted against the price level (a macroeconomic AD/AS diagram).Shifts right/left due to the same underlying causes as PPF shifts (Section 15) — they are, in effect, the same event described in two different diagrams.
Trend Rate of GrowthThe average, sustainable rate at which an economy's productive potential grows over the long run, plotted over time (Fig 1's dashed line).Rises faster when LRAS/PPF shift outward more rapidly (e.g. through strong productivity growth or rapid capital accumulation); rises more slowly, or even falls, when supply-side conditions are weak.
Bringing It All Together: a rightward shift of the PPF, a rightward shift of LRAS, and an increase in the economy's trend growth rate are three ways of describing the exact same underlying real-world event — the economy's productive capacity has genuinely expanded. Whether an exam question asks about the PPF, LRAS, or the trend growth rate, the same causes (Sections 5, 12 of the companion PPF guide; Section 15 here) and the same real-world examples (Section 16's South Korea and Brexit cases) apply directly across all three framings — recognising this equivalence is one of the most valuable exam-technique insights in the entire A-Level macroeconomics course.

Exam Technique & Model Chains of Reasoning

The Golden Chain: A Cost-Push Shock Moving Through SRAS

A global commodity price shock (e.g. rising oil/gas prices) raises the cost of key inputs for domestic firms → SRAS shifts to the left, since firms now require a higher price to supply the same quantity of output at unchanged profit margins → at the original price level, firms reduce output → the price level rises (cost-push inflation) and real output falls → if the shock is severe and sustained enough, this can also raise the risk of hysteresis (Section 13) if firms respond by permanently cutting capacity, potentially shifting LRAS left too.

The Golden Chain: Recovering From a Recession — SR Growth Without LR Growth

A negative demand shock (e.g. a financial crisis) causes a recession — actual output falls below the economy's unchanged potential output (LRAS/PPF) → as confidence returns and AD recovers, firms re-employ previously idle labour and capital → real GDP rises rapidly (short-run growth) → but because LRAS/the PPF never actually shifted during this process, this recovery simply closes the negative output gap, rather than representing any genuine increase in the economy's long-run potential → once the output gap is fully closed, further sustained growth requires genuine supply-side improvement (Sections 4, 12), not just continued demand-side recovery.

Key Exam Tips

1

Always distinguish short-run (actual) growth from long-run (potential) growth explicitly — a huge number of marks are lost by conflating the two.

2

Use the correct "law" terminology for AD and SRAS (the real balance/interest rate/international trade effects for AD; the fixed short-run cost assumption for SRAS) rather than simply asserting the curves slope in a given direction.

3

Never draw the Keynesian LRAS as a single vertical line — its three distinct sections (flat, rising, vertical) are precisely what distinguishes it from the Classical view, and are frequently tested directly.

4

Explicitly link the PPF, LRAS, and trend growth rate together (Section 17) whenever a question touches on any one of them — showing you understand they represent the same underlying concept earns significant credit.

5

Bring in real-world context for maximum marks: the 2020 UK recession figures (−9.9% annual, −20.4% Q2, +16.1% Q3 recovery), the 2022 energy shock, and South Korea's LRAS shift are all precise, high-value examples.

6

Evaluate — never just describe. For questions on demand-side vs supply-side policy, weigh their different time horizons and objectives explicitly, rather than treating them as interchangeable tools.
