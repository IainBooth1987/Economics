The Shock
💥 Nixon Closes the Gold Window
In a surprise Sunday-evening TV address, Nixon suspends dollar–gold convertibility, imposes a 90-day wage-price freeze, and levies a 10% import surcharge. No allied governments were consulted.

Failed Fix

G10 finance ministers meet in Washington to salvage a fixed-rate system. The dollar is devalued against gold to $38/oz; other currencies revalued upward. Nixon calls it "the greatest monetary agreement in history." It collapses within 14 months.

Background
🏦 Foundations of the Peso Peg
Mexico adopts a crawling-band peg to the dollar. Large current-account deficits (up to 8% of GDP) are financed by short-term foreign capital inflows, creating dangerous vulnerabilities masked by NAFTA optimism.

🤝 NAFTA Takes Effect

PRI presidential candidate Luis Donaldo Colosio is shot in Tijuana. Mexican stocks crash 10%. Capital flight accelerates as political risk premium soars and foreign investors begin questioning Mexico's stability.

📉 The Tesobono Trap
To retain investor confidence, Mexico issues Tesobonos — short-term bonds indexed to the dollar. This shores up short-run financing but creates a time bomb: $30B of dollar-denominated debt maturing in early 1995.

💥 The Fatal Devaluation
President Zedillo announces a "controlled" 15% peso devaluation. Markets panic immediately — the move signals weakness and triggers massive speculation. Reserves burn at $4B in 48 hours. On 22 December, Mexico is forced to float.

🌊 The "Tequila Effect" Spreads
Panic spreads to Argentina, Brazil, and other emerging markets. Investors flee anything that looks risky. Argentine bank deposits fall 18% and the peso-dollar convertibility board comes under severe pressure.

🏦 Clinton's $50B Rescue
The US Treasury and IMF assemble an unprecedented $50B package — using the Exchange Stabilisation Fund (bypassing a hostile Congress) and oil-export revenues as collateral. Harsh austerity and structural reforms required in return.

🔗 Connecting the Crises — Common Threads

📌 The Peg Problem

All three crises involve fixed or semi-fixed exchange rate regimes that became unsustainable. Bretton Woods (1971), the peso crawling band (1994), and Asia's dollar pegs (1997) all eventually collapsed under speculative pressure once markets judged the peg as inconsistent with fundamentals.

📌 Short-Term Capital Flows

Hot money — short-term, volatile foreign capital attracted by higher yields — played a central role in both the build-up and the sudden stop of all three crises. Mexico's Tesobonos and Asia's short-term bank borrowing illustrate how maturity mismatches amplify vulnerability.

📌 Contagion and Confidence

Market psychology and herd behaviour spread crises far beyond the original epicentre. The "Tequila Effect" hit Argentina; the Asian crisis reached Korea and Russia. Each collapse damaged confidence in similar economies, regardless of their actual fundamentals.

📌 IMF Conditionality Debate

The IMF's response to both Mexico (1994) and Asia (1997–98) attached harsh austerity conditions that critics argue deepened the recessions. This sparked a major rethink of IMF doctrine and influenced the development of precautionary credit lines in the 2000s.

📌 Legacy: Reserve Accumulation

A key lesson from all three crises was the importance of adequate foreign exchange reserves as a buffer. After 1998, Asian economies dramatically increased reserve holdings — partly explaining global "savings glut" dynamics ahead of the 2008 financial crisis.

📌 AQA Exam Application

These cases are rich for Paper 4 (EC04) evaluation questions: comparative advantage, exchange rate systems, managed vs. floating rates, the role of international institutions (IMF/World Bank), financial globalisation, and the arguments for/against capital controls.
