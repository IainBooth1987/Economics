🎓 Teacher Reveal — Step-by-step Discussion

The rational strategy is: never bid.
Working backwards: if both players reason that the other will keep bidding past £1.00, the expected value of entering is negative. The only Nash Equilibrium is for neither player to bid — each earns £0, but avoids a guaranteed loss. In practice, almost no one applies this logic, which is precisely what makes the auction such a powerful demonstration of bounded rationality.
The trap begins the moment the first bid is placed.
Once Player 1 bids 5p, Player 2 faces a choice: bid 10p (and might win £1.00 for 10p) or don't bid (Player 1 wins £1.00 for 5p). The expected value of bidding is positive. But this logic applies at every step — and once both players have bid, the second-place bidder always has an incentive to bid again to avoid a certain loss. The trap is self-reinforcing.
No pure strategy Nash Equilibrium exists once the auction is entered.

At every point above £0.50, bidding loses less than quitting — so the "dominant" move is always to bid. This creates an escalation spiral with no natural stopping point short of one player's budget.

Theory & Debrief

Sunk Cost Fallacy · Escalation of Commitment · Behavioural Economics

What is the Dollar Auction?

Devised by economist Martin Shubik in 1971, the Dollar Auction is a paradox in which rational-seeming individual decisions lead to collectively irrational outcomes. Two players bid for a prize worth £1. The twist: both the highest and second-highest bidder must pay their bids, but only the winner receives the prize. This single rule creates a powerful escalation trap.

The Sunk Cost Fallacy

Once a player has bid, say, 70p, they face a choice: quit and lose 70p, or bid 80p and risk losing only 80p if they win (net loss 80p − 100p prize = −80p). The sunk 70p feels like it justifies the next bid, even though rational analysis says sunk costs are irrelevant to future decisions.

Exam-ready definition: The sunk cost fallacy occurs when a decision-maker continues an action on account of previously invested resources (time, money, effort) rather than on the basis of future expected value.

Escalation of Commitment

Once bids exceed £1.00, both players are making net losses — yet bidding continues because the winner loses less than the loser. This is irrational from a group perspective but individually "logical" given the trap already entered. It mirrors:

  • Patent wars — firms spending more in litigation than the patent is worth
  • Arms races — both nations worse off, but neither can unilaterally stop
  • eBay bidding wars — final price exceeds rational valuation
  • Project overruns — continuing a failed project to "recoup" past investment

Nash Equilibrium and the Rational Exit

The theoretically rational strategy is never to enter the auction. If both players reason backwards, they should recognise the trap before the first bid. Yet in experiments, participants almost always enter and frequently bid past £1.00. This is a powerful demonstration that real human behaviour departs from the rationality assumption underpinning classical microeconomics.

Rational strategy: bid £0.00. Expected payoff = £0. Any other strategy risks a negative payoff.

Group Inefficiency

Every round where total bids exceed £1.00 destroys value. If the two players bid a combined £1.80, the seller (auctioneer) collects £1.80 for a prize worth £1.00 — an 80p transfer of wealth away from the bidders. This illustrates how strategic interaction without coordination can produce Pareto-inefficient outcomes.

AQA relevance: This game is directly applicable to Paper 3 behavioural economics contexts — specifically bounded rationality, loss aversion, and the gap between normative (rational) and positive (actual) models of decision-making.

Nash Equilibrium: both vendors at the centre (position 50%).
Neither vendor can increase market share by moving alone. If A moves right of centre, B moves just right of A — and vice versa. The only stable position is where both are at the midpoint, splitting the market 50/50. This is a Nash Equilibrium but NOT a social optimum.
Social Optimum: Vendor A at 25%, Vendor B at 75%.
At the quarter/three-quarter positions, every customer is at most 25% of the street away from a vendor. This minimises total travel distance across all customers. Compare this to the Nash Equilibrium — customers at the ends of the street travel twice as far. The social optimum requires coordination or regulation; neither firm has an incentive to move there unilaterally.

Theory

Hotelling (1929) · Principle of Minimum Differentiation · Spatial Economics

What is Hotelling's Location Model?

Developed by Harold Hotelling in 1929, this model explains why competing firms often cluster together geographically — even when spreading out would better serve consumers. Two vendors (or firms) choose positions along a line; customers go to the nearest. Each vendor wants to maximise their customer base.

The Nash Equilibrium

Working through the logic: if Vendor A is to the left of centre, Vendor B can move just left of A, capturing more than half the market. A then moves right of B, and so on. This process of undercutting continues until both vendors occupy the exact centre — the only position where neither can unilaterally improve.

Nash Equilibrium: a* = b* = 0.5 (centre of the market)
Market boundary = (position_A + position_B) / 2

The Principle of Minimum Differentiation

The equilibrium demonstrates a counterintuitive result: competitors tend to become similar, not different. This seems to contradict conventional business wisdom about differentiation. It occurs because moving toward the centre always captures some of the competitor's market.

Is the outcome socially optimal? No. If the vendors were at the ¼ and ¾ positions, customers would travel half as far on average. The Nash equilibrium is privately optimal but socially inefficient — a market failure.

Real-World Applications

  • Retail clustering: Why McDonald's and Burger King locate near each other; why petrol stations appear on opposite corners
  • Supermarkets: Tesco and Sainsbury's offering near-identical product ranges
  • Politics: The median voter theorem — why mainstream parties converge on centrist policies
  • Broadcasting: TV channels scheduling similar programmes at similar times
  • Product differentiation: Cola brands, budget airlines, smartphone manufacturers

Extensions and Limitations

Price competition — adding price as a strategic variable alongside location can produce very different equilibria (Bertrand competition).

Three or more players — with three vendors there is often no pure strategy Nash equilibrium; vendors cycle around each other.

Circular markets — competing around a loop (as in a town centre) changes the geometry of the equilibrium.

Theory & Optimal Strategy

Rubinstein (1982) · Subgame Perfect Equilibrium · Bargaining Power

The Core Problem

Two players must divide £1.00. Player A makes an offer; B accepts or rejects. If rejected, B counter-offers — but the pie has shrunk. Each round of delay costs both players, because money now is worth more than money later (time preference). The question: what will rational players agree, and how quickly?

Real-world parallels: wage negotiations between unions and management; trade deal negotiations; divorce asset settlements; business partnership disputes.

Why Time Matters — Discount Factors

Each player has a discount factor δ ∈ (0,1) representing how much they value future money. δ = 0.9 means £1 next round is worth only £0.90 today. A lower δ means greater impatience — you want to settle now. A higher δ means patience — you can credibly threaten to wait.

Value in round 2 = δ × value in round 1

Rubinstein's Solution (1982)

Ariel Rubinstein proved that with alternating offers and discount factors, there is a unique subgame perfect equilibrium: agreement is reached immediately in round 1, with the split determined by the discount factors alone.

Player A's share = (1 − δ_B) / (1 − δ_A × δ_B)
Player B's share = δ_B × (1 − δ_A) / (1 − δ_A × δ_B)

Theory & Mixed Strategies

Mixed Strategy Nash Equilibrium · Zero-Sum Games · Minimax

What Type of Game Is This?

This is a zero-sum game — every outcome that benefits Red harms Black by exactly the same amount, and vice versa. Zero-sum games have no scope for mutual gain through cooperation; one player's win is the other's loss. This contrasts with the Prisoner's Dilemma, where cooperation can benefit both.

The Payoff Matrix

Mapping out who wins each combination of card choices reveals the structure of the game:

Black: KBlack: ABlack: 2Black: 3
Red: KRedBlackBlackBlack
Red: ABlackBlackRedRed
Red: 2BlackRedBlackRed
Red: 3BlackRedRedBlack

Red wins 6 of 16 combinations with pure random play — a 37.5% win rate. Black has a structural advantage.

Pure vs Mixed Strategies

A pure strategy means always playing the same card. Check the matrix: for every card Red might play, Black has a best response that beats it — and vice versa. There is no pure strategy Nash equilibrium in this game.

A mixed strategy means randomising over cards according to a probability distribution. The mixed strategy Nash equilibrium is the distribution where each player is indifferent between their options — neither can improve by shifting their mix.

Minimax principle: In zero-sum games, the optimal strategy minimises your maximum possible loss. Each player should choose their mixed strategy so the opponent cannot exploit any pattern in their play.

Real-World Applications

  • Penalty kicks in football: Striker and goalkeeper each mix left/right strategies
  • Military strategy: Unpredictable attack directions; mixed patrol patterns
  • Pricing: Retailers randomising promotional timing to prevent competitors anticipating discounts
  • Poker: Bluffing at the right frequency to remain unexploitable
  • Advertising: Firms mixing media channels to prevent rival firms targeting the same audiences

Connecting to Nash Equilibrium

John Nash's general theorem (1951) proved that every finite game has at least one Nash equilibrium, possibly in mixed strategies. This game illustrates why: with no dominant pure strategy, the equilibrium can only exist in mixed strategies. Auto-playing 1,000 games with random play converges to approximately 37.5% Red wins — close to the theoretical baseline, though the exact mixed strategy equilibrium win rate depends on the specific probability distributions used.

Theory

Cournot (1838) · Bertrand (1883) · Oligopoly · Nash Equilibrium

Oligopoly and Strategic Interdependence

An oligopoly is a market dominated by a small number of large firms. Unlike perfect competition (where firms are price-takers) or monopoly (where one firm sets price), oligopolists must think strategically — each firm's best decision depends on what it expects rivals to do. This mutual dependence is the defining feature of oligopoly.

Nash Equilibrium definition: A set of strategies where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their own strategy, given what all other players are doing.

Cournot Competition (1838)

Antoine Cournot's model assumes firms compete by choosing output quantities simultaneously. Each firm takes the other's quantity as given and maximises profit accordingly. The market price is determined by total industry output via the inverse demand function.

Price = a − b(Q₁ + Q₂)  ·  Profit = (Price − Cost) × Quantity
Cournot Nash Equilibrium: Q* = (a − c) / 3b  (symmetric firms)

At equilibrium, each firm produces one-third of the competitive quantity. The market outcome lies between perfect competition (more output, lower price) and monopoly (less output, higher price).

Real-world parallel: Oil producers (OPEC members) deciding how much crude to pump. Airlines deciding how many seats to offer on a route. Steel producers setting annual output targets.

Bertrand Competition (1883)

Joseph Bertrand critiqued Cournot by arguing firms compete on price, not quantity. The logic is brutal: if your rival charges £50, you can steal all their customers by charging £49. They respond with £48. This continues until price equals marginal cost — the competitive outcome — even with just two firms.

Bertrand Nash Equilibrium: P* = c (marginal cost) → zero economic profit
The Bertrand Paradox: Two firms are enough to produce the perfectly competitive outcome — a result that seems counterintuitive given how much market power a duopoly should have. In practice, capacity constraints, product differentiation, and repeated interaction prevent prices from collapsing this far.

Best Response Functions

Each firm has a best response function — the profit-maximising strategy for every possible choice by the rival. In Cournot, if Firm B increases output, Firm A's best response is to reduce output (strategic substitutes). The Nash Equilibrium is where the two best response functions intersect.

Firm A best response: Q₁* = (a − c − b·Q₂) / 2b
Nash Equilibrium = intersection of both best response functions

AQA Exam Relevance

Both models are examinable at A-Level as illustrations of how oligopolists behave under different competitive conditions. Key evaluation points include:

  • Cournot is more realistic where firms set capacity in advance (e.g. airline routes, energy generation)
  • Bertrand is more realistic where products are homogeneous and prices are instantly visible (e.g. petrol forecourts, online retail)
  • Both models assume rational, profit-maximising firms with perfect information — behavioural economics challenges these assumptions
  • In reality, repeated interaction can lead to tacit collusion — firms learn to match rather than undercut each other

"Explain the difference between Cournot and Bertrand competition, and the Nash Equilibrium in each model."

For 8 marks, develop:
  • Cournot: firms compete on quantity; market price determined by total output; NE is Q* = (a−c)/3b each — above competitive output, positive profits
  • Bertrand: firms compete on price; the cheapest firm captures all demand; NE is P* = MC — zero economic profit (Bertrand Paradox)
  • Key difference: Cournot NE is Pareto-superior to Bertrand NE for firms (higher profits) but Pareto-inferior for consumers (higher price, lower output)
  • Cournot fits capacity-constrained industries; Bertrand fits homogeneous goods markets with instant price visibility

"Evaluate the view that oligopolistic competition always results in prices above marginal cost."

For full marks:
  • Case for: Cournot competition produces P > MC — market power persists even with multiple firms
  • Case against: Bertrand competition drives P = MC with just two firms — the Bertrand Paradox shows competition can be fierce
  • Evaluation: whether Cournot or Bertrand is more realistic depends on market structure (capacity constraints, product homogeneity, transparency)
  • Kinked demand curve: explains price stickiness — firms fear starting a price war
  • Tacit collusion: repeated interaction allows firms to sustain P > MC without explicit agreement
  • Contestability: even in oligopoly, threat of entry can force P ≈ MC

"Using game theory, explain why firms in an oligopoly might choose to collude rather than compete."

For 6 marks:
  • Show the Prisoner's Dilemma structure: competing leads to lower profits for both; colluding gives both firms higher profits
  • If firms act as a monopoly (joint profit maximisation), total industry profit is maximised — each firm prefers this to the Cournot/Bertrand outcome
  • Explain the instability: each firm has an incentive to defect from the cartel (undercut/overproduce) — collusion requires credible enforcement
  • Link to real world: OPEC; lysine cartel; price-fixing investigations
