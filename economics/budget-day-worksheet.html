Your brief

The polls are unanimous: Forward Britain will win Thursday's election, and Hannah Cole will be Prime Minister. Within weeks her government must deliver its first Budget — the statement that sets the nation's taxes and spending for the year.

Your team is a Treasury advisory unit, competing against rival units for the PM's ear. Each round she'll put a question to you: read the evidence, do the maths, commit to a recommendation. After each round her verdict is revealed — the closer your advice, the more points your unit earns.

Before you start: turn to the manifesto extract on page 3. Read every sentence. Underline anything that sounds like it might matter later. Everything in that document is there for a reason — and at least one sentence will be quoted back at you before this game is over.

The Manifesto Extract — and the PM-in-Waiting

READ CAREFULLY

Hannah Cole did not grow up around power. Her mother worked nights as an NHS nurse in Leeds, and Cole has said it plainly: “the health service held my family together.” Her politics were forged early — and finished, perhaps, five years ago, when her constituency flooded twice in as many winters. “I have waded through people’s living rooms,” she told conference. “I will not wade through them again.”

That conference speech carried her signature line on schools: “you cannot harvest what you never plant.” The manifesto repeats the thought, calling education “the seed corn of the economy.” Yet Cole resists easy labels. Pressed on public ownership, she shrugs: “the state should steer, not row” — there will be no renationalisation, not even of the railways. Corporation tax will stay “competitive with our neighbours,” though a windfall levy on energy profits is “under review.” Where she is firm, she is very firm: “where markets will not build, the state must.” And on the public finances, one sentence of iron: “borrowing will never exceed 3p in every pound this economy produces.” Defence, the manifesto notes briefly, will be “held steady.” And tax? “I will not promise tax cuts I cannot fund.”

From the Treasury transition briefing: the economy (GDP) is valued at £2,500bn a year; the national debt stands at £2,400bn. Next year’s tax bases are forecast as: taxable income £1,200bn (average income tax rate 25%); earnings subject to National Insurance £1,000bn (rate 15%); VAT-able spending £850bn (rate 20%); company profits £400bn (corporation tax 25%). Duties will raise £80bn, council tax and business rates £90bn, and all other taxes £110bn.

Forecast the Revenue

MAX 15 PTS

“Before I promise anything to anyone, tell me what’s coming in. The forecast is the foundation — get it wrong, and everything we build on it collapses.”

HANNAH COLE · PM-IN-WAITING
JARGON CHECKDirect taxes (income tax, National Insurance, corporation tax) are taken from incomes and profits. Indirect taxes (VAT, duties) are added to spending.
Worked example — copy this method: tax revenue = the tax base (the thing being taxed) × the rate. Vehicle excise duty: 40 million vehicles × £180 average = £7.2bn. Now do the same for the four big taxes — every base is in paragraph 3 of the manifesto extract.

The Gilt Market’s Verdict

MAX 15 PTS

“We borrow the difference by selling gilts — and the buyers have been watching everything we just did. Tell me what this Budget does to the national debt, what the market will charge us… and why I shouldn’t just borrow more.”

HANNAH COLE · PM-IN-WAITING
JARGON CHECKA gilt = a UK government bond — an IOU sold to investors (mostly pension funds and insurers) to borrow money. Epilogue — The Year Unfolds

★ BONUS 5 PTS
JARGON CHECKThe gilts sold on Budget Day later trade between investors in the secondary market — the government receives none of that money, but the yields move daily with confidence in Britain. The national debt is never really “paid off”: it’s judged as a ratio to GDP, because what matters is the burden relative to the economy’s size.
