Teaching point at the end of the animation: the government doesn't print this money or simply take it — it borrows from willing lenders, mostly pension funds and insurers managing ordinary people's savings. The 4% yield is the price they demand, set by their confidence, not by the Chancellor. Bids of £80bn for £70bn of gilts (1.1× covered) is what a credible Budget looks like — Round 5 showed what happens when credibility slips. New national debt: £2,470bn, and notice debt-to-GDP ticking up to 98.8%… remember that number for What's Next.

What Happens Next — The Year Unfolds

★ Bonus: max 5 pts

The Budget is law. Through the year the money flows — hospitals hire, schools rebuild, flood defences rise. The gilts, meanwhile, trade between investors every day in the secondary market, their yields moving with confidence in Britain. And the debt? It never really gets "paid off" — it sits there, rolled over gilt by gilt, judged not in pounds but as a ratio to the size of the economy.

Which sets up the year's big question: the government borrowed £70bn this year — so did the debt burden actually get heavier? The answer is sneakier than it looks.

FINANCIAL PRESS · ONE YEAR AFTER THE FIRST BUDGET

Growth surprises as the investment lands

Twelve months on, the flood defences are rising and the school rebuilding programme is ahead of schedule. The economy grew by 2% — helped, economists say, by the multiplier effect of the infrastructure spending: every pound the government spent became someone's income, which they spent in turn. GDP has risen from £2,500bn to £2,550bn. The national debt now stands at £2,470bn.

Teams: calculate the debt-to-GDP ratio today — and compare it with a year ago. Then answer: why do economists watch the ratio, not the £ amount of debt?

The answers

Debt-to-GDP = £2,470bn ÷ £2,550bn = 96.9% — the burden barely moved, despite £70bn of new borrowing
Why the ratio is the number that matters
  1. It measures the ability to pay. £2.4 trillion of debt sounds apocalyptic — until you know the economy produces £2.5 trillion every single year. Same reason a £200,000 mortgage is fine on a £60,000 salary and a catastrophe on a £20,000 one.
  2. You can shrink the burden without repaying a penny. If the economy grows faster than the debt, the ratio falls — which is how Britain worked off post-war debt of over 250% of GDP without ever "paying it back". Growth is the quiet escape route.
  3. It's what lenders price. Gilt investors compare debt-to-GDP across countries and across time — it drives the yield the government pays, which drives the £100bn debt-interest line from the starter.
  4. And the warning in reverse: if growth stalls while borrowing continues, the ratio climbs even with a "small" deficit — debt interest swells and crowds out the very spending the Budget was for. The ratio is the early-warning light.

A Budget, step by step — you just did all of this

The manifesto sets the promises ROUNDS 1 & 2

Priorities and ideology are chosen at the ballot box — the Budget's job is to keep them.

The independent forecast ROUND 3

In real life the Office for Budget Responsibility forecasts the revenue — independently, so Chancellors can't mark their own homework.

The spending review ROUND 4

Departments bid; the Treasury settles. The fixed lines — entitlements and debt interest — are gone before the arguing even starts.

The fiscal rules check ROUND 5

The sums are tested against the government's own borrowing rules — the credibility test the markets watch.

The speech and the red box

The Chancellor announces it all to Parliament — by tradition, the only speech at which alcohol is permitted at the despatch box.

Parliament votes — the Finance Act

No vote, no taxes: the Budget only becomes law when the Commons passes it.

The gilt auctions fund the gap BUDGET DAY

The Debt Management Office borrows the deficit, auction by auction, all year long.

The year unfolds — and the next forecast begins ROUND 6 & BEYOND

Growth, yields and the debt ratio deliver their verdict — and the whole cycle starts again.
