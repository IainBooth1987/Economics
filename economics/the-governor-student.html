BRIEFING

The Governor's Briefing — Read This First

The Governor has set out the principles for this office. Every team should read it carefully — it contains evidence you'll need later, and it may be relevant in ways that aren't obvious yet.

G
The Governor
"Six months ago every bank in this country lent to every other bank overnight without a second thought. That has stopped, almost overnight. Nobody knows which of our banks is sitting on toxic assets — so nobody will lend to any of them, just in case."

On the two banks that have already come to us: "One needed cash for a few weeks while a good loan book matured — it was solvent the whole time, just briefly illiquid. The other is a different story: its balance sheet does not add up, however you look at it, and emergency lending will not fix that."

On the mood in the market right now: "Every bank in this room is now treating every other bank as a suspect, not a partner. That is not how a banking system survives a Tuesday, let alone a recession."

On how I operate: "I will lend freely, against good collateral, at a rate that punishes lateness — not because I enjoy punishing banks, but because a central bank that bails out recklessness without cost teaches every bank to take the same risks again. Equally, a central bank that does nothing while a solvent bank dies for lack of overnight cash has failed at the only job it has."

On where we stand today: Base Rate sits at 5% today. We have already cut it twice this year and mortgage approvals have barely moved — the transmission mechanism is jammed. The average outstanding mortgage in this country is £150,000.

On the limits ahead: "If rates reach zero and lending still will not unfreeze, we will need tools nobody in this country has used before."

On every tool we use: "Every tool we use must pass the credibility test. Markets must believe we will act the same way next time, conditions and all — and we will never quietly extend support without a review date attached. The moment markets believe we will bail out anything, forever, no questions asked, is the moment our promises stop meaning anything."

1 Round 1 — Diagnose the Crunch & the Bagehot Line

Why Have Banks Stopped Lending to Each Other?

Part A of 2  ·  Max 20 pts total for Round 1  ·  8 minutes

G
"Six months ago every bank in this country lent to every other bank overnight without a second thought. That has stopped, almost overnight. Two banks have already come to me for emergency support — tell me why, before I lend a penny to either of them."
Jargon

Banks stop lending to each other for different reasons: liquidity risk (a solvent bank can't get short-term cash), solvency risk (a bank is genuinely broke), counterparty risk & contagion (fear of who else is exposed), or adverse selection (lenders can't tell good banks from bad ones, so they freeze out everyone). The first job of any central bank is diagnosis: which of these is actually driving the freeze?

Worked example — how to match a quote (different scenario)

Quote: "Investors trust the company's accounts, so they keep lending it money even during a bad year."
Which concept does this fit? It's about a lender's belief in the borrower — trust, not cash flow. That points to confidence/trust, not liquidity or solvency directly.
Ask yourself: is this quote about cash running out, about being broke, or about who trusts whom? That's the test to apply below.
Your task

Match each Briefing quote below to the concept it supports, then use your matches to rank the three most responsible for the interbank freeze (1 = biggest cause). All six concepts are real — the points are for matching the concept to this specific freeze.

A Solvency risk

  • A bank's assets are worth less than its liabilities
  • It's genuinely broke — no amount of short-term lending fixes that

B Liquidity risk

  • A solvent bank can't get short-term cash because no one will lend overnight
  • The bank itself is fine — the funding market has frozen

C Counterparty risk & contagion

  • Banks fear which other banks are exposed
  • Trust collapses bank-to-bank faster than the underlying problems justify

D Moral hazard

  • Banks take bigger risks because they expect to be bailed out if it goes wrong
  • A consequence of how rescues are designed, not a cause in itself

E Adverse selection

  • Lenders can't tell healthy banks from toxic ones
  • The safest move is to lend to none of them — even the healthy ones get frozen out

F Asymmetric information

  • One side of a deal knows more than the other
  • "I'll remember the risk I'm taking" — but the other side can't verify it

Part B — The Bagehot Ladder

Where Does the Governor Stand?

Part B of 2  ·  4 minutes

G
"Half my board wants me to let weak banks fail and teach the market a lesson. The other half wants me to save everyone, no questions asked. You've read my Briefing — where do I actually stand?"
Jargon

Central banks sit on a ladder of response, gentlest to most forceful. Climbing the ladder buys stability — and costs the discipline that stops banks taking reckless risks. The classic middle position is Bagehot's dictum (Walter Bagehot, 1873): lend freely, against good collateral, at a penalty rate — gentle enough to stop a panic, harsh enough to stop recklessness.

2 Round 2 — Cut Rates — Until You Can't

What Is One More Cut Actually Worth?

Max 15 pts  ·  6 minutes

G
"I've cut twice already and mortgage approvals have barely moved. Before I cut again, tell me in pounds and pence what one more cut is actually worth to this economy — and what doing nothing instead would cost us."
Jargon

Cutting the Bank Rate is supposed to flow through the transmission mechanism: lower rates → cheaper mortgages and loans → more spending → higher output. But the mechanism can jam, and as rates approach zero, each further cut buys less and less — the first warning sign of a liquidity trap.

3 Round 3 — Hit the Floor, Choose Your Tool

Commit to One Tool — the Live Market Decides

Max 10 pts  ·  5 minutes to commit, then the simulation runs live — no separate tab, it all happens right here

G
"Rate's at zero. The arithmetic from last round has run out of road — cutting further buys us nothing. Four advisers on my desk, four different tools, and not one of you agrees. Commit to one. The market decides who was right."
Jargon

Once the Bank Rate is at (or near) zero, conventional cuts stop working — the zero lower bound. An economy stuck there is in a liquidity trap: Keynes described it as "pushing on a string" — you can offer more cheap money, but nobody will pull on it. Central banks then reach for unconventional monetary policy.

Your task

Choose one tool. Then: (a) match each tool to its mechanism, (b) circle the justification that correctly links your tool to your Round 1 diagnosis, and (c) tick the weakness that genuinely applies to your tool. Then hold your nerve — the simulation runs immediately after, in this same round, and decides the points.

A Forward Guidance Only

  • Promise rates stay low for 3 years — no balance sheet action
  • Cost: essentially free

B QE: Government Bonds

  • Newly created money buys gilts on the open market
  • Cost: the size of the programme, e.g. £75bn

C QE: Corporate Bonds Too

  • Credit easing — money flows more directly to firms
  • Cost: similar scale, smaller share of the total programme

D Negative Interest Rates

  • Charge commercial banks for holding reserves with us
  • Cost: squeezes bank profit margins

4 Round 4 — The Recovery

Build Your Portfolio — and Pass the Credibility Test

Max 20 pts  ·  6 minutes

G
"Here's the recovery plan: a fixed budget, and your pick of three measures from the menu. Maximise the recovery you buy per pound. And before you choose — I'd reread my Briefing, every line of it. Some of you skimmed the bit about the credibility test."
Jargon

Support extended quietly, indefinitely, with no conditions or review date attached looks generous in the short run — but it fails the credibility test central banks live or die by. Markets need to believe a central bank will act the same way next time, with the same conditions. Once that belief goes, every promise the bank makes is worth less.
