How to Run This — Quick-Start Guide

Written for the version of you teaching this cold, five minutes before the bell.

Before the lesson (2 minutes)

Open The_Cartel.html on the board/projector — this is the only screen students see, with its own Setup tab for team names and team count (3–5 firms works best for a class of 24–30). Print or hand out The_Cartel_Student_Workbook.html, one per firm, not per student. Keep this answer booklet open or printed beside you — every reveal, rubric and "watch for" note below matches what's projected live in the console.

The shape of every round

  1. Brief. The console shows the round's task and the option cards. Read it aloud once.
  2. Work. Start the on-screen timer. Firms discuss and write on the matching workbook page. Circulate — don't reveal anything yet.
  3. Reveal. Click "Reveal" on the console. The decision and reasoning appear on screen — identical to what's printed in this booklet for that round.
  4. Score. Use this booklet's rubric to mark each firm's worksheet answer, type the score into the console's score grid — the league table updates itself.
  5. Move on. Click the next round in the console's nav bar.
This is a connected story, not four separate scenarios. Round 2's invitation only matters because of how firms behave afterwards, Round 3 tests whether an agreement holds, and the 🚨 Dawn Raid is where every firm's Round 2–3 choices catch up with them at once — a 0 / −3 / −6 point penalty depending on exposure (adjusted for leniency), applied before the pitch is scored. Tell firms at the start to keep their Round 1–3 worksheet answers in front of them; Round 4 is unmarkable without them.

★STARTER

Spot the Cartel Signal — answer

The real evidence strength, strongest to weakest

  • 1. Identical cover-price bids, delivered minutes apart — direct contact about a specific price, no innocent explanation. Real 2009 OFT case: 100+ UK construction firms fined for exactly this on public-sector tenders
  • 2. Auto-matching pricing software — no human contact, but the CMA found this unlawful price-fixing in a real 2016 case (Trod Ltd & GB eye Ltd); automating an agreement doesn't make it legal
  • 3. The trade body lunch — completely legal until price is actually discussed; real cases have used exactly this kind of meeting (minutes, calendar invites) as the evidence trail
  • 4. Parallel pricing alone — suspicious, but on its own never enough evidence; firms can rationally watch and match each other lawfully
  • 5. A shared cost shock — zero evidence of collusion; every firm faces the same higher cost and responds independently
Teaching point

Cards 4 and 5 are completely legal, however suspicious they look. Cards 1 and 2 are explicit collusion. Card 3 is the grey zone every real cartel hides in — and it's exactly where today's Round 2 decision sits.

1ROUND 1

Set Your Price — answer

Max 15 points

What happened

Whatever a rival chose, undercutting always earned more individually — yet if every firm follows that logic, all firms end up worse off than if everyone had simply held.

Why "obviously rational" choices make everyone poorer

The dominant strategy: if the rival HOLDS, undercutting earns £70k vs £40k for holding too. If the rival UNDERCUTS, undercutting earns £15k vs just £5k for holding. Undercutting beats holding whatever the rival does.

The trap: every firm following this logic lands the market on (undercut, undercut) — £15k each — when (hold, hold) would have given both £40k. The classic prisoner's dilemma.

Why it matters for the rest of the game: this exact trap is why firms in concentrated markets are tempted to collude — an agreement to hold price is an attempt to escape it. The next two rounds show why that escape is illegal and unstable.

Marking rubric — max 15

  • 4 pts — correctly identifying UNDERCUT as the dominant strategy
  • 4 pts — explaining why, using actual figures from the matrix
  • 4 pts — naming the prisoner's dilemma (or Nash equilibrium) correctly
  • +3 pts — explaining why (hold, hold) doesn't happen without coordination

Note for Round 4: this round sets the tone — no exposure penalty stems from Round 1 itself, only from Rounds 2–3.

2ROUND 2

The Trade Body Invitation — answer

Max 15 points

What happened

Firms that attended priced almost identically — exactly the parallel-pricing pattern that exists whether collusion is real or coincidental, which is precisely why parallel pricing alone is never enough evidence in a competition law case.

Where the legal line actually sits

1. Tacit vs explicit: watching and matching published prices is lawful tacit collusion. The Forum dinner is different — if price is discussed or agreed there, that conversation itself is the illegal act, the moment it happens.

2. Attending isn't automatically illegal — trade bodies discuss real, legitimate things. But real cases have used exactly this kind of meeting (minutes, calendar invites, expense claims) as the evidence trail once price came up.

3. From this point on, every firm that attended carries a piece of evidence that didn't exist before — write this down, it matters in the Dawn Raid.

Marking rubric — max 15

  • 5 pts — correctly distinguishing tacit collusion from explicit collusion
  • 5 pts — explaining why this meeting decision is the critical evidence point for the rest of the game
  • 5 pts — correctly noting that attending alone isn't proof; only discussing or agreeing price is

3ROUND 3

Hold the Line or Break Ranks — answer

Max 15 points

What happened

Cartels are unstable by design — every member has a private incentive to cheat on the agreement, which is exactly why most real-world cartels eventually collapse, get reported by an insider, or get caught.

Profit changes. Legal exposure doesn't.

The instability: if everyone else honours the price and you secretly undercut, you get cartel-level prices everywhere else plus your own bigger market share — exactly why every cartel member is constantly tempted to cheat.

The crucial twist: whether a firm honoured or cheated, attending the Round 2 meeting is already enough evidence to be investigated. Cheating doesn't make a firm innocent — it just changes how much money they made.

Why real cartels get reported: a member who's been cheating, or who falls out with the others, often has the strongest incentive to confess first and claim leniency — exactly the choice in the Dawn Raid.

Marking rubric — max 15

  • 5 pts — correctly explaining why cartel members have a private incentive to cheat
  • 5 pts — linking this to cartel instability (or a credible-commitment problem)
  • 5 pts — correctly noting that honouring vs cheating changes profit but not legal exposure

Note for Round 4: flag which firms attended Round 2 and which honoured/cheated in Round 3 — it determines their Dawn Raid exposure penalty.

🚨THE DAWN RAID

The Regulator Arrives — marking guide

Exposure penalty

Declined Round 2 throughout = CLEAN, no penalty. Attended Round 2 AND honoured the price in Round 3 = FULL EXPOSURE, apply a −6 point penalty to Round 4. Attended Round 2 but secretly undercut in Round 3 = PARTIAL EXPOSURE, apply a −3 point penalty — attending is enough evidence on its own, but a shorter, less stable cartel typically draws a smaller fine.

Leniency

The first exposed firm to confess before you reveal has their penalty wiped to 0 entirely (full immunity, exactly like the CMA's real leniency policy). The second firm to confess has their penalty halved. Run the "does anyone want to confess?" moment live, before clicking reveal — the tension is the lesson, more than any single number.

4ROUND 4

The Compliance Pitch — answer

Max 20 points (after exposure penalty)

What separates a strong pitch from a generic one

No single policy fixes every round — firms that pitched ONE policy matching their own worst round scored highest, not the most impressive-sounding policy.

Matching policy to problem

A firm exposed mainly because it attended Round 2 needs a fix aimed at not attending — compliance training or a flat refusal policy. A whistleblowing line doesn't touch this; the firm was never inside a cartel that needed reporting.

A firm exposed because it cheated on an agreement in Round 3 needs a fix aimed at instability — an internal whistleblowing route, or genuinely applying for leniency early.

A firm that declined throughout and stayed clean can honestly pitch that hard, independent competition (Round 1's UNDERCUT) was always the only fully legal strategy.

Marking rubric — max 20 (after exposure penalty)

  • Exposure penalty: 0 / −3 / −6 from the Dawn Raid (adjusted for leniency) applied first
  • 6 pts — policy genuinely matches the firm's own worst round
  • 6 pts — accurate use of correct vocabulary (cartel, collusion, leniency, dominant strategy / Nash equilibrium, parallel pricing)
  • 5 pts — explicitly references the firm's own Round 1–3 choices
  • +3 pts — acknowledges one limitation or cost of their chosen policy

📈EPILOGUE

One Year On — answer

★ Bonus — max 5 points

Model answer

A market built for collusion doesn't need a meeting to behave collusively. Even with the cartel broken and the fines paid, four firms in one concentrated market can land on the same prices simply by watching each other and following a price leader — perfectly legally. Competition law punishes explicit collusion relatively easily; tacit collusion arising from market structure is far harder to prove, and arguably impossible to fully eliminate without breaking up the market itself.

Bonus marking

Award up to 5 points for two well-justified, distinct reasons why tacit collusion is hard to stop, drawn from the toolkit table (price leadership, pricing algorithms, market concentration, transparency rules backfiring, slow CMA studies).

Essay model answer notes (25 marks): "Evaluate the view that breaking up a price-fixing cartel guarantees a genuinely competitive market."

KAA: oligopoly & concentration ratios · game theory, dominant strategy & Nash equilibrium (Round 1) · explicit vs tacit collusion (Rounds 2–3) · cartel instability (Round 3) · CMA powers — fines up to 10% of turnover, leniency, criminal cartel offence (Dawn Raid) · price leadership & concentration (Epilogue).

Evaluation: tacit collusion can reproduce cartel-like outcomes without anyone breaking the law · market structure makes coordination easy regardless of legality · fines as deterrent vs compliance cost · whether genuine competition needs structural remedies, not just punishing the cartel that got caught.

✓GLOSSARY

Key terms — model definitions

Oligopoly & interdependence

A market dominated by a small number of large firms, where each firm's pricing and output decisions depend on, and affect, the decisions of its rivals.

Dominant strategy / Nash equilibrium

A dominant strategy earns the best outcome for a player whatever their rival does. A Nash equilibrium is reached when no player can improve their outcome by changing strategy alone, given what rivals are doing.

Tacit collusion

Firms reaching similar prices or behaviour by independently observing and responding to each other, without any direct communication or agreement. Legal.

Explicit collusion / cartel

A direct agreement between rival firms to fix prices, restrict output, or divide markets. Illegal under UK and EU competition law from the moment the agreement is made.

Parallel pricing

Firms moving prices in the same direction at similar times. On its own, not proof of collusion — can arise from common cost shocks or lawful tacit coordination.

Leniency programme

A regulatory policy (used by the CMA and EU) offering reduced or zero fines to cartel members who voluntarily report the cartel and cooperate with the investigation — the first to confess typically receives full immunity.

Price leadership

A form of tacit collusion where one dominant firm sets a price change and rivals follow it, without any explicit agreement to do so.
