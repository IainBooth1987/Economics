What Is Globalisation?

Globalisation is the process by which the world's economies, societies, and cultures become increasingly interconnected and interdependent, through the growing international movement of goods, services, capital, people, technology, and information.

Economic Globalisation
The growing integration of national economies through trade, foreign direct investment (FDI), capital flows, and the international fragmentation of production (global supply chains).
Social & Cultural Globalisation
The spread of ideas, information, media, and cultural products across borders — accelerated by the internet and social media — leading to greater cultural exchange, but also concerns about cultural homogenisation.
Political Globalisation
The growing role of international institutions and agreements (the WTO, UN, IMF, World Bank, trade blocs) in shaping national policy, and the increasing coordination of policy across borders on issues from trade to climate change.
Why This Topic Sits Alongside International Trade: globalisation is the broader phenomenon of which international trade liberalisation (see the companion BIZ-OMICS International Trade guide) is one central part — but globalisation also encompasses capital flows, migration, the spread of multinational corporations, and cultural exchange, all covered in this guide.

The History of Globalisation

Globalisation is not a purely modern phenomenon — it has proceeded in distinct waves, each driven by different technologies and institutional developments.

1
Early Trade Networks (Ancient Times – 1400s)

The Silk Road connected China to the Mediterranean from around 130 BCE, while maritime trade routes linked the Indian Ocean, Arabia, and East Africa — early, limited forms of long-distance economic exchange, constrained by slow transport and communication.

2
The Age of Exploration & Colonial Trade (1400s–1800s)

European maritime exploration from the late 15th century opened new transatlantic and transpacific trade routes, integrating the Americas, Africa, Asia, and Europe into a single (though deeply unequal and often coercive) global trading system, including the transatlantic slave trade.

3
The First Wave of Modern Globalisation (1870–1914)

Falling transport costs (steamships, railways), the telegraph, and relatively liberal trade policy produced a first genuine wave of modern economic globalisation — world trade grew rapidly, and international capital flows reached levels not seen again until the late 20th century. This wave was abruptly reversed by the First World War, followed by the interwar collapse into protectionism (including the 1930 Smoot-Hawley tariffs — see the companion BIZ-OMICS International Trade guide) and the Great Depression.

4
The Bretton Woods Era & GATT (1944–1970s)

Post-war institutions — the IMF, World Bank, and GATT (see the companion BIZ-OMICS International Trade guide's WTO history) — rebuilt a rules-based international economic order, progressively lowering tariffs through successive negotiating rounds.

5
Hyperglobalisation (1990s–2008)

The fall of the Soviet Union, China's market reforms and 2001 WTO accession, the rise of container shipping and the internet, and the creation of the WTO in 1995 combined to produce an unprecedented acceleration in trade, capital flows, and the fragmentation of production into global supply chains — the period some economists call "hyperglobalisation." World trade as a share of global GDP rose from around 25–30% in the 1960s to a peak of roughly 60% by 2008.

6
"Peak Globalisation" & Slowbalisation (2008–present)

Following the 2008 financial crisis, global trade's share of GDP plateaued and has since fluctuated without returning decisively to its pre-2008 growth trajectory — a period commentator Bruce Nussbaum termed "peak globalisation" in 2010. The 2016 Brexit vote and Trump's first-term election, the COVID-19 pandemic, and the 2018 and 2025 US-China tariff conflicts (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Balance of Payments guide) have all reinforced a trend some economists call "slowbalisation" — explored fully in Section 14.

Measuring Globalisation

Because globalisation covers economic, social, and political dimensions simultaneously, economists use several different measures to track it over time.

The KOF Globalisation Index
Developed by Axel Dreher at ETH Zurich's KOF Swiss Economic Institute and first published in 2002, this is the most widely used academic measure, scoring 190 countries from 1970 to the present across three dimensions — economic, social, and political — each further split into "de facto" (actual flows and activity) and "de jure" (formal policies and openness) measures. In the most recent (2025) edition, the Netherlands ranked as the world's most globalised economy, with a score of 88.9 out of 100.
Trade-to-GDP Ratio
The simplest economic measure — total exports plus imports as a share of GDP. This rose from around 25–30% in the 1960s to a peak of roughly 59–60% in 2008, before plateauing and drifting slightly lower through the 2010s, one of the clearest pieces of evidence behind the "slowbalisation" debate (Section 14).
FDI Flows
Total global foreign direct investment flows are another common proxy, tracking how much capital multinational corporations (Sections 6–8) are investing across borders each year.
Why Measurement Matters for Evaluation: whether globalisation is "increasing," "plateauing," or "reversing" is not simply a matter of opinion — it depends on which measure is used and over what time period, which is exactly why Section 14's deglobalisation debate requires careful, evidence-based argument rather than a simple assertion either way.

The Driving Forces of Globalisation

Falling Transport Costs
Containerisation (standardised shipping containers, from the 1950s–60s) and larger, more efficient container ships dramatically reduced the cost of shipping goods internationally, making it economical to fragment production across multiple countries.
Falling Communication Costs
The internet, mobile telephony, and modern logistics software have collapsed the cost of coordinating complex international supply chains and communicating across time zones in real time.
Trade Liberalisation
Progressive rounds of tariff reduction under GATT/WTO, and the proliferation of trade blocs and free trade agreements, have directly reduced the cost of importing and exporting (see the companion BIZ-OMICS International Trade guide).
Capital Market Liberalisation
The removal of capital controls in many economies from the 1980s onwards has allowed capital to flow far more freely across borders, funding foreign direct investment and portfolio investment (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Balance of Payments guide).
The Rise of Multinational Corporations
MNCs and TNCs (Sections 6–8) have been both a driver and a consequence of globalisation, actively building the cross-border supply chains and investment flows that define it.
Political & Institutional Change
The end of the Cold War, China's economic reforms and 2001 WTO accession, and the creation of the WTO itself (1995) all opened vast new populations and markets to international trade and investment.
★ REAL WORLD: Malcom McLean & the Container Shipping Revolution
In 1956, American trucking entrepreneur Malcom McLean converted an old tanker, the SS Ideal-X, to carry standardised metal shipping containers rather than loose ("break bulk") cargo loaded and unloaded piece by piece by hand. This single innovation — allowing goods to move seamlessly between ship, rail, and truck without ever being unpacked — is credited with cutting the cost of loading and unloading a ship by over 90% within a couple of decades, and is widely regarded by economic historians as one of the single most important enablers of the entire hyperglobalisation era described in Section 2, making it economically viable to manufacture components on one continent, assemble them on another, and sell the finished product on a third.

Glocalisation

Glocalisation — a blend of "globalisation" and "localisation" — describes the strategy multinational firms use to adapt their global products, services, and marketing to fit local tastes, cultures, and regulations, rather than selling an identical, undifferentiated product everywhere in the world.

★ REAL WORLD: McDonald's Glocalisation
McDonald's operates a globally standardised brand and operating model, but adapts its menu significantly by market: it serves no beef products in India (offering the McAloo Tikki and Maharaja Mac made with chicken instead, out of respect for Hindu dietary practices and the large vegetarian population), offers a McSpicy Paneer burger in India, a Teriyaki Burger in Japan, and McShawarma products in parts of the Middle East. This allows McDonald's to capture the efficiency benefits of a globally recognised brand and standardised supply chain, while still meeting local cultural and dietary expectations.
★ REAL WORLD: Coca-Cola's Local Adaptation
Coca-Cola sells thousands of different product variants worldwide, adjusting sweetness levels, packaging sizes, and even core product lines to local preferences and price points — offering smaller, more affordable pack sizes in lower-income markets, and entirely different flavour lines (e.g. Vegitabeta in Japan) tailored to local tastes.
Why Glocalisation Matters Economically: glocalisation represents a middle path between full standardisation (maximising economies of scale, but risking rejection by local consumers) and full localisation (maximising local relevance, but sacrificing the cost efficiencies of global scale) — helping explain why globalisation has not simply produced a single, homogeneous global culture and product market, contrary to some cultural-homogenisation criticisms explored in Section 13.

Multinational Corporations (MNCs) — What They Are

A Multinational Corporation (MNC) is a firm that owns and controls productive assets — factories, offices, subsidiaries — in more than one country, typically retaining a clear "home" headquarters from which strategic decisions are ultimately made.

Key Features of an MNC
Operations spanning multiple countries; a clearly identifiable home country and headquarters; centralised strategic decision-making, with foreign subsidiaries typically implementing rather than setting overall group strategy; the ability to shift production, investment, and profits between countries in response to costs, taxes, and regulation.
★ REAL WORLD: Apple's Global Supply Chain
Apple is headquartered in Cupertino, California, but its products are assembled almost entirely outside the US — principally by Foxconn in China (with growing production in India and Vietnam as part of a diversification strategy) — using components sourced from dozens of countries including South Korea (Samsung displays and memory chips), Taiwan (TSMC semiconductors), and Japan. Apple retains centralised control over design, branding, software, and strategic decisions from its US headquarters, while outsourcing the vast majority of physical manufacturing — a textbook example of how a modern MNC organises global production while keeping core strategic control centralised.

MNCs vs TNCs — The Differences

While often used loosely as interchangeable terms, economists sometimes draw a meaningful distinction between a Multinational Corporation (MNC) and a Transnational Corporation (TNC).

MNC (Multinational Corporation)TNC (Transnational Corporation)
HeadquartersA single, clearly identifiable home country, where core strategic decisions are madeDecision-making and management can be genuinely decentralised across multiple countries, without one dominant national base
StructureForeign subsidiaries typically implement group strategy set centrally at head officeRegional or national subsidiaries may operate with much greater strategic autonomy, tailoring products and decisions to local markets independently
National IdentityUsually still identified with, and culturally associated with, its home country (e.g. "American company," "Japanese company")Often deliberately cultivates a more globally neutral identity, less tied to any single national base
ExampleMost large firms, including Apple, Toyota, and Tesco, are more accurately described as MNCs in this stricter senseUnilever (with genuinely dual UK/Netherlands historical roots and highly decentralised regional management) and Nestlé are often cited as closer to the TNC model
Why the Distinction Is Often Blurred in Practice: in everyday use — including in most news media and even much economics teaching — "MNC" and "TNC" are used interchangeably, since very few firms are purely one or the other; most large international firms sit somewhere on a spectrum between a centralised MNC and a fully decentralised TNC. The distinction matters most when specifically discussing corporate governance and decision-making structure, rather than international trade and investment activity itself.
★ REAL WORLD: Unilever's Dual Anglo-Dutch Heritage
Unilever was formed in 1929 through the merger of British soap-maker Lever Brothers and Dutch margarine producer Margarine Unie, and for nearly a century operated as a genuinely dual-headquartered structure — with separate UK (Unilever PLC) and Netherlands (Unilever N.V.) parent companies, boards, and share listings, unified only by an equalisation agreement rather than a single corporate hierarchy. This unusually decentralised structure made Unilever one of the most frequently cited real-world examples of a true transnational corporation, until the company simplified into a single unified UK-based legal entity in 2020 — itself an interesting illustration of how even a long-standing TNC structure can eventually consolidate back towards a more conventional, centralised MNC model when the benefits of dual-listing no longer outweigh its complexity.

The Role of Multinationals in Driving Globalisation

Multinational corporations are not merely a product of globalisation — they are among its most powerful active drivers, through several distinct channels.

1
Building global supply chains

MNCs actively fragment production across countries to exploit each location's comparative advantage (see the companion BIZ-OMICS International Trade guide) — components sourced where cheapest, assembly where labour costs are lowest, R&D concentrated where skilled talent is available.

2
Directing Foreign Direct Investment

MNCs are the primary vehicle for FDI flows (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Balance of Payments guide), building factories, offices, and infrastructure abroad and directly integrating host economies into global production networks.

3
Transferring technology and management practices

MNC investment frequently brings new technology, working practices, and managerial expertise to host countries, raising local productivity beyond what domestic firms could achieve alone.

4
Lobbying for trade liberalisation

MNCs, with a direct commercial interest in freer cross-border trade and investment, are frequently influential advocates for further trade liberalisation and for international rules (e.g. via the WTO) that protect their cross-border operations.

The Benefits & Drawbacks of Multinational Behaviour

Sections 10–11 examine globalisation's effects at the level of whole economies. This section looks instead at the behaviour of MNCs themselves — the strategic choices they make, and the direct consequences of those choices, wherever they operate.

✓ Benefits of MNC Behaviour

Economies of scale

By spreading enormous fixed costs — R&D, branding, product development — across a genuinely global customer base, MNCs can achieve unit costs no purely domestic firm could match, often passing at least some of this saving on to consumers worldwide.

Risk diversification

Operating across many countries and currencies means an MNC's overall performance is less exposed to any single national recession, currency crisis, or local disruption than a purely domestic firm would be.

Genuine technology and management transfer

MNCs frequently introduce production techniques, quality standards, and management practices that raise productivity well beyond what the host economy's existing firms were achieving.

Building supplier ecosystems

Large MNCs often cultivate extensive networks of local subcontractors and suppliers, creating knock-on employment and business opportunities considerably beyond their own direct workforce.

✗ Drawbacks of MNC Behaviour

Market power and crowding out local competition

An MNC's sheer scale can allow it to undercut, acquire, or simply out-compete smaller domestic firms, potentially reducing competition and consumer choice in the host market over time.

"Footloose" capital and capital flight risk

Because MNCs can relocate production relatively quickly if costs rise or conditions change elsewhere, host communities that become dependent on a single large employer are left vulnerable to sudden divestment decisions made in a distant head office.

"Pollution haven" behaviour

Some MNCs have been accused of deliberately locating the most polluting stages of production in countries with the weakest environmental enforcement, effectively exporting environmental harm to jurisdictions least able to resist or regulate it.

Political and lobbying influence

The scale and economic importance of large MNCs can give them significant influence over host-government policy — sometimes constructively, but sometimes in ways that override local community or environmental concerns.

★ REAL WORLD: Toyota & the Spread of Lean Manufacturing
Toyota's globally influential "Toyota Production System" — built around just-in-time inventory, continuous improvement ("kaizen"), and rigorous quality control — has been studied and adopted, in various forms, by manufacturers across the world. As Toyota expanded its own manufacturing footprint internationally, it directly transferred these techniques into new host economies, widely credited with raising manufacturing productivity standards well beyond the automotive sector alone — a clear positive example of MNC-driven technology and management transfer.
★ REAL WORLD: Nike's 1990s Labour Practices Scandal — and Its Aftermath
Through the 1990s, Nike faced sustained international criticism and consumer boycotts over poor working conditions, low pay, and child labour allegations in supplier factories across Indonesia, Vietnam, and Pakistan — a defining case study of the labour exploitation concerns explored in Section 11. In response, Nike became one of the first major MNCs to publish a supplier code of conduct and submit to independent factory monitoring, a shift later followed by many other global apparel brands — illustrating both the genuine drawback of unmonitored MNC supply chain behaviour, and how sustained public and consumer pressure can change that behaviour over time.
★ REAL WORLD: Shell in the Niger Delta
Royal Dutch Shell's decades of oil extraction in Nigeria's Niger Delta have been associated with severe environmental damage — oil spills, gas flaring, and contamination of local farmland and waterways — generating sustained conflict with local Ogoni communities from the 1990s onwards. The environmental and human rights controversy became internationally prominent following the 1995 execution of writer and activist Ken Saro-Wiwa by the Nigerian military government, after he led protests against Shell's operations — a stark real-world illustration of how MNC extraction activity can become entangled with host-country political conflict and human rights concerns, well beyond the purely economic costs and benefits.

The Benefits & Costs of Globalisation for Developed Economies

✓ Benefits for Developed Economies

Cheaper imported goods

Consumers gain access to a far wider range of goods, often at lower prices than domestic-only production could offer, raising real living standards (see the "China effect" — the companion BIZ-OMICS Macroeconomic Objective Conflicts guide).

Access to larger export markets

Domestic exporters gain access to vastly larger global markets than the domestic market alone, supporting growth in internationally competitive sectors (e.g. the UK's financial and business services — see the companion BIZ-OMICS Balance of Payments guide).

Efficiency gains from specialisation

Firms and workers can specialise according to comparative advantage (see the companion BIZ-OMICS International Trade guide), raising overall productivity and living standards.

✗ Costs for Developed Economies

Deindustrialisation and structural unemployment

Manufacturing industries exposed to lower-cost foreign competition can decline sharply, generating structural unemployment in affected regions and industries (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Unemployment guide) — precisely the dynamic underlying much of the anti-globalisation movement (Section 13).

Downward pressure on wages in exposed sectors

Competition from lower-wage economies can suppress wage growth for workers in tradeable-goods sectors, contributing to the growth-vs-inequality tension explored in the companion BIZ-OMICS Macroeconomic Objective Conflicts guide.

Loss of tax revenue to profit-shifting

MNCs' ability to shift profits to low-tax jurisdictions (Section 12) erodes the domestic tax base of developed economies, a major and ongoing policy concern.

11

The Benefits & Costs of Globalisation for Developing Economies

✓ Benefits for Developing Economies

Export-led growth and poverty reduction

Export-oriented industrialisation, often built on foreign investment and access to developed-country markets, has driven some of history's fastest reductions in absolute poverty — most dramatically in China, where hundreds of millions of people were lifted out of extreme poverty from the 1980s onwards through export-led manufacturing growth.

Foreign direct investment and technology transfer

FDI brings capital, technology, and skills that developing economies often cannot generate purely domestically, supporting faster productivity growth than would otherwise be possible.

Employment creation

MNC investment and export manufacturing create large numbers of jobs, often paying more than the available domestic alternatives, even where wages remain low by developed-country standards.

✗ Costs for Developing Economies

The "race to the bottom" concern

Critics argue competition to attract mobile MNC investment can pressure developing countries into weakening labour rights, environmental standards, and tax rates, in a self-defeating "race to the bottom" that benefits MNCs more than host populations.

Poor working conditions ("sweatshop" concerns)

Export manufacturing has been repeatedly linked to low pay, long hours, and unsafe working conditions in some supply chains — the 2013 Rana Plaza factory collapse in Bangladesh, which killed over 1,100 garment workers producing for major Western clothing brands, remains the most widely cited real-world example, prompting the Bangladesh Accord on Fire and Building Safety and renewed scrutiny of global supply chain labour standards.

Profit repatriation

A significant share of the profits generated by foreign-owned production may be repatriated to the MNC's home country rather than reinvested locally, limiting the domestic benefit relative to the headline investment and output figures.

Environmental costs

Some developing economies have attracted highly polluting production that developed economies increasingly restrict domestically, echoing the Environmental Kuznets Curve debate covered in the companion BIZ-OMICS Balance of Payments guide.

MNCs, Tax Avoidance & Transfer Pricing

The same global structure that makes MNCs powerful drivers of globalisation also gives them unique opportunities to legally minimise their tax bills by shifting profits between countries — a major and ongoing source of political controversy.

Transfer Pricing
The pricing of transactions between different subsidiaries of the same MNC (e.g. one subsidiary "selling" intellectual property, components, or services to another) — legitimately used to allocate costs and revenues across a global group, but also open to manipulation to shift recorded profits into low-tax jurisdictions, regardless of where the genuine economic activity actually occurred.
Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS)
The general term for the range of legal tax-planning strategies MNCs use to shift profits from higher-tax to lower-tax jurisdictions, eroding the tax base of the countries where genuine economic activity takes place.
★ REAL WORLD: The OECD's Global Minimum Tax (Pillar Two)
Agreed by the OECD/G20 Inclusive Framework in December 2021 and covering over 140 countries, "Pillar Two" introduces a global minimum effective corporate tax rate of 15% for large MNE groups (with consolidated annual revenue above €750 million), specifically to combat the kind of profit-shifting described above. Rules began taking effect from 2024 (the UK implemented its version in December 2023), with the first major compliance filings due in 2026. However, in a striking illustration of how difficult international tax coordination remains, in January 2026 the OECD agreed a "Side-by-Side" package granting US-headquartered MNE groups an effective exemption from the core Pillar Two rules for fiscal years from 2026 onwards — the only jurisdiction to secure such treatment — highlighting the genuine limits of multilateral cooperation even on an agreement most major economies have formally signed up to.
Why This Matters for the Globalisation Debate: MNC tax avoidance is one of the clearest examples of how globalisation can create genuine tension between global economic integration and national sovereignty over tax policy — a recurring theme in the anti-globalisation critique explored in Section 13.

The Anti-Globalisation Movement

The anti-globalisation movement encompasses a diverse range of political and social critiques of economic globalisation, spanning both the political left and right, united by scepticism that globalisation's benefits have been fairly or widely shared.

Core Strands of the Anti-Globalisation Critique

Job losses and deindustrialisation

Concentrated job losses in manufacturing regions of developed economies, as production shifted to lower-cost countries — a central grievance behind the 2016 Brexit vote and the rise of protectionist politics in the US (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Balance of Payments guide's coverage of the 2025 tariffs and anti-globalisation).

Rising inequality

Critics argue globalisation has disproportionately benefited capital owners, highly-skilled workers, and MNC shareholders, while wages for lower-skilled workers in exposed sectors have stagnated (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Macroeconomic Objective Conflicts guide's growth-vs-inequality conflict).

Exploitation of workers in developing countries

NGOs and labour rights campaigners have long highlighted poor working conditions and low pay in some global supply chains (Section 11), organising consumer boycotts and campaigns against specific brands.

Loss of national sovereignty

Some critics argue international institutions (the WTO, IMF, EU) and powerful MNCs constrain elected governments' ability to set their own labour, environmental, and tax policy — a concern reflected in both the Brexit debate (see the companion BIZ-OMICS International Trade guide) and broader nationalist political movements.

Cultural homogenisation

Concerns that globally dominant brands and media can erode distinctive local cultures and traditions — though Section 5's glocalisation discussion shows this process is more nuanced than simple homogenisation in practice.

Environmental damage

Critics link globalised production and long-distance shipping to rising carbon emissions and environmental degradation, particularly where production has shifted to countries with weaker environmental regulation.

★ REAL WORLD: The 1999 Seattle WTO Protests
The WTO's 1999 Ministerial Conference in Seattle was disrupted by tens of thousands of protesters — a coalition of labour unions, environmental groups, and anti-poverty campaigners — in what became one of the most visible early expressions of the organised anti-globalisation movement, helping derail the intended launch of a new WTO negotiating round and cementing "anti-globalisation" as a recognised political and social force through the 2000s.

Deglobalisation & "Slowbalisation"

Since around 2008, evidence has mounted that the rapid "hyperglobalisation" of the 1990s and 2000s (Section 2) has slowed, stalled, or in some respects reversed — a trend widely referred to as "slowbalisation" or, in its more extreme form, deglobalisation.

Slowbalisation
A slowing in the pace of growth of global trade, investment, and economic integration, without trade and investment necessarily falling in absolute terms — the more widely supported characterisation of the post-2008 trend among economists.
Deglobalisation
A more extreme, active reversal of economic integration — falling trade and investment flows, rising protectionism, and the genuine unwinding of established global supply chains.

Evidence and Drivers

1
The 2008 financial crisis

Global trade collapsed sharply during the crisis and, while it recovered, world trade's share of global GDP never returned to its pre-2008 growth trajectory, plateauing at around 55–59% rather than continuing to rise.

2
Rising protectionism and trade tensions

The 2018 and 2025 US-China tariff conflicts (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Balance of Payments guide) and the broader resurgence of unilateral tariff action (see the companion BIZ-OMICS International Trade guide's WTO Appellate Body crisis) directly reversed decades of tariff reduction between the world's two largest economies.

3
COVID-19 supply chain disruption

The pandemic exposed the fragility of long, complex global supply chains (particularly for medical equipment and semiconductors), prompting many governments and firms to prioritise supply chain resilience over pure cost minimisation for the first time in decades.

4
Reshoring and "friend-shoring"

Governments have actively incentivised bringing strategically sensitive production (semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, defence-related manufacturing) back onshore or to politically-aligned "friendly" countries — the US CHIPS Act and the EU Chips Act both provide large subsidies for domestic semiconductor manufacturing, directly reversing decades of offshoring in that sector.

5
Geopolitical fragmentation

Rising US-China strategic rivalry, the Russia-Ukraine war and resulting sanctions (see the companion BIZ-OMICS International Trade guide's embargoes section), and broader "de-risking" strategies have encouraged firms and governments to reduce economic dependence on geopolitical rivals, even at some efficiency cost.

The Balanced View: most economists judge that outright deglobalisation remains the exception rather than the rule — global trade and investment have not collapsed, and remain far more integrated than in the pre-1990s era. "Slowbalisation" — a genuine slowing of the pace of further integration, combined with selective reshoring in strategically sensitive sectors — is a more accurate characterisation of the post-2008 trend than a full reversal of globalisation itself.

Exam Technique & Model Chains of Reasoning

The Golden Chain: How Globalisation Can Cause Structural Unemployment

Falling transport and communication costs, alongside trade liberalisation, make it economical for MNCs to relocate production to lower-cost countries → domestic firms in the exposed industry (e.g. manufacturing) face intense foreign competition on price → uncompetitive domestic firms scale back production or close entirely → workers in the affected industry lose their jobs → because their skills are specific to the declining industry, they cannot immediately move into new roles → structural unemployment rises in affected regions (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Unemployment guide) → this concentrated regional impact — even if the wider economy benefits in aggregate from cheaper imports — fuels the political backlash explored in the anti-globalisation movement (Section 13).

The Golden Chain: MNC Profit Shifting via Transfer Pricing

An MNC has subsidiaries in both a high-tax country (where genuine economic activity/production occurs) and a low-tax country → the low-tax subsidiary charges the high-tax subsidiary an inflated price for intellectual property, services, or components → this artificially reduces the high-tax subsidiary's recorded profit (raising its costs) and artificially increases the low-tax subsidiary's recorded profit → the MNC's overall global tax bill falls, even though genuine economic activity has not actually moved → the high-tax country's government loses tax revenue on profits that were substantively earned within its borders → this base erosion is precisely what the OECD's Pillar Two global minimum tax (Section 12) is designed to prevent.

Key Exam Tips

1

Always distinguish globalisation's economic, social, and political dimensions — a strong answer recognises globalisation is broader than just international trade.

2

Never claim globalisation's costs and benefits fall evenly across developed and developing economies — always distinguish which group of countries, and which specific groups within them, actually gain or lose.

3

Distinguish MNCs from TNCs carefully, but note in your answer that the terms are often used interchangeably in practice — showing awareness of this nuance itself earns credit.

4

Use "slowbalisation" and "deglobalisation" precisely — these describe different degrees of the same post-2008 trend, and conflating them loses marks.

5

Bring in real-world context for maximum marks: China's poverty reduction, the Rana Plaza disaster, the OECD's global minimum tax and its 2026 US exemption, the 1999 Seattle protests, and the US/EU Chips Acts are all precise, high-value examples.

6

Evaluate — never just describe. For questions on whether globalisation has been beneficial, weigh the aggregate efficiency gains against the real, concentrated distributional costs, and consider whether the answer differs for developed vs developing economies.
