Internal Economies of Scale

As the firm increases its scale of production, the Long Run Average Cost (LRAC) falls. Fixed costs are spread over more units of output and the firm benefits from specialisation, bulk purchasing, and operational efficiency.

Theory & Exam Preparation

A-Level Economics content with key definitions, analysis, and examiner guidance.

Internal Economies of Scale

Occur when a firm's own growth reduces its long run average cost (LRAC). These arise from within the firm itself.

  • Technical economies — Larger firms invest in more efficient, specialised capital; fixed costs of machinery spread over greater output.
  • Purchasing (buying) economies — Bulk buying gives discounts and improves supplier bargaining power.
  • Managerial economies — Specialist functional managers (HR, finance, operations) increase efficiency; overhead cost per worker falls.
  • Financial economies — Larger firms access credit more cheaply (lower interest rates, no collateral), reducing cost of capital.
  • Risk-bearing economies — Diversification across products and markets reduces revenue volatility.
  • Marketing economies — Advertising cost per unit falls as campaigns are spread over larger output volumes.

External Economies of Scale

Occur when the whole industry grows, benefiting all firms regardless of their own size.

  • Development of skilled labour pools in geographic clusters (e.g. tech workers in Silicon Valley / Shoreditch).
  • Specialist supplier networks and infrastructure investment shared across firms.
  • Industry-specific training institutions, reducing individual firm recruitment costs.

On the LRAC diagram, external economies cause a downward parallel shift of the entire LRAC curve — every output level becomes cheaper.

✏️ Examiner tip

Distinguish clearly between internal EoS (firm grows) and external EoS (industry grows). Examiners frequently award marks for this distinction. Internal EoS is a movement along the LRAC; external EoS is a shift of the LRAC.

Internal Diseconomies of Scale

When a firm grows beyond its optimal scale, average costs begin to rise due to internal inefficiencies.

  • Coordination problems — Managing complex multi-departmental operations becomes increasingly costly and error-prone.
  • Communication failures — Information distortion increases through multiple management layers; slow decision-making raises costs.
  • Worker alienation — Employees in large organisations feel disconnected, reducing productivity and raising absenteeism and labour turnover.
  • Technical diseconomies — Scaling plant beyond its optimal size causes mechanical stress, higher downtime, and increased maintenance costs.
  • Loss of control — Monitoring quality across thousands of dispersed locations becomes expensive and imperfect.
  • Inflexibility — Large sunk costs and bureaucratic inertia make strategic pivots slow and costly.

External Diseconomies of Scale

Affect all firms as the industry as a whole grows too large.

  • Resource shortages — Skilled labour becomes scarce, bidding up wage costs across all firms.
  • Rising input costs — Increased industry demand pushes up raw material prices.
  • Congestion costs — Transport, land, and infrastructure costs rise in heavily industrialised areas.
✏️ Examiner tip

On a 25-mark essay, evaluate whether diseconomies are inevitable. Firms like Amazon and Toyota have managed extraordinary scale while controlling average costs through investment in technology, lean management, and decentralised structures. Counter-arguments raise evaluation marks.

Minimum Efficient Scale (MES)

The lowest level of output at which a firm can fully exploit economies of scale and minimise long run average cost.

  • Shown on the LRAC diagram where the curve first becomes flat (or at its minimum point if U-shaped).
  • Industries with a high MES relative to market size tend to be dominated by a small number of large firms (e.g. steel, aerospace, car manufacturing).
  • Industries with a low MES can support many small firms (e.g. hairdressing, artisan bakeries, law firms).
✏️ Examiner tip

MES explains industry structure. Use it to link scale of production to market structure (monopoly, oligopoly, perfect competition). This earns analysis marks in 15 and 25-mark questions.

Productive Efficiency

  • Definition: Achieved when a firm produces at the lowest possible average cost.
  • Location on diagram: The minimum point of the LRAC curve.
  • A firm is productively efficient when it cannot produce the same output at lower cost — no resources are wasted.

The LRAC as the Envelope of SRAC Curves

  • The LRAC is tangent to each Short Run Average Cost (SRAC) curve at exactly one point.
  • Each point on the LRAC represents the optimal scale of plant for that output level.
  • In the short run, the firm is constrained to a single SRAC curve (fixed capital). In the long run, it can move to a different SRAC curve by changing all factors.
✏️ Diagram tip

When asked to draw the LRAC diagram, sketch 3–4 U-shaped SRAC curves, each tangent to the LRAC at one point. This demonstrates envelope theory and earns marks for diagram accuracy.

Short Run vs Long Run Cost Behaviour

A critical distinction in Economics. The short run features at least one fixed factor of production; the long run is defined as the period when all factors are variable.

Law of Diminishing Returns (Short Run)

As variable factors (e.g. labour) are added to a fixed factor (e.g. capital), marginal product eventually falls, causing marginal and average variable costs to rise. This explains the upward-sloping portion of the SRAC curve — not to be confused with diseconomies of scale.

✏️ Common exam error

Students often confuse diminishing returns (short run, one fixed factor) with diseconomies of scale (long run, all factors variable). These are distinct concepts on different diagrams. Always state the time period when discussing cost curves.
