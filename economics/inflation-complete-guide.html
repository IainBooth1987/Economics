What Is Price Stability?

Price stability is the MPC's primary objective (see the companion BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide) — it does not mean prices never change, and it does not mean 0% inflation. It means keeping the general price level rising at a low, stable, predictable rate, currently defined by the UK government as 2% CPI inflation per year.

Price stability matters because it allows households and firms to make long-term decisions — saving, borrowing, investing, wage bargaining — with confidence about what money will actually be worth in the future. When prices are stable and predictable, the price mechanism can do its job of signalling genuine changes in relative scarcity and demand, rather than being drowned out by economy-wide inflation noise.

A Common Misconception: price stability is not the same as prices staying the same. It means the rate of change of prices — the inflation rate — is low and predictable, not that the price level itself is frozen.

Inflation, Deflation & Disinflation — Definitions

Inflation
A sustained rise in the general price level over time, resulting in a fall in the purchasing power of money — each unit of currency buys progressively fewer goods and services.
Deflation
A sustained fall in the general price level over time — the opposite of inflation. The purchasing power of money rises, but (as Section 12 explores) this is not always good news for the economy.
Disinflation
A fall in the rate of inflation — prices are still rising, but more slowly than before. This is very different from deflation: prices are not falling, they are simply rising less quickly.
The Classic Confusion — Disinflation vs Deflation: if inflation falls from 8% to 3%, that is disinflation — prices are still 3% higher than a year ago. Only if the inflation rate itself turns negative — prices actually falling in absolute terms — is that deflation. Students very commonly confuse the two; examiners specifically reward getting this distinction right.

Index Numbers — Measuring Price Changes

Because prices of thousands of different goods and services all change by different amounts, economists need a single summary figure to track the overall price level over time. This is done using an index number.

An index number expresses a value relative to a chosen base year, which is always set to 100. Every other year's index number then shows the percentage change relative to that base year.

Index Number = (Value in Current Year ÷ Value in Base Year) × 100

Worked Example

Suppose a basket of goods cost £80 in the base year (2020) and £92 in 2024:

Index Number (2024) = (92 ÷ 80) × 100 = 115

An index number of 115 means prices are 15% higher than in the base year. If prices had instead fallen to £76, the index number would be (76 ÷ 80) × 100 = 95 — 5% lower than the base year.

Reading Inflation From an Index: the inflation rate is simply the percentage change in the index between two points in time, not the index number itself. If the index reads 115 in 2024 and had read 110 the year before, the inflation rate over that year is (115 − 110) ÷ 110 × 100 = 4.5%, not "115%" or "15%".

Weighted Price Indices — Construction & Worked Examples

A simple average of price changes across a basket of goods would be misleading, because households do not spend the same proportion of their income on every item — a small rise in the price of housing matters far more to the average household's cost of living than an equal percentage rise in the price of, say, matches. A weighted price index corrects for this by giving each item a weight reflecting its share of average household spending.

Weighted Index = Σ (Price Index of Item × Its Expenditure Weight)

Worked Example — A Simplified Three-Item Basket

ItemWeight (share of spending)Price Index (this year)Weighted Contribution
Housing0.401200.40 × 120 = 48.0
Food0.351100.35 × 110 = 38.5
Entertainment0.251050.25 × 105 = 26.25
Weighted Index = 48.0 + 38.5 + 26.25 = 112.75

The overall weighted index is 112.75 — implying 12.75% inflation since the base year. Notice this is much closer to housing's 20% rise than a simple (unweighted) average of the three price rises (20%, 10%, 5%, averaging 11.7%) would suggest is warranted purely by chance in this example — but crucially, the weighting ensures housing's 20% increase, which affects the largest share of household spending, is properly reflected as the dominant influence on the overall figure, rather than being averaged away equally alongside less significant items.

Why Weights Must Be Updated Over Time: as spending patterns change (e.g. households spend a smaller share of income on food and a larger share on digital services than a generation ago), the weights used in national price indices like the CPI must be periodically updated — based on detailed household expenditure surveys — to ensure the index continues to reflect what people actually buy, rather than an outdated basket from years earlier.

Measuring UK Inflation — RPI vs CPI (vs CPIH)

The UK produces several different official measures of inflation, and it is important to know both how they differ and which one government policy actually targets.

CPI (Consumer Prices Index)
Based on around 700 goods and services, using an internationally standardised methodology (originally the Harmonised Index of Consumer Prices, used for EU/international comparisons). This is the measure the UK government's 2% inflation target is set on, and the measure the MPC aims to keep at target.
CPIH
Identical to CPI, but also includes owner-occupiers' housing costs and Council Tax. Since March 2017, CPIH has been the ONS's own lead/headline measure of UK inflation, though the government's official target remains set on CPI specifically.
RPI (Retail Prices Index)
The UK's oldest, longest-running measure. It lost its "National Statistic" accreditation in 2013 due to acknowledged methodological flaws (it uses the arithmetic mean rather than the geometric mean, which is now understood to create an upward statistical bias), and the ONS actively discourages its use for new purposes. It remains legally required to be published, however, because it is still used to calculate payments on index-linked gilts, many pension schemes, and some regulated prices (e.g. certain rail fares).

Why does the UK government use CPI for its inflation target? CPI follows an internationally standardised methodology, allowing UK inflation to be compared meaningfully with other countries and the Eurozone, and its geometric-mean approach avoids the well-documented upward statistical bias built into RPI's arithmetic-mean calculation. Since December 2003, the government's inflation target has been set on CPI (previously RPIX) specifically for these reasons.

★ REAL WORLD: The RPI-CPI "Wedge" — Why the Choice of Index Matters for Money
Because RPI has historically run higher than CPI/CPIH — a gap sometimes called the "wedge," estimated at around 0.9 percentage points in recent years — the choice of which index applies to a given payment has real financial consequences. This is precisely why index-linked gilts (paying interest tied to RPI) have historically offered holders more generous inflation protection than an equivalent CPI-linked instrument would. From February 2030, the government has confirmed RPI will be aligned with CPIH methodology — a change expected to reduce payouts to holders of long-dated index-linked gilts, and one that has already prompted legal challenges from pension funds and institutional investors who argue they are receiving less than originally contracted.

Why Governments Target Low, Stable Inflation

Almost every major central bank in the world now targets a low, positive rate of inflation — typically around 2% — rather than 0%. This is a deliberate choice, not an accident.

1
A small buffer above zero avoids the risk of deflation

Aiming for exactly 0% risks tipping into actual deflation during a downturn, which — as Section 12 explores — can be considerably more damaging and harder to escape than mild inflation.

2
It allows relative prices to adjust smoothly

Wages and some prices are "sticky downward" — firms and workers strongly resist accepting nominal pay cuts. A little background inflation allows relative wages to adjust (some rising faster than others) without anyone needing an outright nominal cut.

3
It anchors inflation expectations

A clear, credible, low target helps firms and workers plan wage and price decisions confidently, itself helping to keep actual inflation low and stable — a self-reinforcing effect (see the BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide's discussion of forward guidance and credibility).

4
It gives monetary policy room to manoeuvre

With inflation and interest rates both sitting a little above zero in normal times, the MPC retains room to cut rates in a downturn, rather than immediately hitting the Zero Lower Bound.

The Dangers of Inflation — Costs & Real-World Examples

✗ The Costs of Inflation

Menu costs

The real resource costs firms incur updating prices — reprinting menus, catalogues, price tags, and vending machines, and updating websites and till systems. The higher and more volatile inflation is, the more frequently firms must do this.

Shoe-leather costs

The time and effort people spend minimising how much cash they hold as inflation erodes its value — making more frequent trips to the bank or ATM to withdraw only what is needed, rather than holding cash that is constantly losing purchasing power. This effect becomes extreme under hyperinflation (Section 8).

Redistributive effects between savers and borrowers

Unexpected inflation transfers real wealth from savers and lenders (whose fixed nominal returns are worth less in real terms) to borrowers (who repay their debts in progressively cheaper money) — an arbitrary redistribution unrelated to merit or need.

International competitiveness

If a country's inflation rate persistently exceeds its trading partners', its exports become progressively less price-competitive (see the BIZ-OMICS Exchange Rates guide's discussion of the real exchange rate).

Erosion of savings

Money held in cash or low-interest accounts loses real purchasing power, disproportionately harming those on fixed incomes (e.g. pensioners) who are least able to adjust their income to compensate.

Fiscal Drag — Inflation as a "Stealth Tax"

Fiscal drag occurs when inflation pushes nominal incomes upward while income tax thresholds and allowances stay frozen in cash terms — dragging more of a person's income into higher tax bands, or into taxation at all, even though their real (inflation-adjusted) income has not actually risen. Because no politician has to vote for an explicit tax rise for this to happen, it is often called a "stealth tax."

Worked Example

Suppose someone earns £49,000, just below the £50,270 threshold at which the 40% Higher Rate of income tax begins, and thresholds are frozen in cash terms. If inflation is 5% and their employer raises their pay by exactly 5% to compensate — meaning their real income is completely unchanged — their new nominal salary is:

£49,000 × 1.05 = £51,450

This now exceeds the frozen £50,270 threshold by £1,180 — meaning that portion of their income is taxed at 40% instead of 20%, an extra £236 in tax (20 percentage points × £1,180) purely because of inflation, despite the person being no better off in real terms at all. Multiplied across millions of taxpayers, fiscal drag can raise very substantial extra government revenue without any government ever announcing a headline tax rise.

Why Fiscal Drag Matters for Policy Evaluation: because thresholds are set in cash (nominal) terms by law, a government wanting to avoid fiscal drag must actively choose to raise them each year in line with inflation. Freezing them — as the UK has done with most income tax thresholds since 2021, with the freeze extended into the later 2020s — is a deliberate, low-visibility way of raising real tax revenue, and is one reason the UK's overall tax burden (see the BIZ-OMICS Fiscal Policy guide) has been rising even without explicit headline tax rate increases.

Industrial Action — The Wage-Price Spiral Risk

When inflation persistently outpaces wage growth, workers' real wages — their pay adjusted for the cost of living — fall, even if their nominal (cash) pay is unchanged or rising slowly. This commonly leads to more assertive pay demands and, where employers resist, industrial action (strikes) as workers try to restore their lost purchasing power. If wage rises are then granted broadly across the economy without a matching rise in productivity, firms' costs rise, and they may pass this on as higher prices — feeding back into the very inflation that triggered the original pay demands in a self-reinforcing wage-price spiral.

★ REAL WORLD: The UK's 2022–23 Cost-of-Living Strike Wave
As UK CPI inflation hit 11.1% in October 2022 — the highest in four decades — a wave of industrial action swept across the public and private sectors, with an estimated 741,000 working days lost to strikes between June and September 2022 alone. Rail workers (RMT, ASLEF) rejected pay offers of 4–8%, seeking rises closer to double-digit inflation and warning that a 10.6% increase was needed just to restore wages frozen during the pandemic. Nurses, via the Royal College of Nursing, staged their first-ever national strike, citing a roughly 20% real-terms pay fall since 2010/11 according to the Nuffield Trust. Junior doctors demanded a 35% pay rise, arguing the British Medical Association's own analysis showed a 26% real-terms cut since 2008, while teachers' unions demanded rises closer to the 12% inflation rate against a 3.5% government offer. Each dispute illustrates the same underlying mechanism: as inflation eroded real incomes faster than pay was rising, workers used industrial action to try to restore lost purchasing power — with the eventual settlements (typically in the 5–7% range across most disputes) representing a genuine compromise between workers' cost-of-living demands and employers' concerns about further fuelling inflation through higher costs.
Why This Matters for Monetary Policy: the risk of a wage-price spiral is precisely why central banks watch wage growth data so closely when setting interest rates during a period of high inflation (see the BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide) — if inflation expectations become embedded in wage bargaining, cost-push pressures can persist well beyond the original shock that triggered them, making inflation considerably harder to bring back under control.

The Misery Index, Shrinkflation, Stagflation & Hyperinflation

The Misery Index
Devised by economist Arthur Okun, this simply adds the unemployment rate to the inflation rate to give a single, crude summary measure of macroeconomic hardship — the higher the combined figure, the more "miserable" the state of the economy is judged to be for the average person.
Shrinkflation
Where a firm reduces the size or quantity of a product while keeping its price the same (or raising it only slightly) — a disguised form of price increase per unit, since the true price per gram/millilitre/unit rises even though the shelf price looks unchanged.
Stagflation
The simultaneous combination of stagnant economic growth (or recession), high unemployment, and high inflation — a toxic combination that is very difficult for policymakers to address, since conventional demand-side tools that fight one problem tend to worsen the other (explored fully as the "stagflation dilemma" in the BIZ-OMICS Unemployment guide).
Hyperinflation
Extremely rapid, out-of-control inflation, conventionally defined as exceeding 50% per month. At this pace, money loses value so quickly that it becomes almost unusable as a store of value or medium of exchange, and economies can begin reverting towards barter.
★ REAL WORLD: Shrinkflation on UK Supermarket Shelves
UK shoppers have repeatedly noticed familiar products shrinking while the price on the shelf stayed the same or rose only slightly — a well-publicised example was Toblerone's 2016 redesign, which widened the gaps between its triangular peaks and reduced the bar's weight, effectively raising the price per gram without changing the sticker price. Consumer groups argue shrinkflation is especially difficult for shoppers to notice and factor into genuine cost-of-living comparisons compared with a straightforward price rise.
★ REAL WORLD: The 1970s Oil Shocks — Textbook Stagflation
Following the 1973 OPEC oil embargo, oil prices roughly quadrupled almost overnight. This drove up costs across the entire economy (cost-push inflation, Section 10) at exactly the same time as the resulting squeeze on real incomes and business costs pushed many economies into recession — combining high inflation with high unemployment and stagnant growth. US annual inflation peaked around 13.5% by 1980, alongside high unemployment — a defining real-world case of stagflation that badly undermined the previously dominant simple Phillips Curve trade-off between inflation and unemployment.
★ REAL WORLD: Hyperinflation — Weimar Germany, Zimbabwe & Venezuela
Weimar Germany (1923): forced to pay huge war reparations in gold or foreign currency, the government printed vast quantities of unbacked Papiermarks, with monthly inflation peaking at around 29,500% in October 1923 — people were reportedly paid twice a day and rushed to spend wages before prices rose again, and some resorted to using wheelbarrows of banknotes to buy everyday essentials.

Zimbabwe (2008): unchecked money creation to finance government spending (following earlier land reforms that collapsed agricultural output) pushed monthly inflation to an estimated 79.6 billion% in November 2008 — prices were doubling roughly every 24.7 hours, and the government eventually issued a 100 trillion Zimbabwean dollar note, before abandoning the currency entirely in early 2009 in favour of foreign currencies including the US dollar.

Venezuela (2013–2018): overspending and unchecked money printing under President Nicolás Maduro's government produced a cumulative price rise exceeding 1,000,000% over the period — by 2018, a single chicken cost millions of bolívars.
Shoe-Leather Costs Under Hyperinflation: in each of these episodes, holding cash for even a short time became extremely costly, as its value could halve within a day or less. This drove extreme shoe-leather costs — workers spending wages within hours of receiving them, businesses converting cash into foreign currency or physical assets almost immediately, and in Venezuela's case, shops investing in money-counting machines simply to process the sheer physical volume of near-worthless banknotes needed for everyday transactions.

Demand-Pull Inflation

Demand-pull inflation is caused by Aggregate Demand growing faster than the economy's ability to increase output to match it — "too much money chasing too few goods." As AD rises against a fixed or slower-growing productive capacity, firms respond to excess demand for their goods by raising prices.

Causes of Demand-Pull Inflation

Any factor that shifts AD to the right can generate demand-pull inflation: falling interest rates or Quantitative Easing (boosting consumption and investment — see the BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide), expansionary fiscal policy (higher G, lower T — see the BIZ-OMICS Fiscal Policy guide), a depreciating exchange rate (making exports more competitive and imports relatively more expensive, both raising net trade demand for domestic output — see the BIZ-OMICS Exchange Rates guide), or rising consumer/business confidence encouraging higher spending and investment.

Fig 1: Demand-pull inflation. AD shifts right (AD1 → AD2) along the upward-sloping SRAS curve — both the price level (P1 → P2) and real output rise together, since firms are willing to supply more output only at higher prices as the economy approaches capacity.

The Quantity Theory of Money & the Fisher Equation

Monetarist economists explain demand-pull inflation through the Quantity Theory of Money, formalised in the Fisher Equation (covered fully in the BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide's Monetarism section):

MV = PT

Where M is the money supply, V is the velocity of circulation, P is the price level, and T is the volume of transactions (real output). If V and T are assumed to change only slowly, then any rapid growth in the money supply (M) must show up as a roughly proportional rise in the price level (P) — the monetarist explanation for why excessive money supply growth is the ultimate cause of demand-pull inflation, and why "inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon," in Milton Friedman's phrase.

The Dangers of Demand-Pull Inflation

Left unchecked, demand-pull inflation can become self-reinforcing: rising prices lead to higher wage demands to protect real incomes, which raise firms' costs and are passed on as further price rises (a wage-price spiral), while persistently loose monetary or fiscal policy can also risk asset price bubbles as excess demand spills into property and financial markets.

★ REAL WORLD: Post-COVID Demand-Pull Pressures
Following the COVID-19 pandemic, several economies saw a demand-pull element to inflation as large fiscal support packages and historically loose monetary policy (including extensive QE) combined with pent-up consumer demand and high accumulated household savings, all pushing AD upward sharply just as supply chains were also still recovering — illustrating how demand-pull and cost-push pressures (Section 10) can compound one another in practice.

Cost-Push Inflation

Cost-push inflation is caused by rising costs of production, which reduce Short-Run Aggregate Supply and push up prices independently of the state of demand — firms pass higher costs on to consumers as higher prices, even if AD itself has not changed.

Causes of Cost-Push Inflation

Rising raw material or energy costs (e.g. oil price shocks), a depreciating exchange rate raising the domestic-currency cost of imported inputs (see the BIZ-OMICS Exchange Rates guide), rising wage costs (particularly if wage growth exceeds productivity growth), and higher indirect taxes (e.g. a VAT rise, which is built directly into prices — see the BIZ-OMICS Fiscal Policy guide) can all reduce SRAS and generate cost-push inflation.

Fig 2: Cost-push inflation. SRAS shifts left (SRAS1 → SRAS2) while AD stays unchanged — the price level rises (P1 → P2), but real output falls at the same time, unlike demand-pull inflation. This simultaneous combination of rising prices and falling output is precisely the mechanism behind stagflation (Section 8).

The Dangers of Cost-Push Inflation

Cost-push inflation is generally considered more dangerous for policymakers than demand-pull inflation, precisely because it creates the stagflation dilemma (explored in depth in the BIZ-OMICS Unemployment guide): raising interest rates to fight the inflation would further reduce AD and deepen the accompanying fall in output and rise in unemployment, while cutting rates to support output would risk fuelling the inflation further. There is no single policy tool that addresses both problems simultaneously.

★ REAL WORLD: The 1973 Oil Embargo
OPEC's 1973 oil embargo — a direct, sudden supply-side shock — roughly quadrupled oil prices within months, sharply raising costs across virtually every sector of Western economies (transport, manufacturing, heating) simultaneously, and is the textbook real-world illustration of cost-push inflation triggering a full-blown stagflationary episode.

Deflation — Malign vs Benign, Good vs Bad

Deflation is not automatically bad news — its economic consequences depend entirely on why prices are falling.

Benign (Good) Deflation
Falling prices caused by an increase in Aggregate Supply — improved productivity, technological progress, or falling input costs allow firms to produce more, more cheaply, passing the savings on as lower prices. This is generally accompanied by rising real output and rising real incomes.
Malign (Bad) Deflation
Falling prices caused by a collapse in Aggregate Demand — firms cut prices in a desperate attempt to sell products nobody wants to buy at current prices. This is generally accompanied by falling real output, rising unemployment, and a shrinking economy.
Fig 3: Good/benign deflation. LRAS shifts right (LRAS1 → LRAS2) against unchanged AD — real output (potential GDP) rises and the price level falls (P1 → P2), since the economy can now produce more without needing higher prices to ration scarce capacity.
Fig 4: Bad/malign deflation. AD shifts left (AD1 → AD2) against an unchanged SRAS — real output falls and the price level also falls (P1 → P2). Unlike good deflation, this is accompanied by falling output and (typically) rising unemployment.

Why Even "Good" Deflation Can Turn Bad

Even deflation that starts out benign carries a genuine risk of tipping into something more damaging over time, through the following mechanism:

1
Consumers start to expect further price falls

Once falling prices persist for a while, households and firms may rationally expect prices to keep falling.

2
Spending and investment are postponed

If a good will be cheaper next month, there is a direct financial incentive to delay purchasing it — why buy a car or a washing machine today, when waiting will save money?

3
Aggregate Demand falls as a result

Widespread postponement of spending directly reduces AD — even though the original cause of falling prices was a benign AS-side improvement, the resulting behaviour change now drags AD down too.

4
The economy tips towards a demand-side (malign) deflationary spiral

Falling AD reduces output and raises unemployment, causing firms to cut prices further to try to attract the now-more-cautious consumers, reinforcing the very expectation of further price falls that started the cycle — a self-reinforcing deflationary spiral that is very difficult to escape (see Section 13, Japan's Lost Decade).

The Real Debt Burden Problem — "Debt Deflation": deflation also mechanically increases the real value of existing debt, since loan repayments are fixed in nominal terms while the price level (and often wages) fall around them. This can force struggling borrowers to cut spending further just to keep servicing debt that is effectively growing in real terms — another channel through which even initially benign deflation can compound into a deeper, self-reinforcing downturn.
13

Japan's Lost Decade

Japan provides the definitive real-world case study of malign deflation becoming deeply entrenched, and is referenced repeatedly across AQA Economics precisely because it illustrates so many concepts at once — the liquidity trap, hysteresis, and the difficulty of escaping deflation once expectations take hold (see the BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide's discussion of the liquidity trap for the monetary policy side of this story).

1
A massive asset price bubble bursts (1991)

Japanese property and stock market prices had risen to extraordinary levels through the 1980s; when the bubble burst, asset prices collapsed, wiping out enormous amounts of household and corporate wealth.

2
The negative wealth effect crushes consumption and investment

Households and firms, suddenly far poorer on paper and often heavily indebted from the bubble years, sharply cut back on spending and investment.

3
Aggregate Demand collapses, and deflation takes hold

Falling AD pushed the price level down persistently through the 1990s and into the 2000s — a textbook case of malign, demand-side deflation.

4
Deflationary expectations become entrenched

Once consumers came to expect continued price falls, spending was further postponed, exactly as described in Section 12 — reinforcing the deflationary spiral over an extended period.

5
Conventional monetary policy loses traction (the liquidity trap)

The Bank of Japan cut interest rates to zero by 1999 and pioneered the world's first large-scale QE programme in 2001, but with confidence shattered and the economy in a liquidity trap, the extra money largely sat unused rather than being spent or lent — see the BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide's liquidity trap chain.

6
Growth remained anaemic for an extended period

What became known as Japan's "Lost Decade" (often extended to a "Lost Two Decades" given how long weak growth and deflationary pressure persisted) saw minimal economic growth and persistent deflationary pressure throughout the 1990s and much of the 2000s.

★ REAL WORLD: The Enduring Lesson of Japan
Japan's experience directly shaped how the Bank of England and US Federal Reserve responded to the 2008 financial crisis and COVID-19 — both were determined not to repeat what many economists judged to be Japan's key mistake: acting too cautiously and too slowly in the initial stages of the deflationary spiral, allowing damaging expectations to become entrenched before responding with sufficient force.
14

Solving Deflation

As with inflation, the correct policy response to deflation depends entirely on which type is present.

Benign (Good) Deflation

Since this reflects a genuine improvement in the economy's supply-side capacity, no corrective policy response is generally needed at all — indeed, policymakers should be careful not to mistake healthy, productivity-driven price falls for a demand problem requiring stimulus, since responding as though it were malign deflation could risk creating unnecessary demand-pull inflation instead.

Malign (Bad) Deflation

This requires aggressive demand-side stimulus before deflationary expectations become entrenched — expansionary monetary policy (cutting rates towards the Zero Lower Bound, and QE once conventional room is exhausted) and expansionary fiscal policy (higher G, lower T) to restore AD as quickly and credibly as possible. Clear, credible central bank communication (forward guidance) is especially important here, specifically to break the expectation of further price falls before it becomes self-fulfilling — precisely the lesson drawn from Japan's slow initial response in Section 13.

Exam Technique & Model Chains of Reasoning

The Golden Chain: Demand-Pull Inflation

A component of AD rises (e.g. interest rate cuts, expansionary fiscal policy, a weaker pound, or rising confidence) → Aggregate Demand shifts right along the upward-sloping SRAS curve → firms respond to excess demand by raising prices → both the price level and real output rise together → if AD continues rising faster than the economy's productive capacity, workers demand higher wages to protect real income → higher wage costs feed into firms' prices, risking a self-reinforcing wage-price spiral → the MPC responds by raising interest rates to bring AD growth back in line with the economy's sustainable capacity.

The Golden Chain: Cost-Push Inflation & the Stagflation Dilemma

A supply-side cost shock occurs (e.g. an oil price spike, a weaker pound raising import costs, or a VAT rise) → firms' costs of production rise → SRAS shifts left → the price level rises while real output falls (stagflation) → the MPC faces a dilemma: raising interest rates to fight the inflation would reduce AD further and deepen the fall in output and rise in unemployment, while cutting rates to support output risks further fuelling the inflation → without a clean policy tool available, the MPC often judges whether the shock is temporary and can be "looked through" within its medium-term target window (see the BIZ-OMICS Monetary Policy guide's supply-side shocks section) or requires some tightening despite the short-run output cost.

The Golden Chain: A Deflationary Spiral (Japan-Style)

An asset price bubble bursts → the negative wealth effect crushes consumption and investment → Aggregate Demand collapses → the price level falls (malign deflation) → consumers come to expect further price falls → spending and investment are postponed in anticipation of even lower prices tomorrow → this postponement further reduces AD → output falls further and unemployment rises → the real burden of existing nominal debt increases, forcing further spending cuts by indebted households and firms → the cycle reinforces itself, and conventional monetary policy loses traction once rates hit the Zero Lower Bound (a liquidity trap) → only sufficiently aggressive, credible demand-side stimulus, delivered before expectations become entrenched, can break the spiral.

Key Exam Tips

1

Never confuse disinflation with deflation — disinflation is a falling rate of inflation (prices still rising); deflation is prices actually falling. This distinction is heavily tested.

2

Always distinguish demand-pull from cost-push using the AD/AS diagram: demand-pull raises both price and output together; cost-push raises price while output falls — this single difference is the key to identifying which type a scenario describes.

3

Never assume all deflation is bad: always ask whether it is being driven by a rightward AS shift (benign) or a leftward AD shift (malign) before evaluating its economic consequences.

4

Use precise measurement vocabulary: CPI is the UK's official target measure; RPI is a legacy measure retained mainly for index-linked gilts and specific contracts, no longer used for the inflation target itself.

5

Bring in real-world context for maximum marks: Weimar Germany, Zimbabwe, and Venezuela for hyperinflation; the 1973 oil shock for cost-push stagflation; Japan's Lost Decade for malign deflation and the liquidity trap; and the 2030 RPI-CPIH alignment for the practical stakes of index choice.

6

Evaluate, don't just describe: for 25-mark questions, weigh which policy response is appropriate given the specific cause of the inflation or deflation described, rather than reaching for a generic "raise/cut interest rates" answer regardless of context.
