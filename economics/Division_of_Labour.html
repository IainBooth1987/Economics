What is Productivity?

Productivity measures how efficiently inputs are converted into outputs. Higher productivity means getting more output from the same resources — vital for economic growth and rising living standards.

Productivity = Output ÷ Input

Common Measures

Labour Productivity: Output per worker or per hour worked (e.g. 50 units/worker/day)

Capital Productivity: Output per unit of capital (e.g. revenue per £ of machinery)

Total Factor Productivity (TFP): Output relative to all inputs combined

📊 Example: A Bakery

Before: One baker produces 20 loaves per day

After training & investment: One baker produces 40 loaves per day

Result: Labour productivity has doubled — a 100% increase

Division of Labour & Specialisation

Division of Labour is the separation of the production process into distinct tasks, with different workers specialising in each task rather than every worker making complete products from start to finish.

"One man draws out the wire, another straights it, a third cuts it, a fourth points it, a fifth grinds it at the top for receiving the head; to make the head requires two or three distinct operations…"
— Adam Smith, The Wealth of Nations (1776)

Workers focusing on a single operation develop near-perfect technique. Every task switch costs time — putting down tools, moving workstations, refocusing mentally. And specialists who repeat the same motion are best placed to invent the machines that replace it.

Specialisation & the Need for Money

When workers specialise, they produce a surplus of one good but nothing else. This creates a fundamental problem: how do they get everything else they need?

The Barter Problem

Barter requires a "double coincidence of wants" — you must find someone who has what you want AND wants what you have. A specialised shoemaker must separately find a baker, weaver and toolmaker, each of whom wants shoes. This is impractical at scale.

💰 Money as the Solution

Medium of exchange: Everyone accepts money, eliminating the coincidence problem.

Store of value: Sell now, buy later when you need to.

Unit of account: All goods priced in one common unit, making comparison easy.

🔑 Key Connection: Without money, extensive specialisation and division of labour would be impossible. Money enables the modern economy where individuals produce one thing but consume thousands of different goods and services.

Key Insight — 240× Productivity Gain

Smith observed that 10 workers using division of labour could produce 48,000 pins per day, while 10 workers each making complete pins individually would produce only 200 pins per day. The same number of people, the same working hours, the same raw materials — but an output 240 times larger.

This single example became the opening argument of the book that launched modern economics.

Smith's broader insight: Division of labour is limited by the extent of the market. Larger markets → more specialisation possible → higher productivity → more wealth. This explains why cities outperform rural areas and why globalization enables hyper-specialisation.

Why the PPF Shifts Outward

An outward shift of the PPF represents economic growth — the economy can produce more with the same resources. Division of labour achieves this by:

• Increasing skill through repetition → more output per worker-hour

• Eliminating time lost switching tasks → same hours produce more

• Enabling task-specific tools and machines → capital productivity rises

• All production possibilities increase, not just one good — the entire frontier expands
