How to Run This — Quick-Start Guide

READ THIS FIRST

Written for the version of you teaching this cold, five minutes before the bell. If you only read one page in this booklet before your first run, read this one.

Before the lesson (2 minutes)
  1. Open budget-day-teacher.html on the board/projector — this is the only screen students see. It has its own “Setup” tab where you type in team names and pick team count (3–5 teams works best for a class of 24–30).
  2. Print or hand out budget-day-worksheet.html — one per team, not per student. Teams write on it together; that’s the point.
  3. Keep this answer booklet (budget-day-answers.html) next to you, on paper or a second screen — it has the reveal scripts, scoring rubrics and the "watch for" notes you’ll want mid-round, not after.
  4. Decide 50 or 65 minutes now, before you start the Starter — the run sheets on the previous page are tuned to be run start-to-finish, not improvised mid-lesson.
The shape of every round (it repeats five times)
  1. Brief. The teacher console shows the PM's task and the option cards on screen. Read the brief aloud once.
  2. Work. Start the on-screen timer. Teams discuss and write their answer on the matching worksheet page. Circulate — don’t reveal anything yet.
  3. Reveal. Click “Reveal” on the teacher console. The correct decision and the reasoning appear on screen for everyone — this is also exactly what’s printed in this booklet for that round, so you never need to paraphrase from memory.
  4. Score. Use this booklet’s rubric for that round to mark each team’s worksheet answer (typically 30–60 seconds per team while the reveal is still on screen). Type each team’s score into the score boxes on the console — the league table updates itself.
  5. Move on. Click the next round in the nav bar at the top of the console.
Scoring — what you actually do

You mark; the console adds up. For each round, read that round’s rubric in this booklet (it’s a short bullet list, e.g. “2 pts for X, up to 3 for Y”), glance at what each team wrote, type a number 0–max into that team’s box on the console, and move on. Students never need to add anything up themselves during the game — the league table (visible to the class) does it live. At the very end, students total their own worksheet using the Final Score box on the last workbook page, as a self-check against the league table.

Common first-run mistakes
  • Confirming manifesto clues early. The whole Round 4 payoff depends on the “3p in every pound” rule being hidden until then. If a team asks “is this important?” in Round 1, just say “good advisers read carefully” and move on.
  • Scoring before the reveal. Always reveal first, then score — the reveal gives you (and the students) the exact answer to mark against, so you’re not relying on memory.
  • Letting Round 2 run over. It’s the round most groups get stuck on. The worked VED example exists precisely so you can unstick a struggling team in 30 seconds rather than reteach the whole calculation.
  • Forgetting the bonus is optional. If you’re running 50 minutes, skip 📈 What’s Next and the ★ bonus entirely — don’t feel you owe students those 5 points; the worksheet’s totals work fine without them.
If something breaks: the console autosaves team names and scores as you go (it’s a single self-contained HTML file, no server, no login). Refreshing the page mid-lesson will not lose your scores.

Why these figures land well
  • Welfare & pensions at £310bn: bigger than Health and Defence combined. Most students predict Health first. The gap between perception and reality is the opener — “what else don’t we know about how money works?”
  • Debt interest at £100bn: this is the number that consistently shocks. Nearly double the entire defence budget — every year, before a single nurse is paid. The same £100bn appears on p.3 of the manifesto and flows directly into Round 5’s gilt maths. Make that connection explicit at the reveal.
  • Defence at £55bn (5th): students often rank it higher. The gap between public perception and reality is a useful teaching point in itself.
Model answer — why debt interest matters for today’s game

Every £1 the government borrows (Round 4) generates a future interest payment that crowds out future spending. The yield the gilt market charges (4% vs 5% in Round 5) directly determines how much of next year’s tax revenue is pre-committed before a single spending choice is made. The starter’s “gasp” at £100bn is the emotional anchor for the whole fiscal credibility argument.

Debrief lines that land
  • “Debt interest is nearly double the entire defence budget — and it buys nothing new. That’s the price of past borrowing.”
  • “Who decides that £100bn number? Not the Chancellor — the gilt market. That’s Round 5.”
  • “By the end of today, you will have written the Budget that sets all these numbers for next year.”
Note on the figures: these are ballpark figures calibrated to the game’s numbers — update them if you want to use the latest OBR data. The ranking and the debt-interest shock are what matter; the precise £bn is secondary. The teacher console also displays and reveals these figures live on-screen.

Know Your Chancellor — Answers

MAX 15 PTS · 5 MIN
Ranking: 1st A (NHS) · 2nd C (schools) · 3rd E (flood defences) — and Cole sits at 6–7 on the continuum: a centre-interventionist who works through markets
Part A — model justification for the ranking
  1. A — the NHS. “The health service held my family together.” Her mother’s nursing career makes this personal before it is political.
  2. C — schools. “You cannot harvest what you never plant”; the manifesto calls education “the seed corn of the economy” — that’s investment language, not a throwaway.
  3. E — flood defences. “I have waded through people’s living rooms” — a priority shaped by constituency scar tissue.

The decoys, each ruled out by her own words: B — “I will not promise tax cuts I cannot fund”; D — defence “held steady”; F — “the state should steer, not row” (owning railways is rowing).

Part B — model answers for the continuum evidence boxes
Evidence pulling toward 0 (free market)Evidence pulling toward 10 (interventionist)
“The state should steer, not row” — no nationalisation
Corporation tax kept “competitive with our neighbours”
The iron fiscal rule binds government spending		 Major spending on health, schools and green infrastructure
“Where markets will not build, the state must”
Windfall levy on energy profits “under review”

The synthesis: interventionist ends, market means — she wants active public outcomes but delivers them through markets, private firms and fiscal constraint. 6–7, not 9. Interventionist ≠ anti-market is the misconception to tackle at the reveal.

The console’s continuum suggestion (you still enter the combined total yourself)

Typing a team’s 0–10 position into the console shows a suggested base: 6 or 7 → 6 pts · 5 or 8 → 4 pts · 4 or 9 → 2 pts · otherwise 1 pt, plus up to 2 for evidence. Add that to the ranking score below and type the combined total (max 15) into the points box.

Scoring — max 15
  • Ranking (max 7): 2 pts per correct pledge (A/C/E) anywhere in top three — max 6 — plus 1 pt for justification quoting manifesto evidence
  • Continuum (max 8): base points from the team’s position (6/7→6 · 5/8→4 · 4/9→2 · else 1) plus up to 2 pts for citing evidence in each direction — award the evidence pts only if it was actually written in the boxes, not just spoken
  • Benchmark: sensible-but-generic on both halves with no manifesto evidence ≈ 6–8 pts total
Watch for: teams ranking B first “because tax cuts win elections” (good political instinct, zero textual evidence — her own sentence kills it), and continuum placements of 9–10 justified by “she spends loads” (ask: what does she refuse to own? What tax does she keep low?). Both halves teach the same A-Level lesson: evidence beats plausibility. If most teams are still drifting to 9+, name the fiscal rule and “steer, not row” explicitly before Round 3.

Forecast the Revenue — Answers

MAX 15 PTS · 6 MIN
Total forecast revenue = £1,000bn
The five worksheet steps, completed
  1. Income tax = £1,200bn × 25% = £300bn — the single biggest earner
  2. National Insurance = £1,000bn × 15% = £150bn
  3. VAT = £850bn × 20% = £170bn — the biggest indirect tax
  4. Corporation tax = £400bn × 25% = £100bn
  5. Given items: £80bn + £90bn + £110bn = £280bn → TOTAL £1,000bn
Debrief points

This is the Office for Budget Responsibility’s job in real life — done independently so Chancellors can’t mark their own homework. Sensitivity is the teaching gold: a 2% error on income tax alone = £6bn, half a school-building programme, appearing or vanishing. Also worth naming the direct/indirect split: the four calculated taxes are three direct + one indirect (VAT).

Scoring — max 15
  • 3 pts per correct big-four calculation (£300 / £150 / £170 / £100) — max 12
  • +3 pts for the grand total of £1,000bn
  • Method marks: correct method with one arithmetic slip earns 2 of the 3 for that tax
Watch for: percentage slips (25% of £1,200bn coming out as £30bn or £3,000bn) — a quick “does that look like a quarter?” sense-check sorts most of them. Walk the VED worked example aloud before starting the timer for weaker groups, and insist every team writes the £1,000bn somewhere prominent: Rounds 3 and 4 die without it.

The Settlements — Answers

MAX 10 PTS · 5 MIN
Cole’s settlements: HIGH Health £255 · HIGH Education £130 · HIGH Green £70 — LOW Defence £55 · LOW Everything else £150 → total £1,070bn
The reasoning
  • Priorities get funded: Round 1’s answer becomes Round 3’s money — High on the three manifesto priorities is the manifesto being kept, not generosity.
  • Restraint is the hard part: defence was promised only to be “held steady” (Low), and “everything else” takes Low too — real choices with real losers.
  • The structural trap (save for the reveal): even all-Low = £410bn + £595bn = £1,005bn — already above the £1,000bn forecast. A deficit was inevitable the moment Cole ruled out tax rises; teams only controlled how big.
The range (useful when scoring Round 4)

All-Low £1,005bn · Cole’s Budget £1,070bn · All-High £1,090bn. Fixed lines £410bn in every case.

Scoring — max 10
  • 2 pts per Round-1 priority set to High (Health, Education, Green) — max 6
  • +1 pt per non-priority (Defence, Everything else) at Low or Medium — max 2
  • +2 pts for justification linked to manifesto evidence
  • Do not score affordability here — Round 4 exists to punish it properly
Watch for: gleeful all-High teams. Say nothing. They score a respectable 8 here — and walk smiling into Round 4, where £1,090bn = a 3.6% deficit and a broken rule. Letting consequences arrive a round late is the best lesson in the game.

The Reckoning — Answers

MAX 20 PTS · 5 MIN
The hidden rule: “borrowing will never exceed 3p in every pound this economy produces” = deficit ≤ 3% of GDP = £75bn
Worked examples for the board
  • All-High: £1,090bn → deficit £90bn = 3.6% × — every promise kept, rule broken
  • Cole’s Budget: £1,070bn → deficit £70bn = 2.8% ✓ — priorities funded and inside the rule, but only via Low settlements elsewhere
  • All-Low: £1,005bn → deficit £5bn = 0.2% ✓ on the rule — and a betrayal of every priority
The reveal, staged

Ask first: “did anyone budget to a target? What target?” Then quote the manifesto line back — “borrowing will never exceed 3p in every pound this economy produces” — and do the conversion aloud: 3p in every pound = 3%, of GDP £2,500bn, = £75bn. Teams who used the one-revision allowance to recalculate into the zone should be celebrated by name: that’s literally what Treasury officials do.

Scoring — max 20 (console auto-scores from each team’s total spending)
  • Base: rule met (≤3%) → 12 · narrowly broken (≤3.4%) → 6 · worse → 3
  • + up to 8 for written working: total correct (2) · deficit correct (3) · deficit-to-GDP correct (3)
  • +1 bonus pt if the team explicitly quotes the “3p in every pound” line in their written proof — rewards the manifesto-reading the whole game has been building
Watch for: the team that hits 0.2% and claims victory. Their rule-keeping is real — but point at their Round 3 score: austerity kept the rule by breaking the manifesto. The narrow sweet spot (priorities High, restraint elsewhere, ≤£1,075bn) is the whole lesson: fiscal rules and manifestos pull against each other, and governments live in the gap.

The Gilt Market’s Verdict — Answers

MAX 15 PTS · 6 MIN
B — the market’s trust is the cheapest thing the government owns
Worked numbers (Cole’s £70bn Budget — adjust for each team’s deficit)
  • New national debt = £2,400bn + £70bn = £2,470bn
  • Debt-to-GDP = 2,470 ÷ 2,500 = 98.8%
  • Annual interest on the new borrowing = £70bn × 4% = £2.8bn every year — roughly 60 new secondary schools a year, spent on nothing new
  • Rule-breakers pay 5%: the same £70bn costs £3.5bn — £700m a year extra, purely for lost credibility
Why not A or C?
  • A: some investment genuinely pays for itself — “always” is the word doing criminal work. Each £1bn adds permanent interest, and markets watch the trajectory.
  • C: off-the-books borrowing is how sovereign debt crises start — markets always find it, and the punishment arrives all at once.
  • The anchor: the 2022 UK mini-budget announced large unfunded borrowing; gilt yields surged within days, pension funds wobbled, and the Chancellor was gone within weeks.
Scoring — max 15 (use each team’s OWN deficit in the calcs)
  • New debt correct (2) · debt-to-GDP correct (2) · annual interest at the right yield (3)
  • 4 pts for B · up to 4 pts justification: compounding interest, credibility/yields, or crowding out
  • A with strong reasoning: max 6 total · C: max 3
Watch for: rule-breaking teams “forgetting” to use 5%. Hold the line — the differentiated yield is the round’s whole point. And give A-choosers their due before correcting them: the growth round in What’s Next shows investment can outrun its interest — the argument is about “always,” not “ever.”

Epilogue — Answers

★ BONUS MAX 5
Debt-to-GDP one year on = £2,470bn ÷ £2,550bn = 96.9% — up just 0.9 points despite £70bn of borrowing
The on-screen figures (📈 What’s Next tab)
  • National debt now: £2,470bn · GDP now: £2,550bn (grew 2%, helped by the multiplier from the infrastructure spending)
  • Ratio = 2,470 ÷ 2,550 = 96.9%, vs 96.0% a year ago → +0.9 percentage points
Why the ratio, not the £ amount (any two for the bonus)
  1. Ability to pay: £2.4tn of debt means nothing without the £2.5tn the economy produces every year — a mortgage is judged against a salary.
  2. Growth shrinks the burden without repayment: Britain worked off post-war debt of over 250% of GDP without “paying it back” — the economy outgrew it.
  3. It’s what lenders price: gilt investors compare the ratio across countries and time; it drives the yield, which drives the £100bn debt-interest line from the starter.
  4. The early-warning light in reverse: if growth stalls while borrowing continues, the ratio climbs even with a “small” deficit.
Budget steps in order (workbook p.9 ordering task)

1 The manifesto sets the promises → 2 The independent revenue forecast (OBR) → 3 The spending review → 4 The fiscal rules check → 5 The Chancellor’s speech and the red box → 6 Parliament votes (the Finance Act) → 7 The gilt auctions borrow the deficit → 8 The year unfolds and the next forecast begins.

Map each back to the round where teams lived it (1→R1, 2→R2, 3→R3, 4→R4, 7→Budget Day, 8→R5 & epilogue).

★ Bonus scoring — max 5
  • 2 pts ratio correct (96.9%) · 1 pt the +0.9-point comparison · up to 2 pts for two distinct “why the ratio” reasons
The closing question: “The government borrowed £70bn and the burden barely moved. So is the real fiscal weapon the Budget… or growth?” The honest answer is that they feed each other — and that tension runs through the whole A-Level macro course.

Key Terms — Model Definitions

TEACHER COPY

AQA-appropriate language throughout. Students should have filled these in during the game; these are the model answers for comparison.

Budget deficit

Government spending exceeds tax revenue in a given year. Expressed as a % of GDP to allow meaningful comparison across years and countries.

E.g. spending £1,070bn, revenue £1,000bn → deficit £70bn = 2.8% of GDP.

National debt

The total stock of outstanding government borrowing accumulated over all years. Grows when there is a deficit each year; falls (as a ratio) when GDP grows faster than the debt stock.

Fiscal rule

A self-imposed constraint on government borrowing or debt, typically expressed as a % of GDP. Designed to signal credibility to financial markets and prevent unsustainable borrowing.

E.g. Cole’s rule: deficit ≤ 3% of GDP (“3p in every pound”).

GDP (Gross Domestic Product)

The total value of all goods and services produced in an economy in a year. The denominator in all fiscal ratios — it measures the economy’s ability to bear its debts.

Direct tax

A tax levied directly on income, profits or wealth. Paid by the person or firm on whom it falls. Examples: income tax, National Insurance, corporation tax.

Indirect tax

A tax levied on expenditure, collected by the seller and passed to the government. The burden may be shifted to the consumer. Examples: VAT, fuel duty, alcohol duty.

Mixed economy

An economic system combining private markets and government intervention. Most real economies are mixed — the debate is about where on the spectrum, not whether to have a state.

Fiscal policy

Government use of taxation and public spending to influence macroeconomic objectives (growth, employment, price stability). The Budget is its primary instrument.

Gilt (government bond)

A tradeable IOU issued by the UK government to raise money. The government promises to pay a fixed coupon and return the principal at maturity. Mostly held by pension funds and insurers.

Gilt yield

The effective interest rate on a gilt, set by the market price at which investors trade it. Falls when confidence in the government rises; rises when it falls. The market’s verdict on fiscal credibility.

OBR (Office for Budget Responsibility)

The UK’s independent fiscal watchdog, which produces the official revenue and growth forecasts used in each Budget. Independence prevents Chancellors from marking their own homework.

Crowding out

When government borrowing raises interest rates, reducing private-sector investment as firms face higher borrowing costs. One mechanism by which a large deficit can slow long-run growth.
