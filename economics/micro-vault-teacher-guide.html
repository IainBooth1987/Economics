  4. The slides are deliberately light — instructions and timing only, not the questions themselves (those are all in the workbook).

Round 4 answers — Market Failure Policy Decisions

Warm-up matching key: Externality → C (cost/benefit to a third party) · Merit good → A (under-consumed, e.g. vaccination/education) · Demerit good → B (over-consumed, e.g. tobacco/sugary drinks) · Public good → D (non-excludable/non-rivalrous, under-provided by the market) · Information failure → E (decisions made without full/accurate information) · Moral hazard → F (greater risk-taking once protected from consequences) · Tragedy of the commons → G (shared resource overused because no one bears the full cost) · Free rider problem → H (benefiting without paying, due to non-excludability).

This warm-up now feeds part of Vault Key 4 below — it's not just vocabulary pre-loading.

Scenario 1 — correct answer: D (Tradable pollution permits). A small number of identifiable large polluters makes permits practical to allocate and monitor. The clue about wanting a guaranteed cap on total pollution is the key distinction: a tax only sets a price signal and lets the quantity of pollution emerge, whereas tradable permits fix the total quantity directly and let the market set the price. That's the classic taxes-vs-permits distinction examiners look for.

Scenario 2 — correct answer: F (Information campaign / nudge). The vaccine is already free, so a subsidy (B) or state provision (E) changes nothing — cost was never the barrier. The barrier is misinformation, so the correct tool is directly addressing the information failure.

Scenario 3 — correct answer: A (Indirect tax). A tiered tax based on sugar content (like the real UK Soft Drinks Industry Levy from Round 1) gives producers a direct financial incentive to reformulate — a flat ban (C) doesn't achieve that and is flagged as impractical to enforce, and a subsidy or information campaign wouldn't address the "producers should change their recipes" requirement as directly as a tax tied to sugar content.

Vault Key 4 (4 characters)

• Matching — "Tragedy of the commons" is matched to option G → G

• Scenario 1 → D, Scenario 2 → F, Scenario 3 → A

Key 4 = GDFA

The matching warm-up is now required alongside all three scenarios — a team that skipped straight to the scenarios (as in earlier versions of this resource) can no longer get the full key.

Running it as a non-specialist / cover teacher

  • You don't need to understand the economics — every answer and its reasoning are above.
  • A "hint" just means reading a team the relevant clue sentence (Round 4) or pointing at the correct row in the data table (Round 3) — log 60 seconds against their time, doesn't need to be precise.
  • Round 4 is the trickiest to mark cold: the "why" column above tells you exactly which clue rules out which wrong answers, so you can defend the mark scheme if a team pushes back.
  • If a team's vault key doesn't match, tell them "not quite — check your order/working again" and move to another team rather than debugging their maths live.
