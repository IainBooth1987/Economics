What you'll learn
The Marketing Mix — The 4 Ps
Every business that sells a product has to make four big decisions. Together these are called the Marketing Mix — or the 4 Ps.

🎒 Product — What are you selling? What makes it special?
💲 Price — How much will you charge? Why that amount?
🏪 Place — Where can customers actually buy it?
📣 Promotion — How will people find out it exists?

Get all four right and your business can succeed. Get one wrong and it can sink even a great idea.

Key Idea
What is a Market Map?
A market map (also called a perceptual map) shows where different products sit in a market. We use two axes — in this case Price and Style. Products that are similar cluster together. A gap in the market is an empty space where customers might want a product but nobody is selling one yet.
🎒 Real world: Before launching, Herschel Supply Co. spotted a gap for stylish-but-affordable bags between fashion brands and plain school rucksacks. That gap made them a global brand.

Key Idea
The Product P — Adding Value
Adding value means making a product worth more than its raw materials. A bag made from £8 of fabric and zips can sell for £95 if the brand, design, and quality make customers feel it's worth it. That difference — between cost and selling price — is where profit comes from.
👟 Real world: Nike trainers cost roughly £25 to make. They sell for £120+. The extra value comes from brand, design, and what wearing Nike means to people.

Key Idea
Demand — Why Price Matters So Much
When a price goes up, most customers buy less. When it goes down, they buy more. This is called demand. The chart below shows this relationship — the higher the bar, the more bags you'd sell at that price.

The catch: selling more bags at a low price doesn't always mean more profit. You need to find the sweet spot between volume and profit per bag.
☕ Real world: When Greggs raised its sausage roll price from £1 to £1.15, they sold slightly fewer — but made more money overall because the margin per roll increased.

Key Idea
The Place P — Channels of Distribution
A distribution channel is the route your product takes from you to the customer. There are two main types:

🏪 Indirect — you sell to a retailer (like Tesco or JD Sports), who sells to the customer. The retailer takes a cut of the price — called their margin.

💻 Direct — you sell straight to the customer (online store, market stall). You keep all the profit, but you're responsible for everything.
👟 Real world: Nike sells through JD Sports (indirect) AND through nike.com (direct). Direct sales are more profitable per pair — which is why brands are pushing DTC (Direct-To-Consumer) harder every year.

📣 Phase 5: Spread the Word
You have £10,000 left for promotion. How will customers find out about PACK? Spend wisely — different channels reach very different people.
💡
Key Idea
The Promotion P — Reaching Your Target Market
Your target market is the group of people most likely to buy your product. Promotion only works if it reaches them — not just anyone.

There are two types of promotion:
📺 Above the Line (ATL) — mass media like TV, radio, billboards. Reaches everyone but expensive.
📱 Below the Line (BTL) — targeted activity like social media, school events, leaflets. Cheaper and more focused.
🎒 Real world: A bag brand targeting 11–16 year olds will spend most of its budget on TikTok and Instagram — not newspaper ads. The platform has to match the audience.

Before You Launch
Why businesses face unexpected events
Even the best marketing plan meets the real world. Competitors react. Trends change. A social media post can make or break a launch overnight. Real businesses have to be flexible — adapting their marketing mix when things don't go to plan.

As you play through each term, think about which P you're adjusting and why.
