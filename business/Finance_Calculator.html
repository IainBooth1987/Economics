Contribution and Breakeven

Contribution per Unit

Contribution is what each unit sold contributes towards covering fixed costs — and then to profit once fixed costs are paid.

Contribution per Unit = Selling Price - Variable Cost per Unit
ExampleSelling price £10, variable cost £4. Contribution = £10 - £4 = £6 per unit. Each unit sold contributes £6 towards fixed costs of, say, £3,000. Once 500 units are sold, fixed costs are covered and the business breaks even.

Breakeven Output

The breakeven point is the level of output at which total revenue exactly equals total costs — no profit, no loss.

Breakeven Output = Fixed Costs / Contribution per Unit
ExampleFixed costs = £3,000. Contribution = £6. Breakeven = £3,000 / £6 = 500 units. Always round up — you cannot sell a fraction of a unit.

Margin of Safety

The margin of safety shows how far sales can fall before the business tips into a loss.

Margin of Safety = Actual Output - Breakeven Output

Can also be expressed as a percentage: (Margin of Safety / Actual Output) x 100

ExampleActual sales = 700 units. Breakeven = 500 units. Margin of Safety = 200 units, or (200 / 700) x 100 = 28.6%.
Uses and Limitations
Uses of breakeven analysisHelps assess financial viability of a new product; satisfies lenders who require a breakeven forecast; sets minimum sales targets; makes the impact of price changes immediately visible.
Assumes all output is sold — unsold inventory means actual revenue falls short of the projected total revenue line.
Assumes a single, constant selling price — real businesses offer discounts, promotions, and tiered pricing.
Fixed costs may not stay fixed — rent rises, insurance changes, and loan rates move over time.
Static snapshot — cannot capture changing market conditions, competitor actions, or cost inflation.
Based entirely on forecast data — if estimates are wrong, the breakeven point is wrong.
Only suitable for single-product businesses — multiple product lines require more sophisticated analysis.
Evaluation tipDespite its limitations, breakeven analysis remains useful because of its simplicity and clarity. Strong answers treat it as a planning tool and starting point rather than an absolute truth, and acknowledge that sensitivity analysis (changing key variables) improves robustness.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception: "The breakeven point is the sales target"

Students often treat breakeven as the goal — as if reaching it means the business is doing well.

Reality: Breakeven is the minimum acceptable outcome — the point at which the business avoids a loss. The real goal is to operate well above breakeven, generating profit. A business that consistently only just breaks even is financially fragile.

Misconception: "A lower breakeven point is always better"

Students assume minimising the breakeven output is the key objective.

Reality: A low breakeven point might reflect very low fixed costs — which could mean the business is under-investing in capacity, marketing, or staff. Context matters. A higher breakeven arising from investment in growth infrastructure may be entirely rational.

Misconception: "Contribution is the same as profit"

Contribution and profit are frequently conflated in student answers.

Reality: Contribution = Revenue - Variable Costs. Profit = Revenue - ALL Costs (fixed and variable). Contribution is what remains to cover fixed costs. Only once fixed costs are fully covered does additional contribution become profit.

Misconception: "A large margin of safety guarantees the business is safe"

Students treat a high margin of safety as conclusive evidence of financial health.

Reality: A large margin of safety tells you about distance from the loss zone — not about overall financial health, cash flow, return on investment, or long-term sustainability. A business could have a comfortable margin of safety but still face a cash flow crisis or be generating poor returns on capital.
Profit Types and Margins

Gross Profit

Gross profit measures trading performance — revenue minus the direct costs of producing the goods or services sold.

Gross Profit = Revenue - Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
Gross Profit Margin (%) = (Gross Profit / Revenue) x 100
ExampleRevenue = £200,000. COGS = £120,000. Gross Profit = £80,000. GPM = (80,000 / 200,000) x 100 = 40%.

Net Profit

Net profit deducts all operating expenses from gross profit — including fixed overheads such as rent, salaries, and utilities. It reflects the true bottom-line performance.

Net Profit = Gross Profit - Operating Expenses
Net Profit Margin (%) = (Net Profit / Revenue) x 100
ExampleGross Profit = £80,000. Operating expenses = £50,000. Net Profit = £30,000. NPM = (30,000 / 200,000) x 100 = 15%.

Interpreting Profit Margins

  • High GPM, low NPM: The business trades well but has high overheads — suggests scope to reduce fixed costs.
  • Falling NPM: Overheads rising faster than revenue, or pricing under pressure — requires investigation.
  • Industry context: A 5% NPM is excellent in supermarkets; poor in luxury goods. Always compare against sector benchmarks.
Total Contribution and Profit

Total Contribution

Total Contribution = Contribution per Unit x Units Sold
Profit = Total Contribution - Fixed Costs
ExampleContribution per unit = £6. Units sold = 800. Total Contribution = £4,800. Fixed Costs = £3,000. Profit = £4,800 - £3,000 = £1,800.
Why use contribution rather than profit per unit?Profit per unit changes as output changes (because fixed costs are spread across more units). Contribution per unit stays constant regardless of output, making it more useful for pricing and output decisions.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception: "Profit and cash flow are the same thing"

Students routinely conflate profitability with having cash available.

Reality: A business can be profitable on paper but have a cash flow crisis. Profit is accounting-based and includes non-cash items (depreciation, accruals). Cash flow is actual money received and paid. A business selling on credit may show high profit but have no cash to pay its suppliers.

Misconception: "A higher profit margin is always better"

Students often rank businesses purely by profit margin without considering the bigger picture.

Reality: A low-margin, high-volume business (e.g. a supermarket at 3-5% NPM) can be more profitable in absolute terms than a high-margin, low-volume one. Margin must be considered alongside volume, return on investment, and the business model.

Misconception: "Revenue increase always means profit increase"

Students assume that more sales automatically means more profit.

Reality: If variable costs rise proportionally with sales, profit only increases by the contribution per unit sold. If the revenue increase comes from cutting price (thereby reducing contribution), profit could actually fall even as revenue rises.
Budgeting and Variance Analysis

What is a budget?

A budget is a financial plan that estimates income and expenditure for a specific period. It is a management tool used to plan, coordinate, and control business activities. Variance analysis compares actual results against budgeted figures to identify differences and understand their causes.

Calculating Variances

Variance = Actual Result - Budgeted Result
  • Sales variance: Positive = Favourable (more sales than planned). Negative = Adverse.
  • Cost variance: Positive = Adverse (spent more than planned). Negative = Favourable.
  • Profit variance: Positive = Favourable. Negative = Adverse.
Note the reversal for costsFor revenue, higher than budget is good. For costs, lower than budget is good. Students frequently apply the same interpretation to both and lose marks.
Benefits and Limitations of Budgeting

Benefits

  • Planning and coordination: Forces structured thinking about future costs and revenues
  • Performance measurement: Provides benchmarks for variance analysis
  • Control mechanism: Sets spending limits and identifies overspend
  • Motivation: Clear targets can motivate if set realistically
  • Resource allocation: Directs spending to strategic priorities

Limitations

  • Inflexibility: Budgets can become outdated in fast-changing markets
  • Demotivation: Unrealistic targets can demoralise staff
  • Gaming: Managers may manipulate figures to hit targets
  • Short-term focus: Encourages deferral of essential investment
  • Assumption-based: Wrong forecasts make the whole budget unreliable

Common Misconceptions

Misconception: "A favourable variance is always good news"

Students assume any favourable result should be celebrated.

Reality: A favourable cost variance (spending less than budgeted) could mean maintenance has been deferred, staff training cut, or quality compromised. Equally, a favourable sales variance might result from a competitor leaving the market — a temporary advantage. All variances require investigation, not just adverse ones.

Misconception: "The cost variance formula works the same way as the sales variance"

Students apply the same favourable/adverse logic to costs as they do to sales.

Reality: For sales: Actual > Budget = Favourable. For costs: Actual > Budget = Adverse (spent more than planned). For costs: Actual < Budget = Favourable (spent less than planned). The direction reverses because higher revenue is good, but higher costs are bad.

Misconception: "Budgets are always accurate if prepared carefully"

Students sometimes treat a well-prepared budget as reliable.

Reality: Budgets are forecasts based on assumptions about future conditions. Even carefully prepared budgets can be invalidated by external shocks (inflation, competition, economic change) that were impossible to predict. The value of a budget is in the planning process it forces, not in the accuracy of its figures.
Cash Flow Fundamentals

Key Formulas

Net Cash Flow = Total Cash Inflows - Total Cash Outflows
Closing Balance = Opening Balance + Net Cash Flow
ExampleOpening balance = £10,000. Inflows = £18,000. Outflows = £14,500. Net Cash Flow = £3,500. Closing Balance = £10,000 + £3,500 = £13,500.

Cash Flow vs Profit

  • Cash flow — actual money received and paid; shows liquidity; ability to pay bills right now
  • Profit — revenue minus expenses (including non-cash items like depreciation and credit sales); shows accounting performance
A business can be profitable but cash-flow negativeIf customers pay on 90-day credit terms but suppliers demand payment in 30 days, the business may run out of cash even while profitable. This is why cash flow forecasting is critical — especially for growing businesses.

Causes of Cash Flow Problems

  • Overtrading: Growing faster than cash inflows can support
  • Poor credit control: Customers paying late or defaulting
  • Seasonal demand: Revenue concentrated in certain periods while costs are constant
  • Unexpected costs: Equipment failure, emergency repairs, tax bills
  • Low profit margins: Insufficient margin to build cash reserves

Solutions to Cash Flow Problems

  • Overdraft / short-term borrowing: Provides immediate liquidity but incurs interest
  • Invoice factoring: Selling outstanding invoices to a third party for immediate cash
  • Sale of assets: Releasing cash tied up in non-essential fixed assets
  • Tighter credit control: Reducing debtor days; offering early payment discounts
  • Negotiating better supplier terms: Extending creditor days to preserve cash
  • Cutting discretionary spending: Deferring non-essential expenditure

Common Misconceptions

Misconception: "A negative net cash flow means the business is making a loss"

Students conflate negative cash flow with losses.

Reality: Net cash flow is about money movement in a period, not profitability. A business investing heavily in new equipment may have negative net cash flow while remaining highly profitable. Net cash flow and profit are distinct measures.

Misconception: "A positive closing balance means the business is financially healthy"

Students treat a positive closing balance as a sign of health.

Reality: A positive closing balance only tells you the business currently has cash. It says nothing about whether it is profitable, growing, servicing its debts, or generating returns for investors. A business could be burning through reserves with a declining balance each month.

Misconception: "Cash flow problems only affect struggling businesses"

Students associate cash flow issues exclusively with failing firms.

Reality: Rapidly growing businesses are among the most vulnerable to cash flow problems — a phenomenon called overtrading. High growth requires cash for stock, staff, and premises before revenue arrives. Many successful businesses have failed not from lack of profit but from lack of cash at critical moments.
