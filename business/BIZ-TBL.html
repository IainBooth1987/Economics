Triple Bottom Line

People, Planet, Profit — theory, strategic context, UK examples, framework comparison, quiz, and misconceptions

What is the Triple Bottom Line?

The Triple Bottom Line (TBL) is a business performance framework developed by John Elkington in 1994. Rather than measuring success solely through financial profit, TBL proposes that businesses should account for their performance across three interconnected dimensions: People (social impact), Planet (environmental impact), and Profit (economic performance). The framework challenges the traditional view that businesses exist purely to maximise shareholder returns.

Elkington's 2018 "Recall"In a Harvard Business Review article, Elkington himself called for a "recall" of the TBL concept — arguing it had been adopted as a box-ticking accounting exercise rather than a genuine challenge to shareholder primacy. He wrote that TBL was intended as a provocation to transform capitalism, not as a reporting tool. This is a powerful evaluation point: the framework's most significant weakness may be not its concept but its widespread misuse.
The Three Pillars
👥

People

The social pillar measures a business's impact on all human stakeholders — employees, customers, suppliers, communities, and wider society. It asks: is the business treating people fairly and contributing positively to human wellbeing?

Key Considerations
  • Fair wages and safe working conditions
  • Diversity, equity, and inclusion
  • Employee training and development
  • Ethical supply chain management
  • Community investment and engagement
  • Customer health and product safety
  • Human rights across operations
Example Metrics
  • Gender pay gap and diversity ratios
  • Staff turnover and satisfaction scores
  • Workplace accident rates
  • Community investment as % of profit
  • Supplier audit compliance rate
🌎

Planet

The environmental pillar assesses how the business affects the natural world — resource use, emissions, waste, and ecological damage. It asks: is the business operating within planetary boundaries and actively reducing its environmental footprint?

Key Considerations
  • Carbon emissions and climate impact
  • Energy efficiency and renewable use
  • Water consumption and conservation
  • Waste reduction and circular economy
  • Sustainable sourcing of materials
  • Biodiversity and land use impacts
  • Product lifecycle environmental impact
Example Metrics
  • Carbon footprint (tonnes CO₂e)
  • % renewable energy used
  • Water usage per unit of output
  • Waste to landfill (tonnes)
  • % sustainably sourced materials
💰

Profit

The economic pillar measures traditional financial performance — but in the context of long-term viability rather than just quarterly returns. It includes not only shareholder returns but the broader economic value the business creates for society.

Key Considerations
  • Revenue growth and profitability
  • Long-term financial sustainability
  • Economic value distributed to stakeholders
  • Job creation and local economic impact
  • Tax contributions to public finances
  • Innovation investment for future growth
  • Supply chain economic multiplier effects
Example Metrics
  • Revenue, gross and net profit margins
  • ROCE and return on investment
  • Jobs created or supported
  • Tax paid vs tax avoided
  • Investment in R&D as % of revenue
The intersections are as important as the pillarsTBL's power lies in how the three pillars interact. Where People and Planet intersect: environmental justice (who bears the cost of pollution?). Where Planet and Profit intersect: the green economy (can environmental investment reduce costs?). Where all three overlap: sustainable competitive advantage — businesses that genuinely integrate all three are building resilience that competitors find difficult to replicate.
Why Businesses Adopt TBL

🏆 Competitive Differentiation

Genuine TBL commitment creates differentiated positioning. Conscious consumers, ESG investors, and purpose-driven employees increasingly prefer businesses with strong TBL credentials — creating pricing power, lower cost of capital, and talent retention advantages that compound over time.

⚠️ Risk Mitigation

Proactive environmental and social management reduces exposure to regulatory changes, reputational crises, supply chain disruptions, and climate-related risks. Businesses that act ahead of regulation face lower compliance costs and avoid crisis-driven reactive expenditure.

🚀 Innovation Driver

Sustainability constraints force creative problem-solving. Reducing packaging waste, eliminating carbon, or improving supply chain conditions generates process innovations that also reduce costs. The circular economy offers genuine profit opportunities within Planet constraints.

🌏 Long-term Resilience

Businesses with strong stakeholder relationships — built through TBL commitment — prove more resilient during economic downturns and crises. The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated that companies with strong employee and community relationships recovered faster and retained customer trust.

✎ Evaluation & Exam Angle

The strongest evaluation distinguishes between businesses that genuinely embed TBL into strategy (Patagonia, Interface, The Co-operative Group) and those that use TBL rhetoric as marketing while maintaining shareholder primacy in practice — what critics call "greenwashing" or "people-washing." Elkington's 2018 recall suggests this distinction is the central challenge for TBL in practice.

Limitations of the TBL Framework

1. Measurement and Comparability Problems

Unlike financial profit, social and environmental performance cannot be expressed in a single comparable unit. How do you weigh 50 tonnes of carbon saved against 200 jobs created? Without standardised metrics, TBL reports are difficult to compare across businesses or verify independently.

Counter-argument: ESG reporting frameworks (GRI, SASB, TCFD) are increasingly standardising TBL measurement. B Corp certification provides third-party verification. The absence of perfect metrics does not invalidate the framework — it identifies an area for ongoing development.

2. Trade-off Management Without a Decision Framework

When the three pillars conflict — as they regularly do — TBL provides no mechanism for deciding which should take priority. A business that could create 500 jobs by building a factory in an environmentally sensitive area faces a genuine Planet/People tension that TBL identifies but cannot resolve.

Counter-argument: Providing a decision framework is not TBL's purpose. It is a diagnostic tool that identifies where tensions exist, not a prescriptive guide. This limitation applies equally to most strategic frameworks.

3. Open to Selective Reporting and Greenwashing

Without mandatory standards, businesses can cherry-pick favourable TBL metrics while suppressing unfavourable ones. A company might publish detailed community investment data while concealing supply chain labour violations — technically "doing TBL" while deceiving stakeholders.

Counter-argument: Mandatory ESG disclosure requirements, introduced in the UK for large companies from 2022, and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive are reducing this loophole. Selective reporting is a governance failure, not a conceptual flaw in TBL itself.

4. Elkington's Own 2018 "Recall"

The framework's creator publicly called for its recall, arguing TBL had been "captured" by mainstream business — used to produce sustainability reports that satisfied investors and PR needs without fundamentally challenging the primacy of financial returns. He wrote that TBL was intended to "change the way we think and talk about the role of business in society," not to produce accounting reports.

Counter-argument: The misuse of a framework does not invalidate the framework. Many businesses do use TBL to drive genuine strategic change. Elkington's recall is better read as a call for higher-quality application than as an abandonment of the concept.
✎ Exam Evaluation Tip

The best A-level answers treat TBL's limitations not as reasons to reject the framework but as reasons to apply it critically. A business that uses TBL as a genuine strategic lens — accepting the measurement challenges, being transparent about trade-offs, and pursuing all three pillars simultaneously — is using the framework as intended. The question is always: does this business treat TBL as strategy or as marketing?

People Pillar — UK Examples
People — Employees
John Lewis Partnership

JLP's model gives employees ("Partners") a share of annual profits and voting rights in governance. All employees — including part-time retail staff — receive the same access to company facilities, discounts, and benefits. The model embeds People pillar thinking into legal structure rather than treating it as a discretionary add-on.

Evaluation: JLP's TBL People performance is genuine because it is constitutionally entrenched — it cannot be quietly removed when profits fall. This structural commitment differentiates it from firms that offer generous benefits only when commercially convenient.
People — Supply Chain
Marks & Spencer — Plan A

M&S's Plan A programme includes rigorous ethical trading standards, mandatory supplier audits, living wage commitments across its UK supply chain, and an active programme to support smallholder farmers in its global sourcing. By 2022, over 80% of M&S food products sourced from its top 25 supplier countries met enhanced ethical trading standards.

Evaluation: M&S's supply chain People work is more credible than many competitors because it is independently audited and publicly reported with specific metrics. However, critics note that ethical sourcing standards still do not fully eliminate exploitation in complex global supply chains.
People — Community
Greggs

The Greggs Foundation distributes millions annually to breakfast clubs in deprived UK communities, hospice care, and local regeneration projects. Over 800 primary schools receive free breakfasts via Greggs-funded clubs. The company also pioneered a "pay it forward" scheme allowing customers to pre-purchase items for those in need.

Evaluation: Greggs' community investment is genuine and substantive — but it sits at Carroll's philanthropic level (desired, discretionary). The stronger TBL evaluation asks about Greggs' labour practices, supplier relationships, and wage levels: do these satisfy the ethical and legal layers that sit beneath the philanthropic activity?
Planet Pillar — UK Examples
Planet — Carbon Reduction
Unilever UK

Unilever committed to net-zero emissions across its value chain by 2039, and halved its carbon footprint per consumer use compared to 2010 levels by 2021. In the UK, all manufacturing sites run on 100% renewable electricity. Unilever's "Climate Transition Action Plan" is independently reviewed and ties senior executive pay to sustainability targets.

Evaluation: Linking executive pay to environmental targets is a structural commitment that makes TBL more than rhetoric. However, "value chain" carbon targets (Scope 3 emissions) are the hardest to verify and control — and Unilever has faced criticism for slow progress on reducing plastic packaging despite high-profile commitments.
Planet — Circular Economy
Tesco

Tesco committed to halve food waste by 2025 and now publishes detailed food waste data across its UK operations — becoming the first major UK retailer to do so transparently. Its Redistribution programme donated over 15,000 tonnes of food to community groups and charities in 2022. Tesco also operates "wonky veg" ranges and surplus food partnerships with community organisations.

Evaluation: Tesco's transparency on food waste data is a strong indicator of genuine TBL Planet commitment — publishing data even when it reveals poor performance. The circular economy initiatives also demonstrate the People-Planet intersection: reducing food waste both protects the environment and addresses food insecurity.
Planet — Failing
UK Water Companies

Between 2020 and 2023, English water companies discharged raw sewage into rivers on over 300,000 occasions, despite charging customers rising bills. Thames Water, Southern Water, and others faced regulatory fines while simultaneously paying dividends to shareholders. This represents a clear Planet pillar failure where profit extraction took precedence over environmental stewardship.

Evaluation: The water companies case illustrates TBL's most important point — Profit and Planet are not always compatible under short-term shareholder pressure. The regulatory framework (Ofwat) was inadequate to enforce TBL balance, and the consequence was both environmental damage and public trust collapse. TBL works best when embedded in governance, not dependent on regulation alone.
Profit Pillar & Integrated TBL — UK Examples
Profit — Long-term vs Short-term
Cadbury / Mondelez

When Cadbury was acquired by Kraft (now Mondelez) in 2010, the acquirer quickly closed a Somerdale factory it had promised to keep open and shifted focus to short-term margin improvement. The Cadbury brand's People and Planet commitments (Fairtrade cocoa, UK employment) were substantially weakened. This illustrates how ownership change can collapse TBL when new owners prioritise the Profit pillar above the other two.

Evaluation: The Cadbury case is a powerful argument for embedding TBL in legal structure rather than relying on management goodwill. B Corp status or John Lewis-style ownership models protect People and Planet commitments from being eroded by short-term shareholder pressure.
All Three Pillars — Integrated
Patagonia UK

Patagonia donates 1% of sales to environmental causes, uses recycled and organic materials, offers lifetime product repairs (reducing consumption), pays living wages, and maintains radical supply chain transparency. In 2022, founder Yvon Chouinard transferred ownership to a climate trust — ensuring profits fund environmental causes in perpetuity. This is arguably the most complete example of genuine TBL integration.

Evaluation: Patagonia's model is exceptional precisely because TBL is embedded in ownership structure, not just strategy. This makes it genuinely different from companies that produce impressive sustainability reports while maintaining shareholder primacy. The limitation is that Patagonia's model requires private ownership — it could not survive as a listed PLC answerable to quarterly earnings expectations.
TBL Washing — Cautionary Case
BP — "Beyond Petroleum"

BP rebranded as "Beyond Petroleum" in 2000, investing heavily in sustainability marketing. However, the Deepwater Horizon disaster (2010) and continued massive investment in oil and gas exploration revealed the rebranding as largely cosmetic. BP's Profit pillar consistently dominated strategic decision-making despite high-profile TBL communications.

Evaluation: BP is the paradigmatic case of TBL washing. The lesson for evaluation is that TBL credibility requires evidence of genuine trade-offs accepted in pursuit of Planet and People goals — not marketing investment. When Profit always wins in practice, TBL is performative rather than strategic.
TBL and Competitive Advantage

How TBL Builds Core Competences

Core competences are the unique capabilities that give a business sustainable competitive advantage — they must be valuable, rare, difficult to imitate, and non-substitutable (Barney's VRIN framework, 1991). When TBL is genuinely embedded rather than performative, it can create exactly this kind of hard-to-copy advantage.

Patagonia example: Patagonia's competitive advantage is not just its outdoor clothing — it is the combination of technical excellence, verified supply chain ethics, authentic environmental activism, and ownership structure that directs profits to environmental causes. No competitor can simply copy this because it is embedded in decades of consistent action, culture, and governance. This is TBL as a genuine core competence.

TBL Creates Differentiation

Businesses with credible TBL credentials can command price premiums from values-aligned consumers, attract ESG-focused investors at lower cost of capital, and retain talent who want meaningful work. These advantages are mutually reinforcing — strong People performance attracts good employees, who deliver better Planet performance, which strengthens brand positioning, which supports Profit.

TBL Reduces Long-run Risk

Proactive TBL management mitigates regulatory risk (acting ahead of mandatory requirements), reputational risk (reducing crisis exposure), climate risk (reducing physical asset exposure), and supply chain risk (building ethical supplier relationships that prove resilient under pressure). These risk reductions translate into more stable long-term financial performance.

✎ Evaluation Angle

The tension between TBL and competitive advantage is real: genuinely sustainable products often cost more to produce, reducing competitiveness on price. The strategic argument for TBL is that this price premium is recoverable through brand loyalty and perceived quality — but this only holds in markets where customers are sufficiently informed and affluent to pay it. In commodity markets and low-income consumer segments, TBL investment may not be commercially viable without external incentives.

The central tensionTBL investments often impose short-term Profit costs in exchange for long-term People and Planet gains — and eventually, long-term Profit gains. Managing this tension is the primary challenge for business leaders seeking to implement TBL genuinely.

The Trade-off in Practice — UK Example

A UK manufacturer investing £3 million in solar panels and efficiency upgrades will see reduced short-term net profit — potentially disappointing investors focused on quarterly returns. However, over 15 years: energy costs fall by an estimated 40%, the business qualifies for green supply chain contracts (expanding the customer base), carbon reporting obligations are met ahead of regulatory mandates (avoiding future compliance costs), and brand positioning improves among environmentally conscious customers. TBL requires patience from investors and explicit communication of the long-term value creation rationale.

TBL and Stakeholder Theory

The Connection to Freeman's Stakeholder Theory (1984)

TBL and Stakeholder Theory are complementary frameworks. Stakeholder Theory argues that businesses should create value for all stakeholders (employees, customers, communities, suppliers, environment, and shareholders) rather than maximising returns for shareholders alone. TBL provides a practical structure for how to measure and report on this multi-stakeholder value creation.

People = employees, customers, communities, suppliers — the human stakeholders Freeman identified. Planet = the natural environment — an implicit stakeholder whose interests are increasingly recognised in law and regulation. Profit = shareholders and economic contributors — the traditional focus of business strategy. TBL gives Stakeholder Theory an operational measurement framework.

A-level synthesis pointThe most sophisticated evaluation connects TBL to multiple theoretical frameworks: Elkington's TBL provides the three-pillar structure; Freeman's Stakeholder Theory explains why it matters; Carroll's CSR Pyramid shows where TBL fits within a hierarchy of obligations; Porter and Kramer's Creating Shared Value pushes TBL beyond reporting into strategic integration. A student who can navigate these connections demonstrates genuine command of the topic.
Comparing Business Performance Frameworks

TBL is one of several frameworks for assessing business performance. The table below compares the main approaches across key dimensions. Most businesses use multiple frameworks simultaneously — the question is which is most appropriate for which decision.

How TBL Relates to Other Frameworks

TBL as an umbrella: TBL incorporates elements from multiple frameworks. The Profit pillar uses financial ratios and KPIs. The People and Planet pillars align with Stakeholder Analysis. All three can be tracked through a Balanced Scorecard. In practice, TBL provides the strategic rationale while other frameworks provide the measurement tools.

When TBL is most valuable: Businesses under stakeholder pressure on ESG issues; companies seeking ESG investor access; organisations building long-term brand positioning on sustainability; businesses in high-impact industries (energy, food, fashion, transport) facing regulatory change; and any business with global supply chains where People and Planet risks are concentrated.

✎ Exam Evaluation Tip

The comparison question is almost always about context: financial ratios are not inferior to TBL — they are more appropriate for different purposes. A bank evaluating a loan application needs financial ratios; a pension fund evaluating long-term investment risk needs TBL. Strong answers avoid declaring one framework "better" and instead explain which is most appropriate for the specific decision, stakeholder, and time horizon in the question.

Common Misconceptions
The Framework Itself

Misconception: "TBL means a business should prioritise People and Planet over Profit"

Students interpret TBL as an argument for putting social and environmental concerns above financial performance.

Reality: TBL argues for simultaneous, balanced performance across all three dimensions — not a ranking or trade-off. Elkington explicitly stated that without economic viability, businesses cannot fund the People and Planet investments that make TBL meaningful. A business that sacrifices financial sustainability in pursuit of social and environmental goals will ultimately fail — and with it, its People and Planet commitments.

Misconception: "TBL is just about charitable donations and green marketing"

Students reduce TBL to philanthropy (Carroll's pyramid top level) and environmental PR rather than strategic performance measurement.

Reality: TBL is a comprehensive performance framework covering every aspect of business operations — how employees are treated throughout the supply chain, how resources are consumed in production, how profits are generated and distributed, and how all of these interact. Charitable donations are a minor component of the People pillar. A business that donates to food banks while exploiting warehouse workers is not meeting TBL standards.

Misconception: "TBL and CSR are the same thing"

Students conflate these two related but distinct frameworks.

Reality: CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility) is a broader concept describing a business's responsibilities to society. TBL is a specific measurement and reporting framework that operationalises how those responsibilities are tracked across three pillars. Carroll's CSR Pyramid is a model of what responsibilities exist (economic, legal, ethical, philanthropic). TBL is a tool for measuring performance against them. They complement rather than duplicate each other.
Applying the Framework

Misconception: "A company that publishes a sustainability report is using TBL"

Students treat TBL reporting as equivalent to TBL performance.

Reality: Publishing a sustainability report is a communication exercise. TBL performance requires genuine operational changes: reducing carbon emissions, improving supply chain conditions, paying fair wages. Elkington's 2018 recall was precisely about this distinction — many businesses adopted TBL reporting without TBL strategy. The report is not the evidence; verifiable outcomes are the evidence.

Misconception: "Financial ratios are inferior to TBL for assessing business performance"

Students conclude that because TBL is more comprehensive, it is always the better measure.

Reality: Financial ratios and TBL serve different analytical purposes for different audiences. A supplier assessing whether a business can meet contractual payment obligations needs liquidity ratios, not TBL data. An investor evaluating 20-year portfolio risk needs TBL data, not just historical profit margins. Neither framework is superior — appropriateness depends on the decision being made, the stakeholder making it, and the time horizon that matters.

Misconception: "The three TBL pillars always conflict with each other"

Students assume People, Planet, and Profit are necessarily in tension.

Reality: While tensions exist, the pillars can be mutually reinforcing. Energy efficiency reduces both carbon emissions (Planet) and energy costs (Profit). Fair wages reduce staff turnover (People) and recruitment costs (Profit). Supply chain transparency prevents labour scandals (People) and reputational crises (Profit). Porter and Kramer's "Creating Shared Value" concept is built on precisely this insight: the most powerful TBL strategies find where social and environmental value creation also creates commercial value.
Evaluation Errors

Misconception: "Elkington's 2018 recall means TBL should be rejected"

Students treat the creator's criticism as a definitive verdict against the framework.

Reality: Elkington called for a recall of how TBL was being applied — as a box-ticking accounting exercise — not of the underlying concept. He argued TBL needed to evolve from a measurement tool into a genuine driver of systemic change. His article reinforced the importance of TBL's original ambition while critiquing its diluted implementation. The recall is a powerful evaluation point about the gap between theory and practice, not evidence that the framework is conceptually flawed.

Misconception: "TBL only applies to large multinational companies"

Students assume TBL is only relevant for large, publicly-listed corporations with sustainability departments.

Reality: TBL applies to all businesses at every scale — but the way it manifests differs. A small bakery's TBL might involve: People (paying the Real Living Wage, offering flexible hours), Planet (sourcing local ingredients, minimising packaging waste), and Profit (pricing that sustains the business long-term). The principles are universal even if the metrics and reporting tools available to SMEs are simpler than those used by multinationals.
