Good Customer Service

The sale itself is only the middle of the story. What happens before and after it — advice, engagement, and support once the money has changed hands — decides whether a customer ever comes back.

Customer service sits at the meeting point of operations, marketing (reputation, loyalty) and human resources (staff training).
The final piece of Business Operations

Why good customer service matters

This is the last chapter of Topic 3.3, and it brings together threads from across the whole course. The stakeholders you met in 3.1.4 include customers directly; the quality you studied in 3.3.3 is only half the story if the service around a product falls short; and the technology from 3.2.1 has completely reshaped how customer service is actually delivered.

AQA wants you to understand the sales process as three connected stages — what happens before, during and after a sale — and to recognise that good service at any single stage can be undone by poor service at another.

Before the sale

Product knowledge and advice help a customer choose the right product with confidence.

During the sale

Customer engagement creates a positive, personal experience at the point of purchase.

After the sale

Post-sales service keeps the relationship going once the transaction is complete.

How it's actually delivered

Methods of good customer service

Product knowledge

Staff who can answer questions confidently and recommend the right product build trust and reduce the chance of a customer buying something unsuitable.

Customer engagement

A friendly greeting, active listening, and a genuinely personalised experience make customers feel valued rather than just processed.

Post-sales service

User training, helplines, servicing and warranties reassure customers that support doesn't disappear the moment they've paid.

In the real world: John Lewis has built its reputation partly on extensive staff training, with its "Partners" known for detailed product knowledge and a genuinely helpful approach on the shop floor. Apple's Genius Bar and AppleCare programme are widely recognised examples of post-sales service, offering ongoing troubleshooting, repairs and support long after a customer has bought their device.
Getting it right — and wrong

Benefits of good service, dangers of poor service

Benefits of good customer service

  • Increased customer satisfaction
  • Stronger customer loyalty and repeat purchases
  • Increased spend per customer, through trust and upselling
  • Higher profitability, since retaining existing customers is usually cheaper than winning new ones

Dangers of poor customer service

  • Dissatisfied customers who don't return
  • Damaged reputation, spread quickly through word of mouth
  • Reduced revenue as customers switch to competitors
  • Negative reviews that can be seen by thousands of future potential customers
In the real world: water companies such as Thames Water have faced widespread criticism over customer service standards in recent years, and part of the reason such complaints persist is that customers in a given area typically have no alternative supplier to switch to — a useful link back to 3.2.6's point that businesses facing little or no competition often feel less pressure to maintain high service standards, since dissatisfied customers have nowhere else to go.
Technology changing the experience

How ICT has developed customer service

The rise of digital technology covered back in 3.2.1 has transformed how businesses deliver customer service, largely for the better, though not without new challenges.

Websites

FAQs, order tracking and self-service account management let customers solve simple problems themselves, any time of day.

E-commerce

Live chat and chatbots built into online stores can answer common questions instantly, without a customer needing to phone or wait.

Social media

Customers now expect fast, public responses to queries and complaints — putting real pressure on businesses to respond well and quickly.

This cuts both ways, just as it did in 3.2.1: a quick, helpful public reply on social media can turn a frustrated customer into a loyal one in full view of everyone watching, but a slow or dismissive response is just as visible, and can do lasting damage to a business's reputation within hours.

In the real world: many UK banks and energy providers now use AI-powered chatbots on their websites and apps to handle simple, high-volume queries — such as checking a balance or reporting a lost card — instantly and at any hour, freeing up human staff to focus on more complex problems that genuinely need a person's judgement. Airlines including easyJet and British Airways run dedicated social media customer service teams specifically because so many customers now expect to raise a complaint or ask a question through a tweet or message rather than a phone call.

Exam practice

Have a go

2 marks

State two methods of good customer service.

Structure guide: two correctly identified methods — 1 mark each, no explanation required.

Case study — Harlow Appliances: Harlow Appliances sells kitchen appliances both online and from a single showroom. To reduce costs, it recently cut its in-store staff training budget and closed its post-sales customer helpline. Since then, online reviews mentioning poor product advice and lack of support have increased, and repeat customer purchases have fallen by 15%. A nearby rival retailer, which invests heavily in staff training and offers a dedicated aftercare helpline, has seen its own repeat custom rise over the same period.
4 marks

State and explain two ways poor customer service appears to be affecting Harlow Appliances.

Structure guide: state an effect grounded in the case study (1 mark), explain it (1 mark) — repeated twice over.

6 marks

Analyse how reinstating its post-sales helpline might affect Harlow Appliances' profitability.

Structure guide: a single developed line of reasoning grounded in the case study (eg: reinstating the helpline could directly address the "lack of support" complaints appearing in online reviews → helping rebuild the trust needed to reverse the 15% fall in repeat purchases → since retaining existing customers is usually cheaper than attracting new ones → this could improve profitability even though the helpline itself has a running cost).

9 marks

Recommend whether Harlow Appliances should reverse its recent cost-cutting and reinvest in staff training and its post-sales helpline. Justify your answer using the case study.

Structure guide: a "recommend" question needs a justified judgement — weigh the cost savings from the recent cuts against the 15% fall in repeat custom and the rival's improving performance over the same period, before reaching a clear final recommendation.

Key terms

Glossary

Sales process
The stages of a transaction: before, during and after the sale.
Product knowledge
Staff understanding of a product, allowing them to advise customers confidently.
Customer engagement
Creating a positive, personal experience for the customer during their interaction with a business.
Post-sales service
Support provided after a purchase, such as helplines, servicing or user training.
Customer loyalty
The tendency of a customer to keep returning to and buying from the same business.
