The Dynamic Nature of Business

Businesses don't appear in a vacuum — every new idea is a reaction to something changing: technology moving on, what people want shifting, or an old product simply falling out of use.

This opens Theme 1 — the small-business lifecycle from spotting an opportunity through to making it happen and keeping it running.
Starting the course

Why business is dynamic

This is the very first sub-topic of the Edexcel course, and it sets up a theme that runs through everything you'll study across both Theme 1 and Theme 2: businesses exist inside a world that keeps changing, and the ones that spot and respond to that change are the ones that get started, grow, or survive. A dynamic business environment simply means one that doesn't stand still — technology moves on, customer tastes shift, and yesterday's popular product can become tomorrow's forgotten one.

Understanding why new business ideas come about isn't just background colour — it's the practical starting point for topic 1.1 as a whole. Once you understand what triggers a new idea (this sub-topic), you're ready to look at the risk and reward that idea carries (1.1.2), and the role the entrepreneur plays in actually making it happen (1.1.3).

Three triggers

Why new business ideas come about

Edexcel identifies three specific reasons a new business idea might appear — each is a different kind of change in the world around a business, rather than something the entrepreneur necessarily controls.

Changes in technology

New technology can make something possible that simply wasn't before, opening the door to an entirely new type of business built around it.

Changes in what consumers want

Tastes, values and priorities shift over time, and a business idea that wouldn't have worked a decade ago can become highly successful once demand catches up with it.

Products/services becoming obsolete

When an existing product or service stops meeting people's needs — often because of the two triggers above — it creates a gap a new idea can step into.

These three triggers frequently work together rather than in isolation. A new technology often creates a genuinely new consumer want at the same time (nobody wanted a smartphone app for ordering food until smartphones existed to make it possible), and that same shift can be exactly what makes an older way of doing things become obsolete.

In the real world: smartphone technology made it possible to summon a car with a few taps, which is exactly the innovation Uber was built around — a business idea that simply couldn't have existed before smartphones and GPS were widely available. Growing consumer interest in plant-based eating has driven a wave of new vegan food brands in the UK, responding directly to a shift in what customers want rather than any single new technology. Blockbuster's video rental stores became obsolete once streaming services like Netflix offered instant access to films and shows without a trip to a physical store — a clear case of an existing product losing relevance as customer expectations moved on.
Two routes to a new idea

How new business ideas come about

Once a trigger creates the opportunity for a new business idea, Edexcel wants you to recognise that the idea itself can arrive in one of two different ways.

Original ideas

A genuinely new concept or invention that didn't really exist in the market before — often built around a new technology or a solution nobody else had thought of.

Adapting existing products, services or ideas

Taking something that already exists and improving it, combining it with something new, or applying it in a different way — rather than inventing something from scratch.

Adapting an existing idea is often lower-risk than a completely original one, since there's already evidence the underlying concept works — the entrepreneur's job is to improve on it, not prove it from nothing. A genuinely original idea can carry more risk (you'll look at this properly in 1.1.2), but can also open up a market with no existing competitors at all if it succeeds.

In the real world: James Dyson's bagless vacuum cleaner was a genuinely original idea — a completely new way of separating dust from air that didn't exist in any vacuum cleaner on the market before it. Airbnb, by contrast, adapted an idea that already existed — people renting out spare rooms, much like a traditional bed and breakfast — and built a technology platform around it that made the process far easier to find, book and pay for, without inventing the underlying concept of paid short-term accommodation.

Exam practice

Have a go

Standalone questions below are typical of Section A — no case study needed. The 6 and 9-mark questions are built around a Source Booklet–style case study, matching how Section B and C actually work in the real exam.

1 mark
Select one answer

Which one of the following is an example of a product becoming obsolete?

A. A new smartphone model being released
B. A DVD rental store closing as streaming grows in popularity
C. A supermarket introducing self-checkout tills
D. A café adding a loyalty card scheme

Answer: B. Section A multiple-choice questions test straightforward recall of specification content.

2 marks
Outline

Outline one way a new business idea can come about.

Structure guide: two linked points — eg "A new business idea can come from adapting an existing product (1), such as improving on a competitor's design to make it more convenient (1)." Points must connect to each other; unlinked points cap the mark at 1.

3 marks
Explain

Explain one reason why changes in technology can lead to new business ideas.

Structure guide: 1 mark for identifying a reason, plus 2 further marks for developing it — eg "New technology can make something possible that wasn't before (1), such as smartphones enabling location-based apps (1), which can create an entirely new market that didn't previously exist (1)."

Case study — Marlowe Print: Amara runs Marlowe Print, a small printing shop. She has noticed growing customer demand for eco-friendly stationery. She is considering two options: developing an entirely original biodegradable pen made from a new plant-based material she has been researching, or adapting an existing popular product — reusable notebooks — by adding her own branding and a local delivery service.
6 marks
AnalyseSection B style

Analyse the impact on Marlowe Print of changing consumer wants for eco-friendly stationery.

Structure guide: application (AO2) and analysis (AO3a) marked together — eg identify that growing demand for eco-friendly products (AO2) gives Marlowe Print a genuine gap to move into, then analyse how this could increase sales if Amara acts on it quickly, but could also attract new competitors if the trend continues to grow, meaning Marlowe Print's advantage may not last (AO3a).

9 marks
JustifySection C style

Justify whether Amara should develop the original biodegradable pen or adapt the existing reusable notebook idea.

Structure guide: a "Justify" question asks you to choose one of the two named options and support that choice using the case study. Apply knowledge to Marlowe Print specifically (AO2), analyse points on both sides (AO3a), then reach a clear, justified choice between the pen and the notebook (AO3b) — eg the notebook idea may be lower-risk since demand is already proven elsewhere, while the pen offers a genuine USP but requires unproven new material development.

A 12-mark "Evaluate" question — the largest command word in the exam — usually draws on a fuller business scenario spanning several topics at once, so you'll see one properly built once a few more sub-topics are covered.

Key terms

Glossary

Dynamic business environment
A business environment that is constantly changing, requiring businesses to adapt.
Original idea
A genuinely new business concept or invention not previously seen in the market.
Adapting
Improving on, combining, or reapplying an existing product, service or idea.
Obsolete
No longer useful or in demand, typically because something better has replaced it.
