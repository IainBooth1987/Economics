The Competitive Environment

If you want to open a lemonade stand, you need to know if the kid across the street already sells lemonade, what it tastes like, and how much they charge. Every business faces exactly this question, just at a bigger scale.

This closes Topic 1.2, bringing customer needs, research and segmentation together with a clear-eyed look at the rivals chasing the same customers.
Closing Topic 1.2

Why competitors matter as much as customers

This final sub-topic of 1.2 brings everything together. You know what customers need (1.2.1), how to research them properly (1.2.2), and how to group them into targetable segments (1.2.3) — including using a market map to spot a gap in the market. But a market map only tells half the story unless you also understand exactly who else is already fighting for those same customers.

A competitive environment is one in which many businesses sell similar products or services, each trying to attract the same customers. If you were launching a new brand of running shoes, your competitive environment would include established giants like Nike and Adidas, right down to the smaller independent sports shops on the local high street — all genuinely part of the picture a new entrant has to consider.

Five factors to assess

Strengths and weaknesses of competitors

Edexcel wants you to be able to judge exactly how strong a competitor is using five specific factors — several of which you'll recognise directly from earlier in this topic.

Price

How a competitor's prices compare to your own

A rival charging noticeably less is a genuine threat on the price need you met in 1.2.1, potentially pulling price-sensitive customers away without a business having to do anything wrong itself.

Quality

How well-made or reliable a competitor's offer is

A competitor known for superior quality can justify charging more and still win customers who value that reassurance over a cheaper alternative.

Location

How convenient a competitor's site is for customers

A rival based somewhere more convenient — a busier street, closer to public transport — has a genuine locational advantage regardless of how good its actual product is, echoing the location segment from 1.2.3.

Product range

How wide a choice a competitor offers

A competitor stocking many more variations gives customers more reasons to shop there instead, directly recalling the choice need from 1.2.1.

Customer service

How well a competitor treats and supports its customers

Excellent service can build loyalty that's genuinely hard for a rival to break, even when price or product range aren't quite as strong.

Assessing a competitor properly means looking at all five together, not picking just the one that happens to make a rival look weak. A competitor charging more (a weakness on price) might still be a serious threat if its quality and customer service are excellent enough to justify that higher price to plenty of customers.

What competition actually changes

The impact of competition on business decision making

  • A business often has to differentiate itself — finding a genuine USP (see 1.1.3) — rather than offering something identical to a rival
  • Prices are kept under constant pressure, since customers can easily compare against competitors offering something similar
  • Ongoing improvement becomes necessary rather than optional, since standing still while rivals improve means gradually falling behind
  • A business must continuously monitor what competitors are doing, rather than treating research as a one-off task
  • Operating in a genuinely competitive market raises the overall risk covered in 1.1.2, since customers always have an alternative to switch to
In the real world: a newly qualified mechanic setting up an independent car repair workshop in an area already served by several established garages faces a genuinely competitive environment from day one. To compete effectively, they might need to differentiate their service — offering a free basic check with every full service, for example — rather than simply hoping customers will switch over on their own, illustrating exactly how competition forces a new business to make deliberate, considered decisions rather than simply copying what already exists.

Exam practice

Have a go

Standalone questions below are typical of Section A — no case study needed. The 6 and 9-mark questions are built around a Source Booklet–style case study, matching how Section B and C actually work in the real exam.

1 mark
Select one answer

Which one of the following best describes a competitive environment?

A. A market with only one business operating
B. Many businesses selling similar products, each trying to attract the same customers
C. A market where prices never change
D. A business with no customers at all

Answer: B.

2 marks
Outline

Outline one way in which operating in a competitive market might impact a business's decision making.

Structure guide: two linked points — eg "A business may need to differentiate its product (1), by offering something rivals don't, to give customers a clear reason to choose it (1)." Points must connect; unlinked points cap the mark at 1.

3 marks
Explain

Explain one factor a business should consider when assessing a competitor's strengths and weaknesses.

Structure guide: 1 mark identifying a factor, plus 2 further marks developing it — eg "A business should consider a competitor's product range (1), since a rival offering more variety gives customers more reasons to shop there instead (1), potentially drawing customers away even if price and quality are similar (1)."

Case study — Priya's salon: Priya is planning to open a hairdressing salon in a town centre already home to four other salons. One nearby competitor, Style Studio, charges lower prices but has mixed reviews for customer service. Another competitor, The Cutting Room, is more expensive but has an excellent local reputation for quality and offers a wide range of services.
6 marks
AnalyseSection B style

Analyse the impact on Priya's business of operating in a highly competitive local market.

Structure guide: application (AO2) and analysis (AO3a) together — eg identify that Priya faces four established competitors covering both the price end (Style Studio) and quality end (The Cutting Room) of the market (AO2), then analyse how this leaves little obvious space for a new salon unless Priya can clearly differentiate herself, and that competing directly on price against Style Studio or quality against The Cutting Room without a genuine point of difference could make it hard to attract customers away from either (AO3a).

9 marks
JustifySection C style

Priya is considering two options: pricing her services lower than The Cutting Room to compete with Style Studio on price, or focusing on excellent customer service and a wide range of services to compete with The Cutting Room on quality. Justify which option Priya should choose.

Structure guide: apply knowledge to Priya's specific situation (AO2), analyse points on both sides (AO3a) — competing on price directly challenges Style Studio's core strength and risks a damaging price war, while competing on service and range targets The Cutting Room's exact strengths but may require greater upfront investment and time to build a reputation — then reach a clear, justified choice (AO3b).

Key terms

Glossary

Competitive environment
A market in which many businesses sell similar products or services, each trying to attract the same customers.
Differentiate
Making a product or service genuinely different from competitors, often through a USP.
