Learning Objectives
Students should be able to distinguish between private, public and voluntary sector organisations; explain different ownership structures and objectives; analyse stakeholder interests across sectors; and evaluate how each sector approaches the same service differently.
💼
Private Sector
Privately-owned enterprises operating for profit in competitive markets
Ownership Structures & Liability ▼

Types of Liability

Unlimited Liability: Personal assets can be seized to pay business debts — no legal separation between owner and business.

Limited Liability: Personal assets protected — liability limited to amount invested in the business.

Joint and Several Liability: In partnerships, each partner can be held responsible for all partnership debts.

Sole Traders — Unlimited Liability

No separate legal identity — owner and business are legally the same entity. Personal assets (home, savings) can be seized to pay business debts. Business income taxed as personal income.

Key Risks
  • Could lose personal home if business fails
  • Suppliers and lenders can pursue personal assets
  • Business failure can lead to personal bankruptcy
Partnerships — Unlimited Liability

Partnership Act 1890 governs relationships. Each partner personally liable for ALL partnership debts — joint and several liability.

Advantages
  • Shared expertise and workload
  • More capital than sole trader
  • Simple to establish
Risks
  • Unlimited liability for all partners
  • Joint responsibility for partner actions
  • Potential disputes
LLP — Limited Liability Partnerships

Hybrid structure combining partnership flexibility with limited liability protection. Popular with lawyers and accountants.

Private Limited Companies (Ltd) — Limited Liability

Separate legal entity. Shares cannot be sold to general public. Shareholders' personal assets protected.

Limited Liability Benefits
  • Personal home and savings are safe from business debts
  • Encourages investment — risk limited to amount invested
  • Company continues despite changes in ownership
  • Banks more willing to lend to incorporated businesses

Exceptions to Limited Liability

Personal Guarantees: Directors may give personal guarantees for loans.

Fraudulent Trading: Directors personally liable if company trades while knowingly insolvent.

Piercing the Corporate Veil: Courts may ignore separate legal personality in cases of fraud.

Public Limited Companies (Plc) — Limited Liability

Can offer shares to general public via stock exchanges. Maximum regulation and disclosure requirements. Must have minimum £50,000 share capital.

Advantages
  • Access to large capital through share sales
  • Enhanced public profile and credibility
  • Shares used as acquisition currency
Disadvantages
  • Loss of control through share dilution
  • Short-term shareholder pressure
  • Hostile takeover vulnerability
  • High compliance costs
Objectives & Financial Performance ▼

Profit as the Primary Objective

Private sector organisations primarily pursue profit maximisation — generating the maximum difference between total revenue and total costs. This drives shareholder value, enables reinvestment, and signals business health to investors.

Financial Objectives
  • Profit Maximisation: Maximum difference between revenue and costs
  • Revenue Growth: Increasing sales to build market share
  • Return on Investment: Generating returns above cost of capital
  • Shareholder Value: Maximising share price and dividends
  • Liquidity: Maintaining cash flow for operational needs
Non-Financial Objectives
  • Market Share: Growing proportion of total market sales
  • Brand Reputation: Building customer trust and loyalty
  • Employee Satisfaction: Reducing turnover, improving productivity
  • Social Responsibility: Environmental and community commitments
  • Innovation: New product development and technology adoption

Shareholder vs Stakeholder Theory

Shareholder Theory (Friedman): The sole responsibility of business is to increase profits for shareholders within legal rules.

Stakeholder Theory (Freeman): Businesses should serve all stakeholders — employees, customers, suppliers, communities — not just shareholders.

A-Level Application: Most modern businesses adopt a hybrid approach, balancing profit objectives against reputational and social concerns.

Case Study: Apple Inc. (Plc equivalent)

Structure: Public company listed on NASDAQ, owned by millions of shareholders worldwide. World's most valuable company by market capitalisation (c.$3 trillion).

Financial Performance

Revenue (2023): $383 billion | Net Profit: $97 billion | Profit Margin: ~25%

Shareholder Returns: Paid $15bn in dividends and repurchased $85bn of its own shares in 2023 — prioritising shareholder value.

Shareholders

Share price growth, dividends, buybacks, market leadership

Employees

160,000 staff, stock options, competitive salaries, innovation culture

Customers

Premium product experience, ecosystem lock-in, brand loyalty

Suppliers

Massive supply chain, strict labour standards, long-term contracts

A-Level Analysis: Apple demonstrates how Plc status enables massive capital formation for R&D and global expansion. Its approach illustrates shareholder primacy — but also shows how brand reputation requires balancing stakeholder interests.

Myth Buster: "Private Sector Always Outperforms Public Sector"

The Reality: This oversimplifies a complex relationship. Private sector efficiency advantages come from competitive markets — but where markets fail (natural monopolies, public goods, externalities), private provision often under-delivers.

Academic Context: Market failure theory shows that profit-maximising behaviour can produce socially suboptimal outcomes. This is why regulation and public sector provision coexist with private markets.

🏛️
Public Sector
Government-owned and funded organisations delivering public services
Types of Public Sector Organisation ▼
Government Departments

Executive arms of government implementing policy and delivering core public services. Directly accountable to Parliament through ministers. Financed through taxation via annual budgets.

Examples
  • Department of Health: NHS oversight, public health policy
  • Department for Education: School standards, curriculum
  • HM Treasury: Economic policy, government finances
Public Corporations

Government-owned enterprises with greater commercial freedom than departments. Day-to-day operational autonomy while remaining under ultimate government control.

Advantages
  • Commercial viability possible
  • Operational flexibility
  • Professional management
Challenges
  • Political interference risk
  • Conflicting objectives
  • Limited capital access
Local Authorities

Elected councils with democratic mandate to represent local communities. Statutory duties to provide specific services.

Revenue Sources

Council Tax: Local property-based taxation

Business Rates: Commercial property taxation

Government Grants: Central funding for specific services

Fees & Charges: User payments (parking, leisure facilities)

Public Sector Objectives & Performance ▼

Public sector objectives differ fundamentally from private sector due to democratic accountability and public service mission.

Public Value Theory (Mark Moore)

Public Value Creation: Delivering outcomes that citizens value through democratic processes.

Authorizing Environment: Political legitimacy from elected representatives.

Strategic Triangle: Balance between public value, legitimacy, and operational feasibility.

Primary Objectives
  • Service Quality: High-standard public services
  • Equity & Access: Fair access regardless of ability to pay
  • Democratic Accountability: Transparency and responsiveness
  • Value for Money: Efficient use of public resources
  • Policy Implementation: Delivering government priorities
Performance Measurement Challenges
  • Multiple Stakeholders: Citizens, politicians, taxpayers, users
  • Intangible Outcomes: Difficult to measure social value
  • Long-term Impact: Benefits may not be visible for years
  • Political Pressure: Short electoral cycles vs long-term planning

Case Study: BBC (British Broadcasting Corporation)

Structure: Public service broadcaster established by Royal Charter, funded primarily through licence fee, serving 67 million people in the UK.

Financial Model

Revenue (2023): £5.33bn total (£3.83bn licence fee, £1.50bn commercial) | Licence Fee: £169.50 annually per household

Licence Fee Payers

Value for money, quality programming, impartial news, universal access

Government

Public service delivery, cultural objectives, democratic accountability

Employees

22,000 staff, public service ethos, editorial independence

Global Audiences

BBC World Service: 426 million weekly in 40+ languages

A-Level Analysis: Demonstrates how public sector organisations can achieve efficiency and innovation while serving public objectives. BBC iPlayer (2007) was a global first in streaming. Shows the tension between commercial pressure and public service mission.

Myth Buster: "Public Sector is Inherently Inefficient"

Different Objectives: Public sector prioritises equity and social outcomes that private companies would not pursue profitably. Efficiency must be balanced against other goals.

Scale Achievements: The NHS treats 1.7 million patients weekly; the education system serves 10+ million students — remarkable efficiency at scale.

Innovation Examples: Public sector drives innovation in internet technology (ARPANET), GPS, medical research, and renewable energy.

Academic Context: Relates to public choice theory and principal-agent problems — institutional design affects performance outcomes significantly.

❤️
Voluntary Sector
Non-profit organisations driven by social mission rather than profit
Types of Voluntary Organisation ▼
Charities & Non-Profits

Must demonstrate clear public benefit and operate within strict legal frameworks. Governed by trustees. Regulated by the Charity Commission.

Revenue Streams
  • Donations: Individual giving, corporate sponsorship, legacy income
  • Grants: Government funding, foundations, lottery
  • Trading Income: Charity shops, social enterprises
  • Fundraising Events: Galas, challenges, campaigns
Mutuals & Co-operatives

Owned and controlled by members. Profits (surpluses) distributed to members rather than external shareholders. Democratic governance — one member, one vote.

Advantages
  • Democratic member control
  • Long-term sustainability focus
  • Aligned member-owner interests
  • Community investment
Challenges
  • Slower decision-making
  • Limited external capital
  • Member disengagement risk
Social Enterprises

Businesses trading primarily to achieve social or environmental goals. Profits reinvested into the social mission rather than distributed to shareholders.

Social Entrepreneurship

Blending commercial techniques with social missions to create sustainable impact. Examples: Divine Chocolate (Fairtrade), The Big Issue, Greyston Bakery.

Financial Management & Sustainability ▼

Voluntary organisations must be financially sustainable to achieve their mission — they need surplus to reinvest and build resilience.

Financial Management Principles

Reserve Policy: Typically 3-6 months operating costs held in reserves for stability.

Fundraising Ratio: Best practice — fundraising costs should not exceed 25% of income.

Programme Efficiency: Aim for 75%+ of expenditure directly supporting charitable objectives.

Investment Policy: Ethical investment aligned with charitable purposes.

Case Study: The Co-operative Group (UK)

Structure: Consumer cooperative owned by 4.6 million members, operating food retail, funerals, insurance, and legal services. Annual revenue: £9.5bn.

Financial Performance

Revenue Model: Combines retail margins with member loyalty. Members receive annual dividends (typically 1-2% of purchases).

Members

Democratic control, dividend payments, ethical alignment

Employees

46,000 jobs, profit-sharing schemes, democratic culture

Communities

Local sourcing, community investment fund, environmental initiatives

Suppliers

Fair trade commitments, long-term ethical partnerships

A-Level Analysis: Demonstrates how democratic ownership creates competitive advantage through member loyalty. Practical application of stakeholder theory — members are both owners and customers, aligning interests.

Myth Buster: "Non-Profits Don't Need Financial Objectives"

The Reality: Fundamentally incorrect. Voluntary organisations must generate surplus (profit) to reinvest in services, build resilience against funding cuts, and fund innovation.

Why Mutuals Can Be Fairer: Democratic governance gives equal voting rights regardless of financial contribution. Surpluses benefit members not external shareholders. Long-term focus without quarterly earnings pressure.

Academic Framework: Connects to stakeholder theory vs shareholder theory — mutuals exemplify stakeholder primacy in practice.

Healthcare — How Each Sector Operates

Private Sector

Profit-focused
Example: Nuffield Health, BUPA clinics, HCA hospitals
  • Patients pay fees or use private insurance
  • Fast access — short waiting times
  • Premium facilities and services
  • Competitive market drives quality
High quality but expensive — only for those who can afford it or have insurance

Public Sector

Service-focused
Example: NHS hospitals, GP surgeries, A&E departments
  • Free at point of use — funded by taxation
  • Available to everyone regardless of income
  • May have waiting lists due to high demand
  • Treats 1.7 million patients per week
Universal access but can be slower due to high demand and resource constraints

Voluntary Sector

Mission-focused
Example: Macmillan Cancer Support, Mind, British Heart Foundation
  • Specialised support services for specific conditions
  • Funded by donations and grants
  • Fills gaps left by NHS and private providers
  • Often relies on trained volunteers
Targeted help for specific groups or conditions — complements rather than replaces other sectors

Discussion Questions

Should healthcare be entirely publicly provided, privately provided, or a mix? What are the trade-offs of each approach? Use the comparison above as evidence.
The NHS is funded through general taxation — who bears the cost and who benefits? Is this a fair system? Consider different stakeholder perspectives.
Why might the voluntary sector provide services that neither the private nor public sector offers? Link your answer to market failure theory.
Education — How Each Sector Operates

Private Sector

Profit-focused
Example: Independent schools (Eton, Harrow), private tutoring companies, online learning platforms
  • Parents pay school fees (often £15,000-£45,000/yr)
  • Selective admission process
  • Small class sizes and extensive resources
  • Focus on academic results and university entry
High-quality education but only for families who can afford it — raises social mobility concerns

Public Sector

Service-focused
Example: State primary and secondary schools, sixth form colleges, universities (with regulated fees)
  • Free education for all children aged 5-16
  • Follows national curriculum
  • Managed by local councils or academy trusts
  • Open to all children in the local area
Free, universal education — but resource constraints can affect quality and outcomes in deprived areas

Voluntary Sector

Mission-focused
Example: Teach First, Action for Children, after-school clubs, Scouts, DofE
  • Support for disadvantaged pupils
  • Charity and grant-funded
  • Extra mentoring and pastoral support
  • Focus on social mobility and life skills
Additional support to help struggling students — particularly effective in bridging gaps left by public provision

Discussion Questions

Private schools educate 7% of UK pupils but produce a disproportionate share of senior politicians, judges and executives. Is this a market failure? What are the arguments for and against restricting private education?
Should university education be free at the point of use (as in many European countries) or funded through student loans? Evaluate the trade-offs for different stakeholder groups.
Why do voluntary organisations like Teach First exist if we already have a public education system? What does this tell us about the limitations of public sector provision?
Transport — How Each Sector Operates

Private Sector

Profit-focused
Example: Uber, private taxi firms, easyJet, National Express (private routes)
  • Pay per journey — price reflects demand
  • Convenient app-based booking
  • Flexible and responsive to customer needs
  • Dynamic (surge) pricing in high demand
Flexible and often fast but can be expensive — particularly for low-income users and rural communities

Public Sector

Service-focused
Example: Local bus services, London Underground, Network Rail, Transport for London
  • Subsidised affordable fares
  • Serves all areas including remote routes
  • Accessible for disabled users (legal obligation)
  • Regulated timetables and safety standards
Affordable and universal but less flexible — route decisions based on social need not profitability

Voluntary Sector

Mission-focused
Example: Community transport schemes, Royal Voluntary Service, charity minibuses
  • Serves elderly, disabled and isolated people
  • Funded by donations and local authority grants
  • Volunteer drivers — very low cost
  • Fills gaps in commercial and public routes
Essential transport for vulnerable people who cannot use other options — typically the last resort

Discussion Questions

Bus services in rural areas are often unprofitable for private operators. Should the government subsidise these routes, run them publicly, or allow them to close? Evaluate the stakeholder impacts of each option.
Uber and other gig-economy transport firms use dynamic pricing — prices rise when demand is high. Is this an example of an efficient market or an ethical problem? Consider multiple stakeholder perspectives.
Using the three transport examples above, explain what is meant by "market failure" and why it leads to government intervention in transport markets.
