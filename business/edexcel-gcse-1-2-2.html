Market Research

A business doesn't just guess what customers want — that's a great way to lose money. Market research is how entrepreneurs act like detectives, finding out the truth before spending a single penny.

Research is how the customer needs from 1.2.1 get turned into real evidence, ready for segmentation in 1.2.3.
Turning guesswork into evidence

The purpose of market research

In 1.2.1 you saw that customers need price, quality, choice and convenience — but a business can't actually know which of these matters most to its specific customers without asking. Market research is the process of gathering and analysing information about customers and the market a business operates in, and Edexcel identifies four specific purposes it serves.

Identify and understand customer needs

Finding out exactly what people want, rather than assuming — direct evidence rather than guesswork.

Identify gaps in the market

Spotting an underserved need nobody else is meeting well — a genuine opportunity, echoing 1.1.1.

Reduce risk

Evidence-based decisions are less likely to end in the business failure or financial loss covered in 1.1.2.

Inform business decisions

Pricing, product design and location decisions (see 1.4) can all be grounded in real evidence rather than a hunch.

It's worth being precise about that second purpose: research doesn't remove risk entirely — an entrepreneur is still taking a risk however good their research is — but it makes that risk a genuinely calculated one, exactly the idea you met back in 1.1.2.

Two sources of information

Methods of market research

Primary research (also called field research) gathers brand new information, first-hand, specifically for the business's own question. Secondary research (also called desk research) uses information that already exists, collected by someone else.

Primary research methods

Survey / questionnaire

A structured set of questions given to a sample of customers

Can reach a large number of people relatively cheaply, producing mostly quantitative data that's easy to compare and analyse.

Focus group

A small group discussion, guided by the business

Generates rich, qualitative detail about opinions and reasons, though with a much smaller and less representative sample than a survey.

Observation

Watching how customers actually behave

Shows what customers genuinely do — such as which shelves they linger at — rather than relying on what they say they'd do.

Secondary research methods

Internet research

Existing online sources, competitor websites, reviews

Fast and inexpensive, though reliability can vary depending on the source.

Market reports

Research already compiled by a specialist agency

Often detailed and professionally produced, but not tailored to one specific business's exact question.

Government reports

Official statistics on population, spending and the economy

Free, reliable and wide-ranging, though not written with any single business in mind.

In the real world: a café owner might observe how many customers visit at different times of day to decide when to schedule extra staff — a simple but genuinely useful example of observation as primary research. The UK's Office for National Statistics (ONS) publishes detailed government reports on population, employment and household spending that many businesses use as free secondary research when deciding where to locate or who to target.
Making sense of the findings

The use of data in market research

Quantitative data

Numerical, factual information that can be counted, measured and easily turned into a chart — for example, research into how many cans of a drink were sold across the UK last year.

Qualitative data

Descriptive information about opinions and feelings, harder to reduce to a single number — for example, research into whether customers think a shop's customer service is genuinely good.

Quantitative data tells a business what is happening; qualitative data helps explain why. Many businesses deliberately combine both — a survey might reveal that a large proportion of customers are unhappy with a product, while a focus group can then dig into exactly what's driving that dissatisfaction.

The role of social media

Social media has transformed how quickly and cheaply a business can gather market research. Businesses can monitor what's trending in real time, read honest, unprompted comments and reviews left publicly by customers, and run quick polls on platforms to get instant feedback from a large audience at little to no cost.

Why reliability matters

Not all research is good research. Data is only genuinely useful if it's reliable — meaning it comes from a large enough sample to be representative, is recent rather than out of date, and is free from bias in how it was collected. A business that bases a major decision on a tiny, unrepresentative sample, or on outdated figures, risks making exactly the kind of expensive mistake that good research is supposed to prevent in the first place.

Exam practice

Have a go

Standalone questions below are typical of Section A — no case study needed. The 6 and 9-mark questions are built around a Source Booklet–style case study, matching how Section B and C actually work in the real exam.

1 mark
Select one answer

Which one of the following is an example of primary market research?

A. Reading an industry report
B. Observing customers in a shop
C. Searching the internet for competitor prices
D. Reading a government statistics report

Answer: B.

2 marks
Outline

Outline one purpose of market research for a business.

Structure guide: two linked points — eg "Market research can help reduce risk (1), by giving a business evidence to base decisions on rather than guesswork (1)." Points must connect; unlinked points cap the mark at 1.

3 marks
Explain

Explain one reason why the reliability of market research data is important.

Structure guide: 1 mark identifying a reason, plus 2 further marks developing it — eg "Reliability matters because data must come from a large enough sample (1), otherwise it may not genuinely represent the wider market (1), which could lead a business to make a costly, poorly-informed decision (1)."

Case study — Nadia's café: Nadia is planning to open a small vegan café in a town centre. She is considering two research options before finalising her menu: running a questionnaire survey of 200 local residents to gather quantitative data on likely demand, or organising a focus group of 8 local residents to gather qualitative feedback on her proposed menu.
6 marks
AnalyseSection B style

Analyse the impact on Nadia's business plan of using a focus group to gather qualitative feedback on her menu.

Structure guide: application (AO2) and analysis (AO3a) together — eg identify that a focus group of 8 people gives detailed, qualitative reasoning about what customers do and don't like about the proposed menu (AO2), then analyse how this could help Nadia refine specific dishes before launch, but that such a small group may not be representative of the wider town centre population, meaning her final menu decisions still carry some risk (AO3a).

9 marks
JustifySection C style

Nadia is considering two options for her market research: a questionnaire survey of 200 local residents, or a focus group of 8 local residents. Justify which option Nadia should choose.

Structure guide: apply knowledge to Nadia's specific situation (AO2), analyse points on both sides (AO3a) — the survey offers a larger, more representative sample better suited to judging overall demand, while the focus group offers deeper insight into exactly which menu items to include — then reach a clear, justified choice (AO3b).

Key terms

Glossary

Market research
Gathering and analysing information about customers and the market a business operates in.
Primary research
New information collected first-hand for a specific business purpose.
Secondary research
Existing information originally collected by someone else.
Quantitative data
Numerical, factual information that can be measured and analysed statistically.
Qualitative data
Descriptive information about opinions, feelings and reasons.
Reliability
Whether research data can genuinely be trusted, based on sample size, how current it is, and freedom from bias.
