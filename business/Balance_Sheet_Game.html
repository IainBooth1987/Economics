Understand balance sheet structure, master liquidity ratio calculations, and construct balance sheets from scratch across three activities.

A balance sheet (or statement of financial position) is a snapshot of what a business owns, owes, and is worth at a specific point in time. It must always balance because of the fundamental accounting equation. Click any card to explore the full theory.

The Accounting Equation
Assets = Liabilities + Equity
Everything a business owns (assets) must be funded by either borrowing (liabilities) or owner investment and retained profits (equity). This equation always holds — it is the foundation of double-entry bookkeeping.
Balance Sheet Sections
Section 1
Non-Current Assets
Assets held for more than one year — the long-term foundations of the business.
Section 2
Current Assets
Assets expected to convert to cash within twelve months.
Section 3
Current Liabilities
Debts and obligations due within the next twelve months.
Section 4
Non-Current Liabilities
Long-term debts and obligations due after more than one year.
Section 5
Equity
The owners' stake — share capital, retained earnings and reserves.
Liquidity Ratios
Ratio 1
Current Ratio
Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities
Ratio 2
Acid Test Ratio
(Current Assets − Inventory) ÷ Current Liabilities
Ratio 3
Receivables Days
(Receivables ÷ Revenue) × 365
Ratio 4
Payables Days
(Payables ÷ Cost of Sales) × 365
