HR Flow — Core Theory

The key concepts, formulas, and strategic context you need before exploring the interactive tools.

What is HR Flow?

HR flow describes the movement of employees into, through, and out of an organisation. Effective HR flow ensures the business has the right people, in the right roles, at the right time — directly supporting the achievement of business objectives.

The HR Planning Cycle

Assess current workforce — skills audit, headcount, age profile, turnover data. Forecast future needs — based on business strategy, market trends, technology, and expected growth or contraction. Identify the gap — surplus or shortage. Plan the response — recruit, train, redeploy, or manage redundancies.

Internal vs External InfluencesInternal factors: business growth strategy, technology investment, staff retirement profile, productivity improvements. External factors: economic conditions, labour market tightness, changes in employment law, demographic trends. Strong evaluation answers always contextualise HR decisions within these drivers.
Labour Turnover

Definition and Formula

Labour turnover measures the rate at which employees leave an organisation and need to be replaced.

Labour Turnover (%) = (Number of Leavers in a Year / Average Number Employed) x 100

Example: 30 leavers from a workforce of 200 = (30/200) x 100 = 15%

Causes of High Turnover

  • Low wages relative to competitors
  • Poor management or leadership
  • Lack of development opportunities
  • Unsatisfactory working conditions
  • Better external opportunities (tight labour market)
  • Lack of job security or poor culture

Costs of High Turnover

  • Direct: Recruitment advertising, agency fees, interview time
  • Indirect: Loss of knowledge, reduced productivity, training new staff, effect on team morale
  • High turnover is often a symptom of deeper HR or management problems
  • Some turnover is healthy — brings fresh ideas and avoids stagnation
Evaluation pointHigh labour turnover is not always bad — in low-skill, high-volume roles (e.g. fast food), some churn is expected. In specialist or knowledge-based roles, turnover is more costly and damaging. Context determines significance.
The Five HR Flow Responses

1. Workforce Planning

The strategic process of forecasting future workforce needs and aligning HR decisions with business objectives. Involves analysing current skills, predicting future requirements, and identifying gaps — the foundation for all other HR responses.

2. Recruitment

Internal recruitment — promoting or moving existing staff. Faster, cheaper, preserves knowledge, but limits fresh thinking and creates a secondary vacancy. External recruitment — hiring from the labour market. Brings new skills and perspectives but costs more, takes longer, and carries selection risk.

Key documents: Job description (the role's duties and responsibilities) and Person specification (the ideal candidate's skills, qualifications and attributes). These are distinct — a common exam error is confusing them.

3. Training and Development

  • Induction training: Orientation for new starters — policies, culture, health and safety. Reduces time to productivity.
  • On-the-job training: Learning in the workplace, often through shadowing. Cost-effective but may embed poor practices.
  • Off-the-job training: External courses, qualifications. More expensive and removes staff temporarily but builds transferable, certified skills.

4. Redeployment

Moving existing employees into different roles, locations, or departments rather than making them redundant. Often combined with targeted retraining. Retains institutional knowledge, reduces recruitment costs, and is generally better for morale. UK employment law requires employers to consider redeployment before proceeding to redundancy.

5. Redundancy

When a role ceases to exist — not a dismissal for performance. Compulsory redundancy is imposed by the employer. Voluntary redundancy invites employees to apply — often at enhanced rates. Statutory redundancy pay is a legal entitlement after 2 years' continuous service, calculated using age, service, and weekly pay (capped at £643/week, 2024/25).

Consultation requirements: 20–99 redundancies: minimum 30 days. 100+ redundancies: minimum 45 days. Failure to consult is grounds for unfair dismissal claims.

The hierarchy of HR responsesBest practice: exhaust internal options first. Redeploy and retrain before recruiting externally. Explore redeployment before redundancy. This approach is cheaper, retains knowledge, and demonstrates ethical HR management — all valid evaluation points.

Why HR Flow Matters

HR flow ensures a business has the right people, in the right place, at the right time. Poor HR planning leads to skills gaps, high turnover, increased costs, and inability to deliver on strategic objectives.

Exam Tip

In evaluation questions, consider internal vs external factors — e.g. a recession makes redundancy more likely; rapid growth demands urgent recruitment. Always link HR decisions back to business strategy and context.

How Statutory Redundancy Pay is Calculated

Statutory redundancy pay is based on age, length of service, and weekly pay. The formula awards 0.5 weeks' pay per year under age 22, 1 week's pay per year aged 22-40, and 1.5 weeks' pay per year aged 41 or over. Service is capped at 20 years and weekly pay is capped at £643 (2024/25). The first £30,000 is tax-free. Employees must have at least 2 years' continuous service to qualify.

Common Misconceptions

The most frequently lost marks in HR Flow exam questions.

Recruitment

Misconception: "Internal recruitment is always better than external"

Students often list internal recruitment's advantages and conclude it is the preferred option without evaluating context.

Reality: Internal recruitment is faster and cheaper and preserves organisational knowledge — but it limits the injection of fresh ideas, may create resentment among unsuccessful internal applicants, and creates a secondary vacancy that still needs filling. External recruitment brings new perspectives and specialist skills that may simply not exist internally. The right choice depends on the role, urgency, available talent pool, and strategic needs.

Misconception: "A job description and a person specification are the same document"

One of the most common errors in recruitment questions.

Reality: A job description outlines the duties, responsibilities, and reporting relationships of the role — what the job involves. A person specification describes the ideal candidate — the skills, qualifications, experience, and personal attributes required. They are complementary but distinct documents, and examiners test this distinction directly.
Training

Misconception: "On-the-job training is always cheaper and better"

Students assume that keeping training in-house is always the optimal approach.

Reality: On-the-job training is cost-effective and immediately relevant, but it can entrench existing poor practices if the trainer has bad habits, is not verified as competent, or lacks teaching skills. Off-the-job training, while more expensive, provides externally validated qualifications, exposure to industry best practice, and removes the risk of poor habits being passed on. For regulated industries (healthcare, finance, construction), off-the-job certified training is often legally required.

Misconception: "Training is a cost, not an investment"

Students treat training expenditure as a pure cost to be minimised.

Reality: Well-designed training reduces errors and wastage, improves productivity, supports employee retention (reducing costly turnover), enables succession planning, and can differentiate a business competitively. The ROI on effective training is typically positive. Cutting training to save money is a false economy that often appears to improve short-term margins while undermining long-term capability.
Redeployment

Misconception: "Redeployment is only relevant when closing a business"

Students associate redeployment exclusively with major restructuring or site closures.

Reality: Redeployment is relevant whenever roles change — automation eliminating manual tasks, new product lines requiring different skills, changing customer needs, or departmental reorganisation. It is a day-to-day HR tool, not just a crisis measure. Businesses that embed redeployment into their HR planning avoid the disruption of unnecessary external recruitment and demonstrate commitment to their people.
Redundancy

Misconception: "Voluntary redundancy is always the fairer option"

Students assume voluntary redundancy is ethically superior to compulsory redundancy in all cases.

Reality: Voluntary redundancy can backfire if the wrong people leave — typically the most skilled and mobile employees, who have the best external options, tend to volunteer. This can leave the business with a skills deficit and lower overall capability. Compulsory redundancy, conducted fairly and transparently, may better preserve the workforce capabilities the business needs. The fairness of redundancy lies in the process, not just the method.

Misconception: "Redundancy means the employee was dismissed for poor performance"

A fundamental definitional error that appears regularly in exam answers.

Reality: Redundancy occurs when a role ceases to exist — because the business has changed its structure, closed a location, automated the work, or reduced its scale. It is entirely separate from the individual's performance. An excellent employee can be made redundant; a poor performer cannot be dismissed "for redundancy." Confusing redundancy with dismissal for conduct or capability is a serious error with legal implications.
Labour Turnover

Misconception: "High labour turnover is always a problem"

Students treat any high turnover figure as a sign of poor HR management.

Reality: Labour turnover must be interpreted in context. In high-volume, low-skill sectors (retail, hospitality, fast food), turnover of 25-40% can be normal and manageable. In professional, knowledge-based roles, even 10% might be highly damaging due to the cost and time of replacement. Zero turnover can also be problematic — it may indicate stagnation, prevent fresh thinking, or block progression for ambitious employees. The quality of the leaver matters as much as the quantity.
