Price Elasticity of Demand — Theory

Definition & Formula

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED) measures how responsive the quantity demanded of a good is to a change in its price. It tells a business how much sales volume will change when they raise or lower their price.

PED = % Change in Quantity Demanded ÷ % Change in Price
Sign Convention — Important for Exams

The Law of Demand means PED is almost always negative — when price rises, quantity demanded falls. Economists usually quote PED as an absolute value (ignoring the negative sign) to make comparisons easier. So PED = |−2.0| = 2.0. In exam answers, either convention is acceptable — but always explain which you are using.

Types of PED

Elastic (PED > 1)

Quantity changes more than price. A 10% price rise causes more than 10% fall in demand. Revenue falls when price rises.

Examples: designer trainers, restaurant meals, package holidays

Inelastic (PED < 1)

Quantity changes less than price. A 10% price rise causes less than 10% fall in demand. Revenue rises when price rises.

Examples: petrol, prescription medicines, cigarettes, salt

Unitary (PED = 1)

Quantity and price change by exactly the same percentage. Total revenue stays constant regardless of price changes.

Perfectly Inelastic (PED = 0)

Quantity does not change at all when price changes. Demand curve is vertical. Very rare — insulin for diabetics is the classic example.

PED and Total Revenue — The Key Relationship

Price changeElastic demand (PED > 1)Inelastic demand (PED < 1)
Price risesRevenue FALLS — volume loss outweighs price gainRevenue RISES — small volume loss, big price gain
Price fallsRevenue RISES — large volume gain outweighs price cutRevenue FALLS — small volume gain, big price cut

Factors Affecting PED

  • Number of substitutes: More alternatives means consumers switch easily — higher PED. Petrol has few substitutes (inelastic); a specific brand of cola has many (elastic).
  • Necessity vs luxury: Necessities (food, heating, medicine) are inelastic because consumers must buy them regardless of price. Luxuries are elastic — easy to cut back.
  • Proportion of income: Expensive items relative to income (holidays, cars) are more elastic — consumers think twice. Cheap items (matches, salt) are inelastic.
  • Time period: In the short run, demand is more inelastic as consumers cannot easily change habits. Over time, they find substitutes and demand becomes more elastic. Petrol is inelastic short-run but more elastic long-run as people switch to EVs.
  • Brand loyalty and differentiation: Strong brands reduce elasticity. Apple can raise iPhone prices with minimal volume loss because loyal customers perceive no adequate substitute.
  • Habit-forming goods: Addictive goods (cigarettes, alcohol) are highly inelastic because demand persists regardless of price rises.

Income Elasticity of Demand — Theory

Definition & Formula

Income Elasticity of Demand (YED) measures how responsive the quantity demanded of a good is to a change in consumer income. It tells businesses how their sales will change as the economy grows or contracts.

YED = % Change in Quantity Demanded ÷ % Change in Income
Sign Convention — Important for Exams

Unlike PED, the sign of YED matters and must not be ignored. A positive YED means demand rises with income (normal/luxury goods). A negative YED means demand falls as income rises (inferior goods). Always state whether YED is positive or negative in exam answers.

Types of Good — Classified by YED

Luxury Good (YED > 1)

Demand rises faster than income. A 10% income rise causes more than 10% rise in demand. Strong business cycle sensitivity.

Examples: overseas holidays, designer clothing, luxury cars, fine dining

Normal/Necessity (0 < YED < 1)

Demand rises with income but less than proportionally. Stable, recession-resistant demand.

Examples: basic food, clothing, utilities, public transport

Inferior Good (YED < 0)

Demand falls as income rises — consumers switch to superior alternatives. Counter-cyclical: demand rises in recessions.

Examples: own-brand groceries, bus travel (when switching to cars), fast food

YED and the Business Cycle

Good typeDuring a BoomDuring a Recession
Luxury (YED > 1)Demand surges — highly sensitive to income gainsDemand collapses — first thing cut from budgets
Necessity (0 < YED < 1)Demand rises modestlyDemand falls modestly — relatively recession-proof
Inferior (YED < 0)Demand falls — consumers trade upDemand rises — consumers trade down to save money

Businesses with a portfolio weighted toward luxury goods face high volatility across the economic cycle. Businesses selling necessities (e.g. supermarkets, utilities) are more recession-resilient.

Business Strategy Applications

  • Product portfolio planning: A business with high YED products (luxury goods) should consider adding lower YED products to stabilise revenue across the cycle. Primark (low YED) and ASOS (higher YED) serve very different income sensitivities.
  • Investment timing: Capital investment in luxury goods capacity is best timed ahead of a boom. Investment in inferior/necessity goods can be more counter-cyclical.
  • Supermarket strategy: Tesco and Morrisons stock both premium own-labels (higher YED) and value ranges (lower/negative YED) to capture consumers across all income levels.
  • Recession marketing: Inferior goods businesses (budget retailers, discount food brands) can market aggressively during downturns knowing their YED works in their favour.

Exam Tips

PED — Common Exam Mistakes
  • Always show the formula before substituting values — it picks up method marks even if arithmetic is wrong
  • State clearly whether you are using the absolute value of PED or the signed value
  • Link PED directly to the revenue impact — this is the most common application question
  • When asked about factors, always give a real business example alongside the explanation
  • Avoid saying "people buy less if it's more expensive" — use the correct terminology: "a rise in price leads to a fall in quantity demanded"
YED — Common Exam Mistakes
  • Never ignore the sign — a negative YED is the key indicator of an inferior good and must be stated
  • Link YED to the business cycle in evaluation — luxury goods firms suffer most in recessions
  • Distinguish clearly between normal goods (0 < YED < 1) and luxury goods (YED > 1) — both are "normal" in everyday language but different in economics
  • Give real examples — examiner mark schemes reward application. Mention specific brands: Aldi (low YED), Waitrose (higher YED)

Evaluation Points

Limitations of using elasticity in business decisions

Elasticity values are calculated from historical data and assume ceteris paribus (all else equal) — in reality, many variables change simultaneously. A business raising prices while a competitor lowers theirs will see a much larger fall in demand than PED alone would predict. Elasticity also changes over time and across different customer segments, so a single PED figure can be misleading.

Why the same product can have different PED values

PED is not fixed — it varies by price level, time period, geography and customer segment. Budget airline seats have elastic demand at high prices but inelastic demand for last-minute travellers who have no alternative. This explains why airlines use dynamic pricing to extract maximum revenue from different customer groups with different elasticities.
