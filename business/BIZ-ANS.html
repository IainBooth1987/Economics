Ansoff Matrix

Strategic growth framework — theory, UK case studies, evaluation angles, and interactive practice

What is the Ansoff Matrix?

The Ansoff Matrix, developed by Igor Ansoff in 1957 and published in the Harvard Business Review, is a strategic planning tool that helps businesses identify and evaluate growth opportunities. It is built on two dimensions — products (existing vs new) and markets (existing vs new) — generating four distinct strategic options with progressively increasing risk.

Core logicRisk increases as you move away from what you know. Using existing products in existing markets (Market Penetration) carries the lowest risk because both dimensions are familiar. Developing new products for new markets (Diversification) carries the highest risk because both dimensions are unknown territory.

The Four Strategic Options
Market Penetration
Existing products — Existing markets

Objective: Increase market share or usage frequency within current markets using existing products. The lowest-risk growth strategy because both the product and the customer are already known.

How businesses do it:

  • Price reductions to undercut competitors
  • Increased advertising and promotional activity
  • Loyalty programmes to increase purchase frequency
  • Improved distribution to reach more of the existing market
  • Acquisition of a competitor to absorb their market share

When it works best: When the market has room to grow and the business has competitive advantages it can exploit (brand, cost position, distribution). Less effective in saturated, mature markets with little room for share gains.

Product Development
New products — Existing markets

Objective: Develop new or improved products for existing customers. Medium risk — the market is familiar but the product requires investment and faces uncertainty.

How businesses do it:

  • R&D investment to create new product lines
  • Product extensions and variants (new flavours, sizes, formats)
  • Technology upgrades to existing product ranges
  • Licensing in new technology to bolt onto existing products
  • Responding to evolving customer needs within known segments

When it works best: When a business has deep customer insight, strong R&D capabilities, or when the market is demanding innovation. Relies on the existing customer relationship to de-risk new product launches.

Market Development
Existing products — New markets

Objective: Enter new markets with existing products. Medium risk — the product is proven but the new market introduces unfamiliarity in customer behaviour, competition, and regulations.

How businesses do it:

  • Geographic expansion — new countries or regions
  • Targeting new customer segments with existing products
  • New use cases or applications for existing products
  • New distribution channels reaching different audiences
  • Rebranding or repositioning for a new segment

When it works best: When the product has broad appeal that extends beyond current markets, and when the business has sufficient resources to manage the costs and complexity of new market entry without jeopardising its existing operations.

Diversification
New products — New markets

Objective: Develop new products for new markets simultaneously. The highest-risk strategy as neither the product nor the market is familiar.

Two types:

  • Related (concentric): Synergies with existing business — shared technology, brand, or customer base e.g. a vehicle manufacturer entering the energy storage market
  • Unrelated (conglomerate): Entirely separate from core business — no operational link e.g. a tobacco firm buying a food company

Why businesses diversify: Risk spreading across multiple markets; exploiting underutilised capabilities; strategic response to declining core market; pursuit of higher-growth sectors. High failure rate — studies suggest 50–70% of diversification moves fail to create value.

Exam Evaluation Point

The Ansoff Matrix is a prescriptive framework — it describes strategic options but does not determine which one is best. The correct choice depends on the business's resources, competitive position, market conditions, and risk appetite. A cash-rich, mature business facing a saturated market may rationally choose diversification even though it is highest risk, because its core market offers no growth. Context always determines which quadrant is appropriate — there is no universally "best" strategy.

Igor Ansoff and the Framework's Origins

Background

Igor Ansoff (1918–2002) was a Russian-American applied mathematician and business manager, widely regarded as the father of strategic management. He developed the matrix in 1957, publishing it in the Harvard Business Review, at a time when strategic planning as a formal discipline barely existed.

His core contribution was arguing that growth decisions should be systematic — not intuitive. By mapping products against markets, he gave managers a simple tool to categorise and debate strategic options.

Legacy and Limitations

The matrix remains one of the most widely taught strategy frameworks 65+ years after its creation. Its durability reflects genuine insight: the product-market tension is a fundamental strategic question for any growth-seeking business.

However, Ansoff's framework predates the digital economy, platform businesses, and the blurring of product and service boundaries. A streaming platform simultaneously penetrating existing markets, developing new products, and diversifying into hardware — all at once. Modern businesses often operate across multiple quadrants simultaneously.

Market Penetration — UK Case Studies

Gaining more from existing products in existing markets. The test: same product, same customer type, higher volume or frequency.

Market Penetration
Tesco Clubcard

Strategy: Tesco used Clubcard loyalty data to personalise offers, increase visit frequency, and reward existing customers with points redeemable against future purchases.

Key mechanism: By using purchase data to target relevant promotions, Tesco increased basket size and frequency among existing shoppers rather than seeking entirely new customers. The card also created switching costs — accumulated points reduced the incentive to shop elsewhere.

Result: Clubcard is credited with saving Tesco from serious competitive threat in the 1990s. Terry Leahy, then CEO, described it as "the most powerful piece of retailing intelligence" in the business. Tesco's market share grew from 16% to 31% over the period of Clubcard's rollout.

Evaluation

Clubcard's success depends on customers valuing the data trade-off — sharing purchase behaviour in exchange for discounts. Increasing consumer awareness of data privacy may reduce willingness to participate. Additionally, competitors have since launched equivalent schemes (Nectar, Boots Advantage), eroding the differentiation Clubcard once provided. Market penetration strategies are vulnerable to competitive imitation.

Market Penetration
JD Wetherspoon — Pricing Strategy

Strategy: Wetherspoon has consistently used low prices as the primary mechanism to grow market share in the UK pub and dining market — serving the same food and drink offer as competitors at lower prices.

Key mechanism: Efficient operations (centrally negotiated supplier contracts, no live music, standardised menus) enable prices 20–30% below comparable competitors. This grows the customer base and visit frequency within existing pub-going markets.

Result: Over 800 pubs nationally, strong market position in a declining overall pub sector. Growth driven by absorbing customers from independently operated pubs unable to match the cost structure.

Evaluation

A pure price-based market penetration strategy is only sustainable if the cost advantage is structural and durable. Wetherspoon's model works because of genuine operational efficiency — not price subsidisation. The risk is that further minimum wage increases and alcohol duty rises compress margins to a point where the price gap becomes impossible to maintain. Market penetration through price is highly vulnerable to input cost changes.

Product Development — UK Case Studies

New products for existing customers. The test: known customer base, new or significantly improved product.

Product Development
Dyson — From Vacuums to Hair Care

Strategy: Dyson developed premium hair dryers, straighteners, and stylers (Supersonic, Airwrap) for customers already using Dyson's brand proposition of premium engineering and superior performance.

Key mechanism: Dyson leveraged its existing brand equity and engineering reputation with existing premium home appliance customers. The hair care range uses the same motor technology and "no heat damage" positioning that defines the Dyson brand — new product, same customer values.

Result: The Dyson Airwrap retails at £400–500 and created a new premium segment in hair styling. Hair care became one of Dyson's fastest-growing categories, reaching an estimated £1.5bn in revenue by 2023.

Evaluation

Dyson's product development success depends on its ability to command premium prices justified by genuine technological differentiation. This is product development at its most effective — the new product category is plausible to existing customers precisely because of brand trust. The risk is brand stretch: if Dyson enters too many categories, the engineering premium becomes harder to justify and brand dilution may undermine the core vacuum business.

Product Development
Marks & Spencer — Per Una and Food Innovation

Strategy: M&S has repeatedly used product development to rejuvenate stagnating ranges — the Per Una clothing line for younger female shoppers, and continuous food innovation (the "dine in for £10" meal deal, premium ready meals) for existing M&S food shoppers.

Key mechanism: Rather than entering new markets, M&S develops new product lines that deepen the relationship with existing customers at different occasions and life stages. The customer base is known — the challenge is expanding what they buy.

Result: Food has become M&S's most profitable division, driven by consistent product innovation within a customer base already familiar with the M&S quality proposition.

Evaluation

M&S illustrates that product development is not always about technology — it can be about deepening customer occasions. However, M&S has also shown the danger of misfiring product development (numerous failed clothing ranges) when the new product does not align with what existing customers actually want. Strong customer insight is the prerequisite for successful product development.

Market Development — UK Case Studies

Existing products in new markets. The test: same product (or minimally adapted), new customer segment or geography.

Market Development
Greggs — International and Travel Hub Expansion

Strategy: Greggs has expanded beyond its traditional Northern England high-street base into airports, motorway services, and transport hubs — and has explored international franchising — using its existing bakery product range.

Key mechanism: The core product (sausage rolls, bakes, sweet treats) remains unchanged. The strategy is reaching new customer groups — travellers, commuters, and international consumers — who were not previously served by Greggs' traditional high-street model.

Result: By 2024, Greggs had over 2,400 shops in the UK, with growing presence in travel locations. International pilots remain cautious — market development risk increases substantially in cross-cultural contexts where product-market fit is uncertain.

Evaluation

Market development to travel hubs is lower-risk than international expansion because the customer (a British consumer) is effectively the same — just in a different location. True international market development is genuinely risky: what sells in Greggs' Northern English heartland may not translate culturally or competitively in, say, France or Germany. Market development risk scales with how different the new market actually is from the existing one.

Market Development
Rolls-Royce — Power Systems for Marine and Energy

Strategy: Rolls-Royce applied its existing gas turbine and power engineering expertise to marine propulsion systems and industrial power generation markets — new customer segments (shipping companies, power utilities) for fundamentally similar technology.

Key mechanism: The engineering capabilities developed for aerospace were repurposed for adjacent industrial markets. The customers are entirely different (defence/aerospace vs shipping/energy) but the core product technology has significant overlap.

Result: Power Systems became a significant revenue stream for Rolls-Royce, reducing dependence on the highly cyclical commercial aviation market.

Evaluation

Rolls-Royce's market development is an example of leveraging technological capabilities across different customer segments — sometimes called "adjacent market entry." The risk is lower than true diversification because the core competence transfers. However, different markets have different competitive dynamics and procurement processes: winning contracts in marine or energy requires industry relationships that don't automatically transfer from aerospace.

Diversification — UK Case Studies

New products for new markets. The test: the business is entering genuinely unfamiliar territory on both dimensions simultaneously.

Diversification — Related
Virgin Group — From Music to Airlines to Banking

Strategy: Richard Branson used the Virgin brand to enter radically different markets — from Virgin Records (music), to Virgin Atlantic (aviation), Virgin Money (financial services), Virgin Galactic (space tourism). Each represented new products for new market segments.

Key mechanism: Virgin's diversification is linked by brand — the common thread is a challenger positioning, consumer-facing irreverence, and premium-value proposition. The brand provides a degree of relatedness even across unconnected sectors.

Result: Mixed. Virgin Atlantic is a long-established airline. Virgin Money became a credible challenger bank. But Virgin Cola, Virgin Brides, Virgin Cars, and Virgin Clothing all failed — demonstrating the high failure rate of brand-led diversification into markets where no underlying competitive advantage exists.

Evaluation

Virgin illustrates both the appeal and the danger of brand-led diversification. A strong brand reduces entry barriers (customers give new ventures the benefit of the doubt) but cannot substitute for operational capability. When Virgin entered aviation, it had no aircraft, no pilots, no maintenance infrastructure, and no routes — brand alone was insufficient. Where Virgin succeeded, it was because it could genuinely disrupt existing players; where it failed, it was because the brand premium couldn't overcome incumbents with superior capabilities.

Diversification — Unrelated
Tata Group — Jaguar Land Rover Acquisition

Strategy: Indian conglomerate Tata (primarily known for steel, IT services, and consumer goods in India) acquired the Jaguar Land Rover premium automotive brand from Ford in 2008 for £1.15 billion — entering the premium vehicle market as a new product in a new market.

Key mechanism: Tata brought capital and operational discipline; JLR brought engineering heritage, brand equity, and UK manufacturing. The synergies were financial and managerial rather than operational — an unrelated diversification justified by the acquisition price and growth potential.

Result: JLR transformed under Tata ownership, growing revenues from £6.5bn to over £23bn by 2023. Tata's patient capital approach (willing to accept short-term losses in pursuit of long-term brand development) proved well-suited to premium automotive cycles.

Evaluation

Tata-JLR is a rare successful unrelated diversification, and its success reveals why most fail: Tata did not attempt to run JLR as an Indian conglomerate subsidiary. It retained the management, engineering culture, and UK identity that made the brand valuable. Many diversification failures occur when the acquirer imposes its own culture and systems on a business that operates by fundamentally different logic. Knowing what not to change is as important as knowing what to fix.

Strengths of the Ansoff Matrix

Simplicity and Clarity

The 2×2 structure makes strategic options immediately communicable. A board can use the matrix as a shared language — "this is product development, not diversification" — to align debate and prevent strategic drift. Complex strategic discussions often benefit from simple organising frameworks.

Risk Visibility

The matrix explicitly maps risk onto strategic choice, which forces organisations to acknowledge the risk implications of growth ambitions. Many businesses fail by treating diversification as equivalent in risk to market penetration. Ansoff makes the difference explicit.

Resource Allocation Logic

Each quadrant has different resource requirements. Penetration requires marketing and operational efficiency. Product development requires R&D. Market development requires market research and distribution. Diversification requires the most capital and capability. The matrix helps prioritise investment.

Universal Applicability

The framework applies to businesses of any size, in any sector, at any stage of development. A startup, an SME, and a FTSE 100 corporation can all use the same framework to structure growth decisions.

Limitations of the Ansoff Matrix

Oversimplification of "New" vs "Existing"

The binary distinction between new and existing markets/products is often blurred in practice. Is a product that is 20% improved "new"? Is entering Scotland a "new market" for a Yorkshire business? The framework provides no guidance on how to classify ambiguous cases — and misclassification leads to misjudged risk assessment.

No Competitive Analysis

The matrix is internally focused — it considers product and market dimensions but ignores the competitive landscape entirely. A market penetration strategy in a market dominated by a cost-efficient incumbent is vastly more difficult than one in a fragmented market. Porter's Five Forces provides what Ansoff lacks: competitive context.

Ignores Implementation Capability

The matrix identifies options but says nothing about whether the business has the capabilities to execute them. A diversification strategy that requires entirely new competencies is not equivalent to one that leverages existing strengths. The McKinsey 7S Model or competence frameworks are needed to assess execution feasibility.

Static Framework in a Dynamic Environment

Ansoff was writing in 1957. Modern businesses — particularly digital platforms — routinely operate across multiple quadrants simultaneously, and the pace of market change means a strategy formulated today may be obsolete within 18 months. The matrix is a planning tool, not a substitute for ongoing strategic monitoring.

Does Not Address Decline

The matrix is purely about growth options. It provides no framework for managed decline, divestment, or strategic consolidation — all of which are legitimate responses when core markets shrink or competitive positions become untenable.

Risk is Relative, Not Absolute

The matrix labels diversification as "high risk" regardless of context. For a business with declining core revenues, no growth in its existing markets, and specific expertise that transfers well, diversification may actually be lower risk than continuing market penetration in a failing market.

The Strongest Evaluation Argument

The Ansoff Matrix is valuable as a structuring tool rather than a prescriptive guide. Its real utility is in forcing explicit debate about the product-market dimensions of a proposed strategy — not in telling a business what to do. Used in combination with competitive analysis (Porter's Five Forces), environmental analysis (PESTLE), and internal capability assessment (VRIN or McKinsey 7S), Ansoff forms part of a robust strategic toolkit. Used in isolation, it produces an incomplete picture that can generate false confidence.

Exam application — what "evaluate" actually means hereWhen an exam question asks you to "evaluate" the Ansoff Matrix or "assess the most appropriate strategy," you need to do three things: (1) explain the relevant strategic option and why it applies to the case; (2) identify why it might succeed given the business's specific context; (3) identify the specific risks or limitations given that same context. A generic list of strengths and weaknesses scores poorly. A contextually anchored evaluation — "for this business, in this market, with these resources, market penetration is more appropriate than diversification because..." — scores highly.

How to play: Drag each scenario card to the correct Ansoff quadrant. The key question to ask yourself: is this an existing or new product, and is it targeting an existing or new market? Those two questions determine the correct quadrant every time.
