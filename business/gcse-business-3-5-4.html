Bringing marketing together

What the marketing mix actually is

The marketing mix is the combination of four decisions a business makes about how it takes a product to market: price, product, promotion and place — often called the "4Ps." Every idea from earlier in this topic feeds directly into these four decisions: understanding customers (3.5.1) shapes what the product should be, segmentation (3.5.2) decides who each decision targets, and market research (3.5.3) supplies the evidence behind all of it.

This is the longest sub-topic in the whole specification, and for good reason — each of the four Ps involves genuine decisions with real trade-offs, and AQA expects you to understand all of them in enough depth to apply them to an unfamiliar business scenario.

What to charge

Price

Pricing decisions directly affect both how many units a business sells and how much profit it makes on each one — the two rarely move in the same direction, which is exactly why choosing a pricing method matters so much.

Price skimming

Set a high price at launch, then lower it over time

Common for genuinely new or innovative products with little direct competition at launch, this method extracts maximum revenue from customers willing to pay a premium to be first, before the price gradually falls as competitors catch up or demand from early adopters is satisfied.

Price penetration

Set a deliberately low price to enter a market quickly

Used to win market share fast, often when entering an established market against existing competitors, accepting lower profit margins upfront in exchange for building a large customer base that can later be charged more, or that generates profit through high volume.

Competitive pricing

Set a price closely in line with competitors

Common in markets with many similar products where customers can compare prices easily, avoiding the risk of pricing significantly above or below rivals in a market where price is a key factor in the customer's decision.

Loss leader

Price a product very low, even below cost, to attract customers

The loss on that specific product is accepted deliberately, in the expectation that customers drawn in by it will also buy other, more profitable items at the same time — the loss leader's job is to get the customer through the door, not to make money itself.

Cost-plus

Add a fixed markup percentage to the cost of production

Straightforward to calculate and guarantees a profit margin on every unit sold, but takes no direct account of what competitors charge or what customers might actually be willing to pay, risking a price that's either too high to compete or leaves money on the table.

Factors influencing pricing decisions

Costs

The fixed and variable costs from 3.1.6 set a practical floor — price consistently below total cost per unit and a business loses money on every sale.

Nature of the market

A luxury, exclusive market can support high prices that a mass, budget-conscious market simply couldn't.

Degree of competition

Little competition (see 3.2.6) gives a business much more freedom over price than a fiercely competitive market does.

Product life cycle stage

Skimming often suits the introduction stage of a new product, while heavier discounting becomes more common as a product moves towards decline (see below).

In the real world: Apple consistently uses price skimming, launching each new iPhone at a high price before gradually reducing prices on older models as newer versions are released. UK supermarkets have long used milk and bread as loss leaders, pricing them very low specifically to draw customers into the store, where they then buy a full, more profitable weekly shop alongside them.
What's actually being sold

Product

Developing new products keeps a business relevant as customer needs and competitor offers change, but it's genuinely risky — the research and development, design and testing all cost money before a single unit has been sold, and a product that misjudges customers (as covered in 3.5.1) can fail expensively despite that investment. Good product decisions therefore lean heavily on the design quality, the image a product projects, and how precisely it's matched to the needs of its intended target market (see 3.5.2).

Product differentiation

Unique selling point (USP)

A genuine feature or benefit no competitor offers, giving customers a clear reason to choose this product specifically rather than a rival's.

Brand image

The overall impression and reputation a product or business carries — often just as powerful a reason to buy as any functional difference.

In the real world: Dyson's original bagless vacuum cleaner technology was a genuine USP when launched, giving customers a functional reason to choose it over every bag-based rival on the market. Rolex, by contrast, competes largely on brand image and prestige rather than any single unique technical feature, with customers paying a significant premium for what owning the brand itself represents.
A product's journey

The product life cycle

Every product moves through a predictable pattern of stages from launch to eventual withdrawal, and understanding which stage a product is in helps explain the marketing decisions that make sense at that point.

R&D

Cost without any revenue, before launch

Introduction

Low sales, high marketing spend to build awareness

Growth

Sales and profit rise as more customers adopt it

Maturity

Sales peak and plateau, competition intensifies

Decline

Sales fall as tastes or technology move on

Maturity is usually the most profitable stage, since development costs are long since paid off, but it's also when competitors are most likely to be directly copying or undercutting a successful product.

Extension strategies

Rather than simply letting a product decline, a business can use an extension strategy to prolong its life and push sales back upward.

  • Updating packaging — a visual refresh can make an established product feel current again.
  • Adding new features — giving existing customers a genuine reason to consider the product again.
  • Changing the target market — repositioning a product towards a different segment entirely (see 3.5.2).
  • Advertising — a renewed promotional push can remind lapsed customers the product still exists.
  • Price reduction — lowering the price can win back price-sensitive customers who had switched away.
In the real world: Lucozade was originally marketed in the UK as a drink for sick children recovering from illness, sold in a medicinal-looking bottle. Facing decline as that market shrank, it was repositioned in the 1980s as an energy drink for athletes and active young people — a genuine change of target market combined with new branding and advertising — turning a declining product into one of the UK's best-known sports drink brands for decades afterward.
Managing many products at once

Product portfolio and the Boston Matrix

Most businesses sell more than one product, at different stages of the life cycle simultaneously. The Boston Matrix is a tool for categorising a whole product portfolio by market growth and market share, helping a business decide where to invest, where to hold steady, and where to consider withdrawing.

Stars

High growth, high market share — worth continued investment as tomorrow's main earners.

Question marks

High growth, low market share — an uncertain future, needing investment or a decision to withdraw.

Cash cows

Low growth, high market share — reliably profitable, funding investment elsewhere in the portfolio.

Dogs

Low growth, low market share — typically the first candidates for withdrawal.

In the real world: large consumer goods companies such as Unilever manage huge portfolios of brands simultaneously — well-established, reliably profitable brands act as cash cows funding investment in newer or higher-growth products elsewhere in the business, some of which may still be question marks whose long-term success isn't yet guaranteed.
Getting the message out

Promotion

Promotion covers every method a business uses to communicate with customers about a product, and AQA expects you to recognise a wide range of specific methods rather than just "advertising" in general.

Advertising

Paid promotion through newspapers, magazines, television, the internet or billboards, aimed at building awareness across a wide audience.

Public relations (PR)

Managing a business's public image and reputation, often through media coverage that isn't directly paid for like an advert.

Sales promotion

Short-term incentives — 2-for-1 offers, free gifts, samples, coupons and competitions — designed to boost sales quickly.

Point of sale displays

Eye-catching displays positioned where the customer actually makes their buying decision, prompting an in-the-moment purchase.

Sponsorship

Associating a brand with an event, team or individual to build image and reach an audience that shares an interest with the sponsored activity.

Social media

Direct, often two-way promotion and engagement (see 3.2.1 and 3.3.4), increasingly central to how many businesses reach customers today.

Choosing the right promotional mix

Which combination of these methods a business actually uses depends on several factors: how much finance is available, what competitors are already doing, the nature of the specific product and market, and — echoing the whole of this topic — precisely who the target market is and how they're best reached.

Reasons for promotion

  • To inform or remind customers a product exists and what it offers
  • To create or increase sales directly
  • To create or change the image customers hold of a product
  • To persuade customers to choose this product over a competitor's
In the real world: Red Bull has built much of its brand image through extensive sponsorship of extreme sports events and athletes, deliberately associating the drink with energy, adrenaline and adventure rather than relying purely on conventional advertising. UK supermarkets regularly run 2-for-1 offers and coupon promotions specifically to boost short-term sales volumes, particularly around key seasonal shopping periods.
Getting the product to the customer

Place

Place covers the distribution channels a business uses to get its product into the hands of customers.

Retailers

Sell directly to the final customer, often the most visible link in the chain (see the wholesaler/retailer distinction from 3.1.1).

Wholesalers

Buy in bulk from producers and sell smaller quantities on to retailers, a role you first met with Booker back in 3.1.1.

Telesales

Selling directly to customers over the phone, without a physical retail location involved at all.

E-commerce and m-commerce, covered in full back in 3.2.1, are now central to how many businesses handle place — letting a business reach customers directly online without relying on a traditional retailer at all, though as you saw there, this brings its own benefits and risks alongside the traditional channels above.

Bringing it all together

The integrated nature of the marketing mix

The four Ps aren't four separate decisions made in isolation — they only work when they agree with each other. A product with a premium USP and a carefully built brand image needs pricing that matches (skimming, not a loss leader), promotion that reinforces exclusivity rather than mass-market discount offers, and distribution through outlets consistent with that image, not simply the cheapest shelf space available. Get one P badly out of step with the others — a premium product sold through a discount promotion, for example — and the whole mix can undermine itself.

The mix also isn't fixed once and forgotten. As a product moves through the life cycle stages above, the right marketing mix genuinely changes — a product entering decline might combine a price reduction, an extension strategy, and a shift in target market all at once, precisely because the mix that worked during growth no longer fits a product trying to survive maturity or decline.

Exam practice

Have a go

2 marks

State two pricing methods a business might use.

Structure guide: two correctly identified methods — 1 mark each, no explanation required.

Case study — Larkhall Games: Larkhall Games launched "Kingdom Quest," a board game, five years ago. Sales grew quickly in the first two years but have started to decline recently, with several long-standing customers saying the game feels dated. A new competitor has just entered the market with a similar, cheaper board game priced well below Kingdom Quest. Larkhall Games is considering releasing an updated "anniversary edition" with new artwork, extra game pieces and updated rules, alongside a possible change to its pricing.
4 marks

State and explain two reasons why sales of Kingdom Quest might be declining.

Structure guide: state a reason grounded in the case study (1 mark), explain it (1 mark) — repeated twice over.

6 marks

Analyse how releasing an anniversary edition might affect Kingdom Quest's position in its product life cycle.

Structure guide: a single developed line of reasoning grounded in the case study (eg: the anniversary edition combines new artwork and features, a genuine extension strategy → this directly addresses long-standing customers' complaint that the game feels dated → potentially attracting renewed interest and pushing sales back upward → moving Kingdom Quest away from decline and extending its time in a more profitable stage of the life cycle).

9 marks

Recommend whether Larkhall Games should reduce Kingdom Quest's price to compete with the new rival, release the anniversary edition, or do both. Justify your answer using the case study.

Structure guide: a "recommend" question needs a justified judgement — weigh whether the new rival is winning sales on price alone or because Kingdom Quest genuinely feels dated, since a price cut and a genuine product refresh address different underlying problems, before reaching a clear final recommendation.

Key terms

Glossary

Marketing mix
The combination of price, product, promotion and place decisions a business makes.
Price skimming
Setting a high launch price that falls over time.
Price penetration
Setting a deliberately low price to enter a market and build share quickly.
Loss leader
A product priced very low, even below cost, to attract customers who then buy other items.
Unique selling point (USP)
A genuine feature or benefit that differentiates a product from competitors.
Product life cycle
The stages a product moves through: R&D, introduction, growth, maturity and decline.
Extension strategy
An action taken to prolong a product's life and delay decline.
Boston Matrix
A tool categorising products by market growth and market share into stars, cash cows, question marks and dogs.
