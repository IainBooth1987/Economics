Identifying and Understanding Customers

Every decision in marketing — what to sell, at what price, where, and how to promote it — is only as good as the understanding of the customer sitting behind it.

This opens Marketing, the functional area concerned with understanding customers well enough to sell to them profitably.
The foundation of marketing

Why this comes first in Topic 3.5

Everything else in this topic — segmentation (3.5.2), market research (3.5.3), and the marketing mix (3.5.4) — is really just a set of tools for doing one thing well: understanding what customers actually want, and giving it to them better than a competitor would. Get this starting point wrong, and no amount of clever pricing or promotion later on can fully rescue it.

This also brings back the needs-versus-wants distinction from 3.1.1. A need is something a customer must have; a want is something they desire but could live without. Successful businesses rarely just identify needs — they understand which specific wants their particular customers have, in enough detail to design something those customers will genuinely choose over an alternative.

Five concrete reasons

The importance of identifying and satisfying customer needs

AQA identifies five specific reasons why understanding customers matters, and each is worth treating as its own idea rather than a single vague benefit.

1

Provide a product or service customers will actually buy

A business can design the most technically impressive product in the world, but if it doesn't match what customers genuinely want, it simply won't sell. Understanding customers first means designing towards demand that already exists, rather than hoping demand will appear afterwards.

2

Increase sales

A business that understands precisely what its customers value can tailor its product, service and messaging to appeal more strongly than a generic offer would — directly translating into more sales from the same number of potential customers.

3

Select the correct marketing mix

The right price, product, place and promotion (the "marketing mix" you'll meet properly in 3.5.4) all depend entirely on who the customer actually is. A premium product aimed at customers who value exclusivity needs a completely different mix to a budget product aimed at customers who value low prices above all else — and a business can only make that call correctly once it understands its customers.

4

Avoid costly mistakes

Developing, producing and marketing a product all cost real money before a single sale has even happened. If that spending was based on a misjudged assumption about what customers wanted, the entire investment can be lost — a vivid, expensive example of the opportunity cost you first met in 3.1.1.

5

Be competitive

In the competitive markets you studied in 3.2.6, understanding customers better than rivals do is often the clearest way to differentiate — winning and keeping customers even when a competitor is offering something broadly similar.

Learning from real cases

What happens when a business gets this wrong — or right

The clearest way to understand why this topic matters is to look at what happens at the two extremes: a business that launches based on a genuine misjudgement of its customers, and one that responds precisely to a demand its customers were already expressing.

Getting it wrong

In 1985, Coca-Cola changed its secret formula and launched "New Coke" after taste tests suggested people preferred the new, sweeter flavour. What the tests failed to capture was how emotionally attached customers were to the original taste and brand itself — the backlash was so severe that the original formula was brought back within three months as "Coca-Cola Classic." The company had understood taste preference in a laboratory setting, but not the deeper relationship customers had with the product.

Getting it right

Greggs launched its Vegan Sausage Roll in 2019 after recognising a clear and growing demand for plant-based food options among its customers. The launch generated huge social media attention and was widely reported as selling out in many stores within days, going on to become a genuinely significant, ongoing part of Greggs' regular menu rather than a short-lived gimmick.

In the real world: the Sinclair C5, a battery-powered electric vehicle launched in the UK in 1985, is another widely cited example of a product failing partly because its inventor misjudged what customers actually wanted from personal transport — low to the ground, exposed to weather, and with limited range, it raised safety concerns in traffic that outweighed its novelty, and the product was withdrawn within months. Netflix, by contrast, uses detailed data on what its subscribers actually watch to decide which new shows to commission, aiming to base major investment decisions on demonstrated customer preference rather than guesswork.

Exam practice

Have a go

2 marks

State two reasons why it is important for a business to identify and satisfy customer needs.

Structure guide: two correctly identified reasons — 1 mark each, no explanation required.

Case study — Bramble & Co: Bramble & Co, an independent bakery, is considering launching a range of premium sourdough loaves priced significantly higher than its existing bread, based mainly on the owner's own personal taste rather than any customer feedback. A nearby competitor recently introduced a gluten-free bread range after receiving repeated requests from customers, and has seen strong sales as a result.
4 marks

State and explain two risks Bramble & Co faces by launching its new sourdough range without first researching customer demand.

Structure guide: state a risk grounded in the case study (1 mark), explain it (1 mark) — repeated twice over.

6 marks

Analyse why the competitor's gluten-free range is likely to be more successful than Bramble & Co's proposed sourdough range.

Structure guide: a single developed line of reasoning grounded in the case study (eg: the competitor's gluten-free range was introduced in direct response to repeated customer requests, meaning demand was already confirmed before launch → whereas Bramble & Co's sourdough range is based on the owner's personal taste rather than any evidence of customer demand → increasing the risk that Bramble & Co has misjudged what its customers actually want → potentially leading to disappointing sales despite the higher price charged).

9 marks

Recommend what Bramble & Co should do before launching its new sourdough range. Justify your answer using the case study.

Structure guide: a "recommend" question needs a justified judgement — weigh the cost and time of researching customer demand first against the risk of launching on personal taste alone, using the competitor's successful evidence-based approach as a point of comparison, before reaching a clear final recommendation.

Key terms

Glossary

Customer need
Something a customer requires, which a business aims to identify and satisfy through its products or services.
Marketing mix
The combination of price, product, place and promotion decisions a business makes, covered in full in 3.5.4.
Opportunity cost
The value of the next best alternative given up when a choice is made (see 3.1.1) — relevant here when spending on a poorly understood product wastes resources that could have gone elsewhere.
