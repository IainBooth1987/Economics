Customer Needs

Without customers, a business is just an expensive hobby — everything from here on in Theme 1 builds on genuinely understanding what customers actually need.

This opens Topic 1.2, moving from why enterprise exists (1.1) into the practical skill of spotting a genuine opportunity.
Starting to spot an opportunity

Why this opens Topic 1.2

Topic 1.1 established why business enterprise exists at all — to meet customer needs, among other purposes (see 1.1.3). Topic 1.2 now gets practical: how does a business actually identify and understand exactly what those needs are, rather than just assuming? This sub-topic starts with the most basic building block of all — what a customer need actually is.

A customer need is something a consumer requires or wants from a product or service. If a business doesn't provide what the customer needs, the customer will simply take their money elsewhere — which is exactly why understanding customer needs sits right at the start of this whole topic, before market research (1.2.2), segmentation (1.2.3) and the competitive environment (1.2.4) build on top of it.

Four things every customer weighs up

What customer needs are

Price

How much a customer is willing and able to pay for a product or service.

Quality

The standard, reliability and durability a customer expects from what they're buying.

Choice

The range and variety of options available to a customer to pick between.

Convenience

How easy and accessible a product or service is to actually get hold of — echoing the value-adding role of convenience from 1.1.3.

Very few customers weigh up just one of these on its own. A shopper choosing where to buy their groceries is usually balancing all four at once — is it cheap enough, good enough quality, is there enough choice on the shelf, and is the shop actually convenient to get to? Different businesses succeed by getting a different balance of these four right for their specific target customer, rather than trying to win on all four simultaneously.

In the real world: discount retailers such as Aldi and Primark compete primarily by meeting the price need, offering lower prices than many rivals even if this sometimes means a narrower product range. Large supermarkets such as Tesco compete heavily on choice, stocking thousands of different product lines under one roof. Deliveroo and other food delivery apps are built almost entirely around meeting the convenience need, saving customers a trip they'd otherwise have to make themselves.
Why getting this right matters

The importance of identifying and understanding customer needs

Generating sales

A product or service that genuinely matches what customers need is simply more likely to be bought — understanding needs directly drives revenue.

Business survival

Businesses that fail to understand their customers risk losing them to competitors who do, which can threaten the survival of the business over time — directly echoing the business failure risk from 1.1.2.

These two reasons connect closely to ideas you've already met. Generating sales links back to the purpose of business activity in 1.1.3 — a business only meets customer needs, and therefore only generates sales, if it genuinely understands what those needs are in the first place. And business survival is a direct consequence of getting this wrong: a business that consistently misjudges what its customers want will eventually find itself losing out to rivals who understand the market better, no matter how good its original idea seemed.

Exam practice

Have a go

Standalone questions below are typical of Section A — no case study needed. The 6 and 9-mark questions are built around a Source Booklet–style case study, matching how Section B and C actually work in the real exam.

1 mark
Select one answer

Which one of the following is one of the four main customer needs?

A. Location
B. Convenience
C. Profit
D. Branding

Answer: B.

2 marks
Outline

Outline one reason why it is important for a business to understand its customers' needs.

Structure guide: two linked points — eg "Understanding customer needs helps a business generate sales (1), since a product that matches what customers want is more likely to be bought (1)." Points must connect; unlinked points cap the mark at 1.

3 marks
Explain

Explain one customer need a business must consider when selling its product.

Structure guide: 1 mark identifying a need, plus 2 further marks developing it — eg "Price is a customer need (1), as customers will compare how much a product costs against similar products from rivals (1), meaning a business that prices too high risks losing customers to a cheaper competitor (1)."

Case study — Elm Street Deli: Elm Street Deli is a small independent food shop. Since a large supermarket opened nearby, offering lower prices and a wider product range, footfall at Elm Street Deli has fallen noticeably. The owner, Farah, is considering two options: cutting her prices to compete directly with the supermarket, or extending her opening hours until 11pm to offer greater convenience for customers who can't get to the supermarket before it closes.
6 marks
AnalyseSection B style

Analyse the impact on Elm Street Deli of the new supermarket meeting more of its customers' price and choice needs.

Structure guide: application (AO2) and analysis (AO3a) together — eg identify that the supermarket's lower prices and wider range directly meet two of the four customer needs better than Elm Street Deli currently can (AO2), then analyse how this explains the fall in footfall, and that unless Farah responds, this trend is likely to continue and could threaten the survival of her business (AO3a).

9 marks
JustifySection C style

Farah is considering two options: cutting prices to compete directly with the supermarket, or extending opening hours to offer greater convenience. Justify which option Farah should choose.

Structure guide: apply knowledge to Farah's specific situation (AO2), analyse points on both sides (AO3a) — cutting prices directly challenges the supermarket on a need it already meets well, which is risky for a small shop with less buying power, while extending hours targets a need (convenience) the supermarket doesn't meet outside its own opening times — then reach a clear, justified choice (AO3b).
