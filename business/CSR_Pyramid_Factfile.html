Carroll's CSR Pyramid (1991)

Developed by Archie Carroll in 1991, the CSR Pyramid argues that businesses have four distinct but interconnected responsibilities to society. These form a hierarchy — economic viability is the prerequisite foundation, with each successive layer building upon the one below. Click each level of the pyramid to explore its meaning, obligations, and evaluation points.

The Four Responsibilities at a Glance

📈 Economic — Be Profitable

Obligation level: Required
Produce goods and services that society wants. Generate profit for shareholders. Maintain competitive position. Provide employment. Without economic viability, no other responsibility can be fulfilled.

⚖️ Legal — Obey the Law

Obligation level: Required
Comply with all laws and regulations — employment law, environmental regulations, consumer protection, contract law, financial reporting. Society's codified minimum standard for business conduct.

⚖️ Ethical — Do What Is Right

Obligation level: Expected
Act according to moral standards even when not legally required. Treat all stakeholders fairly. Avoid practices that exploit loopholes. Respond to emerging societal norms before the law catches up.

🆕 Philanthropic — Be a Good Citizen

Obligation level: Desired (discretionary)
Make charitable donations. Support education and the arts. Run employee volunteer programmes. Contribute to community wellbeing. Society desires this but does not require it — companies are not unethical for abstaining.

The hierarchy mattersThe pyramid shape is not decorative — it is conceptual. Economic and legal responsibilities are prerequisites: a company that is insolvent or breaking the law cannot meaningfully fulfil ethical or philanthropic obligations. Carroll emphasised that fulfilling lower levels enables, not prevents, fulfilment of higher levels.
Economic Responsibilities — Real Examples
Economic — Be Profitable
Apple Inc.

Apple's relentless focus on premium pricing, ecosystem lock-in, and supply chain efficiency has generated over $100bn in annual profit. This economic success funds billions in R&D, supports 2.4 million US jobs in the supply chain, and generates tax revenues — demonstrating Carroll's argument that economic responsibility is the foundation all other responsibilities rest on.

Economic — Creating Employment
Amazon UK

Amazon employs over 75,000 permanent staff in the UK across fulfilment centres, tech hubs, and offices, plus hundreds of thousands of delivery and temporary workers. Critics argue its employment practices are exploitative (high pressure, surveillance, anti-union); defenders point to the economic value created for workers, communities, and consumers — illustrating the tension within economic responsibility itself.

Economic Responsibility Failing
Thomas Cook (2019)

Thomas Cook's collapse illustrated Carroll's foundational argument dramatically: once economic viability failed, all other responsibilities collapsed instantly. 21,000 jobs were lost, customers were stranded, and decades of community relationships dissolved overnight. Economic sustainability is not optional — it is the platform without which nothing else is possible.

Legal Responsibilities — Real Examples
Legal — Compliance Failure
Volkswagen Emissions Scandal

VW deliberately installed software to cheat emissions tests in 11 million vehicles globally (2015). The resulting fines exceeded $30bn, executive prosecutions followed, and VW's share price fell 30%. A textbook legal responsibility violation — and a demonstration that short-term economic gains from non-compliance destroy shareholder value far more substantially over time.

Legal — Employment Law
Sports Direct (Mike Ashley era)

Sports Direct was found to be paying warehouse workers below the National Minimum Wage, with excessive security checks unpaid. HMRC pursued back pay claims, parliamentary committees investigated, and reputational damage was significant. This case illustrates how legal compliance failures cascade into ethical and reputational damage far beyond the initial legal penalty.

Legal — Compliance Excellence
Unilever

Unilever publishes detailed compliance reports covering anti-bribery, human rights due diligence, and environmental regulatory adherence across 190+ countries. Its legal compliance function is treated as a competitive advantage — demonstrating responsible global supply chain management that protects both the company and its stakeholders.

Ethical Responsibilities — Real Examples
Ethical — Going Beyond the Law
Patagonia

Patagonia donates 1% of sales to environmental causes, uses sustainable materials well beyond regulatory requirements, and offers transparent supply chain mapping. Its "Don't Buy This Jacket" campaign actively discouraged over-consumption. These actions go far beyond legal requirements — they represent ethical commitments that have simultaneously built fierce brand loyalty and a premium pricing capability.

Ethical Responsibility Failure
Philip Morris (tobacco)

For decades, tobacco companies actively suppressed research on smoking health risks and targeted advertising at young people — actions that were largely legal at the time but widely regarded as ethical failures. This illustrates Carroll's distinction: the law is often insufficient and slow. Ethical responsibilities require businesses to ask "is it right?" not just "is it legal?"

Ethical — Fair Treatment
John Lewis Partnership

The John Lewis Partnership model gives employees ("Partners") a share of profits and genuine voice in governance decisions. This structure embeds ethical responsibility into the business model itself — workers are treated as stakeholders with ownership rights, not just cost factors to minimise. Carroll would classify this as strong ethical responsibility operationalised through governance.

Philanthropic Responsibilities — Real Examples
Philanthropic — Community Investment
Greggs Foundation

The Greggs Foundation funds breakfast clubs across deprived UK communities, hospice care, and community projects. Greggs' philanthropic activity is genuine and substantial — but note that it sits atop strong economic foundations (profitable bakery chain) and sound ethical practices. Without the lower pyramid levels, the Greggs Foundation would have no resources to deploy.

Philanthropic — Large Scale
Tesco / Bags of Help

Tesco's "Bags of Help" initiative distributes millions of pounds annually to local community projects voted for by customers. While this is genuine philanthropic contribution, critics note it simultaneously serves brand loyalty goals — raising the question of whether truly strategic philanthropy can be separated from commercial self-interest.

Philanthropic Washing?
Amazon & Climate Pledge

Amazon's $2bn Climate Pledge Fund funds clean energy projects. Critics argue this philanthropy diverts attention from Amazon's own supply chain emissions, warehouse conditions, and tax arrangements — suggesting philanthropic activity cannot substitute for ethical and legal compliance. Carroll's hierarchy implies this ordering too: philanthropy cannot compensate for failures at lower levels.

Critiques of Carroll's CSR Pyramid

Carroll's pyramid has been enormously influential since 1991, but it has attracted significant academic and business criticism. Understanding these critiques — and being able to evaluate them — is essential for A-level extended responses.

1. The Hierarchy Is Artificially Rigid

Carroll presents economic responsibility as the necessary prerequisite for all others — but in practice, firms regularly make ethical decisions that reduce short-term profitability. Patagonia's sustainable materials cost more than conventional alternatives. Ben & Jerry's paid above-market wages before it was profitable to do so. The pyramid implies a strict sequencing that real businesses do not follow.

Counter-argument: Carroll's hierarchy reflects long-run relationships, not short-run trade-offs. A business that consistently sacrifices economic viability for ethics will eventually cease to exist — and with it, all its ethical and philanthropic activity. Patagonia's ethical spending is sustainable precisely because its brand premium generates the profits to fund it.

2. Shareholder Primacy at the Foundation

Placing profitability and shareholder return at the base reflects Milton Friedman's shareholder primacy model. Critics argue this is outdated — Freeman's Stakeholder Theory (1984) proposed that shareholders are merely one stakeholder among many. The Business Roundtable's 2019 Statement of Corporate Purpose, signed by 200 CEOs, explicitly moved away from shareholder primacy toward stakeholder inclusion.

Counter-argument: Carroll's economic foundation need not be interpreted as shareholder primacy. Profitable operations benefit employees (wages), suppliers (payments), communities (tax), and customers (product availability). Economic sustainability is a prerequisite for all stakeholder value creation — not just shareholder returns.

3. Philanthropy as a Distraction

The pyramid's structure risks implying that philanthropic activity — the most visible and easily communicated CSR action — can substitute for ethical or legal compliance. Companies with significant ESG failures (Amazon, fast fashion brands) invest heavily in charitable programmes. Critics argue this creates "philanthropy washing" — where visible generosity distracts from structural harm.

Counter-argument: Carroll explicitly warned against this. The pyramid hierarchy means philanthropy should complement — never compensate for — lower-level obligations. Responsibility for misuse of the framework lies with practitioners, not with the model's architecture.

4. Culturally Specific and Western

Carroll developed the pyramid in a US context. Research by Visser (2006) found that in developing economies, the priority order differs significantly — philanthropic and ethical responsibilities rank higher relative to legal compliance (which may be weakly enforced), while economic survival concerns differ in context. The model may not translate across cultures and governance environments.

Counter-argument: Carroll's 2004 revised model acknowledged this limitation and moved towards a more contextual understanding. The pyramid is best understood as a conceptual framework for discussion rather than a universal prescription.

5. Static — Fails to Capture Systemic Issues

The pyramid frames CSR as a set of discrete categories of obligation, not as a systemic relationship between business and society. Climate change, inequality, and digital privacy are systemic issues that do not map neatly onto economic/legal/ethical/philanthropic categories. Frameworks like Porter and Kramer's Shared Value (2011) and the UN SDGs offer more dynamic, integrated approaches.

Counter-argument: The pyramid's enduring value is precisely its simplicity. It provides a memorable, accessible framework for classifying business responsibilities — a starting point for analysis rather than a complete model of business-society interaction.
A-level evaluation tipThe strongest extended answers do not simply list critiques — they evaluate their weight. A structural criticism (hierarchy too rigid) that has been addressed in later literature is less damaging to the model's utility than a practical criticism (philanthropy washing is actively harmful) that remains largely unresolved. Use critiques to build an argument about the model's overall usefulness, not just its flaws.
Alternative CSR Frameworks

Carroll's pyramid sits within a broader ecosystem of CSR frameworks. Understanding the differences helps in evaluation — and demonstrates command of the topic beyond the specification minimum.

⚖️
Stakeholder Theory
R. Edward Freeman — 1984

Core argument: Businesses should create value for all stakeholders — employees, customers, suppliers, communities, and shareholders — not maximise returns for shareholders at the expense of others.

  • Shareholders are one stakeholder group among many
  • Long-term sustainability requires balancing all stakeholder interests
  • Contradicts the traditional Friedmanite "shareholder primacy" model
vs. Carroll: Stakeholder theory rejects the shareholder-centric economic foundation of Carroll's pyramid entirely. It is a theory of who matters to a business; Carroll's pyramid is a classification of what responsibilities a business has. They are complementary rather than competing.
⚖️
Triple Bottom Line (TBL)
John Elkington — 1994

Core argument: Business performance should be measured across three dimensions simultaneously — People, Planet, and Profit. No single dimension should be optimised at the expense of the others.

  • People: social equity, fair wages, community impact
  • Planet: environmental stewardship, resource consumption, carbon footprint
  • Profit: financial viability and shareholder return
vs. Carroll: TBL rejects the hierarchical ordering of Carroll's pyramid — it treats all three dimensions as simultaneously and equally important. Notably, Elkington himself "recalled" the TBL concept in 2018, arguing it had become a box-ticking accounting exercise rather than a genuine challenge to shareholder primacy.
📈
Creating Shared Value (CSV)
Michael Porter & Mark Kramer — 2011

Core argument: The most powerful form of CSR is when social benefit is directly integrated into the core business model — creating value for both the business and society simultaneously, rather than treating social responsibility as a tax or add-on.

  • CSR as strategy, not obligation or charity
  • Addressing social needs can expand markets and reduce costs
  • Social impact embedded in value chain — not separated from it
vs. Carroll: CSV is more strategically ambitious than Carroll's pyramid. It challenges the separation implicit in Carroll's model between economic objectives and social responsibilities — arguing they should be fused. Critics of CSV (including Carroll) argue it ignores existing obligations to stakeholders in its pursuit of strategic opportunity.
🌐
ESG Reporting
UN Principles for Responsible Investment — 2006 onwards

Core argument: Investors need standardised, comparable data on Environmental, Social, and Governance performance to make informed investment decisions. ESG provides a measurement and disclosure framework.

  • Environmental: carbon emissions, water usage, biodiversity impact
  • Social: labour practices, supply chain conditions, community relations
  • Governance: board composition, executive pay, anti-corruption
vs. Carroll: ESG is a measurement and investment framework; Carroll's pyramid is a conceptual model of responsibility types. ESG data operationalises some of Carroll's categories but is primarily investor-facing rather than a theory of what businesses owe to society. Critics note ESG ratings are inconsistent across providers and easily "greenwashed."
★
B Corporation Certification
B Lab — 2006

Core argument: Legal structure should embed social responsibility into corporate governance, making stakeholder consideration a legal obligation rather than a discretionary management choice.

  • Directors legally required to consider impact on all stakeholders
  • Rigorous third-party verification against social and environmental standards
  • Public transparency requirements
vs. Carroll: B Corp takes Carroll's ethical and philanthropic levels and makes them legally mandatory — effectively elevating them from "expected/desired" to "required" in Carroll's framework. UK certified B Corps include The Big Issue, The Body Shop, and Innocent Drinks. Critics argue B Corp status is more accessible to smaller firms and can be a marketing exercise for large corporations.
Synthesising the frameworksNo single framework is complete. Modern CSR practice often blends them: Carroll's pyramid provides conceptual structure; ESG provides measurable metrics; SDGs provide specific global targets; Stakeholder Theory broadens the definition of who matters; CSV pushes towards strategic integration. A business genuinely committed to CSR draws on all of them — and a strong A-level answer acknowledges this complexity.

Common Misconceptions
The Framework Structure

Misconception: "The pyramid shows that economic responsibility is the most important"

Students interpret "foundation" as meaning most important or most valued.

Reality: The pyramid's hierarchy reflects sequential dependency, not importance ranking. Economic responsibility is at the base because all other responsibilities require economic viability to function — not because profit is more morally important than ethical behaviour. A profitable business breaking the law is failing; a marginally profitable business behaving ethically is succeeding. Position in the hierarchy indicates prerequisite status, not moral priority.

Misconception: "Philanthropic activities make a company more socially responsible than legal compliance"

Students treat philanthropic activities as the "best" or most impressive form of CSR because they are most visible.

Reality: Carroll's framework implies the opposite — a company that donates millions to charity while exploiting workers or evading taxes is a net negative contributor to society. Philanthropy at the top of the pyramid depends on solid foundations. A company excelling at the lower levels (fair treatment, legal compliance, sound economics) contributes far more to social welfare than one that neglects these while pursuing philanthropic glory.

Misconception: "Ethical and philanthropic responsibilities are optional extras"

Students classify anything above "legal" as optional and therefore unimportant.

Reality: Carroll distinguished carefully: ethical responsibilities are expected — companies that routinely act unethically (but legally) face consumer boycotts, talent loss, regulatory action, and reputational damage. They are not legally required but they are strongly socially required. Philanthropic responsibilities are truly discretionary — not ethically expected. The distinction between "expected" (ethical) and "desired" (philanthropic) is an important exam point.
Applying the Framework

Misconception: "Paying the National Minimum Wage is an ethical responsibility"

Students place legal minimum pay compliance in the ethical layer.

Reality: Paying the National Minimum Wage is a legal responsibility — it is required by law. Paying above the National Living Wage, or adopting the voluntary Real Living Wage, goes beyond legal requirements and therefore sits in the ethical layer. The distinction between what the law requires and what morality expects is the defining boundary between Carroll's second and third levels.

Misconception: "A company that is very profitable cannot be socially responsible"

Students treat profitability and CSR as inherently in tension.

Reality: Carroll's framework explicitly argues the opposite — economic responsibility is the foundation of CSR, not its antithesis. Porter and Kramer's "Creating Shared Value" goes further, arguing that the most powerful forms of CSR are those that simultaneously improve business performance. Companies like Unilever and Patagonia demonstrate that ethical leadership and strong financial performance can be mutually reinforcing.

Misconception: "CSR is primarily about charitable donations"

Students equate the entire CSR concept with philanthropy.

Reality: Charitable donations represent only one layer — and the smallest, most discretionary one — in Carroll's framework. The vast majority of a company's CSR impact comes from how it conducts its core business: how it treats employees (ethical), how it handles environmental compliance (legal), how transparently it reports finances (legal and ethical), and how sustainably it manages its supply chain (ethical). Philanthropy is the visible tip of a much larger iceberg.
Evaluation Errors

Misconception: "Carroll's pyramid proves businesses should prioritise social welfare over profit"

Students misread the framework as an argument for business to serve society before shareholders.

Reality: Carroll was explicit that economic viability is the foundation — without profit, a business cannot fulfil any other responsibilities. The pyramid does not argue against profit-making; it contextualises profit within a broader set of obligations. The argument is that profit-making must be conducted within legal, ethical, and ultimately philanthropic constraints — not that it should be abandoned in favour of social welfare.

Misconception: "All four responsibilities are always equally achievable for every business"

Students apply the framework universally without contextual adaptation.

Reality: Context matters enormously. A start-up burning through cash may legitimately defer significant philanthropic activity. A small business in a highly competitive market may lack the margins to offer voluntary enhanced pay. A multinational operating in countries with weak rule of law faces genuine legal compliance dilemmas. Strong evaluation acknowledges that the pyramid's applicability varies significantly by business size, sector, profitability, and operating environment.
