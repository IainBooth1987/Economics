        Exam-Style Positioning Statement
        The STP Process

        STP (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning) is the strategic framework that links market analysis to the marketing mix. Rather than trying to appeal to everyone equally, STP helps businesses identify who to serve, with what, and how to be seen differently from competitors.

        1

        Segmentation

        Divide the total market into distinct groups of customers who share common characteristics or needs. Each segment should be internally homogeneous but different from other segments.

        2

        Targeting

        Evaluate each segment and decide which to serve. The choice depends on segment size, growth potential, competitive intensity and fit with business capabilities. Use the SWARM criteria.

        3

        Positioning

        Design the product, price, promotion and place so that the target segment perceives the offering as distinctly valuable — occupying a clear, differentiated space in their minds relative to competitors.

        The Four Segmentation Bases

        Demographic Segmentation

        Divides the market using measurable population characteristics. The most commonly used base because data is widely available and easy to apply.

        • Age: Under-25s vs over-65s have very different needs and media habits
        • Income: High-income segments can support premium pricing strategies
        • Occupation: Professionals vs students vs manual workers
        • Family status: Singles, young families, empty-nesters each have distinct spending priorities

        UK example: Saga targets over-50s with insurance, travel and financial products specifically designed around retirement income and lifestyle.

        Geographic Segmentation

        Divides the market by location — from broad regional differences to hyperlocal neighbourhood targeting.

        • Regional: North/South divide in income, brand loyalty and retail preferences
        • Urban vs rural: Affects distribution, product range and communication channels
        • Local climate/culture: Barbour targets rural and coastal UK communities with heritage outerwear
        • Population density: High-footfall urban sites suit Greggs; suburban sites suit B&Q

        UK example: Morrisons over-indexes in northern England and Yorkshire, reflecting its Bradford origins and regional customer loyalty.

        Psychographic Segmentation

        Groups customers by attitudes, values, lifestyle and personality — moving beyond demographics to understand the deeper motivations behind purchase decisions.

        • Values: Patagonia targets environmentally conscious consumers willing to pay a premium for ethical credentials
        • Lifestyle: Gym-goers, commuters, home-workers have different product needs even at the same income level
        • Attitudes: Risk-takers vs security seekers; early adopters vs laggards
        • Social class: Still relevant in UK market — influences brand preference in fashion, food and automotive

        UK example: Waitrose targets customers who identify as quality-conscious and aspirational, regardless of actual income level.

        Behavioural Segmentation

        Divides by actual consumer behaviour — purchase frequency, loyalty level, usage patterns and benefit sought. Often considered the most actionable base.

        • Purchase occasion: Everyday vs gifting vs special occasion buyers have different price sensitivities
        • Usage rate: Heavy, medium and light users often warrant different marketing approaches
        • Brand loyalty: Loyal customers have high lifetime value; switchers respond to promotions
        • Benefits sought: Convenience, value, quality, prestige — each requires different positioning

        UK example: Tesco Clubcard segments by actual shopping behaviour, enabling personalised pricing and promotions based on what each customer actually buys.

        UK Brand Positioning — Case Studies
        Waitrose

        Primary segmentation: Psychographic (quality-conscious, aspirational) + Income (ABC1 households)

        PremiumLifestyleBritish sourcing

        Positions on quality, provenance and shopping experience. The "John Lewis of supermarkets" — customers feel the premium is justified by product quality and staff service.

        "Quality food, honestly priced" — differentiates from Harrods Food Hall (unaffordably premium) and M&S Food (special occasions only).
        Greggs

        Primary segmentation: Demographic (working adults, students, low-middle income) + Geographic (high streets, transport hubs, industrial estates)

        ValueConvenienceMass market

        Repositioned from traditional baker to accessible food brand. Vegan sausage roll launch in 2019 expanded into health-conscious segment while retaining core value positioning.

        Occupies the "affordable treat" position — not junk food, not fine dining. Deliberately broad targeting across income levels.
        Rolls-Royce

        Primary segmentation: Income (ultra-high net worth) + Psychographic (heritage, exclusivity, status)

        NicheUltra-premiumConcentrated

        Makes fewer than 6,000 cars per year globally. Deliberately restricts supply to maintain exclusivity. Customers wait months for bespoke configurations. Profits from scarcity.

        Occupies the extreme top-right of any positioning map — maximum price, maximum prestige. No credible direct competitor in the same space.
        Primark

        Primary segmentation: Demographic (16-35, lower-middle income) + Behavioural (fashion-conscious but price-sensitive)

        BudgetFashionNo online sales

        Deliberately targets the fashion-aware customer who cannot or will not spend on premium brands. No online sales — the low price depends on in-store volume and low logistics costs. Large high-street stores are the core asset.

        Positions as "fast fashion without the guilt of overspending" — democratic access to trend-led clothing.
        Burberry

        Primary segmentation: Income (high) + Psychographic (heritage, British identity, fashion authority)

        Heritage luxuryDifferentiatedGlobal British

        Recovered from mid-2000s brand dilution (overexposure of the check) by repositioning firmly at the top end. Now targets aspirational luxury buyers globally while anchoring identity in British heritage.

        The positioning challenge: British heritage must feel authentic globally. Competes with Gucci and Louis Vuitton, not just British brands.
        JD Wetherspoon

        Primary segmentation: Demographic (all ages, lower income) + Behavioural (value drinkers, food-led pub visits)

        ValueMass marketUndifferentiated

        Undifferentiated mass market strategy — identical format, menu and pricing across 800+ UK pubs. No music, sports TV or gimmicks. Competes purely on price. High volume, low margin per transaction.

        Positions as the cheapest sit-down food and drink option in most UK high streets — defended by massive scale economies in purchasing and property.
        SWARM Criteria — Evaluating Target Segments

        Before committing to a segment, businesses should evaluate it against the SWARM criteria. A segment that fails on any criterion is likely to be unprofitable or unachievable.

        SWARM — Five Tests for a Viable Target Segment

        Size

        Is the segment large enough to generate sufficient revenue? Rolls-Royce targets an ultra-small segment but at very high margin per unit.

        Worth

        Does the segment have the purchasing power to support the intended price point? Budget positioning requires volume to be worth pursuing.

        Accessible

        Can the business reach this segment effectively through its chosen channels? Gen Z requires social media; older demographics respond better to TV and print.

        Responsive

        Will the segment respond to the marketing mix? Luxury buyers reject discounting; value seekers respond strongly to promotional pricing.

        Measurable

        Can the segment be clearly defined and its characteristics measured? Psychographic segments are harder to measure than demographic ones.

        The Four Targeting Strategies

        Mass Market (Undifferentiated)

        One for all

        One product or service designed to appeal to the entire market. The same offering is marketed to all customers with a single message and pricing strategy.

        • Maximises economies of scale in production and marketing
        • Simple to manage — one brand, one message
        • Vulnerable to competitors who out-specialise on any segment
        • Works best where needs are genuinely universal
        UK examples: Greggs, JD Wetherspoon, Heinz Baked Beans, Royal Mail — products/services that serve essentially the same need for nearly everyone.

        Differentiated (Multi-Segment)

        Several targets

        The business targets multiple segments simultaneously with different products, brands or offers tailored to each one. Higher cost but broader market coverage.

        • Reduces dependence on any single segment
        • Captures more total market revenue
        • Higher marketing and production costs
        • Risk of brand dilution if sub-brands conflict
        UK examples: Unilever (Dove, Simple, Lynx all targeting different segments); Tesco (Metro, Extra, Express formats for different shopping occasions); Toyota (Aygo, Corolla, Land Cruiser).

        Niche (Concentrated)

        One segment

        The business focuses all its resources on one specific segment, aiming to become the dominant player in that niche. Higher margins from specialisation but higher risk from concentration.

        • Deep understanding of one customer group
        • Premium pricing often achievable through specialist reputation
        • Vulnerable if segment shrinks or competitor enters
        • Ideal for businesses with limited resources
        UK examples: Rolls-Royce (ultra-luxury cars only); Lush (ethical, handmade cosmetics); BrewDog (craft beer enthusiasts); Brompton Bicycle (urban commuter cyclists).

        Micro-Marketing (Individual)

        Personalised

        Uses data and technology to personalise the offering for individual customers or very small groups. The most precise form of targeting — essentially a segment of one.

        • Maximum relevance — customers feel personally understood
        • High customer lifetime value and loyalty
        • Requires significant data infrastructure and capability
        • Privacy concerns and GDPR compliance required
        UK examples: Tesco Clubcard (personalised offers based on actual purchase history); Amazon recommendations; Sky personalised TV packages; bespoke Rolls-Royce configurations.
        Choosing a Targeting Strategy — Decision Framework
        Key questions to determine the right strategy
        Does the business have limited financial and marketing resources?
        → Niche / Concentrated
        Do all customers have essentially the same needs from this product?
        → Mass / Undifferentiated
        Does the business have strong data capabilities and customer database?
        → Micro / Individual
        Does the business operate multiple product lines or brands?
        → Differentiated
        Is the product at an early stage in a niche market?
        → Niche, then expand to Differentiated as resources grow
        Is price competition fierce and margins thin?
        → Niche to escape price competition, or Mass for scale economies

        Positioning Strategies

        What is Positioning?

        Positioning is the deliberate creation of a distinct image or identity in the mind of the target customer, relative to competitors. It answers the question: "Why should I choose your brand over all alternatives?" Good positioning is clear, credible, distinctive and relevant to the target segment.

        Common Positioning Bases

        • Price/value positioning: Aldi and Lidl own "best value for money." Hard to dislodge once established — but vulnerable if quality perception falls.
        • Quality/premium positioning: Dyson, Waitrose and Rolls-Royce position on superior product quality. Supports premium pricing but requires continuous product investment.
        • Heritage/origin positioning: Burberry, Barbour and Fortnum & Mason leverage British heritage as a differentiator — particularly effective in export markets.
        • Lifestyle/values positioning: Patagonia (environmental responsibility), Lush (ethical cosmetics). Powerful but requires genuine brand behaviour alignment — "greenwashing" destroys the position.
        • Benefit/feature positioning: Dyson positions on cyclone technology; Brompton on portability. Works when the feature is genuinely unique and valued by the target segment.
        • Competitor repositioning: BrewDog built its position partly by attacking large mainstream brewers as bland and corporate. Risky — draws attention to competitors and can alienate mainstream buyers.
        Evaluation: Blue Ocean vs Red Ocean Strategy

        Red Ocean strategy means competing in existing market space — fighting for share in a crowded market with established rules. Blue Ocean strategy (Kim & Mauborgne) means creating entirely new market space where competition is irrelevant. Greggs entering the vegan food market was a blue ocean move within an otherwise red ocean market. The risk of blue ocean is that if the space proves profitable, competitors will follow quickly — turning the blue ocean red.

        AO3 — Analysis
        Analyse why Tesco uses a differentiated targeting strategy rather than a mass market approach.
        AO3 — Analysis
        Tesco uses differentiated targeting because supermarket customers have genuinely different needs — convenience shoppers use Tesco Express, weekly family shoppers use Tesco Extra, urban professionals use online delivery. A single mass market offer cannot satisfy all these simultaneously. Differentiated targeting allows Tesco to maximise revenue from each segment while reducing over-reliance on any one customer group.

        AO3 — Evaluation
        Evaluate whether a small UK fashion brand should pursue a niche targeting strategy.
        AO3 — Evaluation
        For: Limited resources are best concentrated on one segment. Premium niche positioning supports higher margins. Deep customer knowledge creates loyalty and word-of-mouth. Avoids direct price competition with mass market brands like Primark.

        Against: Dependence on one segment creates vulnerability — a shift in fashion trends or income shock in that segment could be fatal. Growth potential is limited. A successful niche attracts larger competitors with more resources.
        Examiner tip: always consider both sides and reach a justified conclusion based on context.

        AO1 — Knowledge
        Define psychographic segmentation and give one UK business example.
        AO1 — Knowledge
        Psychographic segmentation divides a market based on consumers' attitudes, values, lifestyle and personality traits rather than measurable demographic characteristics.

        Example: Waitrose segments psychographically by targeting consumers who see themselves as quality-conscious and discerning — even those whose income might allow them to shop at Aldi. The positioning appeals to identity and aspiration, not just income.

        AO3 — Evaluation
        Evaluate whether market segmentation always benefits a business.
        AO3 — Evaluation
        Benefits: Focused marketing spend, higher relevance to target customers, supports premium pricing through differentiation, reduces wasted resource on irrelevant audiences.

        Limitations: Segmentation requires significant research investment. Over-segmenting can make production and marketing too complex. Small segments may not generate enough revenue to justify separate strategies. For commodity products (salt, tap water) segmentation adds cost with little benefit.

        Conclusion: Segmentation benefits businesses where customer needs genuinely differ and where the business has sufficient resources to serve multiple segments effectively.

        Exam Tips & Evaluation Points
        STP & Positioning — Common Exam Mistakes
        • Always state which segmentation base a business is using before explaining it — vague answers about "knowing your customers" score nothing
        • Distinguish clearly between segmentation (dividing the market) and targeting (choosing which segments to serve) — they are different steps
        • On positioning maps, identify the axes first and explain what each end represents before discussing where brands sit
        • When evaluating targeting strategies, always consider both the advantages and the risks — examiner mark schemes reward balanced analysis
        • Link SWARM criteria to the specific business context: "this segment fails the Accessible criterion because the target demographic does not use social media"
        • Micro-marketing requires data infrastructure — always mention GDPR and data privacy as a constraint in UK contexts
        Evaluation: Does the STP framework always work?

        STP assumes businesses can clearly identify segments, choose one or more to target, and control their positioning through the marketing mix. In practice, customer behaviour is messier — segments overlap, consumers hold contradictory preferences, and competitor actions can disrupt carefully planned positions. Greggs did not "plan" to become a cultural icon or a vegan brand — these emerged from market response. The framework is a useful planning tool, but real positioning often emerges iteratively through market feedback rather than deliberate strategic design.
