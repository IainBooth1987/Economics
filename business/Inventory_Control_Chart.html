Understanding Inventory Control

Inventory control is the systematic management of stock levels to ensure a business has the right amount at the right time. It balances two competing pressures: avoiding stockouts and minimising holding costs.

The Central Challenge Too little stock leads to lost sales and disappointed customers. Too much stock ties up working capital and incurs storage, insurance, and obsolescence costs.

1. Buffer Stock (Safety Stock)

Definition: The minimum inventory level maintained at all times as a safety margin.

Purpose: Protects against unexpected demand spikes, supply delays, or quality issues with deliveries.

Trade-off: Set too high leads to excessive holding costs; set too low increases stockout risk during emergencies.

2. Reorder Level

Definition: The stock level that triggers placing a new order with suppliers.

Calculation: Buffer Stock + (Daily Usage Rate x Lead Time)

Logic: The reorder level must account for all stock used during lead time, while keeping the buffer stock as a safety margin.

3. Lead Time

Definition: The time between placing an order and receiving the goods.

Impact: Longer lead times require higher reorder levels because more stock is consumed while waiting.

Variability: Unreliable suppliers with variable lead times require larger buffer stocks to compensate for uncertainty.

4. Reorder Quantity

Definition: The number of units ordered each time an order is placed.

Trade-off: Large orders reduce ordering frequency (lower admin costs) but increase average inventory held (higher holding costs). Small orders have the opposite effect.

5. Maximum Stock Level

Definition: The highest inventory level reached immediately after a delivery arrives.

Calculation: Reorder Level + Reorder Quantity

Significance: Determines warehouse space requirements and maximum capital tied up in inventory.

The Inventory Cycle

Phase 1: Stock Depletion

Starting from maximum stock, inventory gradually decreases as daily usage occurs. The stock line slopes downward at a rate determined by daily consumption.

Phase 2: Reorder Trigger

When stock reaches the reorder level, a new order is placed. Order too early and you hold excess stock unnecessarily; order too late and you risk running out.

Phase 3: Lead Time Period

Stock continues to be consumed while the business waits for delivery. This is why the reorder level must cover usage during this period plus maintain the buffer stock.

Phase 4: Stock Replenishment

When the delivery arrives, inventory jumps vertically back to maximum stock level. This cycle creates the characteristic "sawtooth" pattern on inventory control charts.

Key Performance Indicators

Average Stock Held

Formula: (Maximum Stock + Buffer Stock) / 2

Represents the typical inventory level throughout the cycle. Used to calculate holding costs and working capital requirements.

Stock Turnover Ratio

Formula: Annual Usage / Average Stock

Measures how many times per year a business sells and replaces its entire inventory. Higher turnover indicates efficient inventory management. However, very high turnover may signal inadequate stock levels and stockout risk.

Industry benchmarks: FMCG: 10-20+ times/year | Retail clothing: 4-6 | Furniture: 2-4 | Luxury goods: 1-2

Common Misconceptions

Misconception: "More stock is always safer"

Many students believe holding maximum inventory eliminates all business risk and is always the optimal strategy.

Reality: Excess inventory ties up working capital, incurs storage and insurance costs, and increases obsolescence risk. The optimal strategy balances stockout risk against holding costs.

Misconception: "Reorder level and reorder quantity are the same thing"

Students frequently confuse the trigger point for ordering with the amount to order.

Reality: Reorder level answers "when do we order?" (the stock level that triggers an order). Reorder quantity answers "how much do we order?" (the number of units per order).

Misconception: "Buffer stock is wasted money"

The cost of holding buffer stock seems unnecessary when demand is stable and predictable.

Reality: Buffer stock is insurance, not waste. The cost of stockouts — including lost sales, damaged customer relationships, and emergency ordering at premium prices — typically far exceeds buffer stock holding costs.

Misconception: "Lead time only matters when placing an order"

Students think lead time is just about delivery timing, not understanding its mathematical relationship with reorder levels.

Reality: Lead time directly determines the reorder level calculation. A business using 30 units daily with a 5-day lead time consumes 150 units while waiting for delivery. Ignoring lead time in reorder level calculations guarantees stockouts.

Misconception: "JIT eliminates all inventory costs"

JIT is often presented as a perfect solution with no downsides.

Reality: JIT reduces holding costs but creates new challenges: higher ordering costs from frequent small deliveries, complete dependence on supplier reliability, and limited ability to handle demand spikes. Many businesses use hybrid approaches.
