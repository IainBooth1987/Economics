1. Capacity

What it measures

Capacity is the maximum output a business can produce in a given time period using its current resources. It sets the ceiling for what the business can achieve without investing in additional assets.

Capacity = Number of Machines x Output per Machine x Time Available
Worked example A factory has 5 machines. Each produces 100 units per hour. The factory runs for 8 hours per day.
Capacity = 5 x 100 x 8 = 4,000 units per day

Business context

Capacity is not fixed forever — it can be increased through capital investment (buying more machines), extending operating hours, or improving processes to extract more output from existing resources. However, increasing capacity carries risk if demand does not materialise.

  • Overcapacity: Fixed costs spread across fewer units; unit costs rise; resources idle
  • Undercapacity: Cannot meet demand; lost sales; customers switch to competitors
Key distinction Capacity is the theoretical maximum. Actual output is almost always lower due to maintenance, breaks, changeovers, and quality rejects. This gap is measured by capacity utilisation.
2. Capacity Utilisation

What it measures

Capacity utilisation expresses actual output as a percentage of maximum capacity. It indicates how efficiently a business is using its productive resources.

Capacity Utilisation (%) = (Actual Output / Maximum Capacity) x 100
Worked example Maximum capacity = 4,000 units/day. Actual output = 3,200 units/day.
Capacity Utilisation = (3,200 / 4,000) x 100 = 80%

Interpreting the figure

There is no single "correct" utilisation rate — the optimal level depends on the business context. However, broad patterns hold:

  • Below 70%: Significant spare capacity; high fixed costs per unit; may signal weak demand or excess investment
  • 70–85%: Often considered efficient — enough slack to handle demand spikes and maintenance
  • Above 90%: Very low slack; risk of delays, quality issues, and inability to take on new orders; staff under pressure
  • 100%: Theoretical maximum — unsustainable in practice; no buffer for disruption
Link to unit costs Higher capacity utilisation typically reduces unit costs because fixed costs (rent, machinery depreciation, salaried staff) are spread across more units of output. This is the concept of spreading fixed overheads.
3. Unit Cost

What it measures

Unit cost (also called average cost) is the total cost of producing one unit of output. It is the key figure used to assess operational efficiency and set minimum viable prices.

Unit Cost = Total Costs / Number of Units Produced
Worked example Total costs = £50,000. Units produced = 5,000.
Unit Cost = £50,000 / 5,000 = £10 per unit

Fixed and variable costs

Total costs = Fixed Costs + Variable Costs. Understanding which costs are fixed and which are variable is essential for interpreting changes in unit cost:

  • Fixed costs (rent, machinery, salaried staff) do not change with output — so higher output = lower fixed cost per unit
  • Variable costs (raw materials, packaging, piece-rate labour) rise in proportion to output — variable cost per unit stays roughly constant
Economies of scale As output increases, unit costs typically fall — not because total costs fall, but because fixed costs are divided across more units. This is the main driver of economies of scale and a key competitive advantage for large-scale producers.
4. Labour Productivity

What it measures

Labour productivity measures the output produced per worker in a given time period. It is the most common indicator of workforce efficiency and directly affects unit labour costs.

Labour Productivity = Total Output / Number of Workers
Worked example Total output = 10,000 units/month. Workers = 50.
Labour Productivity = 10,000 / 50 = 200 units per worker

Improving labour productivity

Businesses can raise labour productivity through a range of strategies — but each involves trade-offs:

  • Training and development: Improves skill and efficiency; costly upfront but increases long-term output per worker
  • Motivation and incentives: Performance-related pay, autonomy, recognition; can raise effort and reduce absenteeism
  • Capital investment: Replacing labour with machinery raises output per worker but reduces workforce size and flexibility
  • Lean production: Eliminating waste (time, movement, defects) raises effective output without increasing inputs
Productivity and unit labour cost Higher productivity reduces the labour cost per unit. If a worker producing 200 units earns £2,000/month, labour cost per unit = £10. If productivity rises to 250 units, labour cost per unit falls to £8 — without any change in wages. This is why productivity is central to cost competitiveness.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception: "High capacity utilisation always means the business is performing well"

Students often assume that operating close to 100% capacity is the goal and that any unused capacity is wasteful.

Reality: Very high utilisation (above 90%) creates serious operational risks. There is no slack to absorb demand spikes, equipment cannot be maintained without disrupting output, staff are under constant pressure, and quality often suffers. The optimal utilisation rate balances efficiency with resilience.

Misconception: "Reducing capacity utilisation always increases unit costs"

Students sometimes believe the relationship between utilisation and unit costs is absolute.

Reality: Reducing utilisation increases the fixed cost per unit — but unit costs also depend on variable costs, input prices, and process efficiency. A business with very low variable costs may see relatively modest unit cost increases when utilisation falls. Context matters.

Misconception: "Unit cost and price are the same thing"

A very common error — students conflate the cost of producing a unit with the price charged to customers.

Reality: Unit cost is the internal cost of production. Price is what the customer pays. The difference is the profit margin. A business must know its unit cost to set a viable price — but pricing decisions also involve market factors, competition, and strategy. They are distinct concepts.

Misconception: "Higher labour productivity means workers are working harder"

Students often interpret productivity solely as a measure of worker effort or motivation.

Reality: Labour productivity = output per worker. Output can rise without workers working harder if they are given better tools, improved processes, or more capital to work with. A worker with a more powerful machine is more productive than one without — not because of effort, but because of capital investment. Productivity is shaped by technology, training, management, and investment — not just effort.

Misconception: "Capacity utilisation formula uses actual capacity in the numerator"

Students sometimes invert the formula: dividing maximum capacity by actual output.

Reality: Capacity Utilisation = (Actual Output / Maximum Capacity) x 100. Actual output goes on top — it is the smaller number in most cases, producing a percentage below 100%. Inverting it produces a figure above 100% which has no logical meaning in this context.

Misconception: "Improving productivity is always good for workers"

Productivity improvements are often presented as universally positive without considering workforce implications.

Reality: Productivity improvements driven by automation or process changes can reduce the number of workers needed. While remaining employees may benefit from higher wages tied to performance targets, others may face redundancy. The distributional impact of productivity gains on workers is a legitimate area of evaluation.
