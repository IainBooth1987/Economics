Sources of Finance

Understand, compare and evaluate the key sources of business finance at A-Level

Internal vs External Finance

Internal Sources

Internal sources come from within the business — they do not require approaching outside providers and carry no obligation to repay or share ownership. They are generally the first port of call for funding, particularly for smaller businesses.

External Sources

External sources come from outside the business. They can be split into debt finance (must be repaid, with interest) and equity finance (giving up a share of ownership in exchange for capital, with no fixed repayment obligation).

The debt vs equity trade-off Debt finance preserves ownership and control but creates fixed repayment obligations that can threaten cash flow. Equity finance avoids repayment pressure but dilutes the founders' ownership and may give investors a say in decisions. The right choice depends on the business's cash position, growth ambitions, and how much control the owners wish to retain.

Short-term vs Long-term Finance

  • Short-term (under 1 year): Used for working capital — covering day-to-day operations. Examples: overdraft, trade credit, factoring.
  • Long-term (over 1 year): Used for capital investment — buying assets, expanding capacity. Examples: bank loan, share issue, retained profit, debentures.

A key principle: the type of finance should match the purpose. Using a short-term overdraft to fund a long-term asset creates a mismatch that puts pressure on cash flow.

The Main Sources
💵
Retained Profit
Internal
Profit kept in the business after tax and dividends have been paid. The most common source of long-term finance for established businesses.
Advantages
  • No interest to pay
  • No dilution of ownership
  • No approval needed
  • No repayment obligation
Disadvantages
  • Only available if profitable
  • Opportunity cost — dividends not paid
  • May be insufficient for large investments
  • Limits growth for new businesses
🏠
Sale of Assets
Internal
Selling assets the business no longer needs — property, machinery, vehicles — to raise cash. Often used in restructuring or crisis situations.
Advantages
  • No interest or repayment
  • Raises cash quickly
  • Reduces ongoing maintenance costs
Disadvantages
  • One-time — cannot repeat indefinitely
  • May reduce productive capacity
  • Asset values may be depressed
  • Time-consuming to arrange
🏭
Bank Loan
External — Debt
A fixed sum borrowed from a bank, repaid with interest over an agreed term. Secured against assets (collateral) in most cases. Suitable for medium to long-term capital investment.
Advantages
  • Ownership retained
  • Fixed repayment schedule aids planning
  • Large sums available
  • Interest is tax-deductible
Disadvantages
  • Interest increases total cost
  • Collateral required — risk of losing assets
  • Regular repayments pressure cash flow
  • May be refused for risky ventures
📄
Overdraft
External — Debt
A short-term facility allowing a business to spend more than its bank balance, up to an agreed limit. Used for day-to-day cash flow management.
Advantages
  • Very flexible — only used when needed
  • Interest only on amount used
  • Quick to arrange
  • No long-term commitment
Disadvantages
  • High interest rates
  • Repayable on demand
  • Not suitable for long-term investment
  • Can become a permanent crutch
📈
Share Issue
External — Equity
Selling new shares to raise capital. Limited companies can issue shares to existing or new shareholders. PLCs can raise large sums via the stock market. A rights issue offers new shares to existing shareholders first.
Advantages
  • No repayment obligation
  • No interest payments
  • Large sums can be raised
  • Reduces financial risk
Disadvantages
  • Dilutes existing ownership
  • Shareholders expect dividends
  • Loss of control / influence
  • Costly and complex for PLCs
🤝
Trade Credit
External — Short-term
An agreement to pay suppliers at a later date (typically 30–90 days) rather than immediately. Effectively free short-term finance — you receive goods now and pay later.
Advantages
  • Interest-free if paid on time
  • Improves short-term cash position
  • Easy to arrange with good relationships
Disadvantages
  • May lose early payment discounts
  • Damages supplier relationships if overused
  • Not suitable for capital investment
  • Penalties for late payment
🚗
Leasing
External — Debt
Renting assets (machinery, vehicles, IT equipment) rather than buying them outright. Finance leases effectively act as a loan; operating leases are more like rentals.
Advantages
  • Avoids large upfront capital outlay
  • Access to up-to-date equipment
  • Predictable regular payments
  • Maintenance sometimes included
Disadvantages
  • Costs more than buying over time
  • Asset never owned outright
  • Long-term commitment
  • Early exit penalties
🌞
Crowdfunding
External — Mixed
Raising small amounts from a large number of people, typically via online platforms (Kickstarter, Seedrs). Can be reward-based (no equity given), equity-based (shares given), or loan-based.
Advantages
  • No collateral required
  • Validates market demand
  • Generates marketing exposure
  • Accessible for start-ups
Disadvantages
  • Not guaranteed — campaigns can fail
  • Equity type dilutes ownership
  • Platform fees apply
  • Exposes business idea publicly
Key Factors in Choosing a Source
FactorWhy it matters
Purpose and amountSmall working capital gaps suit an overdraft; major capital investment requires a long-term source. The amount needed may simply be too large for some sources (e.g. retained profit for a start-up).
Business typeSole traders and partnerships cannot issue shares. Only PLCs can access the stock market. Start-ups have limited access to debt finance due to no trading history.
CostInterest rates, equity dilution, fees, and penalties all affect the true cost of finance. Compare total cost, not just headline rate.
ControlEquity finance involves giving up some ownership and possibly decision-making power. Owners who prioritise control will prefer debt finance despite the repayment pressure.
Risk and securitySecured debt puts assets at risk if the business defaults. High gearing (lots of debt relative to equity) increases financial risk and vulnerability to rising interest rates.
SpeedAn overdraft can be arranged in days; a share issue may take months. For urgent needs, speed matters.
Existing financial positionA business already carrying significant debt may struggle to secure more on favourable terms. Lenders assess ability to service debt from cash flow.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception: "A bank loan is always the best way to finance growth"

Students default to recommending a bank loan for almost any financing question without evaluating alternatives.

Reality: The best source depends on the context. A profitable established business should use retained profit first — it's free. A start-up with no track record may not qualify for a bank loan at all. A rapidly growing business might benefit more from equity finance that removes the repayment pressure during a critical growth phase.

Misconception: "Equity finance is free because you don't pay interest"

Students see no interest payments and conclude equity finance has no cost.

Reality: Equity finance has a significant cost — dilution of ownership. Shareholders expect dividends and a share of future profits. If the business grows substantially, the equity given away today could be worth far more in the future. This opportunity cost is the real price of equity finance.

Misconception: "Retained profit is always available"

Students treat retained profit as a default option without checking whether the business is actually profitable.

Reality: Retained profit only exists if the business has made profit and chosen not to distribute it as dividends. Start-ups, loss-making businesses, and those with dividend-hungry shareholders may have little or no retained profit to draw on.

Misconception: "An overdraft is a good source of long-term finance"

Students recommend overdrafts for capital investment because they are easy to access.

Reality: An overdraft is short-term finance designed for cash flow gaps, not capital investment. It carries high interest rates and is repayable on demand — the bank can call it in at any time. Using an overdraft to fund long-term assets creates a dangerous mismatch between the life of the asset and the term of the finance.

Misconception: "Share issues and loans are the only external sources"

Students focus exclusively on the most prominent sources and ignore trade credit, leasing, and crowdfunding.

Reality: Trade credit is often the single largest source of short-term finance for businesses — receiving stock before paying for it is effectively a free short-term loan. Leasing is widely used to fund equipment without a large upfront payment. Crowdfunding has become increasingly significant for start-ups and product launches.

Misconception: "More gearing always means the business is in trouble"

Students treat high debt levels as automatically negative.

Reality: Gearing measures the proportion of capital funded by debt. Moderate gearing can be beneficial — debt is tax-deductible and avoids diluting ownership. The concern arises when gearing is so high that interest payments threaten cash flow, particularly if interest rates rise or revenues fall. Context — the industry, interest rate environment, and business stability — determines whether a level of gearing is dangerous.
