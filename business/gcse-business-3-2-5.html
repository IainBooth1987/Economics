3.2 Influences on Business · 3.2.5

Legislation

Unlike most of the pressures in this topic, legislation isn't a choice a business can weigh up — it's a legal requirement, and getting it wrong can cost far more than getting it right.

Legislation reaches into human resources (pay, safety, equality), marketing (honest advertising) and finance (the cost of compliance) all at once.
Not optional

Why legislation is different from the rest of 3.2

Technology, ethics and the economy all involve a business weighing up costs and benefits and making its own choice. Legislation doesn't work that way — it's the law, and a business has no legal option to simply decide compliance isn't worth the cost. This makes legislation one of the more predictable external influences (laws are usually announced and phased in with notice), but also one of the least flexible once it applies.

AQA only wants brief knowledge of the specific laws themselves — you don't need every clause of an Act memorised. Far more important is your ability to assess the impact legislation has on a business: the extra cost, the training it requires, the effect on recruitment, and what happens if a business simply doesn't comply.

Protecting the workforce

Employment law

National Minimum Wage & National Living Wage

Sets the lowest hourly rate employers can legally pay staff, varying by age.

Employers must pay at least the legal minimum rate for each worker's age group — paying below it is illegal regardless of what an employee might agree to. The government reviews and typically raises these rates each year.

Cost: higher wage bills Hits low-margin, labour-intensive businesses hardest

Equality Act (2010)

Protects employees and customers from discrimination based on protected characteristics.

The Act makes it illegal to discriminate against someone because of characteristics such as age, sex, disability, race or religion — covering recruitment, pay, promotion and how customers are treated.

Training: equality and diversity training for managers Recruitment: fair, non-discriminatory hiring processes
In the real world: large UK employers (with 250 or more staff) are legally required to publish annual gender pay gap reports, a reporting requirement that grew directly out of the drive for equal treatment the Equality Act represents. Retail and hospitality businesses — often employing large numbers of staff on or near the minimum wage — tend to feel the cost impact of National Living Wage rises most heavily each year.
Keeping people safe at work

Health and safety law

Health and Safety at Work Act (1974)

Requires employers to protect the health, safety and welfare of employees, so far as is reasonably practicable.

This is the foundation of UK workplace safety law, placing a legal duty on employers to identify risks, provide proper training and equipment, and maintain a safe working environment — not just for employees, but for anyone affected by the business's activities.

Cost: safety equipment, risk assessments Training: mandatory safety inductions Failure: fines, prosecution, forced closure

AQA specifically wants you to recognise that safety compliance isn't purely a cost to be minimised — a genuinely safe working environment brings real benefits too.

Benefits of a safe working environment
  • Fewer accidents and sick days, keeping staff at work
  • Higher staff morale and productivity
  • Lower staff turnover and recruitment costs
  • Avoids expensive compensation claims and legal costs
Costs of compliance
  • Safety equipment and protective gear
  • Staff time spent on training and risk assessments
  • Potential need to redesign premises or processes
  • Ongoing cost of health and safety officers or advisers
In the real world: the Health and Safety Executive (HSE) can prosecute businesses that breach the 1974 Act, with fines for serious breaches regularly running into hundreds of thousands or even millions of pounds for larger companies — a cost that dwarfs what proper safety training and equipment would have cost in the first place.
Protecting the customer

Consumer law

Trade descriptions

Goods and services must be accurately described — businesses cannot mislead customers about what they're buying.

Consumer law requires that products match how they're advertised and described — in terms of quality, origin, contents or performance. A business found to have misled customers can face fines, forced refunds, and serious reputational damage.

Cost: refunds, compensation Failure: fines, loss of trust
In the real world: Volkswagen's 2015 "emissions scandal" — in which the company was found to have used software to cheat official emissions tests, misleading customers and regulators about how environmentally friendly its diesel vehicles actually were — became one of the most widely reported consumer law failures in recent corporate history, costing the company billions of pounds in fines, compensation and recalls, alongside lasting reputational damage. Airlines including Ryanair have also faced consumer law requirements to compensate passengers for cancelled flights, following well-publicised disruption in past years.
Putting it together

Assessing the overall impact of legislation

Whichever type of law a question focuses on, AQA rewards the same underlying skill: identifying the specific way legislation changes a business's costs, its training needs, its recruitment practices, and the consequences it would face for failing to comply — then applying that to the scenario in front of you rather than describing the law in the abstract.

Cost

New equipment, higher wages, compensation payments, and the ongoing cost of monitoring compliance.

Training

Staff and managers often need specific training to understand and follow new legal requirements correctly.

Recruitment

Hiring processes may need to change to remain compliant, and some legislation requires dedicated compliance roles.

Consequences of failure

Fines, prosecution, compensation claims, and — often most damaging of all — loss of customer and public trust.

Exam practice

Have a go

2 marks

State two examples of legislation that affects UK businesses.

Structure guide: two correctly named laws or types of legislation — 1 mark each, no explanation required.

Case study — Bramwell Logistics: Bramwell Logistics is a private limited company running a warehouse and delivery fleet, employing 85 staff. Following a recent Health and Safety Executive inspection, Bramwell Logistics has been ordered to invest £40,000 in new safety equipment and staff training to comply with the Health and Safety at Work Act. At the same time, a rise in the National Living Wage has increased Bramwell Logistics' annual wage bill by £60,000.
4 marks

State and explain two ways legislation is affecting Bramwell Logistics' costs.

Structure guide: state a cost grounded in the case study (1 mark), explain it (1 mark) — repeated twice over.

6 marks

Analyse the benefits Bramwell Logistics might gain from investing in the new safety equipment and training required by the Health and Safety Executive.

Structure guide: a single developed line of reasoning grounded in the case study (eg: the new safety equipment and training should reduce accidents among Bramwell Logistics' 85 staff → fewer accidents means fewer sick days and lower risk of costly compensation claims → this could also improve staff morale and reduce turnover → helping offset some of the £40,000 cost over time, beyond simply avoiding prosecution).

9 marks

Recommend how Bramwell Logistics could manage the combined £100,000 increase in costs from the new safety requirements and the wage rise. Justify your answer using the case study.

Structure guide: a "recommend" question needs a justified judgement — consider options such as raising prices to customers, absorbing the cost through lower profit, improving efficiency elsewhere in the business, or spreading the safety investment over a longer period, before reaching a clear final recommendation.

Key terms

Glossary

National Minimum Wage
The legal minimum hourly pay rate for workers, varying by age.
National Living Wage
The legal minimum hourly rate for the oldest eligible age band of workers, typically higher than the standard minimum wage.
Equality Act (2010)
UK law protecting people from discrimination based on protected characteristics such as age, sex, disability and race.
Health and Safety at Work Act (1974)
UK law requiring employers to protect the health, safety and welfare of employees, so far as reasonably practicable.
Trade descriptions
Consumer law requiring goods and services to be accurately described to customers.
Compliance
Meeting the requirements set out in relevant laws and regulations.
