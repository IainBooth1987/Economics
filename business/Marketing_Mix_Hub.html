Apple
Case study · Product strategy
Apple manages one of the world's most studied product portfolios — from the original Mac to AirPods to Vision Pro. Every product decision is calculated to extend lifecycle, protect margins and reinforce the brand premium.
$391bn
Apple revenue (2024) — driven by portfolio management
17 years
iPhone product lifecycle — sustained by extension strategies
Product Lifecycle
›
📋
Spec requirement (3.1.3): Significance of the stages of the product lifecycle model — development, introduction, growth, maturity, decline — for marketing planning and other functions.
Development Introduction Growth Maturity Decline Cash flow Marketing spend Other functions
The product lifecycle (PLC) describes the revenue trajectory of a product from pre-launch through to withdrawal. Each stage has different implications — not just for marketing, but for operations (production volumes), finance (cash flow and investment) and HR (staffing needs).
Apple · iPhone lifecycle
The iPhone launched in 2007 (introduction — high spend, low sales). By 2010 it was in rapid growth. The iPhone 6 era (2014–15) represented peak maturity — Apple responded with product differentiation (Plus, Pro, SE) rather than letting it enter decline. This is extension strategy in action.
Key exam point: At maturity, profit is highest but growth stalls. Businesses must decide between extension strategies or accepting decline and focusing NPD resource elsewhere. The spec requires you to link PLC stage decisions to other functions — e.g. at maturity, operations reduces batch sizes; finance shifts from investment to profit extraction.
Extension Strategies
›
📋
Spec requirement (3.1.3): Extension strategies — advertising, rebranding, lowering the price, adapting the product.
Advertising Rebranding Lower price Adapt the product
Extension strategies delay the decline stage, prolonging a product's profitable life. The four named in the spec are not mutually exclusive — Apple uses all four simultaneously.
Apple · extension strategies in practice
Advertising: "Shot on iPhone" campaign repositioned the iPhone as a creative tool — new audience, same product.

Rebranding: iPhone SE launched at a lower price with a heritage design — refreshing interest without cannibalising Pro sales.

Adapting the product: Camera improvements, chip upgrades and new colours each year extend the growth phase and justify premium pricing.

Lowering price: Previous-generation iPhones remain on sale at reduced prices — extending revenue from the product at the maturity/decline crossover.
Benefits
  • Lower cost than full NPD
  • Preserves brand equity
  • Maintains revenue stream
  • Retains existing customers
Drawbacks
  • May delay necessary NPD
  • Price cuts can damage brand image
  • Advertising cost may exceed return
  • Market may be structurally in decline
Boston Matrix
›
📋
Spec requirement (3.1.3): Significance of product portfolio analysis for marketing planning — the Boston Matrix (dogs, cash cows, question marks and stars).
The Boston Matrix classifies products by market share (x-axis) and market growth rate (y-axis). It helps businesses decide where to invest, harvest or divest.
Apple · Boston Matrix 2024
Star: iPhone 15 Pro — high growth market share in premium segment, heavy marketing investment.

Cash Cow: MacBook Pro M-series — dominant market share in a slower-growth professional laptop market; high margins fund R&D.

Question Mark: Vision Pro — early stage, high growth potential market but currently tiny share; Apple is deciding how much to invest.

Dog: iPod (discontinued 2022) — low growth, low share; Apple correctly divested rather than extending.
Exam evaluation point: The Boston Matrix is a snapshot in time — it does not account for future market changes, costs of production, or strategic value (e.g. a "dog" product that builds brand loyalty). It is a planning tool, not a prescription.
New Product Development (NPD)
›
📋
Spec requirement (3.1.3): Purpose, value and challenges of new product development.
NPD is the process of bringing a new product to market — from idea generation through to launch. For Apple, NPD (the Vision Pro, AirPods, Apple Watch) is central to maintaining long-term competitiveness as existing products mature.
Value of NPD
  • Creates future revenue streams
  • Maintains competitive advantage
  • Responds to changing customer needs
  • Can command premium pricing
Challenges
  • High R&D cost — Apple spends ~$30bn p.a.
  • High failure rate across the industry
  • Long lead times before revenue
  • Cannibalisation of existing products
⚠️ Common mistakes — Product ›
Product Lifecycle
✕
Forgetting the "other functions" link. The spec explicitly requires PLC stage decisions to connect to operations, finance and HR — not just marketing.
✓ Fix: for every PLC answer, ask "what does this mean for production volumes, cash flow, or staffing?"
✕
Assuming every product follows a smooth, identical curve. Real products have irregular curves — some skip stages, some re-enter growth after decline (revivals), some never leave introduction.
✓ Fix: use the textbook curve as a model, but acknowledge that real products deviate from it when the question gives specific data.
✕
Misreading slowing growth as decline. A growth rate falling from 300% to 90% is still growth — students often jump straight to "decline" the moment the rate of change slows.
✓ Fix: decline means revenue is falling in absolute terms, not just growing more slowly. Check the actual direction of the numbers.
Extension strategies
✕
Listing extension strategies without explaining why they extend the lifecycle. Students often write "Apple could rebrand the product" and stop there.
✓ Fix: explain the mechanism — rebranding refreshes perception among existing customers and can attract a new segment, which delays the slide into decline.
✕
Recommending an extension strategy at the wrong stage. Extension strategies are a maturity-stage tool — recommending one during growth or introduction misunderstands the purpose (there's nothing to "extend" yet).
✓ Fix: only reach for extension strategies once the data shows slowing growth or a plateau, not while sales are still rising strongly.
✕
Picking a price cut as an extension strategy without weighing the margin cost. "Lowering the price" is named in the spec, but students rarely acknowledge that it reduces the very profit the business is trying to sustain at maturity.
✓ Fix: if you recommend a price cut, state the trade-off — volume may rise, but margin per unit falls, so the net profit effect isn't guaranteed.
Boston Matrix
✕
Treating the Boston Matrix as static. A "Star" today can become a "Cash Cow" or even a "Dog" — students often classify once and never revisit.
✓ Fix: always state the time frame and what would need to change for the classification to shift.
✕
Confusing the product lifecycle with the Boston Matrix. PLC tracks one product over time; Boston Matrix compares multiple products in a portfolio at one point in time.
✓ Fix: PLC = one product's journey. Boston Matrix = a snapshot across the whole range.
✕
Mixing up the two axes. Students sometimes plot market share on the vertical axis and growth on the horizontal, or describe a "Dog" as having high share — both axes and all four labels need to be exact.
✓ Fix: market share = horizontal axis. Market growth rate = vertical axis. Dogs = low on both.
✕
Recommending heavy investment in a "Dog." The whole point of the matrix is that Dogs should usually be divested or left alone, not invested in — investing contradicts the model's own logic.
✓ Fix: use Cash Cows to fund Stars and Question Marks. Dogs are typically withdrawn unless there's a strategic reason to keep them.
New Product Development
✕
Treating NPD as risk-free. Students list the benefits of NPD (new revenue, competitiveness) but skip the spec-named challenges — high cost, high failure rates, long lead times.
✓ Fix: for any NPD recommendation, name at least one specific challenge and how the business might mitigate it.
✕
Recommending NPD as the answer to every problem. If a product is in growth and performing well, NPD is rarely the most relevant response — extension strategies or distribution expansion may fit the context better.
✓ Fix: match the recommendation to the stage and the specific problem in the case study, not a default "launch something new."
🔭 Beyond the specextension ›

Some textbooks and other exam boards extend the marketing mix from 4Ps to 7Ps, adding three extra elements that are particularly relevant to services:

People — the staff who deliver the product or service, and how their training/attitude affects the customer experience.

Process — the systems and procedures used to deliver the product or service to the customer (e.g. how quickly an order is fulfilled).

Physical evidence — the tangible cues that signal quality, such as store design, packaging or website interface.

You might encounter these terms in wider reading. They're useful for general business understanding, but they are not part of the AQA 7138 specification — the spec is built entirely around the original 4Ps (Product, Price, Place, Promotion).

Not examinable on AQA 7138 — included here for context only.

Ryanair
Case study · Pricing strategy
Ryanair is Europe's largest airline by passenger numbers — built entirely on a dynamic, low-cost pricing model that uses every lever the spec identifies. No business better illustrates how price drives volume, revenue and competitive position simultaneously.

Influences on price
›
📋
Spec requirement (3.1.3): Influences on price: marketing objectives, target market, level of demand, PED, costs, competitors' actions, marketing mix.
Marketing objectives Target market Level of demand PED Costs Competitors' actions Marketing mix
Ryanair · what drives its prices?
Costs: Ultra-lean cost base (one aircraft type, secondary airports) — allows pricing below cost for seats when ancillary revenue compensates.

Demand: Base fares are lowest when demand is expected to be weakest (midweek, off-season). Peak routes are priced to capture willingness-to-pay.

Competitors: Ryanair monitors easyJet's fares in real time and adjusts algorithmically. Price is a weapon, not just a signal.

Marketing mix: Virtually zero above-the-line promotion — savings flow into price. Price IS the marketing strategy.
Pricing methods
›
📋
Spec requirement (3.1.3): Methods of pricing: cost based; demand based (penetration, skimming, dynamic); competition based (premium, going rate, discount).
The spec distinguishes three families of pricing method — each reflects a different starting point for the pricing decision:
Cost-based pricing
Cost-plus: Add a fixed % mark-up to total unit cost. Simple and guarantees a margin — but ignores what customers will actually pay. Common in manufacturing and construction.
Demand-based pricing
Penetration: Launch low to build share quickly — then raise prices. Netflix used this in new markets.

Price skimming: Launch high to capture early adopters willing to pay a premium, then reduce. Apple uses this every iPhone generation — Vision Pro launched at $3,499.

Dynamic pricing: Prices adjust in real time based on demand. Ryanair's entire model — the same seat can cost £10 or £200 depending on when you book and how full the flight is.
Competition-based pricing
Premium: Price above competitors — signals quality. Apple Mac vs Windows PCs.

Going rate: Match the market price — common in commodity markets (petrol stations).

Discount: Consistently price below competitors — Ryanair vs full-service carriers.
PED and impact on revenue
›
📋
Spec requirement (3.1.3): Impact of price on sales volume, revenue and profit margins. Effect of price changes on revenue given PED.
If demand is price elastic (PED > 1): a price cut increases total revenue because volume rises proportionally more than the price falls. Ryanair's entire model depends on highly elastic leisure travel demand.

If demand is price inelastic (PED < 1): a price rise increases total revenue because volume falls less than price rises. Apple exploits inelastic demand among brand-loyal customers at launch.
Price elastic demand
  • Cut price → revenue rises
  • Raise price → revenue falls
  • Many substitutes available
  • Example: budget flights
Price inelastic demand
  • Raise price → revenue rises
  • Cut price → revenue falls
  • Few substitutes / strong brand
  • Example: iPhone Pro
⚠️ Common mistakes — Price ›
Pricing methods
✕
Confusing penetration pricing with price skimming. This is the single most common error — students mix up which one starts high and which starts low.
✓ Fix: Penetrate = get in low. Skim = take the cream off the top (high price first).
✕
Treating "premium pricing" and "price skimming" as the same thing. Premium pricing is permanent positioning above competitors; skimming is a temporary launch tactic that lowers over time.
✓ Fix: ask "is this price meant to fall later?" If yes, it's skimming. If the high price is the permanent strategy, it's premium.
✕
Calling every price change "dynamic pricing." Dynamic pricing specifically means automated, real-time adjustment based on live demand/supply data — a planned annual price rise is not dynamic pricing.
✓ Fix: only use "dynamic" when the price is changing frequently and automatically in response to real-time conditions (e.g. surge pricing, airline seats).
✕
Defaulting to cost-plus pricing for every business. Cost-plus ignores the market entirely — it's a poor recommendation for any business operating in a competitive or branded market where customer perception drives price.
✓ Fix: only recommend cost-plus where there's genuinely no comparable competitor product, or for bespoke/made-to-order work.
✕
Recommending going-rate pricing for a highly differentiated product. Going-rate only makes sense for commodities where customers can't distinguish between suppliers — applying it to a branded or unique product ignores the brand's value.
✓ Fix: check whether the product is genuinely a commodity (identical to competitors) before recommending going-rate or discount pricing.
Elasticity
✕
Assuming a price cut always increases revenue. This is only true if demand is price elastic — if demand is inelastic, a price cut reduces total revenue.
✓ Fix: always state the elasticity assumption before predicting the revenue effect of a price change.
✕
Calculating PED but forgetting to interpret the number. Getting "PED = −2.5" right in a calculation question still loses marks if you don't explain what it means for pricing strategy.
✓ Fix: always follow a PED calculation with "this means demand is elastic/inelastic, so a price [rise/fall] would..."
✕
Ignoring the sign of PED. A negative PED value is normal (price and quantity move in opposite directions) — students sometimes treat a negative answer as evidence of a calculation error and "fix" it by removing the minus sign.
✓ Fix: PED is almost always negative for normal goods. Compare the size of the number (ignoring the sign) against 1 to judge elastic vs inelastic.
✕
Confusing PED with YED. Price elasticity responds to a change in price; income elasticity responds to a change in consumer income — mixing these up misapplies the whole answer.
✓ Fix: PED = price change → quantity change. YED = income change → quantity change. Check which variable actually changed in the question.
Influences on price
✕
Naming an influence on price without linking it to this specific business. "Competitors affect price" is AO1 knowledge — it only becomes AO2 application when you name the actual competitor and what they're doing.
✓ Fix: always use the named competitor/context from the case study, not a generic statement about "competitors."
✕
Treating price in isolation from the rest of the marketing mix. The spec explicitly lists "marketing mix" as an influence on price — a premium price needs premium product, place and promotion to be credible.
✓ Fix: when justifying a price level, briefly check it's consistent with how the product is positioned elsewhere in the mix.
🔭 Beyond the specextension ›

The extended marketing mix (7Ps) doesn't add a new pricing concept directly, but wider reading sometimes discusses psychological pricing (e.g. pricing at £9.99 instead of £10) and price bundling (combining products into a single price) as additional pricing tactics.

These can be useful real-world examples to mention briefly, but they sit outside the named methods in the AQA 7138 spec — which are limited to cost-based, demand-based (penetration/skimming/dynamic) and competition-based (premium/going rate/discount) pricing.

Amazon has redefined distribution — from a single online bookshop to a multi-channel giant that has disrupted every traditional intermediary. Its distribution decisions illustrate every channel concept in the spec.

Distribution channels
›
📋
Spec requirement (3.1.3): Purpose and value of a distribution channel. Advantages and disadvantages of direct distribution (including e-commerce), intermediaries (wholesalers and retailers), and multi-channel distribution.
A distribution channel is the route a product takes from producer to consumer. The choice of channel affects cost, control, reach and margin.
Amazon · channel evolution
Direct (e-commerce): Amazon.co.uk is direct distribution — no intermediary, full margin, full data on customers. This is why Amazon invests so heavily in its own logistics rather than relying on Royal Mail.

Marketplace (indirect): Third-party sellers use Amazon as a platform — Amazon becomes the intermediary. Over 60% of Amazon sales now come from third-party sellers, generating fee income without inventory risk.

Multi-channel: Amazon Fresh stores, Amazon Lockers, Whole Foods (acquired 2017) — Amazon operates physical retail alongside digital to capture customers who want immediate access. The data from both channels is integrated.
Direct vs indirect distribution
›
Direct — advantages
  • Full control over customer experience
  • Higher margin — no intermediary cut
  • Direct customer data for CRM
  • E-commerce: 24/7, global reach
Direct — disadvantages
  • High logistics investment required
  • Slower to scale geographically
  • Customer acquisition cost falls on brand
  • No physical presence for tactile goods
Intermediaries — advantages
  • Instant access to established networks
  • Lower upfront cost
  • Retailer handles last-mile logistics
  • Reaches customers who prefer in-store
Intermediaries — disadvantages
  • Reduced margin per unit
  • Less control over presentation
  • Customer data sits with retailer
  • Dependence on retailer relationships
⚠️ Common mistakes — Place ›
Choosing a channel
✕
Assuming direct distribution is always "better." Students often default to praising e-commerce/direct selling without considering whether the product and target market actually suit it.
✓ Fix: always link the channel choice back to the specific product and customer in the question — a £45,000 B2B product needs a completely different channel to a £0.85 snack.
✕
Recommending e-commerce for every product without considering the purchase. High-value, high-consideration purchases (cars, luxury watches, enterprise software) often need a physical or relationship-based channel that pure e-commerce can't replicate.
✓ Fix: ask whether the customer would want to see, touch, try or discuss the product before buying — if yes, pure online distribution is a weak recommendation on its own.
✕
Ignoring the brand-fit of a channel. Distributing a premium or story-led brand through a mass-market discount retailer can destroy the positioning that justifies its price, even though reach increases.
✓ Fix: check that the channel's typical customer and atmosphere match the brand's positioning, not just its reach potential.
Intermediaries
✕
Ignoring the margin trade-off when discussing intermediaries. Students mention "reach" as the advantage but forget retailer/wholesaler commission reduces the margin per unit.
✓ Fix: for every intermediary advantage, name the corresponding margin or control cost.
✕
Confusing wholesalers with retailers. Wholesalers sell in bulk to other businesses (including retailers); retailers sell directly to the end consumer — mixing these up misapplies the whole channel structure.
✓ Fix: wholesaler = business-to-business middle step. Retailer = the final business-to-consumer point of sale.
✕
Forgetting that using an intermediary means losing direct customer data. Selling via a marketplace or retailer means the brand often can't see who actually bought the product — a key disadvantage students leave out.
✓ Fix: when discussing the drawbacks of indirect distribution, mention the loss of direct customer relationship/data alongside the margin loss.
Multi-channel & digital
✕
Confusing "multi-channel" with simply "having a website." Multi-channel specifically means using two or more distinct channels simultaneously (e.g. website AND physical stores).
✓ Fix: name the specific channels being combined when you use the term multi-channel.
✕
Treating e-commerce as cost-free. Students assume online distribution avoids the costs of physical retail entirely, ignoring website development, logistics, returns processing and digital marketing spend needed to drive traffic.
✓ Fix: e-commerce has lower fixed costs than physical stores, but it isn't free — name at least one ongoing cost (logistics, customer acquisition, returns).
✕
Assuming more channels is always better. Adding channels can create conflict (e.g. retailers unhappy that a brand also sells direct at a lower price) and spreads marketing resource more thinly.
✓ Fix: weigh the benefit of extra reach against the risk of channel conflict and diluted brand consistency.
B2B distribution
✕
Applying B2C distribution logic to a B2B scenario. Long sales cycles, multiple decision-makers and high contract values mean B2B distribution usually needs direct sales relationships, not mass channels.
✓ Fix: for B2B with complex/high-value products, lead with direct sales or trusted resellers rather than recommending mass retail or e-commerce.
🔭 Beyond the specextension ›

The extended marketing mix (7Ps) adds Process as a distinct element here — the systems and procedures customers experience when accessing a product or service, such as checkout speed, delivery logistics, or how a complaint is handled.

In services marketing particularly, "Process" is treated as closely related to Place because both concern how the customer actually receives what they're buying. The AQA 7138 spec keeps this folded into Place under "distribution channels" rather than naming it separately.

Nike
Case study · Promotional strategy
Nike spends over $4bn per year on marketing — but what makes it remarkable is how it combines every element of the promotional mix into a coherent brand story. From Cristiano Ronaldo to TikTok creators to in-store personal selling, the entire spec lives in Nike's campaigns.

Annual marketing spend — 10% of revenue
#1
Most followed brand on Instagram globally
The promotional mix
›
📋
Spec requirement (3.1.3): Purpose and value of: advertising; social media and influencers; sales promotions; sponsorship; personal selling (including account managers); building a strong brand and brand loyalty; influences on the promotional mix.
Nike · promotional mix
Advertising: "Just Do It" is one of the most enduring campaigns in history — consistent message across TV, outdoor and digital since 1988. Purpose: mass awareness and emotional brand positioning.

Social media and influencers: Nike works with athletes (Ronaldo, Serena Williams) but also micro-influencers in niche sports. Instagram, YouTube and TikTok campaigns generate billions of organic impressions. Purpose: reach, authenticity, targeting.

Sponsorship: Nike sponsors the England football team, individual athletes and major tournaments. Purpose: brand association with aspiration and performance.

Sales promotions: Nike App exclusive drops, flash sales for members — create urgency and reward loyalty. Purpose: short-term sales uplift and customer retention.

Personal selling: Nike's specialist retail staff (especially in Nike Town flagship stores) are trained to understand and sell the product range. Nike's account managers manage relationships with major retail partners (JD Sports, Foot Locker). Purpose: conversion and relationship management.

Brand building: The Nike swoosh, "Just Do It", and athlete partnerships build long-term brand equity — meaning customers pay a premium and choose Nike even when cheaper alternatives exist. Purpose: sustained pricing power and loyalty.
Influences on the promotional mix
›
📋
Spec requirement (3.1.3): Influences on the promotional mix.
Budget available Target audience Stage of PLC Nature of product Competitors' activity Marketing objectives
Budget: A small startup cannot afford Nike's sponsorship deals — it must rely on social media and word-of-mouth.

Target audience: B2B products use personal selling and trade shows; B2C mass-market products use advertising and social media.

Stage of PLC: At introduction, heavy advertising to build awareness. At maturity, sales promotions to maintain share. At decline, minimal spend.

Nature of the product: Complex or expensive products (insurance, software) require personal selling. Impulse purchases need point-of-sale promotion.
⚠️ Common mistakes — Promotion ›
Choosing promotional tools
✕
Writing "use social media" as a complete answer. This is far too vague to earn application or analysis marks — examiners want to know which platform, what content, and why it suits this specific audience.
✓ Fix: name the platform AND the audience reason — "Instagram, because the target market (18–25s) spends the most time there and responds well to visual content."
✕
Recommending heavy advertising for every B2B scenario. High-value B2B sales depend on personal selling and relationship-building, not mass-market advertising.
✓ Fix: for B2B with a long sales cycle and multiple stakeholders, lead with personal selling, then mention supporting tools (trade shows, content marketing).
✕
Listing all six promotional tools without prioritising. A list of every tool with no judgement on which matters most for this context reads as knowledge (AO1) without application or evaluation (AO2–AO4).
✓ Fix: pick the 1–2 strongest tools for the specific context and justify why they outperform the others here.
✕
Confusing sponsorship with sales promotion. Sponsorship builds long-term brand association; sales promotion drives short-term sales (discounts, competitions).
✓ Fix: sponsorship = brand image over time. Sales promotion = immediate purchase incentive.
✕
Treating personal selling as outdated. Students sometimes assume digital tools have replaced personal selling entirely — but for complex, high-value or relationship-dependent sales, it remains essential.
✓ Fix: personal selling and digital tools usually work together, not as substitutes — check whether the purchase needs human reassurance or negotiation.
Influences on the promotional mix
✕
Ignoring budget constraints entirely. Recommending a Nike-style sponsorship deal for a startup with a £5,000 marketing budget ignores the most basic influence on the promotional mix.
✓ Fix: always check the budget given in the case study before recommending an expensive tool like sponsorship or national advertising.
✕
Not linking the promotional mix to the PLC stage. The spec explicitly names "stage of PLC" as an influence — heavy advertising suits introduction; promotions suit maturity; minimal spend suits decline.
✓ Fix: state the PLC stage first, then choose a promotional tool that matches what that stage needs (awareness vs retention vs defending share).
✕
Ignoring what competitors are already doing. A promotional strategy chosen in isolation, without reference to competitor activity, misses a named spec influence and can leave a business outspent or out-positioned.
✓ Fix: where the case study mentions a competitor's promotional activity, explain how your recommendation responds to or differs from it.
Digital marketing
✕
Confusing SEO with PPC. SEO is about ranking organically in search results over time; PPC is paying for an immediate sponsored placement — these have very different costs and timeframes.
✓ Fix: SEO = free but slow to build. PPC = instant but you pay per click.
✕
Naming a digital metric without explaining what it measures. Students mention "conversion rate" or "click-through rate" without being able to say what it actually tells the business.
✓ Fix: be ready to define each named metric — e.g. conversion rate = the % of visitors who complete a purchase.
✕
Treating CRM as just "a customer database." CRM is about using customer data actively for acquisition, retention, lead generation and pipeline management — not simply storing names and emails.
✓ Fix: link CRM to a specific use (e.g. personalised retention offers, or tracking a sales pipeline), not just "storing customer information."
Branding
✕
Treating "brand loyalty" as something that happens automatically. Students state brand loyalty as a fact ("Nike has brand loyalty") without explaining what built it or how the business sustains it.
✓ Fix: explain the specific mechanism — consistent quality, emotional association from sponsorship, or community-building — that creates and protects loyalty.
🔭 Beyond the specextension ›

The extended marketing mix (7Ps) adds People as a distinct element here — the staff who represent the brand during the promotional and sales process, and how their training, attitude and appearance shape the customer's perception.

It also adds Physical evidence — the tangible signals (store design, packaging, website interface, uniforms) that reinforce a brand's promotional message and signal quality before, during and after a purchase.

Both are common in services marketing textbooks and worth knowing for general business literacy, but the AQA 7138 spec treats staff and tangible cues as part of operations and culture rather than as separate elements of the marketing mix.
