The Role of Business Enterprise

A business exists to make something worth more to a customer than it cost to produce — and the entrepreneur is the person who pulls everything together to make that happen, at their own risk.

This closes Topic 1.1 — bringing together why ideas happen (1.1.1), the risk they carry (1.1.2), and now what they're actually for.
Closing Topic 1.1

What business is actually for

This final sub-topic of 1.1 ties together everything so far. You've seen why new business ideas come about (1.1.1) and the risk an entrepreneur accepts to pursue one (1.1.2). Now it's worth stepping back to ask a more fundamental question: what is business enterprise actually for, and what specific role does the entrepreneur play in making it happen?

Three purposes

The purpose of business activity

To produce goods or services

The most basic function of any business — making a physical product or delivering a service that didn't exist in that form before.

To meet customer needs

Production only matters if it actually satisfies a genuine want or need — echoing exactly the triggers you met in 1.1.1.

To add value

The crucial ingredient: making a product or service worth more to a customer than the cost of the raw materials and effort that went into it.

That third purpose — adding value — is where most of the real skill in running a business actually lies, and Edexcel wants you to know five specific ways it happens.

Five ways to add value

How businesses add value

Convenience

Making something easier, faster or more accessible

Customers will often pay more simply to save time or effort — a pre-chopped bag of vegetables costs more than the same raw ingredients, purely because someone else did the preparation.

Branding

Building a name and reputation customers trust

A strong brand lets a business charge more than an identical, unbranded product, because customers are paying for the trust and reputation attached to the name, not just the physical item.

Quality

Superior materials, craftsmanship or reliability

A product that lasts longer or performs better justifies a higher price, since customers are willing to pay for a genuinely better experience or fewer future replacements.

Design

How a product looks, feels and functions

Thoughtful design can make a product more appealing or easier to use than a functionally identical rival, adding value that has nothing to do with the raw materials used.

Unique selling point (USP)

A distinguishing feature no competitor offers

A genuine USP gives customers a specific reason to choose one business over every other option available to them.

In the real world: Deliveroo adds value primarily through convenience, saving customers the time and effort of collecting food themselves. Nike adds significant value through branding, with customers willing to pay a premium for the swoosh logo alone. Apple is widely recognised for adding value through design, with product appearance and usability treated as central to the customer experience rather than an afterthought. James Dyson's bagless vacuum cleaner, which you met back in 1.1.1 as an original idea, is also a clear example of a genuine USP — a specific feature that, when it launched, no competitor could offer at all.
Making it actually happen

The role of entrepreneurship

An entrepreneur is the person who takes a business idea and actually makes it real. Edexcel identifies three specific things an entrepreneur does.

  • Organises resources — bringing together the people, materials, equipment and finance a business needs to actually produce something, rather than simply having a good idea on paper.
  • Makes business decisions — deciding what to sell, at what price, where to locate, and who to hire, with no one above them to approve or overrule the choice.
  • Takes risks — personally accepting the risk you studied in 1.1.2, in the hope of the reward that comes with getting it right.

These three roles connect directly to each other. Organising resources and making decisions are only possible because the entrepreneur is willing to take the risk in the first place — someone unwilling to accept that risk simply wouldn't be in the position to make those decisions or commit those resources at all. This is exactly why entrepreneurship is defined by all three together, not any one on its own.

Exam practice

Have a go

Standalone questions below are typical of Section A — no case study needed. The 6 and 9-mark questions are built around a Source Booklet–style case study, matching how Section B and C actually work in the real exam.

1 mark
Select one answer

Which one of the following is an example of adding value through design?

A. A product that is easier and more enjoyable to use than rivals
B. A product sold at a lower price than rivals
C. A product delivered faster than rivals
D. A product manufactured using cheaper materials

Answer: A.

2 marks
Outline

Outline one way a business can add value to its product.

Structure guide: two linked points — eg "A business can add value through branding (1), by building a trusted name customers are willing to pay more for (1)." Points must connect; unlinked points cap the mark at 1.

3 marks
Explain

Explain one role of an entrepreneur when starting a business.

Structure guide: 1 mark identifying a role, plus 2 further marks developing it — eg "An entrepreneur organises resources (1), bringing together the people, materials and finance a business needs (1), which allows the business idea to actually be put into practice rather than remaining just a plan (1)."

Case study — Hearth & Home: Kai runs Hearth & Home, a small furniture-making business. He currently sells simple wooden tables at a similar price to several nearby competitors. He is considering two ways to add value to his business: developing a stronger brand identity with distinctive packaging and a memorable logo, or improving the quality of his furniture by using longer-lasting timber and offering a 10-year guarantee.
6 marks
AnalyseSection B style

Analyse the impact on Hearth & Home of adding value through improved quality rather than competing on price alone.

Structure guide: application (AO2) and analysis (AO3a) together — eg identify that longer-lasting timber and a 10-year guarantee (AO2) let Kai charge a higher price than his current competitors, then analyse how this could increase his profit per table sold, but also requires customers to trust that the quality genuinely justifies the higher cost, which may take time to establish (AO3a).

9 marks
JustifySection C style

Kai is considering two options to add value to Hearth & Home: developing a stronger brand identity, or improving the quality of his furniture with a 10-year guarantee. Justify which option Kai should choose.

Structure guide: apply knowledge to Kai's specific situation (AO2), analyse points on both sides (AO3a) — branding can be built relatively quickly through marketing and packaging, while quality improvements involve higher upfront material costs but offer a more concrete, provable reason to charge more — then reach a clear, justified choice (AO3b).

Key terms

Glossary

Adding value
Making a product or service worth more to a customer than the cost of the resources used to produce it.
Unique selling point (USP)
A distinguishing feature of a product or service that no competitor offers.
Entrepreneur
A person who organises resources, makes business decisions, and takes risks to bring a business idea to life.
