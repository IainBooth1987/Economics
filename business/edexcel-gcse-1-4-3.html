The Marketing Mix

Four decisions that only really work when they agree with each other — get one badly out of step with the rest, and the whole mix can undermine itself.

Builds on customer needs (1.2.1) and the competitive environment (1.2.4) — full detail on each element arrives later in Theme 2.
The recipe for reaching customers

What the marketing mix is

The marketing mix is the combination of four decisions a business makes to sell its product effectively: price, product, promotion and place — often called the "4Ps." Think of it as a recipe: get the combination right, and a business is far more likely to attract customers and succeed; get it wrong, and even a genuinely good product can struggle.

A note on scope: this chapter introduces each of the 4Ps and why they matter, then focuses on how they need to work together. The detailed methods within each element — pricing strategies, promotional techniques, and so on — get their own full chapters later, in Theme 2 (2.2.1–2.2.5).
Four elements, one mix

The importance of each element

Product

The actual good or service being sold. If it doesn't genuinely meet a customer need (see 1.2.1), no amount of clever pricing or promotion can rescue it.

Price

What the customer pays. It needs to reflect the product's value and where it sits in the market, relative to competitors.

Promotion

How customers find out about the product and are persuaded to buy it — advertising, offers, word of mouth.

Place

Where and how the product reaches the customer. This isn't just a physical building — for a company like Netflix, "place" is the app on a customer's phone or TV.

No single P works alone

How the elements of the marketing mix work together

A genuinely effective marketing mix is integrated — every element sends the same message. A luxury watch priced at £10 and sold from a car boot sale using a photocopied flyer would confuse customers badly, however excellent the watch itself actually is. The four Ps have to agree with each other, not just each be individually "correct."

Balancing the mix based on the competitive environment

The competitive environment you studied in 1.2.4 directly shapes how a business balances its mix. If a competitor cuts its price, a business doesn't necessarily have to follow suit — it might instead increase promotion to remind customers why its own product is worth the higher price, or improve the product itself to justify the difference.

The impact of changing consumer needs

As customer needs shift (see 1.2.1), the whole mix often has to shift with them. Growing demand for sustainable products, for example, might lead a business to change its packaging (product) while also promoting its environmental credentials more heavily (promotion) — both elements moving together in response to the same underlying change.

The impact of technology

Technology has changed both place and promotion. E-commerce (see 1.4.2) lets a business sell to customers anywhere, without needing a single physical shop. Digital communication lets a business target promotion precisely at people who've already shown genuine interest — searching for a product online can lead directly to seeing an advert for exactly that product on social media shortly afterwards, a far more targeted approach than a generic TV advert reaching everyone regardless of interest.

In the real world: Coca-Cola runs a genuinely integrated marketing mix worldwide — consistent, recognisable branding (product), a price positioned as an affordable treat rather than a luxury, extremely wide availability through shops, vending machines and restaurants (place), and constant, high-profile promotion — all four elements reinforcing the same brand message everywhere in the world. A small start-up, by contrast, often begins with a much simpler mix: a lower price and local, low-cost promotion specifically designed to win its first handful of customers, only building a more elaborate mix as it grows.

Exam practice

Have a go

Standalone questions below are typical of Section A — no case study needed. The 6 and 9-mark questions are built around a Source Booklet–style case study, matching how Section B and C actually work in the real exam.

1 mark
Select one answer

Which one of the following is one of the 4Ps of the marketing mix?

A. Profit
B. Place
C. Productivity
D. Personnel

Answer: B.

2 marks
Outline

Outline one reason why a business's marketing mix elements need to work together.

Structure guide: two linked points — eg "The elements need to work together so customers receive a consistent message (1), since a mismatch, such as a premium product sold very cheaply, can confuse customers about what the business is actually offering (1)." Points must connect; unlinked points cap the mark at 1.

3 marks
Explain

Explain one way in which technology has changed how businesses promote their products.

Structure guide: 1 mark identifying a way, plus 2 further marks developing it — eg "Digital communication allows businesses to target adverts at specific customers (1), such as showing an advert for a product to someone who has recently searched for something similar online (1), making promotion more cost-effective than a generic advert shown to everyone regardless of interest (1)."

Case study — Marlin Toys: Marlin Toys sells handmade wooden toys through its own small website and a market stall. A new competitor has just launched, selling similar wooden toys online at a much lower price. Marlin Toys is considering two options: reducing its own prices to match the competitor, or increasing its promotion to emphasise the quality and safety of its handmade toys.
6 marks
AnalyseSection B style

Analyse the impact on Marlin Toys of a competitor entering the market with significantly lower prices.

Structure guide: application (AO2) and analysis (AO3a) together — eg identify that the new competitor is undercutting Marlin Toys on price (AO2), then analyse how this could draw price-sensitive customers away, but that simply matching the lower price risks a damaging price war that squeezes Marlin Toys' margins, especially since its toys are handmade and likely cost more to produce than a mass-manufactured competitor's (AO3a).

9 marks
JustifySection C style

Marlin Toys is considering two options: reducing its own prices to match the competitor, or increasing promotion to emphasise the quality and safety of its handmade toys. Justify which option Marlin Toys should choose.

Structure guide: apply knowledge to Marlin Toys' specific situation (AO2), analyse points on both sides (AO3a) — matching the price directly challenges the competitor but risks a price war Marlin Toys may struggle to sustain as a small handmade producer, while promoting quality and safety plays to a genuine point of difference but depends on customers actually valuing that difference enough to pay more — then reach a clear, justified choice (AO3b).

Key terms

Glossary

Marketing mix
The combination of price, product, promotion and place decisions a business makes.
Integrated marketing mix
A marketing mix where every element sends a consistent message to the customer.
Digital communication
Using the internet and social media to communicate with and target customers.
