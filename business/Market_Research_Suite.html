      Theory & Reference

      Primary vs Secondary Research

      Primary ResearchSecondary Research
      DefinitionOriginal data collected first-hand for a specific purposeExisting data collected by someone else for a different purpose
      ExamplesSurveys, interviews, focus groups, observation, trialsGovernment statistics, trade reports, competitor websites, past sales data
      CostExpensive — time and resources requiredCheap or free — already exists
      RelevanceHighly relevant — designed for this exact purposeMay not match the business need exactly
      Up to dateCurrent — collected nowMay be outdated — check publication dates
      Best forNew products, specific customer insight, attitudesMarket sizing, competitor analysis, economic trends

      The Four Sampling Methods — Compared

      MethodHow it worksAdvantagesDisadvantages
      RandomEvery member of the population has an equal chance of selectionUnbiased; results are generalisable; mathematically soundMay miss small subgroups; requires complete sampling frame
      StratifiedPopulation divided into subgroups; sample taken proportionally from eachGuarantees representation; more precise than randomRequires knowledge of population structure; more complex
      SystematicEvery nth person from an ordered list; first chosen randomly within first intervalSimple to implement; spread across the list; efficientPeriodic bias if list has a repeating pattern
      QuotaResearcher sets targets per subgroup; fills quotas through convenient selectionFast and cheap; no complete sampling frame neededNon-random within quotas; interviewer bias; cannot calculate sampling error

      Qualitative vs Quantitative Data

      • Quantitative data: Numerical, measurable, can be statistically analysed. Examples: survey ratings, sales figures, market share percentages. Generated by surveys, sales data, experiments.
      • Qualitative data: Descriptive, non-numerical, explores opinions and attitudes. Examples: interview transcripts, focus group discussions, customer reviews. Generated by interviews, focus groups, observation.
      • Which to use: Quantitative is best for measuring the size of a problem; qualitative is best for understanding the reason behind it. Most robust market research uses both.
      • A-Level application: A business launching a new product might use qualitative research (focus groups) first to understand customer attitudes, then quantitative (survey) to measure the scale of demand across a larger sample.

      Correlation, Trend Lines and Extrapolation

      • Correlation: A statistical relationship between two variables. Positive correlation = both move in the same direction; negative correlation = they move in opposite directions. Measured by the correlation coefficient (r), which ranges from −1 to +1.
      • Strength of correlation: r = 1.0 is perfect positive; r = 0.7–0.9 is strong; r = 0.4–0.7 is moderate; r below 0.4 is weak. In business, correlations above 0.7 are generally considered meaningful.
      • Correlation vs causation: A correlation does not prove that one variable causes the other. Ice cream sales and drowning incidents are positively correlated — both are caused by hot weather. This is a classic exam evaluation point.
      • R² (coefficient of determination): Tells you what percentage of the variation in one variable is explained by the other. R² = 0.64 means 64% of the variation in sales is explained by ad spend.
      • Extrapolation: Extending a trend line beyond the data range to predict future values. Risky because it assumes current trends will continue — ignoring competitive responses, economic changes, saturation effects and random shocks.

      Confidence Intervals

      A 95% confidence interval means that if the same research were conducted 100 times, 95 of those samples would produce results within the stated range. It shows how much uncertainty exists around a finding.

      Wider bands mean more uncertainty — caused by smaller sample sizes, greater data variability, or higher confidence level requirements. The key exam point is that all survey findings have a margin of error, and businesses should state the confidence level alongside any statistics.

      Exam Tips

      Market Research — Common Exam Mistakes
      • Always distinguish between primary and secondary research before evaluating — the question usually requires both
      • When evaluating sampling, always link to the specific limitations of that method (quota = interviewer bias; random = may miss subgroups)
      • Never confuse correlation and causation — "the data suggests a relationship between X and Y, but this does not prove X causes Y" earns evaluation marks
      • Confidence intervals: wider band = more uncertain = less reliable for business decisions — say this explicitly
      • Extrapolation evaluation: always mention that it assumes ceteris paribus and that the further into the future, the less reliable the prediction
      • Qualitative vs quantitative: link to business context — a small start-up might only afford secondary research; a Plc can fund large primary surveys
      Evaluation: Is market research always worth doing?

      Market research has costs — financial, time, and delay. For low-cost decisions or fast-moving markets, the cost of research may exceed the value of the information gained. Entrepreneurs sometimes argue that intuition and rapid iteration (test, launch, iterate) is more valuable than expensive research that is out of date by the time results arrive. This is a valid A-Level evaluation point against recommending market research in every context.
